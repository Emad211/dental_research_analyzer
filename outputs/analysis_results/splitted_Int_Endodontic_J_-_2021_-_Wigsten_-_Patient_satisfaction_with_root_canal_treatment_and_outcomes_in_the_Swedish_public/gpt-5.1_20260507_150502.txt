A1. Descriptive statistics

A2. Normality and distributional checks for VAS responses  
(e.g., Shapiro–Wilk test + visual inspection)  

- Purpose:  
  - Assess whether VAS scores (Items 2, 4–7) are approximately normally distributed, to decide between parametric and non‑parametric tests and to guide modeling assumptions.  

- Why appropriate:  
  1. VAS scales, while often treated as continuous, may be skewed and bounded (0–10), particularly for pain, and many satisfaction variables likely show ceiling or floor effects.  
  2. The sample size (N ≈ 159) is moderate; parametric tests may be robust, but checking assumptions avoids misinterpretation.  

- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - Formal normality tests (like Shapiro–Wilk) can be overly sensitive at moderate sample size: trivial deviations may yield statistically significant results.  

- When not to use:  
  - Do not rely exclusively on the p‑value of normality tests to choose methods; always combine with Q‑Q plots/histograms and clinical judgment.  

- Alternatives if assumptions violated:  
  - Use non‑parametric tests (Mann–Whitney U, Kruskal–Wallis) and robust regression, or transform data (e.g., log‑transform) if conceptually reasonable, though transformation is usually not ideal for bounded 0–10 VAS; better to use non‑parametric methods.  

A3. Comparison of responders vs non‑responders (response bias assessment)  
Tests: chi‑square tests and t‑tests / Mann–Whitney U  

- Purpose:  
  - Evaluate whether patients who responded to the questionnaire differ systematically from non‑responders with regard to available baseline characteristics (sex, age, jaw, tooth group, baseline diagnosis, baseline symptoms).  

- Why appropriate:  
  1. Non‑response bias is a major concern in survey‑based cohort studies; baseline characteristics are known for all 243, allowing assessment.  
  2. Differences may affect generalizability and interpretation of satisfaction estimates and retention rates.  

- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - Baseline variables may have missing data; tests on incomplete subsets could be underpowered or misleading.  

- When not to use:  
  - If the number of non‑responders in some categories is extremely small, or if data are entirely missing for certain key variables in non‑responders, formal comparisons may not be informative.  

- Alternatives if assumptions violated:  
  - Use Fisher’s exact test instead of chi‑square when expected cell counts <5.  
  - Use Mann–Whitney U instead of t‑test if age distribution is highly skewed.  
  - Describe differences qualitatively if statistical tests are not feasible.  

A4. Test–retest reliability of VAS items  
Primary test: Intraclass Correlation Coefficient (ICC)  

- Purpose:  
  - Quantify the degree of agreement between first and second measurements of continuous VAS items (pain intensity, pain during RCT, aesthetics, function, cost‑worth).  

- Why appropriate:  
  1. The test–retest design measures the same subjects twice with the same continuous instrument; ICC is the standard measure for reliability of continuous responses (Koo & Li, Shrout & Fleiss).  
  2. ICC provides a single summary index (0–1) of reliability that incorporates both within‑subject and between‑subject variability and allows classification (poor, moderate, good, excellent).  

- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - ICC assumes that differences are normally distributed and that there is no systematic change between test and retest (e.g., no major shift in patient status). If recall bias or real clinical change occurs between time points, ICC will underestimate “instrument” reliability.  

- When not to use:  
  - If time between test and retest is long enough that true patient status is likely to change (e.g., new pain episodes), or if there is evidence of systematic drift between time points.  
  - If the variable shows extreme floor/ceiling effects leading to near‑zero variance, ICC is unstable and uninterpretable.  

- Alternatives if assumptions violated:  
  - Bland–Altman plots to visually assess agreement and possible bias.  
  - Spearman’s rank correlation coefficient as a simpler (but less comprehensive) measure of association, recognizing it assesses association, not agreement.  

Implementation details:  
- Use two‑way mixed‑effects model, single measurement, absolute agreement ICC (e.g., ICC(3,1) per Shrout & Fleiss) – the same raters (questionnaire form) for all subjects.  
- Calculate 95% confidence intervals around ICC.  

A5. Test–retest reliability of categorical items  
Primary test: Cohen’s kappa (or weighted kappa for ordered categories)  

- Purpose:  
  - Quantify agreement between test and retest for categorical items:  
    - Tooth retained vs extracted (Item 1a).  
    - RCT completed yes/no (Item 1b).  
    - Symptom options (Item 3, each symptom as binary indicator).  
    - Item 8: yes/no/uncertain (weighted kappa may be considered if “uncertain” is treated as intermediate).  

- Why appropriate:  
  1. Kappa adjusts for agreement expected by chance, offering a more conservative and interpretable reliability measure than simple percent agreement.  
  2. The number of categories is small; Cohen’s kappa is standard and widely interpretable (e.g., Landis & Koch benchmarks).  

- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - If some categories are very rare (e.g., very few “no” responses in Item 8), kappa can be unstable and paradoxically low despite high observed agreement.  

- When not to use:  
  - If the sample size for a given item at both time points is very small or category frequencies are extremely unbalanced (e.g., almost all “yes”).  

- Alternatives if assumptions violated:  
  - Report simple percent agreement with exact binomial confidence intervals.  
  - For ordinal items, use weighted kappa with appropriate weights if frequencies allow.  

A6. Association between tooth retention and baseline factors  
Primary tests: chi‑square tests or Fisher’s exact tests; logistic regression  

- Purpose:  
  - Examine whether tooth retention (retained vs extracted) 1–3 years after RCT initiation is associated with baseline factors (sex, age category, jaw, tooth group, baseline symptoms, diagnosis).  

- Why appropriate:  
  1. Retention is a key clinical outcome; understanding predictors helps contextualize satisfaction and outcomes.  
  2. Baseline variables are categorical or can be categorized, making chi‑square and logistic regression natural choices.  

- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - The number of extracted teeth may be small; chi‑square assumptions may be violated due to sparse cells; logistic regression may be unstable.  

- When not to use:  
  - When expected cell counts in a contingency table are <5 in most cells: chi‑square should be replaced by Fisher’s exact or categories should be collapsed.  
  - Logistic regression should not be fitted with too many predictors relative to the number of events (e.g., <10 events per predictor parameter).  

- Alternatives if assumptions violated:  
  - Fisher’s exact test for 2×2 tables or small cell counts.  
  - Collapse categories (e.g., combine incisor/premolar vs molar) to increase cell counts.  
  - Use penalized logistic regression (e.g., Firth correction) if events are rare.  

A7. Comparison of patient‑reported outcomes across key groups  
Primary tests:  
- For two‑group comparisons (e.g., male vs female, retained vs extracted where applicable):  
  - Student’s t‑test for independent samples, or Mann–Whitney U test.  
- For three or more groups (e.g., age groups, tooth groups, diagnoses):  
  - One‑way ANOVA, or Kruskal–Wallis test.  

Outcomes considered: VAS scores (Items 2, 4–7) and Item 8 categories.  

- Purpose:  
  - Investigate whether satisfaction/pain/functon/aesthetics/cost‑worth differ across clinically relevant groups:  
    - Sex (male vs female).  
    - Age categories (<40, 40–60, >60).  
    - Tooth group (incisor vs premolar vs molar).  
    - Jaw (maxilla vs mandible).  
    - Baseline diagnosis (pulpitis vs necrosis with apical periodontitis vs others).  
    - Baseline symptoms (asymptomatic vs pain).  
    - Clinical outcome: tooth retained vs extracted; RCT completed vs not (for those retained).  

- Why appropriate:  
  1. These group comparisons directly address patterns of satisfaction and pain across common clinical strata, central to the study question.  
  2. Parametric (t‑test/ANOVA) or non‑parametric (Mann–Whitney/Kruskal–Wallis) methods are standard for comparing continuous outcomes between independent groups.  

- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - VAS data can be skewed or show ceiling/floor effects; ANOVA assumptions (normality, homoscedasticity) may not hold.  
  - Some groups (e.g., extracted teeth or rare diagnoses) might have small sample sizes, reducing test power and reliability.  

- When not to use:  
  - Do not use t‑test/ANOVA if distributions are highly non‑normal with heteroscedasticity and sample sizes are small or unbalanced; in such cases, non‑parametric alternatives are preferable.  
  - Do not use ANOVA with more outcome categories than you can reasonably interpret; be cautious with multiple post‑hoc comparisons without adjustment.  

- Alternatives if assumptions violated:  
  - Use Mann–Whitney U test (two groups) or Kruskal–Wallis test (≥3 groups).  
  - Use robust ANOVA or bootstrapped confidence intervals for mean differences.  

A8. Association between Item 8 (“Would choose RCT again?”) and other factors  
Primary tests: chi‑square test / Fisher’s exact; ordinal logistic regression or multinomial logistic regression  

- Purpose:  
  - Assess whether willingness to choose RCT again (yes/no/uncertain) is associated with:  
    - Clinical outcome (tooth retained vs extracted; RCT completed vs not).  
    - VAS measures (e.g., pain, function, aesthetics).  
    - Baseline characteristics (age, sex, tooth type, diagnosis, baseline symptoms).  

- Why appropriate:  
  1. Item 8 is a central measure of overall satisfaction and perceived appropriateness of RCT; its association with clinical and subjective factors is important.  
  2. Categorical methods (chi‑square, logistic regression) are well suited for a 3‑category outcome.  

- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - Ordinal logistic regression assumes proportional odds (same effect across cut points); this assumption may not hold.  
  - Some categories (e.g., “no”) may have very low frequencies, compromising model stability.  

- When not to use:  
  - Do not use ordinal logistic regression if the proportional odds assumption is clearly violated or if sparsity makes estimation unstable.  

- Alternatives if assumptions violated:  
  - Use multinomial logistic regression (treating the three categories as nominal) with “yes” as reference.  
  - Collapse “no” and “uncertain” into a single “not clearly yes” category and use binary logistic regression, if clinically acceptable and sample sizes are small.

Outcomes:

- Purpose:

- Why appropriate:

B4. Bland–Altman analysis for test–retest agreement of VAS items  
- Purpose:  
  - Complement ICC with graphical analysis of agreement between first and second VAS measurements, detecting systematic bias, trends, or outliers.  

- Why appropriate:

- Why it may not be appropriate:

- When not to use:

- Why it may not be appropriate:

- When not to use:

- Alternatives if assumptions violated:

TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS  
============================  

Below are methods that should generally NOT be used in this study, with explanations.

1. Student’s paired t‑test for test–retest “reliability” as a stand‑alone metric  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Paired t‑test only tests whether mean difference between two time points is zero; it does not measure agreement. It can show non‑significant differences even with poor agreement (high variability).  
- Better alternative:  
  - ICC for continuous items; Bland–Altman plots; kappa for categorical items.  

2. Pearson correlation alone as a measure of test–retest reliability  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Correlation measures linear association, not agreement. A perfect correlation can exist even if all retest scores are systematically higher than test scores.  
- Better alternative:  
  - ICC and Bland–Altman analysis.  

3. Parametric tests assuming normality without checking (e.g., ANOVA/t‑tests on strongly skewed VAS without any assessment)  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - VAS scores for pain and satisfaction often show heavy ceiling/floor effects; normality and homoscedasticity may be violated, invalidating parametric p‑values.  
- Better alternative:  
  - Check distributions; use non‑parametric tests (Mann–Whitney, Kruskal–Wallis) or robust methods.  

4. Repeated measures ANOVA for test–retest with two time points  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - For reliability, we are interested in agreement, not change over time; repeated measures ANOVA tests mean differences and time effects, not reliability. For two time points, it’s inefficient and misaligned with the aim.  
- Better alternative:  
  - ICC and Bland–Altman plots.  

5. Survival analysis / Kaplan–Meier modeling with follow‑up time as currently structured  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - The study collects retention/extraction status at a single questionnaire time point (1–3 years), not full time‑to‑event data with exact extraction dates for each tooth. A single snapshot is insufficient for proper survival analysis.  
- Better alternative:  
  - Logistic regression for retention (yes/no) with time since baseline as a covariate.  

6. Parametric tests on ordinal Item 8 as if it were continuous  
(e.g., treating yes/no/uncertain as scores 0/1/2 and applying t‑test/ANOVA)  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Ordinal scale with three categories is not well approximated by a continuous variable; distances between categories are not meaningfully equal.  
- Better alternative:  
  - Chi‑square/Fisher’s exact tests; ordinal or multinomial logistic regression.  

7. Complex multilevel models (e.g., random effects for clinic) without justification  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Only one tooth per patient, but multiple clinics; while cluster effects at clinic level are possible, the total N=159 and 20 clinics imply small cluster sizes, making random effects estimation unstable. If between‑clinic variability is not a primary focus, simple models with clinic as fixed or ignored may suffice.  
- Better alternative:  
  - If necessary, use cluster‑robust standard errors by clinic; or include clinic as a fixed factor if sample size per clinic allows.  

8. Chi‑square tests with very sparse contingency tables  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - With low expected cell counts (<5), chi‑square approximations are unreliable, yielding invalid p‑values.  
- Better alternative:  
  - Fisher’s exact test; or collapse categories to increase cell counts.

TASK 4: SUMMARY AND JUSTIFICATION OF OVERALL ANALYTICAL APPROACH  
=================================================================  

- The study is a prospective cohort with a primarily descriptive objective: to characterize patient‑reported outcomes 1–3 years after initiation of RCT in general practice, and to evaluate reliability of the questionnaire.  

- The analytical plan therefore centers on:  
  1. Comprehensive descriptive statistics of retention, RCT completion, VAS outcomes, and Item 8 responses.  
  2. Basic inferential comparisons (chi‑square/Fisher, t‑test/Mann–Whitney, ANOVA/Kruskal–Wallis) to explore how satisfaction and pain vary by baseline characteristics and clinical outcomes.  
  3. Limited multivariable modeling (linear and logistic) to adjust for key confounders and quantify adjusted associations, while respecting sample size limitations.  
  4. Rigorous reliability assessment of the questionnaire using ICC and kappa, complemented by Bland–Altman plots for continuous items and percent agreement for categorical items.  

- The plan explicitly incorporates checks of distributional assumptions and offers non‑parametric/robust alternatives as needed, which is appropriate for VAS data and moderately sized samples.  

- Together, these analyses will:  
  - Provide clinically meaningful estimates of patient satisfaction and pain after RCT in general practice.  
  - Identify patient and tooth factors associated with these outcomes.  
  - Demonstrate the reliability of the instrument used to measure patient perceptions.  

This approach balances methodological rigor with feasibility for the available sample size and the observational design of the study.
