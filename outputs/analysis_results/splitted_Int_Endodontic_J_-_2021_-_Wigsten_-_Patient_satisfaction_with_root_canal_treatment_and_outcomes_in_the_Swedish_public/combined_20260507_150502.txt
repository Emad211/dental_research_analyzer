# CATEGORY A – MANDATORY (core analyses)

## A1. Descriptive statistics (including skip-pattern denominators)
**Test/Method:** Descriptive summaries (no hypothesis test)  
**Purpose:** Quantify tooth retention, treatment completion, pain/satisfaction distributions, and response patterns.  
**Why appropriate (≥2):**
1. Primary aim is to *elicit* patient evaluations—requires transparent distributional reporting.
2. Many outcomes are bounded/ordinal-like (VAS); descriptive plots (histograms/boxplots) reveal skewness and floor effects.  
**Why may not be appropriate (≥1):** Not inferential; cannot address associations or predictors.  
**When NOT to use:** Never omit; but don’t use descriptives alone to claim differences/associations.  
**Alternative if assumptions violated:** N/A (nonparametric summarization: medians/IQR).

---

## A2. Test–retest reliability for VAS items (continuous)
**Test:** **Intraclass Correlation Coefficient (ICC)** (two-way mixed effects, absolute agreement; single-measure), with 95% CI  
**Purpose:** Assess stability of repeated VAS responses (Items 2,4–7 as applicable per skip pattern).  
**Why appropriate:**
1. Test–retest reliability for continuous measures is best captured by agreement, not correlation alone.
2. ICC quantifies proportion of variance attributable to between-subject differences vs measurement error.  
**Why may not be appropriate:**
- ICC can be distorted if between-subject variability is low (restricted range), yielding low ICC despite good agreement.  
**When NOT to use:**
- When retest timing is too long and true change is likely (violates “stable trait” assumption).  
**Alternative if assumptions violated:**
- **Bland–Altman analysis** (limits of agreement) as primary agreement metric; or **Lin’s concordance correlation**.

---

## A3. Test–retest agreement for categorical items
**Test:** **Cohen’s kappa** (weighted kappa for ordinal; unweighted for nominal)  
- Item 8 (yes/no/uncertain): **weighted kappa** (ordinal assumption plausible)  
- Item 1 outcomes (retained yes/no; completed yes/no): **unweighted kappa**  
**Purpose:** Quantify agreement beyond chance for categorical/ordinal responses.  
**Why appropriate:**
1. Specifically designed for categorical agreement beyond chance.
2. Allows comparability with common reliability interpretation frameworks (e.g., Landis & Koch—though interpret cautiously).  
**Why may not be appropriate:**
- Kappa is sensitive to prevalence and marginal imbalance (“kappa paradox”).  
**When NOT to use:**
- When categories are extremely imbalanced or when multiple selections per item (Item 3) without adaptation.  
**Alternative if assumptions violated:**
- **Gwet’s AC1/AC2** for prevalence-robust agreement; for multi-label Item 3, use **percent agreement per option** and/or **Fleiss kappa per option** only if framed as separate yes/no items.

---

## A4. Compare respondents vs non-respondents on baseline characteristics (nonresponse bias check)
**Tests:**
- **Chi-square test** (or Fisher’s exact if sparse) for categorical baseline variables (gender, age group, jaw, tooth group, baseline symptoms, diagnosis categories)
- **t-test / Mann–Whitney U** only if any continuous baseline variables exist (not clearly present here; age is categorized)  
**Purpose:** Assess selection/nonresponse bias in who returned the questionnaire.  
**Why appropriate:**
1. Response rate is ~67%; nonresponse could bias patient-reported outcomes.
2. Baseline data exist for the full contacted cohort (n=236), enabling comparison.  
**Why may not be appropriate:**
- Statistical significance can reflect sample size rather than meaningful imbalance; missing baseline data may complicate comparisons.  
**When NOT to use:**
- When baseline variables are heavily missing/non-comparable between groups.  
**Alternative if assumptions violated:**
- Use **exact tests** (Fisher/exact multinomial) or **standardized mean differences (SMDs)** as a non-p-value balance metric (Recommended).

# CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED (improves rigor)

## B1. Modelling predictors of key binary outcomes (retention; completion)
**Test/Model:** **Logistic regression** with clinic-robust SEs or mixed-effects logistic regression (random intercept for clinic)  
**Outcomes:**  
- Tooth retained (yes/no)  
- RCT completed with root filling (yes/no)  
**Predictors (examples from baseline):** gender, age group, tooth group, jaw, baseline symptoms, diagnosis, time-to-questionnaire.  
**Purpose:** Identify factors associated with retention/completion; adjust for confounding.  
**Why appropriate:**
1. Binary outcomes with multiple covariates → logistic regression is standard.
2. Allows adjustment and quantification via adjusted ORs + CIs; accounts for clustering by clinic.  
**Why may not be appropriate:**
- If events are rare in one category (separation) or small cell counts for some diagnoses.  
**When NOT to use:**
- If outcome counts are too low for multivariable modelling (rule-of-thumb EPV issues) without penalization.  
**Alternative if assumptions violated:**
- **Firth penalized logistic regression** (reduces small-sample bias/separation) or **exact logistic regression**.

---

## B2. Modelling VAS outcomes vs baseline predictors
**Test/Model options (choose based on distribution):**
- **Linear regression / linear mixed model** (with clinic random intercept) if residuals acceptable
- **Quantile regression (median regression)** if skewed/floor effects
- **Beta regression** only if VAS is transformed to (0,1) and not piled at boundaries  
**Purpose:** Evaluate which baseline factors relate to pain/satisfaction dimensions (Items 2,4–7).  
**Why appropriate:**
1. Moves beyond unadjusted comparisons; handles confounding.
2. Mixed/robust approaches address clinic clustering and non-normality.  
**Why may not be appropriate:**
- VAS data often have heaping at 0 and non-constant variance; linear model may misfit.  
**When NOT to use:**
- Don’t use linear regression if diagnostics show severe non-normal residuals, strong heteroscedasticity, or heavy boundary mass.  
**Alternative if assumptions violated:**
- **Quantile regression**; or **ordinal regression** if VAS is categorized a priori (but avoid post hoc categorization).

---

## B3. Association between “would choose RCT again” (Item 8) and outcomes/experience
**Test/Model:** **Ordinal logistic regression** (proportional odds) for Item 8 (yes / uncertain / no)  
Predictors: pain now (Item 2), pain during RCT (Item 4), aesthetics, function, cost, plus baseline covariates.  
**Purpose:** Identify which experience/outcome dimensions drive retrospective willingness.  
**Why appropriate:**
1. Item 8 is ordered; ordinal model uses ordering efficiently.
2. Provides interpretable common OR for worse category.  
**Why may not be appropriate:**
- Proportional odds assumption may be violated; “uncertain” may not be strictly between yes/no conceptually.  
**When NOT to use:**
- If category counts are very small or if ordering is not defensible.  
**Alternative if assumptions violated:**
- **Multinomial logistic regression** (nominal); or partial proportional odds model.

---

## B4. Multiple testing control for secondary endpoints
**Method:** Holm correction or False Discovery Rate (Benjamini–Hochberg) across families of comparisons  
**Purpose:** Reduce false positives given multiple questionnaire items and predictors.  
**Why appropriate:**
1. Many outcomes (Items 2–7 plus binary endpoints) invite multiplicity.
2. Clarifies which findings are robust vs exploratory.  
**Why may not be appropriate:**
- Can be overly conservative, increasing false negatives in modest samples.  
**When NOT to use:**
- For strictly pre-specified single primary endpoint analysis (then not needed).  
**Alternative if assumptions violated:** Pre-specify a **primary endpoint** and treat others as exploratory with estimation focus.

---

## B5. Missing data evaluation (and handling)
**Method:** Missingness tables + **Little’s MCAR test** (if feasible), and sensitivity analyses (complete case vs imputed)  
**Purpose:** Understand and mitigate bias from missing baseline covariates and partial questionnaire responses.  
**Why appropriate:**
1. Methods explicitly state missing left empty; ignoring may bias associations.
2. Sensitivity analyses strengthen credibility.  
**Why may not be appropriate:**
- Little’s test is sample-size sensitive and not definitive; missingness mechanisms rarely proven.  
**When NOT to use:**
- When missingness is trivial (<~5%) and clearly ignorable.  
**Alternative if assumptions violated:**
- **Multiple imputation by chained equations (MICE)** for covariates/outcomes where sensible; or inverse probability weighting for response.

## TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS (should NOT be used)

1. **Paired t-test as the sole test–retest method for VAS reliability**
   - Tests mean difference only (bias), not reliability/agreement; can show “no difference” even with poor reproducibility.

2. **Pearson correlation for test–retest reliability (alone)**
   - Measures association, not agreement; perfect correlation can occur with systematic shifts.

3. **One-way ANOVA / multiple t-tests ignoring clinic clustering**
   - Patients are nested within 20 clinics; ignoring clustering can underestimate SEs and inflate type I error in predictor analyses.

4. **Linear regression on categorical outcomes (e.g., tooth retained yes/no)**
   - Violates distributional assumptions; predicted probabilities can be outside [0,1].

5. **Chi-square test for Item 3 without handling multi-response nature**
   - Item 3 allows multiple selections; treating it as a single multinomial outcome is incorrect unless re-coded appropriately.

6. **Post hoc dichotomization of VAS (e.g., “satisfied vs not” by arbitrary cutpoints) as primary analysis**
   - Loses information, reduces power, creates cutpoint-dependent results; only consider if clinically pre-specified.

---

## TASK 4: SUMMARY (overall analytical approach)

A complete analysis should (1) **describe** patient-centred outcomes 1–3 years after RCT initiation with correct denominators reflecting the questionnaire’s skip patterns, (2) formally quantify **test–retest reliability** using **ICC for VAS** and **(weighted) kappa for categorical items**, and (3) strengthen validity by checking **nonresponse bias** and, where inferential aims are pursued, using **multivariable models** that account for **clinic clustering**, report **effect sizes with CIs**, and apply **multiplicity control** for secondary/exploratory comparisons.

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY
1. **Descriptive Statistics**:

2. **Chi-Square Test (or Fisher’s Exact Test)**:  
   - **Purpose**: Compare categorical variables (e.g., tooth retention vs. gender).

   - **Purpose**: Assess test-retest reliability of continuous variables (e.g., VAS scores).  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Evaluates differences between paired responses.  
     2. Suitable for continuous data with normal distribution.

   - **When NOT to Use**: If data are not normally distributed.  
   - **Alternative**: Wilcoxon Signed-Rank Test for non-normal data.

   - **Purpose**: Assess test-retest reliability of questionnaire responses.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Measures consistency or agreement between repeated measures.  
     2. Recommended for reliability studies.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Sensitive to sample size and variability.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If sample size is too small or data are highly inconsistent.

TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS  
============================  

Below are methods that should generally NOT be used in this study, with explanations.

1. Student’s paired t‑test for test–retest “reliability” as a stand‑alone metric  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Paired t‑test only tests whether mean difference between two time points is zero; it does not measure agreement. It can show non‑significant differences even with poor agreement (high variability).  
- Better alternative:  
  - ICC for continuous items; Bland–Altman plots; kappa for categorical items.  

2. Pearson correlation alone as a measure of test–retest reliability  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Correlation measures linear association, not agreement. A perfect correlation can exist even if all retest scores are systematically higher than test scores.  
- Better alternative:  
  - ICC and Bland–Altman analysis.  

3. Parametric tests assuming normality without checking (e.g., ANOVA/t‑tests on strongly skewed VAS without any assessment)  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - VAS scores for pain and satisfaction often show heavy ceiling/floor effects; normality and homoscedasticity may be violated, invalidating parametric p‑values.  
- Better alternative:  
  - Check distributions; use non‑parametric tests (Mann–Whitney, Kruskal–Wallis) or robust methods.  

4. Repeated measures ANOVA for test–retest with two time points  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - For reliability, we are interested in agreement, not change over time; repeated measures ANOVA tests mean differences and time effects, not reliability. For two time points, it’s inefficient and misaligned with the aim.  
- Better alternative:  
  - ICC and Bland–Altman plots.  

5. Survival analysis / Kaplan–Meier modeling with follow‑up time as currently structured  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - The study collects retention/extraction status at a single questionnaire time point (1–3 years), not full time‑to‑event data with exact extraction dates for each tooth. A single snapshot is insufficient for proper survival analysis.  
- Better alternative:  
  - Logistic regression for retention (yes/no) with time since baseline as a covariate.  

6. Parametric tests on ordinal Item 8 as if it were continuous  
(e.g., treating yes/no/uncertain as scores 0/1/2 and applying t‑test/ANOVA)  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Ordinal scale with three categories is not well approximated by a continuous variable; distances between categories are not meaningfully equal.  
- Better alternative:  
  - Chi‑square/Fisher’s exact tests; ordinal or multinomial logistic regression.  

7. Complex multilevel models (e.g., random effects for clinic) without justification  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Only one tooth per patient, but multiple clinics; while cluster effects at clinic level are possible, the total N=159 and 20 clinics imply small cluster sizes, making random effects estimation unstable. If between‑clinic variability is not a primary focus, simple models with clinic as fixed or ignored may suffice.  
- Better alternative:  
  - If necessary, use cluster‑robust standard errors by clinic; or include clinic as a fixed factor if sample size per clinic allows.  

8. Chi‑square tests with very sparse contingency tables  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - With low expected cell counts (<5), chi‑square approximations are unreliable, yielding invalid p‑values.  
- Better alternative:  
  - Fisher’s exact test; or collapse categories to increase cell counts.

    *   *Item 8:* Retrospectively choose RCT again (yes, no, uncertain). [Categorical/Nominal]
*   **Data Collection Methods:** Mailed self-administered questionnaire (adapted from Dugas et al.) with a 10-cm Visual Analogue Scale (VAS) for continuous items. Non-responders received a reminder after 3 weeks and a telephone call.

### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY (Crucial for stated aims)

**1. Intraclass Correlation Coefficient (ICC)**
*   **Purpose:** To evaluate the test-retest reliability of the continuous questionnaire items (VAS scores for Items 2, 4, 5, 6, 7) using the sub-sample of 44 patients.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) It measures absolute agreement and consistency between two time points, which is the gold standard for continuous reliability data. 
    2) It accounts for both within-subject and between-subject variability.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** If the VAS scores are heavily skewed (e.g., almost all patients score "0" for pain), the lack of variance can artificially deflate the ICC score.
*   **When NOT to use:** Do not use for categorical variables (Items 1, 3, 8).
*   **Alternative:** Lin's Concordance Correlation Coefficient, or weighted Cohen’s Kappa (if VAS is collapsed into ordinal categories).

**2. Cohen’s Kappa (Unweighted)**
*   **Purpose:** To evaluate the test-retest reliability of the categorical questionnaire items (Items 1, 3, 8).
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) It measures agreement between two time points for categorical data. 
    2) It accounts for the possibility of agreement occurring by chance.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** Kappa is highly sensitive to trait prevalence; if 95% of patients retained their tooth, the Kappa value might be paradoxically low despite high raw agreement (the "Kappa paradox").
*   **When NOT to use:** Do not use for continuous/VAS data.
*   **Alternative:** Gwet’s AC1 statistic (which is robust against the prevalence paradox) or simple percentage agreement.

#### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED (Enhances quality and rigor)

**1. Mann-Whitney U Test / Kruskal-Wallis H Test**
*   **Purpose:** To compare follow-up VAS scores (pain, aesthetics, function, cost) across baseline patient groups (e.g., male vs. female, asymptomatic vs. pain at baseline, tooth types).
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) VAS scores for pain and satisfaction in dental outcomes are notoriously non-normally distributed (heavily skewed toward "0" pain or high satisfaction). 
    2) These non-parametric tests do not assume a normal distribution.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** They test for differences in mean ranks/medians, not means, which can sometimes be less intuitive for clinical interpretation.
*   **When NOT to use:** When data are perfectly normally distributed (which reduces statistical power compared to parametric tests).
*   **Alternative:** Independent t-test or ANOVA (only if data are transformed or proven to be normally distributed).

**2. Chi-Square Test of Independence (or Fisher’s Exact Test)**
*   **Purpose:** 
    1) To assess non-response bias by comparing baseline characteristics of responders (n=159) vs. non-responders (n=77). 
    2) To test associations between baseline categorical variables and categorical outcomes (e.g., baseline diagnosis vs. tooth retention).
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) It is the standard test for comparing proportions between independent groups. 
    2) It easily handles nominal variables like tooth type or diagnosis.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** If the sample size in specific sub-categories is too small, the test loses validity.
*   **When NOT to use:** When expected cell counts are less than 5 in more than 20% of the cells.
*   **Alternative:** Fisher’s Exact Test (if expected cell counts are <5).

#### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL (Supplementary insights)

**1. Multivariable Logistic Regression**
*   **Purpose:** To identify independent baseline predictors of binary outcomes (e.g., Tooth retained: Yes/No, or Would choose RCT again: Yes/No).
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) It allows for the adjustment of confounding variables (e.g., adjusting for age and gender when looking at the effect of baseline pain on tooth retention). 
    2) It provides Odds Ratios (OR), which are easily interpretable clinical metrics.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** The sample size (n=159) might be too small to include all baseline variables without overfitting the model (rule of thumb: 10-15 events per predictor).
*   **When NOT to use:** When the outcome is continuous (VAS).
*   **Alternative:** Penalized regression (e.g., Firth's logistic regression) if there is quasi-complete separation or low event rates.

**2. Spearman’s Rank-Order Correlation**
*   **Purpose:** To explore relationships between the different VAS outcomes (e.g., does high recollected pain during treatment correlate with feeling the treatment was not worth the cost?).
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) It handles monotonic relationships without assuming linearity. 
    2) It is robust to outliers and non-normal distributions typical of VAS data.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It only measures association, not causation, and does not account for confounding variables.
*   **When NOT to use:** When looking for absolute agreement (reliability) rather than just correlation.
*   **Alternative:** Kendall’s Tau (better for smaller datasets with many tied ranks).

### TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS

*   **Paired t-test (for test-retest reliability):** Should NOT be used to assess reliability of the VAS scores. A paired t-test only checks if the *mean* difference between time 1 and time 2 is zero. It does not measure agreement. If patient scores change drastically but in opposite directions, the mean difference could be zero, falsely implying high reliability.
*   **Pearson Correlation Coefficient (for test-retest reliability):** Should NOT be used. Pearson measures linear relationship, not absolute agreement. If a patient scores exactly 2 points higher on every VAS question the second time, Pearson's *r* would be 1.0 (perfect correlation), but the actual agreement is poor.
*   **Independent Samples t-test / One-way ANOVA (for VAS outcomes):** Should NOT be used unless strict normality checks are passed. Pain and satisfaction scores in endodontics are almost always heavily skewed (e.g., most patients report 0 pain). Using parametric tests on this data violates the assumption of normality and can lead to Type I or Type II errors.
*   **Kaplan-Meier Survival Analysis:** Should NOT be used for this specific dataset. Although the study looks at tooth retention, it is a cross-sectional follow-up at a variable time point (1-3 years). The exact time-to-event (date of extraction) is not collected via this questionnaire, making survival analysis impossible.

### TASK 4: SUMMARY

The overall analytical approach must prioritize two distinct phases: **validation** and **observation**. 

First, the analytical plan must validate the questionnaire using the test-retest sub-sample. Because reliability is a core aim of the study, utilizing Intraclass Correlation Coefficients (ICC) for continuous VAS data and Cohen's Kappa for categorical data is paramount to establish that the instrument measures patient sentiment consistently. 

Second, the observational phase must account for the inherent nature of patient-reported outcomes. Because VAS scores for pain and satisfaction are highly likely to be skewed, the approach relies heavily on robust non-parametric tests (Mann-Whitney U, Kruskal-Wallis) to compare outcomes across baseline clinical characteristics. Furthermore, because a significant portion of the cohort did not respond (32.6%), a non-response bias analysis using Chi-square tests is critical to ensure the findings are generalizable to the broader population of patients receiving RCT in general dental practice. Optional multivariable regressions can be utilized to isolate specific predictors of patient satisfaction, provided the sample size supports the number of variables.
