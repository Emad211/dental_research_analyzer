### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

Given the high attrition rate (9 dropouts) and the small final sample size ($N=21$, roughly 10-11 per group), non-parametric tests will likely dominate the analysis, though parametric tests are listed conditionally.

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS
*(Tests without which the analysis would be incomplete)*

**1. Shapiro-Wilk Test**
*   **Purpose:** To assess the normality of the distribution for continuous variables (root length, root dentin thickness).
*   **Why appropriate:** 1) The sample size is very small ($n \approx 10$ per group), making visual inspection of normality (like Q-Q plots) unreliable. 2) It is highly sensitive and specifically designed for small sample sizes ($N < 50$).
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It can sometimes be underpowered in extremely small sample sizes, failing to detect non-normality.
*   **When NOT to use:** Do not use for categorical or ordinal data (e.g., PAI scores, Healed/Diseased status).
*   **Alternative:** Kolmogorov-Smirnov test (though less preferred for small samples).

**2. Mann-Whitney U Test (or Independent Samples t-test if normal)**
*   **Purpose:** To compare the continuous radiographic changes (gain in root length and root thickness) between the A-PRF and PRF groups.
*   **Why appropriate:** 1) It does not assume a normal distribution, which is highly probable given the small sample size. 2) It is ideal for comparing two independent groups on a continuous or ordinal dependent variable.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It tests for differences in mean ranks, not actual means, which can sometimes make clinical interpretation slightly less intuitive than a t-test.
*   **When NOT to use:** Do not use if the data is perfectly normally distributed and variances are equal (use t-test instead to maximize statistical power).
*   **Alternative:** Independent Samples t-test (if Shapiro-Wilk confirms normality and Levene’s test confirms equal variance).

**3. Wilcoxon Signed-Rank Test (or Paired t-test if normal)**
*   **Purpose:** To evaluate within-group changes from baseline to 12 months for root length and dentin thickness (i.e., did A-PRF significantly increase root length from baseline?).
*   **Why appropriate:** 1) It accounts for the paired nature of the data (same tooth measured twice). 2) It does not require the assumption of normally distributed differences.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It discards the magnitude of the differences if they are extreme, relying only on ranks, which might lose some data granularity.
*   **When NOT to use:** Do not use for comparing the A-PRF group against the PRF group (independent samples).
*   **Alternative:** Paired samples t-test (if the differences between paired values are normally distributed).

**4. Fisher’s Exact Test**
*   **Purpose:** To compare the categorical treatment outcomes (Healed / Healing / Diseased) between the two scaffold groups.
*   **Why appropriate:** 1) The sample size is small ($N=21$), meaning expected cell frequencies in a contingency table will almost certainly be less than 5. 2) It provides an exact p-value for small, sparse datasets.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It can be computationally intensive for tables larger than 2x2, though modern software handles 2x3 tables easily.
*   **When NOT to use:** Do not use if the sample size is large and all expected cell counts are $>5$.
*   **Alternative:** Freeman-Halton extension of Fisher's exact test (specifically for 2x3 tables) or Likelihood Ratio test.

#### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED TESTS
*(Tests that enhance the quality and rigor of the analysis)*

**1. Intra-class Correlation Coefficient (ICC)**
*   **Purpose:** To establish intra-rater reliability for the radiographic measurements.
*   **Why appropriate:** 1) The methodology states a "single investigator" performed the radiographic evaluations; proving that this investigator is consistent with themselves is vital for internal validity. 2) It is the gold standard for assessing reliability of continuous measurements (root length/thickness).
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It requires the investigator to have measured a subset of the radiographs at least twice, blinded to their first measurement. If this wasn't done, ICC cannot be calculated.
*   **When NOT to use:** Do not use for categorical data (e.g., Healed/Diseased).
*   **Alternative:** Cohen’s Kappa (for the ordinal PAI scores).

**2. Baseline Equivalence Testing (Mann-Whitney U / Fisher's Exact)**
*   **Purpose:** To ensure the two groups were statistically similar at baseline regarding age, initial root length, and initial PAI scores.
*   **Why appropriate:** 1) Despite randomization, small sample sizes often lead to baseline imbalances by chance. 2) It proves that any post-operative differences are due to the scaffolds, not pre-existing group differences.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** Some statisticians argue that if randomization was truly random, baseline testing is philosophically redundant.
*   **When NOT to use:** Do not use as the primary outcome measure.
*   **Alternative:** ANCOVA (using baseline scores as a covariate), though assumptions likely won't be met here.

#### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL TESTS
*(Tests that provide supplementary insights)*

**1. Effect Size Calculation (Rank-Biserial Correlation or Cohen’s *d*)**
*   **Purpose:** To determine the magnitude of the difference between A-PRF and PRF, not just the statistical significance.
*   **Why appropriate:** 1) P-values are heavily influenced by small sample sizes; effect sizes tell the clinician if the difference is actually meaningful. 2) It aids future researchers in conducting power analyses for systematic reviews.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** Confidence intervals around effect sizes in small samples can be extremely wide, making them difficult to interpret definitively.
*   **When NOT to use:** Do not use as a replacement for inferential statistics, but rather as an accompaniment.
*   **Alternative:** Number Needed to Treat (NNT) for categorical success rates.

**2. Intention-to-Treat (ITT) Analysis using Multiple Imputation**
*   **Purpose:** To account for the 9 patients who dropped out of the study.
*   **Why appropriate:** 1) A dropout rate of 30% (9 out of 30) introduces severe attrition bias. 2) ITT preserves the benefits of the original randomization.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** Imputing data for nearly 30% of a small sample can introduce artificial variance and relies heavily on the assumption that data is Missing at Random (MAR).
*   **When NOT to use:** Do not use if the reasons for dropout are known to be directly related to treatment failure (Missing Not At Random - MNAR), which requires different modeling.
*   **Alternative:** Per-protocol analysis (analyzing only the 21 completers), which is likely what the authors did, but it overestimates treatment efficacy.

### TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS

*   **Pearson’s Chi-Square Test:** Should NOT be used to compare categorical outcomes (Healed/Healing/Diseased). The final sample size is 21. A 2x3 table will inevitably have expected cell counts below 5, violating the fundamental assumption of the Chi-Square test and leading to Type I errors.
*   **Analysis of Variance (ANOVA):** Should NOT be used to compare the groups unless rigorous normality and homogeneity of variance are proven. Given $n \approx 10$ per group, parametric assumptions are highly unlikely to be met.
*   **Pearson Correlation Coefficient:** Should NOT be used. The study objective is to *compare* outcomes between two distinct groups, not to find the linear relationship between two continuous variables.

### TASK 4: SUMMARY

The overall analytical approach must be highly conservative due to the small final sample size ($N=21$) resulting from a high attrition rate. The analysis should begin with descriptive statistics and baseline equivalence testing to ensure the randomization held despite the dropouts. 

Because the sample size is too small to reliably assume a normal distribution, the core analytical framework should rely on robust **non-parametric statistics**. The Mann-Whitney U test will evaluate the superiority of A-PRF over PRF regarding root thickness and length, while the Wilcoxon Signed-Rank test will validate the overall efficacy of the RET procedure within each group. Fisher's Exact test is strictly required for categorical clinical outcomes due to sparse data. Finally, because a single investigator performed all measurements, establishing intra-rater reliability (ICC/Kappa) is critical to validate the study's radiographic findings. Reporting effect sizes will provide much-needed clinical context to the p-values.
