### CATEGORY A — MANDATORY tests/models

#### A1) Mixed-effects ordinal logistic regression (cumulative logit) **or** GEE ordinal model
- **Purpose:** Compare **12‑month ordinal treatment outcome** (Healed/Healing/Diseased) between A‑PRF and PRF, accounting for clustering (two teeth/person).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Outcome is **ordered categorical** with 3 levels; ordinal model uses ordering efficiently.
  2. Can incorporate **patient-level clustering** (random effects) and adjust for baseline covariates (e.g., baseline PAI).
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - **Proportional odds assumption** may be violated; small sample may yield unstable estimates.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If outcome categories are sparse (e.g., near-zero in one category) causing separation/non-convergence.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Partial proportional odds model**, or **multinomial logistic regression** with cluster-robust SEs, or collapse to binary success/failure and use mixed/GEE logistic regression.

#### A2) Mixed-effects linear model (LMM) for continuous change in root length and dentin thickness (baseline→12 months; and/or repeated timepoints)
- **Purpose:** Compare mean **change** in root length and dentin thickness between groups, accounting for baseline value and clustering.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Continuous endpoints; LMM handles **correlated observations** (two teeth/person; repeated time).
  2. Can adjust for **baseline** measurement, improving precision (ANCOVA-type).
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Change scores may be **non-normal** or contain outliers; measurement error in radiographs may be large.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If residuals are extremely non-normal with heavy tails and transformation fails, or strong heteroscedasticity unaddressed.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Robust regression**, **rank-based ANCOVA**, **quantile regression**, or analyze transformed outcomes; for repeated measures use **GEE with robust SEs**.

#### A3) Fisher’s exact test (or Chi-square if adequate counts) for binary success at 12 months (if chosen as primary or key secondary)
- **Purpose:** Compare **success proportion** (Healed+Healing) between A‑PRF and PRF.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Simple, transparent comparison of proportions in small samples.
  2. Fisher’s exact is valid with **small expected cell counts**.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Ignores **clustering** (two teeth/person) and ignores covariates.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When multiple teeth per subject exist (non-independence) or when adjustment for baseline imbalance is needed.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Mixed-effects logistic regression** or **GEE logistic regression** with robust SEs.

#### A4) Baseline group comparability: standardized mean differences (SMD) + appropriate unadjusted tests
- **Purpose:** Describe whether randomization achieved balance (not for “significance of baseline” per se, but to inform adjustment).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. SMD is not driven by sample size; recommended in RCT reporting.
  2. Identifies potential covariates for adjusted analyses.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Hypothesis testing baseline differences is often misinterpreted.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Do not use baseline p-values to decide inclusion/exclusion of covariates mechanically.
- **Alternative:**
  - Report SMDs and clinically relevant imbalances; adjust using multivariable models regardless of baseline p-values.

### CATEGORY B — RECOMMENDED tests/models

#### B1) ANCOVA (linear regression) for 12-month root length/thickness adjusting for baseline (if only baseline and 12-month available)
- **Purpose:** Compare groups on **12‑month measurement** controlling for baseline (more efficient than change-score t-test).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Controls for baseline measurement error/regression to mean.
  2. Improves power with small samples.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - If clustering (two teeth/person) exists and is ignored.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When repeated measures beyond baseline/12 months exist and are ignored; when within-patient clustering exists unmodeled.
- **Alternative:**
  - **Mixed-effects ANCOVA** (random patient effect) or GEE.

#### B2) Nonparametric alternatives for continuous outcomes: Mann–Whitney U (between-group) / Wilcoxon signed-rank (within-group)
- **Purpose:** Compare distributions when normality is doubtful.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Works with skewed data and outliers.
  2. Simple for small samples.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Tests medians/ranks, not mean difference; cannot easily adjust for baseline/covariates; ignores clustering.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When adjustment for baseline or clustering is required.
- **Alternative:**
  - **Rank-based mixed models**, **quantile regression**, or robust mixed models.

#### B3) Ordinal within-group change over time: Wilcoxon signed-rank or marginal ordinal models (baseline→6→12)
- **Purpose:** Assess improvement in PAI or ordinal status over time within each arm and compare trajectories.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. PAI is ordinal; these respect scale type.
  2. Longitudinal ordinal models can estimate time×group interaction.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Small sample and sparse categories across time.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If PAI treated incorrectly as continuous without justification.
- **Alternative:**
  - Collapse PAI to binary (healed vs not) and use mixed logistic/GEE.

#### B4) Inter-rater / intra-rater reliability (if feasible): weighted kappa (PAI) and ICC (continuous measures)
- **Purpose:** Quantify measurement reliability of radiographic scoring/measurements.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. PAI assignment by one investigator—reliability evidence strengthens validity.
  2. Continuous measurements (root length/thickness) are prone to error; ICC quantifies repeatability.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Requires repeated scoring or second rater; may not be available retrospectively.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When only single measurement exists and cannot be replicated.
- **Alternative:**
  - Bland–Altman analysis for continuous repeated measurements.

#### B5) Adjusted primary analysis (covariate-adjusted models)
- **Purpose:** Increase precision and handle residual imbalance: adjust for baseline PAI, age, apical width, presence of lesion.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Clinically relevant prognostic factors can reduce residual variance.
  2. With attrition, adjustment helps mitigate chance imbalance.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Overfitting risk with small n.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When events are too few (e.g., <10 failures) for multivariable logistic models.
- **Alternative:**
  - Penalized regression (Firth) for binary outcomes; prespecify few covariates.

### CATEGORY C — OPTIONAL analyses

#### C1) Time-to-event analysis (Kaplan–Meier + Cox) for “time to failure/symptoms” (if dates available)
- **Purpose:** Incorporate timing of failures/symptoms, not just 12-month status.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Uses partial follow-up information.
  2. Handles censoring.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Follow-up times are essentially fixed (6 and 12 months) and failure timing may be unknown.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If event times are interval-censored without sufficient resolution.
- **Alternative:**
  - Discrete-time survival model; or analyze status at 6 and 12 months with mixed models.

#### C2) Effect sizes and CIs (always useful): risk difference, risk ratio, odds ratio; mean difference; standardized effect size
- **Purpose:** Quantify magnitude with uncertainty.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Clinically interpretable.
  2. More informative than p-values alone.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Some effect sizes unstable with very small samples.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Avoid standardized effects when measurement scale is clinically interpretable and preferred.
- **Alternative:**
  - Bootstrap CIs.

#### C3) Subgroup / interaction (exploratory): presence of baseline periapical lesion, age group, baseline apical width
- **Purpose:** Hypothesis-generating effect modification.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Trauma severity/lesion status may influence regeneration.
  2. Guides future trials.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Severe multiplicity and very low power.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Do not claim definitive subgroup effects in this small trial.
- **Alternative:**
  - Report stratified descriptives only.

## TASK 3 — Tests that should NOT be used (and why)

1. **Independent-samples t-test on tooth-level outcomes without checking clustering**
   - Violates independence if two teeth per patient; inflates type I error.

2. **Paired t-test between A‑PRF and PRF**
   - Groups are parallel, not paired/matched.

3. **Multiple separate within-group pre/post tests (baseline vs 6, baseline vs 12) without multiplicity control**
   - Inflates false-positive rate; ignores correlation and group×time structure.

4. **Pearson correlation between treatment group and outcomes**
   - Treatment is categorical; correlation is not the right estimand and ignores confounding/clustering.

5. **Treating PAI as continuous and using standard linear regression/ANOVA without justification**
   - PAI is ordinal; linear methods can misrepresent effects unless strong justification/sensitivity analyses are provided.

6. **One-way ANOVA across the three outcome categories (Healed/Healing/Diseased) as if they were groups**
   - Outcome categories are not independent groups; this reverses predictor/outcome roles.

---

## TASK 4 — Summary justification of overall approach

A two-arm randomized parallel design with ordinal and continuous radiographic outcomes requires (i) **between-group comparisons** at clinically relevant follow-up (12 months), (ii) methods that respect **outcome scale** (ordinal for PAI/outcome category), and (iii) models that handle potential **non-independence** (multiple teeth per participant) and **repeated measures** (baseline/6/12). Therefore, the core analysis should use **mixed-effects or GEE frameworks** for ordinal and continuous endpoints, report **effect sizes with confidence intervals**, and include **missing-data and sensitivity analyses** given notable attrition.

### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS
1. **Test Name**: Paired t-test (or Wilcoxon signed-rank test if non-parametric).
   - **Purpose**: To compare preoperative and postoperative changes in root length and dentin thickness within each group (A-PRF and PRF).
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. The data involves paired measurements (pre- and post-treatment) for continuous variables (root length and dentin thickness).
     2. The study aims to assess changes over time within the same group.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:
     - Assumes normal distribution of differences; if violated, a non-parametric test (Wilcoxon signed-rank test) should be used.
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**:
     - If the sample size is too small to reliably assess normality.
   - **Alternative if Assumptions Are Violated**:
     - Wilcoxon signed-rank test.

2. **Test Name**: Independent t-test (or Mann-Whitney U test if non-parametric).
   - **Purpose**: To compare changes in root length and dentin thickness between the A-PRF and PRF groups.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. The study involves comparing two independent groups.
     2. The dependent variables (root length and dentin thickness) are continuous.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:
     - Assumes normality and homogeneity of variances; if violated, a non-parametric test (Mann-Whitney U test) should be used.
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**:
     - If the sample sizes are highly unequal or the data is heavily skewed.
   - **Alternative if Assumptions Are Violated**:
     - Mann-Whitney U test.

3. **Test Name**: Chi-square test (or Fisher’s exact test if expected cell counts are <5).
   - **Purpose**: To compare categorical treatment outcomes (healed, healing, diseased) between the A-PRF and PRF groups.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. The dependent variable (treatment outcome) is categorical.
     2. The study involves comparing proportions between two independent groups.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:
     - If the sample size is too small, leading to low expected frequencies in contingency table cells.
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**:
     - If more than 20% of cells have expected counts <5.
   - **Alternative if Assumptions Are Violated**:
     - Fisher’s exact test.

*   **Why appropriate:** 1) P-values are heavily influenced by small sample sizes; effect sizes tell the clinician if the difference is actually meaningful. 2) It aids future researchers in conducting power analyses for systematic reviews.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** Confidence intervals around effect sizes in small samples can be extremely wide, making them difficult to interpret definitively.
*   **When NOT to use:** Do not use as a replacement for inferential statistics, but rather as an accompaniment.
*   **Alternative:** Number Needed to Treat (NNT) for categorical success rates.

**2. Intention-to-Treat (ITT) Analysis using Multiple Imputation**
*   **Purpose:** To account for the 9 patients who dropped out of the study.
*   **Why appropriate:** 1) A dropout rate of 30% (9 out of 30) introduces severe attrition bias. 2) ITT preserves the benefits of the original randomization.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** Imputing data for nearly 30% of a small sample can introduce artificial variance and relies heavily on the assumption that data is Missing at Random (MAR).
*   **When NOT to use:** Do not use if the reasons for dropout are known to be directly related to treatment failure (Missing Not At Random - MNAR), which requires different modeling.
*   **Alternative:** Per-protocol analysis (analyzing only the 21 completers), which is likely what the authors did, but it overestimates treatment efficacy.

### TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS

*   **Pearson’s Chi-Square Test:** Should NOT be used to compare categorical outcomes (Healed/Healing/Diseased). The final sample size is 21. A 2x3 table will inevitably have expected cell counts below 5, violating the fundamental assumption of the Chi-Square test and leading to Type I errors.
*   **Analysis of Variance (ANOVA):** Should NOT be used to compare the groups unless rigorous normality and homogeneity of variance are proven. Given $n \approx 10$ per group, parametric assumptions are highly unlikely to be met.
*   **Pearson Correlation Coefficient:** Should NOT be used. The study objective is to *compare* outcomes between two distinct groups, not to find the linear relationship between two continuous variables.

### TASK 4: SUMMARY

The overall analytical approach must be highly conservative due to the small final sample size ($N=21$) resulting from a high attrition rate. The analysis should begin with descriptive statistics and baseline equivalence testing to ensure the randomization held despite the dropouts. 

Because the sample size is too small to reliably assume a normal distribution, the core analytical framework should rely on robust **non-parametric statistics**. The Mann-Whitney U test will evaluate the superiority of A-PRF over PRF regarding root thickness and length, while the Wilcoxon Signed-Rank test will validate the overall efficacy of the RET procedure within each group. Fisher's Exact test is strictly required for categorical clinical outcomes due to sparse data. Finally, because a single investigator performed all measurements, establishing intra-rater reliability (ICC/Kappa) is critical to validate the study's radiographic findings. Reporting effect sizes will provide much-needed clinical context to the p-values.
