### TASK 1: INFORMATION EXTRACTION

*   **Study Design:** Randomized Clinical Trial (RCT) utilizing a split-mouth/within-tooth design (paired data).
*   **Objective:** To evaluate whether placing a resorbable collagen barrier significantly impedes the extrusion of obturating material in primary molars undergoing resorption.
*   **Sample Size:** 47 children (from 60 screened). 47 primary mandibular molars. Total of 94 root canals (47 test, 47 control). Power analysis conducted (80% power).
*   **Study Population:** Children aged 7-9 years, Frankel’s Class 3 or 4 behavior, no systemic diseases, requiring pulpectomy in mandibular primary molars with at least 2/3 root length remaining.
*   **Groups (Randomized via coin flip per canal):**
    *   *Test Group (Group A):* Resorbable collagen barrier (Coloplug) placed 2mm short of the radiographic apex + obturation (Metapex).
    *   *Control Group (Group B):* No barrier + obturation (Metapex). Final working length also kept 2mm short of the apex.
*   **Variables:**
    *   *Independent Variable:* Treatment type (Categorical: Collagen barrier vs. No barrier).
    *   *Dependent Variable:* Extrusion of obturating material (Categorical/Dichotomous: "Acceptable/No Extrusion [NE]" vs. "Over filled/Extruded [E]").
    *   *Covariates/Other Variables:* Canal type (Mesial vs. Distal).
*   **Data Collection/Measurements:** Pre- and post-operative digital radiographs (RVG). Evaluated by a single operator.

### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

Given the split-mouth design where two canals within the *same tooth* of the *same patient* receive different treatments, the data is **paired/correlated**. The outcome is **binary** (Extruded vs. Not Extruded). 

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY (Essential for valid analysis)

**1. McNemar’s Test**
*   **Purpose:** To compare the proportion of extrusion between the test and control canals.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) It specifically accounts for the paired nature of the split-mouth design (within-subject correlation). 
    2) It is the standard test for paired nominal/dichotomous data (Extruded vs. Not Extruded).
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** If the number of discordant pairs (cases where the test canal extruded but the control didn't, or vice versa) is extremely small, the standard asymptotic chi-square approximation may yield inaccurate p-values.
*   **When this test should NOT be used:** When the groups are independent (e.g., comparing parallel groups of different patients) or when the outcome is continuous.
*   **Alternative if assumptions are violated:** Exact McNemar’s Test (or Binomial Exact Test for paired data) should be used if the number of discordant pairs is less than 10.

#### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED (Enhances quality and rigor)

**1. Cohen’s Kappa (Intra-rater Reliability)**
*   **Purpose:** To evaluate the consistency of the single operator's radiographic assessments.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) The study relies on a "single operator" to assess a subjective outcome (radiographic extrusion). 
    2) It quantifies the degree of agreement beyond what would be expected by chance for categorical variables.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** If the prevalence of one outcome is extremely high (e.g., almost no canals show extrusion), Kappa can be artificially low due to the "Kappa paradox."
*   **When this test should NOT be used:** When assessing continuous variables (where Intraclass Correlation Coefficient [ICC] is needed) or when comparing more than two raters (where Fleiss' Kappa is needed).
*   **Alternative if assumptions are violated:** Gwet’s AC1 statistic, which is more robust to the prevalence paradox in highly skewed binary data.

**2. Conditional Logistic Regression (or Generalized Estimating Equations - GEE)**
*   **Purpose:** To model the binary outcome while accounting for the paired design and adjusting for potential confounders.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) It allows the researchers to control for the canal type (mesial vs. distal), ensuring that anatomical differences between canals did not confound the results. 
    2) It robustly handles the within-tooth clustering of the data.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** The sample size (47 pairs) might be slightly underpowered for complex multivariable modeling if the event rate (extrusion) is very low.
*   **When this test should NOT be used:** When the data points are completely independent of one another.
*   **Alternative if assumptions are violated:** Stick to the unadjusted McNemar's test.

#### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL (Supplementary insights)

**1. Absolute Risk Reduction (ARR) and Number Needed to Treat (NNT)**
*   **Purpose:** To translate the statistical findings into practical clinical metrics.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) It provides clinicians with an easily interpretable metric (e.g., "How many teeth need the collagen barrier to prevent one case of extrusion?"). 
    2) It is easily calculated from the paired binary proportions.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** If the primary statistical test is highly non-significant, the NNT confidence interval will cross infinity, making it difficult to interpret.
*   **When this test should NOT be used:** When the outcome is continuous or when evaluating time-to-event data without a specific time horizon.
*   **Alternative if assumptions are violated:** Relative Risk (RR) or Odds Ratio (OR) for paired data.

### TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS

*   **Pearson Chi-Square Test / Fisher's Exact Test:** These tests assume *independence* of observations. Because the test and control canals are located in the same tooth of the same patient, they are dependent. Using these tests would artificially inflate the sample size (treating N=94 as independent rather than N=47 pairs), violating core assumptions and potentially leading to a Type I error.
*   **Independent Samples t-test / Paired t-test:** These tests are designed for *continuous* dependent variables (e.g., measuring the exact millimeter of extrusion). The outcome in this study is categorical/binary (Extruded vs. Not Extruded).
*   **Analysis of Variance (ANOVA):** Similar to the t-tests, ANOVA requires a continuous dependent variable and is entirely inappropriate for binary outcomes.

---

### TASK 4: SUMMARY

The overall analytical approach must be dictated by two primary study characteristics: the **split-mouth design** (which creates paired/correlated data) and the **binary outcome** (extrusion vs. no extrusion). Therefore, the statistical analysis must utilize paired non-parametric methods. 

The cornerstone of this analysis is **McNemar's Test**, which correctly accounts for the within-tooth correlation. Treating the 94 canals as independent would be a fatal methodological flaw. Furthermore, because the outcome relies on the subjective radiographic interpretation of a single operator, establishing intra-rater reliability via **Cohen's Kappa** (by having the operator re-evaluate a subset of radiographs blinded at a later date) is highly recommended to validate the integrity of the data collection process.
