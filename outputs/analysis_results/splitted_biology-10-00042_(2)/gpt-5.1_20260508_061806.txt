4. Intervention arms (group definitions)
----------------------------------------

Group 1: XP-endo Shaper (adaptive NiTi system)

- File: XP-endo Shaper, 21 mm, Max-Wire alloy (size 30, taper 0.01 expanding to ~30/0.04 in use).
- Motor: X-smart Plus (Dentsply Maillefer).
- Settings: 800 rpm, 1 Ncm torque.
- Canal preparation:
  - Canal patency checked with #10 K-file; glide path up to #15 K-file.
  - Root canals flooded with warm 2.5% NaOCl.
  - File inserted passively to resistance, then retracted coronally; motor activated; file reinserted.
  - Light vertical strokes, 4–5 times toward working length (WL).
  - At WL: file withdrawn, cleaned; apical patency verified with #15 K-file; canal irrigated with 4 mL warm NaOCl.
  - Additional 15 strokes to WL, considered completion.
  - Final irrigation: 17% EDTA (2 mL) + 4 mL normal saline.

Group 2: Kedo-S pediatric rotary files

- Files: Kedo-S E1 (16 mm).
- Motor: X-smart Plus.
- Settings: 250 rpm, 2.2 Ncm torque.
- Technique:
  - In-and-out motion until WL.
  - If resistance before WL: file withdrawn, canal irrigated with 2 mL NaOCl, patency confirmed with #15 K-file, reintroduction.
  - Once WL achieved, irrigate with 4 mL NaOCl.
  - File introduced again with vertical pecking motion for additional 5 strokes at WL.
  - Final irrigation: 17% EDTA (2 mL) + 4 mL saline.

Group 3: Hand K-files (control)

- Instruments: Stainless steel K-files #15, #20, #25, #30 (0.02 taper).
- Technique: quarter-turn-and-pull.
- Irrigation:
  - 2 mL 2.5% NaOCl between each file.
  - Additional 2 mL NaOCl after last file.
  - Final irrigation with 17% EDTA (2 mL) + 4 mL saline.

TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN  
==================================

Overview:

- Primary contrast: differences among the three instrumentation systems in:
  1) Mean instrumentation time.
  2) Distribution of obturation quality categories.
- Design: three independent groups; 25 observations each; randomized and balanced.

CATEGORY A – MANDATORY ANALYSES
--------------------------------

A1. Descriptive statistics by group
-----------------------------------

**Test / Procedure name**  
Descriptive statistics & exploratory plots.

**Purpose**

- Summarize baseline characteristics and outcomes by group (central tendency, dispersion, distributions).
- Check for gross imbalances and distributional assumptions.

**Why appropriate**

1. Essential for understanding data distribution (e.g., normality, outliers) before formal hypothesis tests.
2. Provides context for clinical interpretation (e.g., actual mean differences in minutes, proportion optimally filled).

**Why it may not be appropriate**

- Not a hypothesis test; does not in itself establish statistical significance.

**When NOT to use**

- Never omitted; but do not over-interpret descriptive differences as causal in absence of formal tests.

**Alternatives / specifics**

- For continuous outcomes (time): mean ± SD if approximately normal; median [IQR] otherwise.
- For categorical (obturation quality): counts and percentages.
- Graphs: boxplots or violin plots for time; bar charts for obturation categories.

A2. Assessment of normality and variance homogeneity for instrumentation time
-----------------------------------------------------------------------------

**Test name**

- Shapiro–Wilk test for normality (within each group) + Q–Q plots.
- Levene’s or Brown–Forsythe test for homogeneity of variances.

**Purpose**

- Evaluate assumptions for ANOVA (or parametric tests).

**Why appropriate**

1. Time data often skewed; explicit checks help choose between ANOVA and nonparametric alternatives.
2. Levene/Brown–Forsythe is robust for small-to-moderate equal sample sizes.

**Why it may not be appropriate**

- With small n (25 per group), Shapiro–Wilk has limited power; minor deviations may not be detected or may be overemphasized.

**When NOT to use**

- Not needed if one plans exclusively nonparametric methods regardless of distribution; but that is less efficient if assumptions hold.

**Alternative if assumptions violated**

- Use Kruskal–Wallis test instead of ANOVA (see A3).

A3. Overall comparison of instrumentation time across groups
------------------------------------------------------------

**Primary test name**

- One-way ANOVA (if assumptions hold).

**Purpose**

- Test whether mean instrumentation time differs among the three instrumentation types.

**Why appropriate**

1. Design is classic: 3 independent, randomized groups; outcome is continuous, single measurement per tooth.
2. ANOVA is standard for comparing means across >2 groups and efficient under approximate normality and equal variances.

**Why it may not be appropriate**

- If time distributions are markedly skewed or variances highly unequal, ANOVA’s Type I error and confidence intervals can be invalid.

**When this test should NOT be used**

- Severe violations of normality (e.g., highly skewed, long-tailed times) even after simple transformations, especially with unequal variances.
- If there are strong outliers that cannot be justified or handled appropriately.

**Alternative if assumptions are violated**

- Kruskal–Wallis test (rank-based nonparametric test) for comparing three independent groups.
- If variances differ but normality reasonable: Welch’s ANOVA instead of classical ANOVA.

A4. Post hoc pairwise comparisons for instrumentation time
----------------------------------------------------------

**Test name**

- If ANOVA significant:
  - Tukey’s Honestly Significant Difference (Tukey HSD).
- If Welch’s ANOVA used:
  - Games–Howell post hoc.
- If Kruskal–Wallis used:
  - Dunn’s test with Bonferroni or Holm adjustment.

**Purpose**

- Identify which specific pairs of groups differ in mean/median instrumentation time (XP vs Kedo-S vs Hand).

**Why appropriate**

1. Multiple comparisons control family-wise error rate.
2. Aligned with omnibus test selected (ANOVA/Welch/Kruskal–Wallis).

**Why it may not be appropriate**

- Reduced power due to multiple-comparison corrections in small samples (n=25 per group).

**When NOT to use**

- If overall ANOVA/Kruskal–Wallis p-value is non-significant; exploratory post hoc comparisons in that scenario should be clearly labeled as such and not central to conclusions.

**Alternative if assumptions violated**

- For nonparametric case: pairwise Mann–Whitney U tests with Holm correction.

A5. Overall comparison of obturation quality across groups
----------------------------------------------------------

**Primary test name**

- Chi-square test of independence (3×3 contingency table: 3 groups × 3 obturation categories).

**Purpose**

- Examine whether the distribution of obturation outcomes (underfilled, optimal, overfilled) differs by instrumentation technique.

**Why appropriate**

1. Data are categorical; groups are independent; sample size moderate (75).
2. Directly addresses the trial’s outcome: quality of obturation distribution by group.

**Why it may not be appropriate**

- If some cells have expected counts <5, chi-square approximation may be unreliable.

**When this test should NOT be used**

- When >20% of cells have expected count <5, or any expected count <1 (standard rule).

**Alternative if assumptions violated**

- Fisher’s exact test (for a 3×3 table) if computationally feasible.
- Collapse categories (e.g., optimal vs suboptimal [under/over]) and use 3×2 chi-square/Fisher’s exact test.

A6. Pairwise comparisons for obturation quality (if overall test significant)
-----------------------------------------------------------------------------

**Test name**

- Post hoc pairwise chi-square tests or Fisher’s exact tests between groups (XP vs Kedo-S, XP vs Hand, Kedo-S vs Hand) with multiplicity adjustment (e.g., Bonferroni, Holm).
- Alternatively, for ordinal nature:
  - Pairwise Cochran–Armitage trend tests or ordinal logistic models (see Category B for more advanced).

**Purpose**

- Clarify which instrumentation systems differ in obturation outcomes.

**Why appropriate**

1. Important clinically to know specific differences (e.g., XP vs Hand, Kedo-S vs Hand).
2. Multiplicity control reduces risk of false positives across multiple pairwise tests.

**Why it may not be appropriate**

- Sample size may be too small for reliable multi-category comparisons when distributions are sparse; results may be unstable.

**When NOT to use**

- If overall chi-square is non-significant, pairwise tests should be considered exploratory.

**Alternative if assumptions violated**

- Collapse categories (e.g., optimal vs non-optimal) and perform pairwise Fisher’s exact tests.

CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED ANALYSES
----------------------------------

B1. Effect sizes with confidence intervals
-----------------------------------------

**Test / Metric name**

- For continuous time:
  - Group means and mean differences with 95% CIs.
  - Standardized effect size: Cohen’s d (pairwise) with CIs.
- For categorical obturation:
  - Proportion optimal in each group; pairwise risk differences or risk ratios with 95% CIs.
  - Cramer’s V as measure of association for 3×3 chi-square.

**Purpose**

- Provide magnitude and precision of effects, not just p-values.

**Why appropriate**

1. Clinically interpretable differences (e.g., “XP reduces instrumentation time by X minutes compared with hand files”).
2. Confidence intervals convey uncertainty; recommended by CONSORT.

**Why it may not be appropriate**

- Cohen’s d assumes reasonably continuous, normally distributed outcomes; with skewed time distribution, interpretation is less straightforward.

**When NOT to use**

- If data highly skewed, avoid overemphasizing standardized effect sizes; present medians and median differences instead.

**Alternative**

- Use Hodges–Lehmann estimator of median difference for time.
- For obturation: odds ratios or ordinal effect size measures.

B2. Ordinal modeling of obturation quality
------------------------------------------

**Test name**

- Proportional odds ordinal logistic regression (cumulative logit model).

**Purpose**

- Model the ordered outcome (underfilled < optimal < overfilled) as a function of group, possibly adjusting for confounders (tooth type, arch, age).

**Why appropriate**

1. Utilizes ordinal information fully, unlike simple chi-square which treats categories as nominal.
2. Allows simultaneous comparison of all groups with a single parameter per group (conditional odds of better vs worse categories).

**Why it may not be appropriate**

- Requires proportional odds assumption; with small sample and 3 categories, model diagnostics may be limited.

**When NOT to use**

- If sample size insufficient (e.g., very few overfilled cases) or proportional odds assumption clearly violated.

**Alternative if assumptions are violated**

- Collapsed binary logistic regression (optimal vs non-optimal).
- Multinomial logistic regression (treating categories as nominal), though interpretationally more complex.

B3. Adjusted analysis for instrumentation time
----------------------------------------------

**Test name**

- Linear regression (or ANOVA with covariates, i.e., ANCOVA).

**Purpose**

- Adjust for potential imbalances in tooth-related or patient-related covariates (arch, first vs second molar, tooth code, age, sex) when comparing groups on time.

**Why appropriate**

1. Randomization should balance covariates, but small sample can yield residual imbalance; adjustment improves precision and reduces confounding risk.
2. Allows exploration of whether certain tooth types systematically require more time.

**Why it may not be appropriate**

- With n=75 and many covariates, overfitting is possible; regression assumptions (linearity, homoscedasticity) may not hold.

**When NOT to use**

- If covariate data not collected or missing in a nonrandom way.
- If there is no evidence of any imbalance and model becomes unstable.

**Alternative if assumptions violated**

- Robust regression or transformation of time data (e.g., log-transformation).
- Nonparametric ANCOVA methods (e.g., rank ANCOVA) but are less standard.

B4. Sensitivity analyses (per protocol vs intention-to-treat)
-------------------------------------------------------------

Given all 75 randomized teeth were analyzed and there was no loss to follow-up:

- Main analysis effectively equals per-protocol and intention-to-treat.
- Still, a sensitivity analysis could be defined for:
  - Exclusion of any protocol deviations (e.g., unexpected fourth canal discovered mid-procedure).
  - Inclusion vs exclusion of outliers in time.

**Test name**

- Repeat main analyses (ANOVA and chi-square) after excluding predefined outliers or protocol violations.

**Purpose**

- Assess robustness of conclusions to such deviations.

**Why appropriate**

1. Increases confidence that primary results are not driven by a few atypical observations.
2. Helps identify if specific tooth types or unusual cases drive findings.

**Why it may not be appropriate**

- Removing data may introduce bias if exclusions are related to treatment or outcome.

**When NOT to use**

- If no protocol deviations or outliers are identified; or if number of exclusions would substantially reduce power.

**Alternative**

- Robust methods (e.g., trimmed means ANOVA) to downweight outliers without excluding them explicitly.

B5. Inter-rater reliability for obturation quality (if multiple raters)
----------------------------------------------------------------------

The methods suggest that “the evaluating observer and the analyst were blinded” but do not specify if more than one radiographic evaluator existed. If there are multiple raters:

**Test name**

- Weighted kappa (κw) for ordinal categories.

**Purpose**

- Assess consistency of obturation quality ratings between observers.

**Why appropriate**

1. Ensures reliability of primary categorical outcome evaluation.
2. Weighted kappa accounts for degree of disagreement across ordered categories.

**Why it may not be appropriate**

- If only one blinded evaluator was used, reliability assessment not possible.

**When NOT to use**

- When only a single rater is involved or when repeated scoring not performed.

**Alternative**

- If repeated scoring by same rater: intra-rater reliability via weighted kappa.

C1. Exploratory subgroup analyses
---------------------------------

**Test name**

- Stratified analyses (e.g., separate ANOVAs or nonparametric tests) by:
  - Maxillary vs mandibular teeth.
  - First vs second molars.
- Interaction tests in regression models (group × tooth type).

**Purpose**

**Why appropriate**

**Why it may not be appropriate**

**When NOT to use**

**Alternative**

C2. Distributional analysis of time (transformations)
---------------------------------------------------

**Test name**

- Log-transformation of time (if highly skewed).
- Compare log-time via ANOVA (or regression).

**Purpose**

- Stabilize variance and approximate normality before modeling.

**Why appropriate**

1. Time measurements often log-normal.
2. Interpretable as percent differences in geometric means.

**Why it may not be appropriate**

- Interpretation less intuitive for clinicians.
- If many values near zero (not the case here), log-transform problematic.

**When NOT to use**

- If normality and homoscedasticity already adequate on original scale.

**Alternative**

- Box–Cox transformation or nonparametric methods as already discussed.

C3. Composite outcome derived from time and obturation
------------------------------------------------------

**Test name**

- Multivariate analysis (e.g., MANOVA) or composite ranking (e.g., assign rank based on time and quality simultaneously).

**Purpose**

- Evaluate overall “performance” of instrumentation technique combining speed and obturation quality.

**Why appropriate**

1. Clinically, both efficiency and quality matter; a technique that is fast but produces poor obturation isn't desirable.
2. Composite measures can help rank techniques holistically.

**Why it may not be appropriate**

- MANOVA assumptions may be hard to satisfy; composite indices introduce subjectivity in weighting components.

**When NOT to use**

- If interpretation or weighting cannot be justified a priori.

**Alternative**

- Present a joint table or plot of time vs probability of optimal obturation and discuss qualitatively.

TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS AND WHY
====================================

Below are commonly misapplied tests that should NOT be used in this study, with reasons.

1. Independent-samples t-tests for each pair without prior ANOVA
----------------------------------------------------------------

- Inappropriate because:
  - Multiple pairwise t-tests (XP vs Kedo-S, XP vs Hand, Kedo-S vs Hand) increase Type I error if not adjusted.
  - Ignores overall 3-group structure and does not exploit omnibus testing.
- Better:
  - One-way ANOVA (or Welch/Kruskal–Wallis) followed by adjusted pairwise comparisons.

2. Paired t-tests (or paired nonparametric tests)
-------------------------------------------------

- Inappropriate because:
  - Data are from independent patients/teeth; no repeated measures or pairing.
- Paired tests assume within-subject or matched observations, which does not hold here.

3. Repeated-measures ANOVA
--------------------------

- Inappropriate because:
  - Only one measurement per outcome per tooth; no repeated time points or multiple conditions per subject.

4. McNemar’s test
-----------------

- Inappropriate because:
  - Designed for paired binary outcomes in matched/paired designs.
  - Our design: independent groups and 3-category outcomes.

5. Correlation tests (Pearson/Spearman) between group and time/obturation coded numerically
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

- Inappropriate because:
  - Group is a nominal factor with three categories; correlation coefficients with an arbitrary numeric coding are meaningless.
- Instead:
  - Use ANOVA or nonparametric group comparisons.

6. Linear regression treating obturation categories as continuous 1–3
---------------------------------------------------------------------

- Inappropriate because:
  - Ordinal outcomes violate assumptions of continuous linear regression; distances between categories (underfilled → optimal → overfilled) are not necessarily equal.
- Instead:
  - Use ordinal logistic regression or appropriate categorical modeling.

7. One-sample t-test vs a fixed time value
------------------------------------------

- Inappropriate because:
  - Research question is comparative among groups, not about deviation from some fixed known mean or benchmark time.

8. Logistic regression treating group as outcome and time/quality as predictors
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

- Inappropriate because:
  - Causal direction reversed; randomization assigned group, which is the exposure, not the outcome.
- Instead:
  - Model time or obturation as outcomes and group as predictor.

9. Chi-square goodness-of-fit comparing each group to a pre-specified distribution
---------------------------------------------------------------------------------

- Inappropriate unless there is a pre-specified theoretical distribution of obturation categories, which is not part of the study question. The interest is in between-group comparisons.

TASK 4: SUMMARY AND JUSTIFICATION OF ANALYTICAL APPROACH
=========================================================

- The trial is a straightforward three-arm parallel-group RCT with two primary outcomes:
  - Instrumentation time (continuous).
  - Quality of obturation (ordered categorical).
- The primary inferential goals are:
  1) Determine whether adaptive XP-endo Shaper and Kedo-S rotary files reduce instrumentation time compared with hand K-files and with each other.
  2) Determine whether these systems yield different distributions of obturation quality.

- For instrumentation time:
  - One-way ANOVA (or Welch/Kruskal–Wallis) is the appropriate primary comparison, given independent, randomized groups and a continuous outcome. Post hoc pairwise tests identify specific differences.
  - Assumption checks (normality, homogeneity) and potential transformations ensure validity.

- For obturation quality:
  - A 3×3 chi-square test assesses differences in distribution across groups; ordinal modeling further exploits the ordered nature of categories.
  - If sparse cells exist, Fisher’s exact or category collapsing will be used.
  - Proportions of optimal obturation and associated risk differences/ratios help quantify performance.

- Recommended extras:
  - Effect sizes, adjusted models for key covariates (e.g., tooth type), and potential ordinal regression enhance rigor and interpretability without overcomplicating the primary questions.
  - Reliability analysis (if multiple raters) ensures the categorical outcome is dependable.

Overall, the proposed plan is consistent with CONSORT recommendations, aligns with the trial’s clearly stated objectives, respects the data structure and assumptions of each method, and balances statistical rigor with clinical interpretability.
