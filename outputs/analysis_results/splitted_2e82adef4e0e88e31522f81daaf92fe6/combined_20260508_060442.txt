# CATEGORY A — Mandatory tests (core analyses)

## A1) Mixed-effects ordinal logistic regression (cumulative logit mixed model)
**Purpose:** Compare **postoperative pain severity over time** between groups while accounting for repeated measures.

**Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):**
1. Pain is recorded on an **ordered categorical** scale (none/mild/moderate/severe), so ordinal modeling matches outcome type.
2. Repeated measurements per patient violate independence; **random subject effects** (or GEE correlation structure) handle within-subject correlation and use all time points efficiently.
3. Allows inclusion of **time**, **group**, and **group×time interaction** (differential pain trajectories).

**Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason):**
- The **proportional odds assumption** may be violated (effects differ across cutpoints).

**When this test should NOT be used:**
- When pain is truly continuous and available as raw VAS (0–10 cm) and normality/linearity assumptions are acceptable (then a continuous mixed model may be preferable), or when sample size is too small for stable ordinal mixed model estimation.

**Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
- **Partial proportional odds mixed model**, or
- **Multinomial mixed model**, or
- **GEE with ordinal link** (robust SE), or
- Collapse categories (e.g., none/mild vs moderate/severe) and use **mixed logistic regression** (with information loss acknowledged).

**Key specification:** fixed effects for group, time (categorical), baseline pain, and group×time; random intercept for subject.

---

## A2) Between-group comparison of bacterial reduction using ANCOVA / linear model on log-transformed CFU
**Test name:** Linear regression/ANCOVA on **log10(CFU+1)** with group as predictor and baseline log CFU as covariate (or analyze change score on log scale).

**Purpose:** Assess whether instrumentation system differs in **post-instrumentation bacterial load**, adjusted for pre-instrumentation levels.

**Why appropriate:**
1. CFU/mL are typically **right-skewed**; log transform often yields approximately normal residuals and stabilizes variance.
2. ANCOVA adjustment for baseline improves **precision** and accounts for any baseline imbalance, outperforming simple change-score comparisons in many settings.

**Why it may not be appropriate:**
- If there are **many zeros** post-instrumentation (zero-inflation), log(CFU+1) may still be problematic and normality may fail.

**When this test should NOT be used:**
- When distribution remains highly non-normal/heteroscedastic after transformation or there is severe zero-inflation making Gaussian models misleading.

**Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
- **Mann–Whitney U test** on change scores (nonparametric), or
- **Quantile regression** (median change), or
- **Negative binomial regression** (or **zero-inflated** NB) treating CFU as counts with offset for dilution/volume if modeled directly.

---

## A3) Within-group bacterial reduction (paired comparison)
**Test name:** Paired t-test on log10(CFU+1) (or Wilcoxon signed-rank)

**Purpose:** Confirm that each system produces **significant reduction** from S1 to S2.

**Why appropriate:**
1. Paired design (same canal before/after) -> **within-subject** comparison.
2. Provides interpretable within-group reduction estimates (mean log reduction or median reduction).

**Why it may not be appropriate:**
- If post CFU includes many ties/zeros and distribution is very non-normal, paired t-test may mislead.

**When this test should NOT be used:**
- When using ANCOVA as the sole inferential framework and you want to avoid multiple testing; still, descriptive paired reductions should be shown.

**Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
- **Wilcoxon signed-rank test** (or sign test if many ties).

---

## A4) Flare-up incidence between groups
**Test name:** Fisher’s exact test (or chi-square if expected counts adequate)

**Purpose:** Compare **proportion with flare-up (Yes/No)** between instrumentation systems.

**Why appropriate:**
1. Outcome is **binary**.
2. Sample size (≈30/group) and potentially **low event rate** → Fisher’s exact often safer than chi-square.

**Why it may not be appropriate:**
- Does not adjust for covariates (baseline pain, lesion presence).

**When this test should NOT be used:**
- When covariate adjustment is needed or there are multiple predictors.

**Alternative if assumptions are violated / need adjustment:**
- **Logistic regression** (possibly Firth penalized logistic if separation/rare events).

# CATEGORY B — Recommended tests (improves rigor)

## B1) GEE ordinal model (population-averaged) as sensitivity to mixed model
**Purpose:** Provide a robustness check for pain trajectory conclusions using marginal (population-average) inference.

**Why appropriate:**
1. Handles repeated ordinal outcomes with robust SE even if random effects distribution misspecified.
2. Estimates **population-average effects**, often preferred for clinical interpretability.

**Why it may not be appropriate:**
- With small samples, robust SE can be biased downward.

**When not to use:**
- Very small sample or sparse outcome categories at some time points.

**Alternative:**
- Bootstrap SE for GEE; or rely on mixed model with careful diagnostics.

## B2) Model for analgesic tablet counts
**Test name:** Negative binomial regression (or Poisson with overdispersion check)

**Purpose:** Compare **number of ibuprofen tablets** between groups.

**Why appropriate:**
1. Tablets are **count data** (0,1,2,…) with likely **overdispersion**.
2. Avoids inappropriate normal-based tests for skewed discrete outcomes; yields rate ratios.

**Why it may not be appropriate:**
- If nearly everyone takes 0 tablets (excess zeros), standard NB may fit poorly.

**When not to use:**
- If outcome is essentially binary (any vs none) due to extreme sparsity.

**Alternative:**
- **Zero-inflated negative binomial**, or
- **Logistic regression** for any analgesic use (Yes/No).

## B3) Swelling severity comparison (ordinal)
**Test name:** Ordinal logistic regression (or Fisher’s exact on collapsed categories)

**Purpose:** Compare swelling grades between groups (among those assessed or overall with none included).

**Why appropriate:**
1. Swelling is **ordered categorical** (none/small/moderate/large).
2. Can incorporate covariates and estimate odds of worse swelling.

**Why it may not be appropriate:**
- Likely sparse counts in moderate/large categories.

**When not to use:**
- If cell counts are too small for stable estimation.

**Alternative:**
- Collapse to **none vs any** and use Fisher/logistic, or use exact methods.

## B4) Baseline covariate-adjusted pain analysis
**Test name:** Mixed-effects ordinal model including covariates (age, sex, periapical radiolucency presence, baseline pain)

**Purpose:** Improve precision and address residual confounding/imbalance.

**Why appropriate:**
1. Pain is influenced by patient/tooth factors; adjustment can reduce unexplained variance.
2. Enhances credibility if any baseline imbalances exist despite randomization.

**Why it may not be appropriate:**
- Overfitting risk with small sample and many covariates.

**When not to use:**
- If events per parameter are too low (sparse severe pain categories).

**Alternative:**
- Parsimonious adjustment (baseline pain + radiolucency only), penalized methods, or prespecified minimal covariate set.

---

## B5) Multiple comparisons control for pain at multiple time points (if doing time-point-specific tests)
**Test name:** Holm correction (or Benjamini–Hochberg FDR)

**Purpose:** Control inflated type I error if performing separate group comparisons at each time point.

**Why appropriate:**
1. Pain is measured at 7 postoperative times; naive testing at each time inflates false positives.
2. Holm is simple and controls family-wise error.

**Why it may not be appropriate:**
- If the primary analysis is the **single mixed model** with interaction, multiplicity adjustment for time-point contrasts may be secondary.

**When not to use:**
- When only reporting model-based global tests without multiple pairwise contrasts.

**Alternative:**
- Report global interaction p-value; limit contrasts to prespecified clinically relevant times (e.g., 24h, 48h).

## TASK 3 — Inappropriate tests (should NOT be used)

1. **Independent-samples t-test on raw CFU/mL**
   - CFU is typically highly skewed, non-negative, often zero-inflated; violates normality/variance assumptions and yields misleading inference.

2. **Repeated-measures ANOVA on ordinal pain categories**
   - Assumes continuous normally distributed outcomes and equal intervals; ordinal categories do not meet these requirements.

3. **Multiple separate chi-square tests at each pain time point without multiplicity control**
   - Inflates type I error; ignores within-subject correlation across times.

4. **Pearson correlation between group (binary) and pain severity**
   - Not appropriate for comparing groups on ordinal repeated outcomes; correlation does not address group effect over time.

5. **Simple linear regression for tablet counts**
   - Count outcome violates linear model assumptions; predictions can be negative; heteroscedasticity and non-normal residuals likely.

---

## TASK 4 — Summary justification of overall analytical approach

The study is a two-arm randomized clinical trial with (i) **longitudinal ordinal pain outcomes**, (ii) **paired pre/post microbiological counts**, and (iii) **binary/ordinal adverse outcomes**. Therefore, the core analysis should use **(1) ordinal mixed-effects (or GEE) models** to properly handle repeated pain measurements and **(2) baseline-adjusted models on log-transformed CFU** (or appropriate count models) to compare bacterial reduction while respecting distributional properties. Binary endpoints (flare-up) should be compared using **exact/ logistic methods** suitable for small samples and rare events, complemented by effect sizes and sensitivity analyses.

TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN  

I’ll organize by outcome and then list tests under categories A–C. I assume typical clinical trial standards: α = 0.05 (two-sided), intention-to-treat as primary; per-protocol sensitivity analyses recommended.

A. GENERAL PRINCIPLES  

- Descriptive statistics:  
  • Continuous/non-normal variables: median, IQR, min, max; also mean, SD if helpful.  
  • Categorical/ordinal: counts and percentages.  
- Group comparability at baseline:  
  • Age, sex, baseline pain category, presence of periapical radiolucency, baseline CFU.  
- Primary contrasts: XP-endo vs iRaCe.  
- Transformations:  
  • CFU/mL: use log10(CFU+1) to stabilize variance; also compute within-subject percent or log reductions.  

B. OUTCOME-SPECIFIC ANALYSIS  

1) Bacterial reduction  

Primary bacterial endpoint options:  
- Log10 CFU at S2 (post-instrumentation), adjusting for S1.  
- Within-subject change in log10 CFU (S2–S1).  
- Percentage reduction: (S1–S2)/S1 × 100% (sensitive to zero baselines).  
Because this is a core objective, we should treat it as at least one primary outcome.

CATEGORY A – MANDATORY  

Test A1: Comparison of bacterial reduction between groups  

Test name:  
- Primary choice: ANCOVA on post-instrumentation log10 CFU with baseline CFU as covariate.  
- If ANCOVA not feasible: independent-samples t-test (or non-parametric equivalent) on change in log10 CFU.  

Purpose:  
- Compare mean bacterial load reduction between XP and iRaCe, accounting for baseline.  

Why appropriate (reasons):  
1. Continuous outcome (log CFU) with approximate normality after transformation.  
2. Two independent groups with randomization justify ANCOVA to increase precision by adjusting for baseline CFU.  
3. Bacterial reduction is conceptualized as difference in post-treatment levels, which ANCOVA handles efficiently.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- CFU distributions may remain highly skewed, zero-inflated (no growth in some samples), or have outliers even after log transformation.  

When this test should NOT be used:  
- If residuals strongly deviate from normality and variance homogeneity;  
- If many subjects have zero CFU at baseline or post, leading to clustering at zero and precluding linear model assumptions.  

Alternative if assumptions are violated:  
- Mann–Whitney U test comparing:  
  • Post-treatment CFU or  
  • Within-subject CFU reduction (S1–S2)  
  using ranks, or  
- Rank ANCOVA (e.g., Quade’s test) with baseline adjustment.  
- Or generalized linear model with appropriate distribution (e.g., negative binomial) if raw counts retained.  

Test A2: Within-group assessment of bacterial reduction  

Test name:  
- Paired t-test on log10 CFU (S1 vs S2) within each group, or  
- If non-normal: Wilcoxon signed-rank test.  

Purpose:  
- Demonstrate that each instrumentation system significantly reduces bacterial counts from baseline.  

Why appropriate:  
1. Paired repeated measurements (S1, S2) on the same canal.  
2. Main clinical claim: instrumentation reduces intraradicular bacteria, not only relative comparison between groups.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- If many subjects have identical counts (ties), or non-normal distributions even after transformation.  

When NOT to use:  
- When CFU data include many zeros or extreme values causing failed normality;  
- For very small sample sizes per group (n<10), where paired t assumptions are weak.  

Alternative:  
- Wilcoxon signed-rank test on CFU changes;  
- Or McNemar-type analysis if dichotomizing “positive vs negative culture” (but this loses information).  

CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED  

Test B1: Mixed-effects model for CFU (optional refinement of A1)  

Test name:  
- Linear mixed-effects model with random intercept per subject: log10 CFU as outcome, fixed effects for time (S1 vs S2), group, and time × group interaction.  

Purpose:  
- Evaluate whether reduction over time differs by group (i.e., is the time × group interaction significant?).  

Why appropriate:  
1. Naturally handles repeated measures (S1 and S2) and correlation within subjects.  
2. Allows estimation of group-specific changes and their CIs, and accommodates unbalanced data (e.g., missing S2).  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Only two time points; simpler paired analyses may suffice.  
- Requires statistical expertise and software; may be overkill for small n.  

When NOT to use:  
- When there are serious model violations, such as extreme non-normal residuals even on log scale, or very small sample size (<20 total).  

Alternative:  
- Two-step approach: within-subject differences (S2–S1) analyzed by independent-samples test as in A1.  

2) Postoperative pain trajectory  

Pain is measured repeatedly, with ordinal categories and frequently skewed distribution; analgesic use may truncate observed pain.

CATEGORY A – MANDATORY  

Test A3: Between-group comparison of pain at each time point  

Test name:  
- Ordinal regression (proportional odds model) at key time points (e.g., 24 h, 48 h), or  
- If simpler: Mann–Whitney U test comparing ordinal pain scores between groups at each time point.  

Purpose:  
- Assess whether XP vs iRaCe differ in pain severity at clinically important times.  

Why appropriate:  
1. Ordinal scale: non-parametric or ordinal regression respects ordered categories.  
2. Independent groups due to randomization; simple to implement and interpret.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Multiple time points raise multiplicity issues; separate tests at each time inflate type I error.  
- Ordinal regression requires enough observations per category; sparse severe pain categories may impair model.  

When NOT to use:  
- When sample sizes per group are very small OR when pain categories collapse (e.g., almost all “none”).  

Alternative:  
- Collapsing categories into “none/mild vs moderate/severe” and using chi-square/Fisher’s exact test at specific times.  
- Or using generalized estimating equations (GEE) for correlated ordinal data (see B2).  

CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED  

Test B2: Longitudinal analysis of pain over time  

Test name:  
- GEE with ordinal logistic link (cumulative logit), or  
- Mixed-effects cumulative logit model with random intercepts.  

Purpose:  
- Model entire pain trajectory, not just isolated time points; assess global difference between groups over 5 days.  

Why appropriate:  
1. Repeated measures and within-subject correlation addressed;  
2. Handles ordinal outcome appropriately;  
3. Provides robust estimates of overall group effect and group × time interaction.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Requires more complex software and expertise;  
- Proportional odds assumption may not hold across categories or times;  
- Sample size (n≈60) may limit power for complex interaction models.  

When NOT to use:  
- When data are extremely unbalanced (e.g., many missing pain entries);  
- When categories collapse (e.g., almost all zero pain after day 2).  

Alternative:  
- Analyze binary “clinically relevant pain” (e.g., moderate/severe vs none/mild) with GEE logistic;  
- Or area under the pain-time curve summarized per patient and compared via Mann–Whitney or t-test.  

Test B3: Comparison of incidence of any postoperative pain  

Test name:  
- Chi-square test (or Fisher’s exact test) comparing proportion of patients with any postoperative pain (yes/no) by group, at prespecified time (e.g., 24 h; worst pain period).  

Purpose:  
- Provide clinically interpretable binary outcome: proportion experiencing pain.  

Why appropriate:  
1. Binary outcome; randomization ensures group independence.  
2. Summarizes clinical relevance simply and avoids overemphasis on minor pain differences.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Ignoring pain intensity; binary collapses categories and timing.  

When NOT to use:  
- When nearly all or almost none have pain (expected cell counts <5);  

Alternative:  
- Fisher’s exact test;  
- Or logistic regression adjusting for baseline pain and periapical radiolucency.  

3) Flare-ups and swelling  

CATEGORY A – MANDATORY  

Test A4: Comparison of flare-up incidence between groups  

Test name:

Purpose:  
- Assess whether new or worsening symptoms (flare-ups) differ by instrumentation system.  

Why appropriate:  
1. Binary outcome; independence across subjects.

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Flare-ups are relatively rare; counts may be small, violating chi-square assumptions.  

When NOT to use:  
- When any expected cell count <5;  

Alternative:  
- Fisher’s exact test;

Test B4: Comparison of swelling severity  

Test name:

Purpose:  
- Explore whether XP vs iRaCe affect the severity of swelling, beyond flare-up incidence.  

Why appropriate:  
1. Ordinal outcome; non-parametric or ordinal regression suits it.  
2. Provides more nuanced information about postoperative inflammation.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Very few cases with any swelling; distribution heavily skewed toward “none”.  

When NOT to use:  
- When only 0–1 non-zero swelling events per group.  

Alternative:  
- Restrict to binary “any swelling yes/no” and analyze via Fisher’s exact test.  

4) Analgesic consumption  

CATEGORY A – MANDATORY (if analgesic intake is a co-primary or key secondary)  

Test A5: Comparison of analgesic use (binary)

Purpose:  
- Determine if XP vs iRaCe differ in need for rescue analgesia.  

Why appropriate:  
1. Binary outcome, straightforward.

Why it may not be appropriate:

Test B5: Comparison of number of tablets (count data)  

Test name:  
- Mann–Whitney U test for count of tablets per subject; or  
- Poisson/negative binomial regression (if counts >0, with offset if needed).  

Purpose:  
- Quantify and compare intensity of analgesic consumption.  

Why appropriate:  
1. Count variable; non-parametric or count regression models capture distribution.  
2. Provides dose–response picture, not just yes/no.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Many zeros (zero-inflated count distribution); small sample.  

When NOT to use:

Alternative:

CATEGORY A – MANDATORY  

Test A6: Baseline balance checks  

Tests:  
- Continuous baseline variables (e.g., age, baseline CFU): t-test or Mann–Whitney U.  
- Categorical baseline variables (e.g., sex, pre-op pain category, radiolucency presence): chi-square or Fisher’s exact test.  

Purpose:  
- Check success of randomization, describe sample; not for selection of covariates mechanically.  

Why appropriate:  
1. Good practice to describe comparability, although randomization theoretically assures it.  
2. Helps interpret outcomes if there is imbalance.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Testing baseline differences is sometimes discouraged (randomization makes tests theoretically unnecessary).  

When NOT to use:

Test B6: Multivariable regression for key outcomes  

Test name:

- For continuous outcomes (CFU reduction): linear regression with same covariates plus baseline CFU.  

Purpose:  
- Assess robustness of group effects after adjusting for relevant clinical predictors.  

Why appropriate:  
1. Controls for potential confounders (even in RCTs, especially if small sample).  
2. Provides adjusted effect sizes (ORs, mean differences) with confidence intervals.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Limited sample size may restrict number of covariates (events-per-variable rule).  

When NOT to use:  
- When events are rare (e.g., <10 flare-ups in total), logistic regression with multiple covariates becomes unstable.  

Alternative:

- Simpler unadjusted analyses with careful interpretation.

TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS  

Below are tests that should NOT be used (or only with strong reservations) in this study, with explanations:

1) Parametric tests ignoring ordinal nature of pain without justification  
- Example: Independent t-test on mean pain scores (coded 1–4) at each time.  
Why inappropriate:  
- Pain categories are ordinal, not interval; distances between categories are not necessarily equal.  
- T-test assumes normality and equal variances, which ordinal limited-scale data rarely meet.  

2) Repeated-measures ANOVA on pain coded as continuous 0–10 without checking assumptions  
Why inappropriate:  
- Pain repeated measures often violate sphericity;  
- VAS data in clinical practice often skew;  
- Many zero values (no pain) distort normality.  
Better: GEE or mixed ordinal models as discussed.  

3) Using multiple independent t-tests on raw CFU counts without transformation  
Why inappropriate:  
- CFU counts typically highly skewed, overdispersed;  
- T-test on raw counts violates normality and constant variance assumptions.  
Better: log-transformation + ANCOVA or non-parametric tests.  

4) Chi-square tests with very low expected counts  
- Example: Flare-ups if only 1–2 cases per group.  
Why inappropriate:  
- Chi-square approximation invalid when expected cell counts <5; p-values unreliable.  
Better: Fisher’s exact test.  

5) Correlation analyses (e.g., Pearson) between CFU reduction and pain using raw scales  
Why inappropriate:  
- Pain ordinal; Pearson correlation assumes both variables continuous and approximately normal.  
- Non-linear relationships and zero inflation likely.  
Better: Spearman rank correlation or regression models tailored to distributions (optional only).  

6) Multiple unadjusted comparisons without controlling for multiplicity when claiming multiple primary outcomes  
- Example: Treating pain at each of 7 time points and bacterial reduction as co-primary, each tested at α=0.05 without adjustment, yet drawing strong confirmatory conclusions.  
Why inappropriate:  
- Inflates type I error;  
- Undermines interpretability of “significant” findings.  
Better: Pre-specify primary time point(s) or adjust α (e.g., Bonferroni) for confirmatory claims; or treat multiple times as exploratory.  

7) Stepwise variable selection for regression  
Why inappropriate:  
- Data-driven, high risk of overfitting;  
- Unstable in small samples.  
Better: Pre-specify key covariates based on clinical rationale.  

8) Using parametric survival models for pain resolution without verifying proportional hazards or hazard functional form  
Why inappropriate:  
- Violated assumptions can yield misleading hazard ratios;  
- Discrete time points not well-suited to continuous-time parametric models without caution.

TASK 4: SUMMARY OF OVERALL ANALYTICAL APPROACH  

- The study is a two-arm parallel-group RCT with repeated measures for pain and pre/post measures for bacterial counts.  
- For bacterial reduction, analyze log-transformed CFU to compare groups using ANCOVA (post CFU ~ group + baseline CFU), and demonstrate within-group reductions with paired tests.  
- For postoperative pain, treat the outcome as ordinal measured repeatedly; use non-parametric tests at key time points and, ideally, an ordinal GEE or mixed model for the full trajectory. Supplement with clinically interpretable binary summaries (any pain, moderate/severe pain, analgesic use).  
- For flare-ups and swelling, use categorical methods (Fisher’s exact, ordinal tests) due to low event rates.  
- Adjust for key initial characteristics (baseline pain, baseline CFU, presence of radiolucency) in regression models to improve precision and robustness.  
- Emphasize intention-to-treat analyses, with careful handling of missing data (e.g., last observation carried forward or, preferably, multiple imputation for pain; baseline observation carried forward not recommended unless justified).  
- This analytical strategy aligns with the study design, measurement scales, and typical distributions of pain and CFU, ensuring both statistical validity and clinical interpretability.
