### TASK 1: INFORMATION EXTRACTION

#### Study Characteristics:
1. **Study Design**: 
   - Randomized clinical trial (RCT), single-blinded (patients and outcome assessors were blinded).

2. **Study Objectives**:
   - To compare the effect of XP-endo Shaper and conventional rotary files (iRaCe) on postoperative pain and bacterial reduction in oval root canals with necrotic pulps.

3. **Study Groups**:
   - **Group A**: XP-endo Shaper instrumentation system.
   - **Group B**: iRaCe rotary instrumentation system.

4. **Sample Size**:
   - Total of 60 participants, with 30 participants in each group.

5. **Eligibility Criteria**:
   - **Inclusion**: Healthy men and women aged 20–60 years, mandibular premolars with single oval root canals and necrotic pulps, with or without periapical radiolucency (<3 mm), and no/mild preoperative pain.
   - **Exclusion**: Pregnant women, recent use of analgesics/antibiotics (past 12 hours), teeth with abnormal occlusal contact, acute periapical abscess, periodontal pockets >5 mm, mobility >grade I, alveolar bone loss >50%, and non-restorable teeth.

6. **Variables**:
   - **Primary Outcomes**:
     - Postoperative pain (measured on a modified visual analogue scale [VAS]).
     - Bacterial reduction (measured as colony-forming units [CFUs/mL]).
   - **Secondary Outcomes**:
     - Incidence of flare-ups (binary outcome: Yes/No).
     - Analgesic consumption (number of tablets).
   - **Covariates**:
     - Patient-related factors (age, gender, tooth type, pulp vitality, periapical status).

7. **Measurements**:
   - **Postoperative Pain**: Assessed using a 10 cm modified VAS at 6, 12, 24 hours, and days 2, 3, 4, and 5.
   - **Bacterial Reduction**: CFUs/mL quantified using bacterial culturing on BHI agar plates.
   - **Flare-ups**: Assessed using a four-point swelling scale and recorded as a binary outcome.

8. **Data Collection Methods**:
   - Randomization: Computer-generated sequence with allocation concealed in opaque envelopes.
   - Blinding: Patients and outcome assessors blinded to group allocation.
   - Pre- and post-instrumentation bacterial samples collected using sterile paper points and cultured.
   - Pain and analgesic use recorded by patients on provided charts.

9. **Intervention Details**:
   - **Group A (XP-endo Shaper)**: Single-file system with specific speed, torque, and brushing strokes.
   - **Group B (iRaCe)**: Multi-file rotary system with specific speed, torque, and irrigation protocols.
   - Both groups used the same irrigation volume (30 mL) and filling technique.

### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY
1. **Test Name**: Independent Samples t-test (or Mann-Whitney U test if non-parametric).
   - **Purpose**: Compare mean postoperative pain scores between the two groups at each time point.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Continuous outcome (VAS scores) with two independent groups.
     2. Randomized design ensures comparability between groups.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Assumptions of normality and homogeneity of variance may not hold.
   - **When NOT to Use**: If VAS scores are heavily skewed or sample size is too small for parametric testing.
   - **Alternative**: Mann-Whitney U test for non-parametric data.

2. **Test Name**: Paired Samples t-test (or Wilcoxon Signed-Rank Test if non-parametric).
   - **Purpose**: Compare pre- and post-instrumentation bacterial counts (CFUs/mL) within each group.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Paired data structure (pre- and post-measurements within the same group).
     2. Continuous outcome (CFUs/mL).
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Non-normal distribution of bacterial counts.
   - **When NOT to Use**: If CFUs/mL data are highly skewed or contain outliers.
   - **Alternative**: Wilcoxon Signed-Rank Test.

3. **Test Name**: Chi-Square Test (or Fisher’s Exact Test for small sample sizes).
   - **Purpose**: Compare the incidence of flare-ups (binary outcome) between the two groups.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Binary categorical outcome (Yes/No).
     2. Two independent groups.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Small cell counts in contingency table.
   - **When NOT to Use**: If expected frequencies in any cell are <5.
   - **Alternative**: Fisher’s Exact Test.

---

#### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED
1. **Test Name**: Repeated Measures ANOVA (or Friedman Test if non-parametric).
   - **Purpose**: Analyze changes in postoperative pain scores over time between groups.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Longitudinal data with repeated measures (pain scores at multiple time points).
     2. Allows assessment of time-group interaction.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Assumes sphericity, which may not hold.
   - **When NOT to Use**: If sphericity assumption is violated and corrections (e.g., Greenhouse-Geisser) are not feasible.
   - **Alternative**: Friedman Test (non-parametric).

2. **Test Name**: Logistic Regression.
   - **Purpose**: Assess predictors of flare-ups (e.g., group allocation, age, gender).
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Binary outcome (flare-up: Yes/No).
     2. Allows adjustment for covariates.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Small sample size may lead to overfitting.
   - **When NOT to Use**: If sample size per predictor variable is insufficient.
   - **Alternative**: Exact Logistic Regression.

---

#### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL
1. **Test Name**: Kaplan-Meier Survival Analysis.
   - **Purpose**: Analyze time to pain resolution between groups.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Time-to-event data (time to pain resolution).
     2. Censored data (patients who do not report resolution within the study period).
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Requires precise time-to-event data, which may not be available.
   - **When NOT to Use**: If time-to-resolution data are incomplete or unreliable.
   - **Alternative**: Descriptive analysis of pain resolution rates.

2. **Test Name**: Effect Size Calculation (e.g., Cohen’s d for continuous outcomes).
   - **Purpose**: Quantify the magnitude of differences between groups.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Provides clinically meaningful interpretation of results.
     2. Complements p-values by assessing practical significance.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Does not account for variability in small samples.
   - **When NOT to Use**: If data are highly skewed or sample sizes are unequal.
   - **Alternative**: Non-parametric effect size measures (e.g., rank-biserial correlation).
