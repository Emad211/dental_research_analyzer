## TASK 1 — Information extraction (study characteristics)

### Study design & setting
- **Design:** Historical (retrospective) cohort with **time-to-event** follow-up.
- **Setting:** Public Dental Service, Norrbotten county, Sweden (29 general dental clinics; in 2006: 149 GDPs performed ≥1 RCT).
- **Index period (baseline):** 1 Jan–31 Dec **2006**.
- **Follow-up window:** Up to **10 years** (ascertainment mainly to 2016; data collection performed **Sep 2016–May 2018** using records + targeted patient follow-up).

### Objectives
- **Primary aim:** Estimate **10-year survival** of root-filled teeth treated by GDPs (survival defined as *tooth retained*, regardless of radiologic apical status/symptoms).
- **Secondary aim:** Identify **prognostic factors** for tooth loss (extraction).

### Study population and sampling
- **Source cohort:** 3,676 individuals with ≥1 completed RCT in 2006 (3,925 teeth).
- **Excluded:** 331 individuals who died during the 10-year period.
- **Sampling method:** Random ordering in Excel using RAND; first **302** invited.
- **Final included:** **280 individuals** (treated by 102 GDPs in 28 clinics).

- **Unit of analysis:** **One tooth per person** (if multiple treated in 2006, one tooth randomly selected).

- **Event:** **Tooth extraction** (yes/no) with **event time** in days from **date of RCT completion** to extraction.

- **Outcome definition explicitly ignores:** apical radiographic status and symptoms for survival endpoint.

## TASK 2 — Statistical Analysis Plan (SAP)

### Overview of analysis principles
- **Primary estimand:** 10-year **tooth survival probability** after GDP-performed RCT (one randomly selected tooth/patient), with time origin = **date of RCT completion**.
- **Secondary estimand:** Association between prognostic factors and **hazard of extraction** over follow-up.
- **Time scale:** days (report also in years).
- **Significance:** two-sided α = 0.05; report 95% CIs. For prognostic modeling, emphasize effect sizes and CIs over p-values.
- **Clustering:** one tooth/person removes within-person clustering; however, teeth are clustered within **GDP/clinic**. Consider robust variance or frailty (recommended).

# CATEGORY A — MANDATORY tests/analyses

## A1) Kaplan–Meier (KM) survival estimation (with Greenwood 95% CI)
- **Purpose:** Estimate survival function and **10-year survival probability**; display survival curve.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Outcome is **time-to-extraction** with **right censoring**.
  2. Nonparametric; no need to assume a baseline hazard form.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - If there is substantial **interval censoring** (unknown extraction dates) KM is not strictly correct (midpoint imputation approximates).
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If event times are largely interval-censored or only known at discrete assessment times without exact times.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Turnbull estimator** for interval-censored survival; or parametric interval-censored models.

## A2) Log-rank test (univariable time-to-event comparisons)
- **Purpose:** Compare survival curves across categorical predictors (e.g., sessions >5 vs <5; acute problems ≥2 vs ≤1).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Standard test for differences in **survival distributions** under censoring.
  2. Simple screening/description before multivariable modeling.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Low power and misleading inference if **non-proportional hazards** (curves cross) or if effects are early/late only.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Clear evidence/expectation of non-PH; heavy ties with discrete-time recording where alternative tests may fit better.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Weighted log-rank tests** (e.g., Fleming–Harrington), or compare **restricted mean survival time (RMST)**.

## A3) Cox proportional hazards regression (multivariable)
- **Purpose:** Estimate adjusted associations (hazard ratios) between prognostic factors and extraction.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Handles **right-censoring** and varying follow-up.
  2. Provides **adjusted** effects for multiple confounders; does not require specifying baseline hazard.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - If **proportional hazards (PH)** assumption is violated for key predictors and cannot be remedied.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Severe PH violations without a workable extension (time-varying effects/stratification), or if event count is too low for stable multivariable modeling.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Extended Cox** with time-varying coefficients; **stratified Cox**; **accelerated failure time (AFT)** models; or **RMST regression**.

## A4) PH assumption diagnostics (Schoenfeld residuals + log(-log) plots)
- **Purpose:** Verify the core Cox model assumption.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Cox inference relies on PH; diagnostics are required for validity.
  2. Identifies predictors needing time-varying effects or stratification.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - With few events, tests have low power and plots can be unstable.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Never “not use” conceptually; but interpret cautiously with sparse events.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Time-varying coefficients, stratification, AFT/RMST approaches.

## A5) Descriptive statistics + baseline comparability and missingness assessment
- **Purpose:** Characterize sample, event counts, follow-up; quantify missing data by variable and by outcome status.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Observational cohort needs transparent description and missingness quantification.
  2. Supports interpretation and informs imputation/sensitivity analyses.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - “Significance testing” for baseline differences is often misused; focus should be descriptive.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Do not use p-values to claim groups “balanced” (not a randomized study).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - If distributions are skewed, use medians/IQR; for categorical sparse data, use exact counts/percentages.

# CATEGORY B — RECOMMENDED analyses

## B1) Competing risks analysis (Fine–Gray or cause-specific Cox), if death during follow-up is relevant
- **Purpose:** Account for **death** precluding extraction (competing event), especially because deaths occurred in the source cohort.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. In older populations, death can meaningfully alter estimated cumulative incidence of extraction.
  2. Provides clinically interpretable **cumulative incidence** of extraction in presence of competing mortality.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - In the analyzed sample, deceased individuals were excluded; competing risk may be unobservable post-exclusion and can’t be fully addressed without including them.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If death information after inclusion is unavailable or deaths are extremely rare.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated / not feasible:**
  - Sensitivity analysis assuming worst/best-case extraction among those who died; or include deaths with competing risk if data available.

## B2) Robust variance / multilevel (frailty) survival model for clustering by GDP/clinic
- **Purpose:** Adjust standard errors (and potentially effects) for correlation of outcomes within providers/clinics.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Teeth treated by same GDP/clinic may share unmeasured practice factors (rubber dam culture, restoration protocols).
  2. Ignoring clustering can underestimate SEs → overconfident CIs.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - If most GDPs contribute very few teeth (sparse clusters), frailty variance may be hard to estimate.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Extremely sparse clusters with near-unique provider per tooth; robust sandwich may still be used cautiously.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Cox with cluster-robust SEs** (sandwich) at GDP/clinic level; or fixed effects for clinic if feasible.

## B3) Multiple imputation (MI) for missing covariates
- **Purpose:** Reduce bias and improve efficiency versus complete-case analysis given missing analogue records/variables.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Many predictors likely missing not completely at random; MI can be better than dropping records.
  2. Preserves sample size and event information for Cox modeling.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - If missingness is extreme for key variables or not plausibly MAR (e.g., systematic loss of whole record types tied to outcome).
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When missingness mechanism is clearly MNAR without auxiliary variables; or too few events to support MI models.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Sensitivity analyses (pattern-mixture, best/worst case), or restrict to variables with acceptable completeness.

## B4) Model building strategy with events-per-parameter control (penalization)
- **Test/Method:** Penalized Cox regression (e.g., ridge, lasso, elastic net) or Firth-type penalization in survival context.
- **Purpose:** Prevent overfitting and instability with limited extractions.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Dental survival studies often have modest events relative to candidate predictors.
  2. Penalization improves predictive stability and shrinks exaggerated HRs.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Harder to interpret; requires careful tuning and validation.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If primary goal is strictly causal inference with prespecified confounder adjustment only (then keep parsimonious standard Cox).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Pre-specify a small confounder set; use AIC/BIC cautiously; or use bootstrap shrinkage.

## B5) Reliability statistics for calibration (inter-/intra-examiner agreement)
- **Purpose:** Quantify measurement reproducibility for record-based assessments.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Many predictors are subjective (radiographic assessments, chart interpretation).
  2. Supports credibility of prognostic factor analyses.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Small calibration samples yield wide uncertainty.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Not applicable for automatically captured variables (age, sex).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - If prevalence is extreme and kappa is unstable, report **percent agreement** and **Gwet’s AC1**; for ordinal variables use **weighted kappa**; for continuous use **ICC**.

# CATEGORY C — OPTIONAL analyses (supplementary insight)

## C1) Restricted Mean Survival Time (RMST) comparison/regression to 10 years
- **Purpose:** Quantify absolute time retained (mean survival time up to τ=10 years) and compare groups without PH assumption.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Clinically interpretable (“months of tooth retention gained/lost”).
  2. Valid even when hazards are non-proportional.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Needs adequate follow-up coverage to τ; censoring before τ can inflate uncertainty.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If few subjects are observed close to 10 years (heavy early censoring).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Use smaller τ (e.g., 5 years) or AFT models.

## C2) Sensitivity analysis for unknown extraction dates (interval censoring)
- **Purpose:** Assess impact of midpoint-imputed extraction times.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Midpoint imputation can bias hazards if intervals are long.
  2. Demonstrates robustness of conclusions.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Requires access to interval bounds (last-present/first-absent dates).
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If interval bounds are unavailable for most cases.
- **Alternative:**
  - Proper **interval-censored survival models**; or repeat analyses using earliest/latest possible extraction times (bounds).

## C3) Influence diagnostics / outlier checking in Cox (dfbetas, deviance residuals)
- **Purpose:** Identify influential observations (e.g., very late/early extractions, unusual covariate patterns).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Small event counts can be dominated by a few cases.
  2. Helps detect data errors and assess robustness.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Not definitive; can encourage over-interpretation.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Don’t remove observations solely due to influence without clinical/data justification.
- **Alternative:**
  - Robust regression approaches; report analyses with/without influential points as sensitivity.

## C4) Nonlinear effects for continuous predictors (restricted cubic splines)
- **Purpose:** Model potential nonlinearity (e.g., elapsed days access→fill; apical size; age).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Clinical relationships are rarely strictly linear on log-hazard scale.
  2. Reduces mis-specification bias vs linear term.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Requires sufficient events; otherwise unstable.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Very low events or highly missing continuous covariates.
- **Alternative:**
  - Categorization using clinically meaningful cut-points (with caution), or fractional polynomials.

## TASK 3 — Inappropriate tests (should NOT be used) and why

1. **Independent-samples t-test / one-way ANOVA on “survival time” ignoring censoring**
   - Censoring makes observed times incomplete; mean time comparisons will be biased.

2. **Chi-square test of extraction (yes/no) at 10 years without accounting for varying follow-up**
   - Discards time information and mishandles censoring; misclassifies censored subjects as non-events.

3. **Standard logistic regression for “extracted by 10 years” as primary analysis**
   - Same issues: ignores censoring/time-to-event; only acceptable as a secondary analysis if everyone has uniform 10-year ascertainment (not the case here).

4. **Paired tests (paired t-test, McNemar)**
   - No paired/repeated measures design on the same unit across two conditions.

5. **Pearson correlation between predictors and survival time**
   - Not meaningful with censoring and mixed variable types; confounded associations.

6. **Linear regression on time-to-extraction**
   - Violates normality/heteroscedasticity and ignores censoring; predicted negative times possible.

## TASK 4 — Summary justification of analytical approach

The endpoint is **time to extraction** with **right censoring**, so the core analysis must use **survival methods**: KM to estimate 10-year survival and Cox regression to evaluate prognostic factors while adjusting for confounding. Diagnostics (PH checks), handling of **missing covariate data**, and attention to **provider/clinic clustering** are necessary to make inferences credible. Sensitivity analyses for uncertain extraction timing and (if feasible) competing risks strengthen robustness and interpretability.

#### A1. Descriptive statistics

#### A2. Kaplan–Meier survival analysis

**Test/Method name**  
- Kaplan–Meier estimator and survival curve.

**Purpose**  
- Estimate the tooth survival function over time; obtain survival probabilities at key time points (e.g., 1, 5, 10 years).  
- Visualize survival across the whole follow‑up.

**Why appropriate**  
1. The outcome is time‑to‑extraction with right censoring; Kaplan–Meier is the standard non‑parametric estimator for survival probabilities.  
2. Some extractions occur at varying times; KM accommodates variable follow‑up and incomplete follow‑up due to censoring.  
3. The study explicitly aims to estimate 10‑year survival; KM facilitates reporting survival at specific time points with CI.

**Why it may not be appropriate**  
- It does not adjust for covariates; crude survival may be confounded by patient/tooth characteristics.  
- Interpretation can be unstable late in follow‑up if few subjects remain at risk.

**When NOT to use**  
- When no time‑to‑event structure exists (e.g., binary endpoint at fixed time).  
- When censoring is clearly informative and cannot be handled.

**Alternative if assumptions are violated**  
- If right‑censoring assumption (non‑informative) is strongly violated:

#### A3. Log‑rank test for unadjusted group comparisons

**Test/Method name**  
- Log‑rank test (Mantel–Cox).

**Purpose**  
- Compare survival curves between groups (e.g., teeth with vs without apical periodontitis; different restoration types) in an unadjusted fashion.

**Why appropriate**  
1. It tests whether there is a statistically significant difference in the entire survival experience between groups, accommodating censoring.  
2. It is non‑parametric (does not assume the shape of the hazard function), suitable for exploratory comparisons of categorical predictors.

**Why it may not be appropriate**  
- It does not adjust for confounding (e.g., tooth type, age).  
- Sensitive to proportional hazards (PH) assumption for interpretation in hazard terms.

**When NOT to use**  
- For continuous covariates (unless arbitrarily categorized – usually not recommended).  
- When there is heavy crossing of survival curves, the log‑rank test has reduced interpretability and power.

**Alternative if assumptions are violated**  
- Weighted log‑rank tests (e.g., Tarone–Ware, Peto–Prentice) if early vs late hazards differ.  
- Regression‑based survival models (Cox, flexible parametric) with interaction or time‑varying effects.

#### A4. Cox proportional hazards regression – univariable

**Test/Method name**  
- Univariable Cox proportional hazards (PH) regression.

**Purpose**  
- Estimate the association between each predictor and time‑to‑extraction, expressed as hazard ratios (HR) with 95% CI, while incorporating censoring.

**Why appropriate**  
1. Outcome is time‑to‑event; Cox PH is the standard semi‑parametric model for such data.  
2. Allows quantification of effect sizes for predictors (e.g., tooth type, apical status, number of sessions) while respecting time dimension and censoring.  
3. Facilitates identification of candidate variables for multivariable modelling and assessment of linearity for continuous predictors.

**Why it may not be appropriate**  
- Cox model assumes proportional hazards; if hazards for a covariate are non‑proportional, estimates may be biased.  
- With a relatively small number of events (~40), univariable models are fine, but multivariable complexity must be limited to avoid overfitting.

**When NOT to use**  
- When the time of event is extremely imprecise or interval‑censored in a way that discrete‑time models would be more appropriate.  
- When the functional form of covariates vs hazard is clearly non‑linear and not modeled properly.

**Alternative if assumptions are violated**  
- If PH assumption fails:
  - Use **time‑varying coefficients** in Cox (interaction with log(time)).  
  - Or **stratified Cox** if proportionality fails for key categorical covariates.  
- If event rate is very low or sample small:
  - Consider **Firth‑penalized Cox** or exact methods.

#### A5. Cox proportional hazards regression – multivariable (core prognostic model)

**Test/Method name**  
- Multivariable Cox proportional hazards regression.

**Purpose**  
- Identify independent prognostic factors for extraction, adjusting for potential confounders and accounting for the grouped confounder structure described by the authors.  
- Generate adjusted hazard ratios for predictors of clinical interest.

**Why appropriate**  
1. Addresses the secondary aim: identification of prognostic factors affecting survival.  
2. Adjusts for confounding (e.g., tooth type, apical status, pulp status, number of sessions) to isolate independent effects.  
3. Retains time‑to‑event nature and censoring.

**Why it may not be appropriate**  
- With only ~40 events, degrees of freedom are limited; including too many predictors will cause overfitting and unstable estimates.  
- PH assumption must hold (at least approximately).  
- Some predictors have very sparse exposure (e.g., sinus tract, prosthetic abutment) making coefficient estimates unreliable.

**When NOT to use**  
- If events‑per‑variable (EPV) ratio is too low (e.g., <10), especially in standard maximum likelihood Cox.  
- If PH assumption severely violated for many key predictors and cannot be handled with stratification or time‑varying terms.

**Alternative if assumptions are violated or sample too small**  
- Use **penalized Cox regression** (e.g., ridge or LASSO, or Firth correction) to reduce overfitting and handle sparse data.  
- If PH assumption fails:
  - Use **flexible parametric survival models** (Royston–Parmar) or **accelerated failure time (AFT) models** (Weibull, log‑logistic).  
- If socio‑clinical interest is limited to 10‑year status:

#### A6. Assessment of proportional hazards assumption

**Test/Method name**

**Purpose**  
- Check the core assumption of Cox model that hazard ratios are constant over time.

**Why appropriate**  
1. Ensures validity of Cox model inference; required before interpreting HRs.  
2. Can detect time‑varying effects (e.g., restoration type influencing early vs late failures differently).

**Why it may not be appropriate**  
- With small sample and few events, residual‑based tests may have low power and be noisy.  
- For categorical variables with few failures in one level, residuals may not be reliable.

**When NOT to use**  
- Not to be omitted when using Cox; but interpretation should be cautious with very small event numbers.

**Alternative if assumptions are violated**  
- Incorporate **time‑dependent covariates** (e.g., interaction with log(time)).  
- **Stratified Cox** by variables violating PH.  
- Non‑PH models (AFT, flexible parametric models) if violation is pervasive.

*   **Quality Control:** Inter-examiner and intra-examiner calibration and agreement assessments.

### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY (Essential for incomplete analysis)

**1. Kaplan-Meier Estimator**
*   **Purpose:** To estimate and plot the overall 10-year survival probability of the root-filled teeth.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) It perfectly handles time-to-event data with right-censored observations (patients lost to follow-up or who haven't had an extraction). 
    2) It provides a non-parametric estimate of survival at any specific time point (e.g., exactly at 5 or 10 years), which directly answers the primary objective.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It cannot adjust for multiple confounding variables simultaneously.
*   **When NOT to use:** When the exact time of the event is unknown for all patients, or if there is heavy interval censoring that cannot be approximated.
*   **Alternative:** Actuarial (Life-Table) method.

**2. Multivariable Cox Proportional Hazards Regression**
*   **Purpose:** To identify independent prognostic factors for tooth extraction while controlling for confounders.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) It allows for the evaluation of multiple categorical and continuous independent variables simultaneously on a time-to-event outcome. 
    2) It calculates adjusted Hazard Ratios (HR), which quantify the risk of extraction associated with each predictor, fulfilling the secondary objective.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It assumes that the hazard ratio between groups remains constant over time (Proportional Hazards assumption).
*   **When NOT to use:** When the Proportional Hazards assumption is severely violated (e.g., a variable is highly protective in year 1 but increases risk in year 9).
*   **Alternative:** Parametric survival models (e.g., Weibull, Exponential) or Extended Cox models with time-dependent covariates.

**3. Cohen’s Kappa (κ) / Intraclass Correlation Coefficient (ICC)**
*   **Purpose:** To quantify inter-examiner and intra-examiner reliability during the calibration phase.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) The methodology explicitly states that records were reviewed by two authors independently to assess agreement. 
    2) Kappa is the gold standard for categorical variables (e.g., apical status: normal/abnormal), while ICC is ideal for continuous variables (e.g., apical size).
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** Kappa can be misleadingly low if the prevalence of a specific finding (e.g., procedural accidents) is extremely low (the "Kappa paradox").
*   **When NOT to use:** When comparing agreement on variables that were automatically extracted by the computer (e.g., age, gender).
*   **Alternative:** Gwet’s AC1 statistic (to overcome the Kappa paradox) or simple percentage agreement.

#### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED (Enhances quality and rigor)

**1. Log-Rank Test**
*   **Purpose:** To compare the survival distributions of two or more independent categorical groups (e.g., acute problems vs. no acute problems) before multivariable modeling.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) It provides a formal statistical test (p-value) to see if survival curves differ significantly. 
    2) It helps in the initial screening of variables to decide which should be included in the multivariable Cox model.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It does not provide an effect size (like a Hazard Ratio), only a p-value.
*   **When NOT to use:** When comparing continuous variables (e.g., age in years) without categorizing them first.
*   **Alternative:** Peto-Peto-Prentice test or Wilcoxon (Breslow) test (gives more weight to early events).

**2. Schoenfeld Residuals Test**
*   **Purpose:** To test the Proportional Hazards (PH) assumption of the Cox regression model.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) Validates the mathematical foundation of the Cox model; without it, the model's conclusions could be spurious. 
    2) It allows the researcher to plot residuals over time to visually inspect for violations.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** In small sample sizes with few events, the test lacks the statistical power to detect violations.
*   **When NOT to use:** When using parametric survival models that do not rely on the PH assumption.
*   **Alternative:** Visual inspection of log-minus-log (LML) survival plots.

#### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL (Supplementary insights)

**1. Shared Frailty Cox Model (Mixed-Effects Survival Model)**
*   **Purpose:** To account for potential clustering effects at the dentist or clinic level.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) The 280 teeth were treated by 102 different GDPs across 28 clinics. A specific dentist's skill level might cluster outcomes. 
    2) It adds a random effect ("frailty") to the model, providing more accurate standard errors.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** The model may fail to converge if the number of events (extractions) per GDP is too low (which is highly likely here, as 280 patients / 102 GDPs = ~2.7 patients per GDP).
*   **When NOT to use:** When there is no hierarchical or clustered structure to the data.
*   **Alternative:** Cox model with robust standard errors (Huber-White sandwich estimator).

**2. Competing Risks Regression (Fine-Gray Model)**
*   **Purpose:** To account for patient death as a competing risk to tooth extraction.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) If a patient dies during the 10-year follow-up, they can never experience the event (tooth extraction). 
    2) Standard Cox models treat deaths as right-censored (assuming they could still have an extraction if they lived), which overestimates the risk of extraction.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** The authors already excluded 331 patients who died *before* the cohort was finalized. If the mortality rate in the remaining 280 is negligible, this complex model is unnecessary.
*   **When NOT to use:** When the competing event is rare or non-existent.
*   **Alternative:** Cause-specific Cox proportional hazards models.

### TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS

*   **Binary Logistic Regression:** Inappropriate because it treats the outcome as a simple binary variable (Extracted: Yes/No) at a fixed point in time, completely ignoring the *time* it took for the event to occur and failing to account for censored patients who dropped out at year 3 versus year 9.
*   **Independent Samples t-test / ANOVA:** Inappropriate for comparing the "time to extraction" between groups. These tests assume normal distribution of continuous data and cannot handle right-censored data (patients whose teeth survived past the study period).
*   **Pearson Correlation:** Inappropriate for assessing the relationship between continuous predictors (e.g., age) and survival time, as it cannot incorporate censored data.

### TASK 4: SUMMARY

The overall analytical approach must be rooted in **Survival Analysis** (Time-to-Event analysis). Because the primary outcome is tooth extraction over a 10-year period, and because patients have varying lengths of follow-up (censoring), standard regression or mean-comparison tests are invalid. 

The strategy should begin with data validation (Kappa/ICC for examiner reliability), proceed to univariable descriptive survival estimates (Kaplan-Meier curves and Log-rank tests), and culminate in a Multivariable Cox Proportional Hazards Regression to identify true prognostic factors while controlling for clinical confounders. Given the hierarchical nature of dental care, exploring clustering effects (frailty models) would represent a gold-standard biostatistical approach.
