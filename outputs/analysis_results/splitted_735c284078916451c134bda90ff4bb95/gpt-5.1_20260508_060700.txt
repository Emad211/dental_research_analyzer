TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

Below is a comprehensive plan structured around the actual design and data: repeated continuous pain measurements (VAS) in two randomized groups.

CATEGORY A – MANDATORY ANALYSES

A1. Descriptive statistics and baseline comparability

Test / Procedure  
- Descriptive statistics + group comparisons at baseline  
  - Tests:  
    - Continuous baseline variables (e.g., age, baseline pain score if available): t-test or Mann–Whitney U.  
    - Categorical variables (e.g., sex, tooth type, PAI category): Chi-square test or Fisher’s exact test.

Purpose  
- To summarize sample characteristics and verify that randomization produced comparable groups at baseline.

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) Randomization with small sample (n=18 per group) can still yield chance imbalances; these summaries document baseline comparability.  
2) Descriptive statistics (mean, SD, median, IQR, counts, percentages) give context and are necessary for interpretation and reproducibility.

Why it may not be appropriate  
- Excessive “significance testing” on baseline differences can be misleading and is not strictly necessary in randomized trials.

When NOT to use  
- Avoid formal hypothesis tests for baseline variables as primary evidence; they should not drive post hoc adjustments unless there is clear clinical rationale.

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- If continuous baseline variables are skewed / non-normal or n is small: use non-parametric Mann–Whitney U instead of t-test.  
- For sparse categorical data (small expected cell counts): use Fisher’s exact test instead of Chi-square.

---

A2. Primary comparison of pain intensity over time (longitudinal)

Primary approach: Linear mixed-effects model (LMM) for repeated measures

Test / Model  
- Linear mixed-effects model with:
  - Outcome: VAS pain score at each time point (continuous).
  - Fixed effects: group (LLLT vs sham), time (categorical), group × time interaction.
  - Random effect: patient-level random intercept (and optionally random slope for time, if supported by data).
  - Covariance structure: unstructured or simpler (e.g., AR(1)) depending on fit.

Purpose  
- To compare postoperative pain trajectories between LLLT and sham across all time points simultaneously.

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) Repeated VAS measures within individuals are correlated; LMM models this correlation structure correctly and yields valid standard errors.  
2) Handles unbalanced data and occasional missing time-point observations without discarding entire cases (if in practice some time points are missing).  
3) Provides a direct test of group, time, and group×time interaction (i.e., whether pain reduction over time differs by treatment).  
4) Continuous VAS scores are suitable for linear models if distribution is approximately symmetric (especially with n~36 and multiple observations).

Why it may not be appropriate  
- If VAS distributions are highly skewed, show ceiling/floor effects, or strongly non-linear trajectories, standard linear assumptions may be questionable.

When NOT to use  
- If VAS is heavily non-normal with extreme outliers and transformations do not help.  
- If the outcome is dichotomized (e.g., pain vs no pain); then linear models on continuous scale are inappropriate.

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Non-parametric longitudinal tests:
  - Rank-based mixed models (e.g., using ranks or robust methods).
  - Repeated measures non-parametric tests like the Brunner–Langer approach (non-parametric ANOVA-type statistics).  
- Generalized linear mixed model (GLMM) with appropriate link (e.g., gamma distribution with log link for skewed data, or ordinal logistic model for categorized pain).

---

A3. Time-specific group comparisons of pain (key time points)

Test  
- Independent-samples t-test (or Mann–Whitney U) at each clinically important time point:
  - E.g., 4 h, 24 h, 48 h (day 2), day 7.
- Ideally pre-specify one or two primary time points (e.g., 24 h) to control multiplicity.

Purpose  
- To provide interpretable, clinically meaningful comparisons of pain at specific times (e.g., immediate vs late postoperative periods).

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) Clinicians often focus on pain at certain times (e.g., night after treatment), so time-specific comparisons are clinically relevant and easily interpretable.  
2) T-tests (or non-parametric equivalents) are simple and robust with balanced group sizes.

Why it may not be appropriate  
- Multiple testing at many time points inflates Type I error if not adjusted or pre-specified.

When NOT to use  
- As the sole analytic method without considering correlation across time; that would ignore the repeated measures nature and reduce efficiency.  
- If sample size is very small at a given time (due to missing data), leading to unstable estimates.

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Mann–Whitney U test if VAS is non-normal or with outliers.  
- For binary outcome (presence/absence of pain), use Chi-square or Fisher’s exact test instead of t-test.

---

A4. Summary measure comparison (e.g., AUC)

Test  
- Independent-samples t-test (or Mann–Whitney U) on:
  - Area under the pain-time curve (AUC) for each patient, or
  - Summary descriptors like mean pain over first 24 h, or
  - “Peak pain” as a single outcome.

Purpose  
- To provide an integrated measure of overall pain burden and compare it between groups.

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) AUC reduces multiple correlated pain measurements into a single clinically interpretable summary (overall pain experience).  
2) Reduces multiplicity problems; a single primary summary test rather than multiple time-specific comparisons.  
3) Works well with equally spaced time points and complete trajectories, as in this study.

Why it may not be appropriate  
- AUC may mask clinically relevant differences at specific critical times (e.g., first night) even if total burden is similar.

When NOT to use  
- If many time-point data are missing or unevenly spaced, making AUC calculation biased or complex.  
- If some time points differ in clinical importance (e.g., first 24 h vs later); then weighted measures are needed instead.

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Non-parametric tests on ranks of AUC (Mann–Whitney U).  
- Weighted AUC or composite scores (e.g., first 24 h only) to reflect clinical priorities.

CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED ANALYSES (Enhancing rigor)

B1. Analysis of proportion of patients with clinically relevant pain

Test  
- Chi-square test or Fisher’s exact test at each relevant time for:
  - Proportion with “moderate or severe pain” vs “none/mild”, based on VAS category thresholds (e.g., 0–3 mild, 4–6 moderate, ≥7 severe).

Purpose  
- To complement mean VAS comparisons with clinically interpretable categorical outcomes (e.g., how many patients had significant pain).

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) Categorical pain levels are often more intuitive for clinicians and patients.  
2) Proportion comparisons can reveal differences that averages may not (especially if distributions are skewed).

Why it may not be appropriate  
- Categorization of continuous VAS leads to information loss and reduced power.

When NOT to use  
- As the primary analysis, if the study was powered and designed for continuous VAS.  
- With many categories and sparse data (low counts in some cells).

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Use exact tests (Fisher’s exact) when expected counts are low.  
- Combine categories (e.g., moderate+severe vs mild+none) to avoid sparse cells.

B2. Sensitivity analyses for analytic set (ITT vs per-protocol)

Test  
- Re-run primary models (LMM or AUC comparisons) including:
  - All randomized participants (intention-to-treat), treating analgesic use as:
    - Censoring (e.g., last observation carried forward), or
    - Covariate or outcome modifier (e.g., indicator variable).
- Compare with per-protocol results (participants who did not take analgesics).

Purpose  
- To evaluate robustness of conclusions to handling of patients who took analgesics and were excluded/replaced.

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) Addresses potential bias introduced by exclusion of participants post-randomization.  
2) ITT-style analysis better reflects real-world conditions and preserves randomization benefits.  
3) Sensitivity analysis increases confidence in the primary conclusion.

Why it may not be appropriate  
- Original design explicitly excluded analgesic users; actual compliance with ITT requires additional assumptions and imputation, which may not be acceptable to investigators.

When NOT to use  
- If detailed data on analgesic users (pain while/after analgesic intake) were not collected, making meaningful inclusion impossible.

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Perform “as-treated” analysis (classify by actually received treatment) as supplementary to per-protocol.  
- Use multiple imputation for missing pain values after analgesic intake, if timing is known.

Test / Model  
- LMM or linear regression including covariates:
  - Baseline VAS pain (if recorded).
  - Age, sex.
  - Tooth type (first vs second molar).
  - PAI score (2 vs 3).
  - Any other clinically relevant variables.

Purpose  
- To increase precision and evaluate whether treatment effect is robust after controlling for potential prognostic factors.

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) Adjusting for baseline pain greatly reduces residual variance and improves power.  
2) Controls for any chance imbalances that may have arisen, particularly with small n (18 per group).  
3) Allows exploration of whether treatment effects differ across subgroups (though this should be secondary).

Why it may not be appropriate  
- With n=36 total, too many covariates could overfit models and reduce reliability.

When NOT to use  
- Avoid adding multiple covariates unless pre-specified and clinically justified; especially avoid data-driven variable selection with this sample size.

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Use parsimonious models with only 1–2 key covariates (e.g., baseline pain, PAI score).  
- Non-parametric ANCOVA-like approaches are rarely used; better to keep models simple and robust.

TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS (for this study) AND WHY

1. Repeated measures ANOVA with listwise deletion
- Why inappropriate:
  - Assumes sphericity; often violated in longitudinal pain data, leading to incorrect inferences.
  - Requires complete data across all time points; any missing measurement leads to exclusion of that subject (inefficient and potentially biased).
  - LMM is superior and more flexible.
- When not to use:
  - When better methods (LMM) are available and feasible, especially with >3 time points and realistic missingness.

2. Paired t-test within each group only
- Why inappropriate:
  - Only compares pre- vs post within each group, ignoring the randomized control.
  - Does not answer the trial’s central question (difference between LLLT and sham).
  - Misuses within-group significance to infer between-group effect.
- When not to use:
  - In randomized controlled trials where between-group comparison is required.

3. Multiple unadjusted independent t-tests at each time point as the sole analysis
- Why inappropriate:
  - Ignores within-subject correlation and the longitudinal nature of data.
  - Inflates Type I error due to multiple comparisons.
  - Less powerful and less informative than a joint longitudinal model.
- When not to use:
  - As main analytic approach when repeated measures modeling is possible.

4. Simple pre–post difference analysis (baseline vs last time point only)
- Why inappropriate:
  - Discards rich information at intermediate time points.
  - Misses differences in time course (e.g., earlier pain reduction).
  - Inadequate for 7 time points.

5. Non-parametric tests like Wilcoxon signed-rank or sign test applied to group differences
- Why inappropriate:
  - These are for paired/within-subject comparisons, not for comparing independent groups across multiple time points.
  - Misapplication would yield misleading results.

6. One-way ANOVA ignoring group factor or time factor
- Why inappropriate:
  - Fails to incorporate both the group and time structures.
  - Reduces complex longitudinal data to a single dimension without justification.

7. Logistic regression treating continuous VAS as binary without justification
- Why inappropriate:
  - Dichotomizing VAS (e.g., pain vs no pain) without clinical rationale loses information and power.
  - More suitable methods exist for continuous VAS.

TASK 4: SUMMARY OF OVERALL ANALYTICAL APPROACH

This trial is a small, randomized, parallel-group sham-controlled study with repeated continuous pain measurements (VAS) over seven time points. The appropriate analytical strategy should:

1) Respect randomization:  
   - Focus on between-group comparisons (LLLT vs sham).

2) Respect longitudinal structure:  
   - Use linear mixed-effects models (or equivalent) to account for within-patient correlation across time and to model group × time interactions.

3) Provide clinically interpretable results:  
   - Summarize primary effects via:
     - A joint model (LMM) testing group and group×time.
     - A summary measure (e.g., AUC or mean pain over first 24 h).
     - Key time-point comparisons (e.g., 24 h) with appropriate control of multiple testing.

4) Ensure robustness:  
   - Descriptive statistics and baseline comparability.
   - Sensitivity analyses (e.g., alternative handling of analgesic use, adjustment for baseline pain and other covariates).
   - Reporting effect sizes and confidence intervals, not just p-values.

Overall, the combination of mixed-effects modeling for the repeated VAS scores, supplemented by summary measures and carefully chosen time-specific comparisons, provides a statistically sound and clinically meaningful evaluation of whether LLLT reduces postoperative pain after single-visit mandibular molar retreatment compared with sham laser.
