## TASK 1 — INFORMATION EXTRACTION (study characteristics)

### Study identification
- **Title/Topic:** Effect of low-level laser therapy (LLLT) on postoperative pain after **single-visit** root canal **retreatment** (ER) of mandibular molars  
- **Design:** **Randomized**, **parallel-group**, **sham-controlled** clinical trial (2 arms)
- **Setting:** Department of Endodontics, Faculty of Dentistry, Tehran Medical Sciences, Islamic Azad University, Iran
- **Recruitment period:** March 2019 – March 2020
- **Ethics/registration:** Ethics approval IR.IAU.DENTAL.REC.1397.028; trial registered IRCT20110212005804N5
- **Funding/COI:** None / none declared

### Objective
- **Primary objective:** Compare the effect of **LLLT vs sham** on **postoperative pain** after single-visit mandibular molar endodontic retreatment.

### Participants
- **Eligibility pool:** Patients requiring root canal retreatment of mandibular first/second molars
- **Inclusion criteria (as provided):**
  - Good general health (medical history + verbal questioning)
  - Scheduled for retreatment of **symptomatic** mandibular 1st/2nd molars
  - Periapical radiographs taken
  - Teeth with previous RCT and **PAI score 2 or 3** (Ørstavik index)
- **Exclusion/withdrawal rule (important):**
  - Patients who **took analgesics postoperatively were excluded and replaced** (this impacts estimands/ITT)

## TASK 2 — STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN (by category)

### Core analysis principles to pre-specify
- **Analysis population (recommend):**
  - Primary: **Intention-to-treat (ITT)** including all randomized participants, regardless of analgesic intake (but the protocol says they were replaced; still, for a rigorous SAP, define ITT and handle analgesic use appropriately).
  - Secondary: **Per-protocol** excluding major deviations (e.g., analgesic use) if such cases exist.
- **Primary estimand:** Difference in postoperative pain trajectories between LLLT and sham across 7 time points.
- **Two-sided α:** 0.05
- **Effect sizes:** Report mean difference (MD) with 95% CI at each timepoint and/or overall (time-averaged) difference; consider standardized effect (Cohen’s d) only as supplemental.
- **Multiplicity:** If making **multiple time-point** comparisons, control family-wise error (e.g., Holm) or pre-specify a single primary contrast (e.g., AUC 0–7d).
- **Missing data:** Plan for incomplete VAS diaries; use model-based methods (MMRM/mixed models) as primary; sensitivity analyses if missing not at random.

# CATEGORY A — MANDATORY (analysis would be incomplete without these)

## A1) Linear Mixed-Effects Model (LMM) / Mixed Model for Repeated Measures (MMRM)
- **Purpose:** Test whether **pain over time** differs between **LLLT vs sham**, accounting for within-patient correlation.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Outcome is **repeated VAS** measures at fixed time points → correlation must be modeled.
  2. Handles **unbalanced/missing** repeated measures under MAR without ad-hoc imputation.
  3. Directly tests **group × time interaction** (treatment effect varies over time), which is clinically plausible.
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - VAS may be **non-normal**, bounded, and potentially zero-inflated, making Gaussian residual assumptions questionable.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When VAS distribution is highly non-Gaussian with heavy ceiling/floor effects and transformations don’t help, or when only ordinal categories exist rather than continuous scores.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Generalized linear mixed model (GLMM)** with suitable family/link (e.g., Gamma with log link for positive skew; Tweedie; or **ordinal mixed model** if VAS treated as ordinal), or **rank-based mixed models**.

**Implementation notes (recommended specification):**
- Fixed effects: group, time (categorical), group×time
- Random effects: subject-level random intercept (± random slope for time if supported)
- Covariance: unstructured or AR(1) depending on fit/sample size
- Primary hypothesis: joint test of group×time (Wald/F test)

---

## A2) Baseline comparability (descriptive + limited inferential checks)
*(Even without baseline pain, demographics should be summarized; inferential “baseline testing” is debated, but some journals expect it.)*
- **Test name:** Two-sample **t-test** (or **Mann–Whitney U**) for continuous baseline variables; **Chi-square/Fisher’s exact** for categorical variables.
- **Purpose:** Describe/assess balance of key baseline covariates (e.g., age, sex, tooth type, PAI=2 vs 3).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. RCT expects balance; reporting supports interpretability and detects gross anomalies.
  2. Helps identify covariates for adjusted/sensitivity models.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Significance testing at baseline can be misleading; imbalance may occur by chance and does not imply bias.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Do not use baseline p-values to decide “whether randomization worked” or to select covariates mechanically.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Use **standardized mean differences (SMDs)** instead of p-values; for continuous skewed variables use medians/IQR.

# CATEGORY B — RECOMMENDED (adds rigor)

## B1) Area Under the Curve (AUC) of pain over 0–7 days + group comparison
- **Purpose:** Provide a **single summary** of pain burden over time (integrated pain experience).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Avoids multiple comparisons across 7 time points; clinically meaningful “overall pain experience”.
  2. Uses full longitudinal information and is robust to short-term fluctuations.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Requires reasonably complete measurement schedule; missing timepoints can complicate AUC estimation.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When many participants have missing/irregular time measurements without a principled handling strategy.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Compare AUC using **Wilcoxon rank-sum** or model AUC via **robust regression**; or estimate AUC within a mixed-model framework (model-based marginal means integrated over time).

## B2) Post-hoc (pre-specified) pairwise contrasts at each time point with multiplicity control
- **Test name:** Estimated marginal means contrasts from LMM/MMRM + **Holm** (or Benjamini–Hochberg if controlling FDR)
- **Purpose:** Identify **when** LLLT differs from sham (e.g., early hours vs days).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Clinically relevant to know the time window of benefit.
  2. Uses model-based estimates that account for correlation.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Multiple testing inflates Type I error if not adjusted; small sample may yield unstable estimates.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If the study is underpowered for timepoint-specific effects and no multiplicity plan exists.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Nonparametric timepoint comparisons with Holm correction, or GLMM-based marginal contrasts.

## B3) Responder analysis (clinically meaningful pain thresholds)
- **Test name:** Logistic regression (or mixed logistic if repeated) comparing proportion with pain above a threshold (e.g., VAS ≥30/100) at key times.
- **Purpose:** Translate findings into **clinically interpretable** outcomes (risk difference/odds ratio).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Pain scales can be hard to interpret; responder rates are intuitive.
  2. Robust to non-normality of VAS.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Requires **pre-specified** thresholds; dichotomization loses information and power.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If no validated/justified threshold is available or sample size yields sparse cells.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Use **ordinal regression** on categorized pain (none/mild/moderate/severe) or keep continuous with robust models.

## B4) Sensitivity analysis for analgesic use (protocol deviation / intercurrent event)
- **Test name:** Sensitivity estimands via (i) include analgesic users with adjustment; (ii) treat analgesic use as outcome; (iii) worst-case imputation bounds.
- **Purpose:** Address bias introduced by excluding/replacing participants who took analgesics.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Analgesic use is likely related to pain → post-randomization exclusion can bias treatment effect.
  2. Provides transparency about robustness.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - If truly no one took analgesics (unclear from excerpt), it may be moot.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When analgesic data are unreliable/unavailable.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Use **pattern-mixture models** or **tipping-point** analyses for missing-not-at-random scenarios.

# CATEGORY C — OPTIONAL (supplementary insights)

## C1) Nonparametric longitudinal comparison (rank-based)
- **Test name:** Brunner–Langer / rank-based mixed models (or nparLD methods)
- **Purpose:** Compare groups over time without relying on normality.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. VAS often skewed and bounded.
  2. Provides robustness check against LMM assumptions.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Less familiar; interpretation focuses on relative effects, not mean differences.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When effect size on original scale (mm on VAS) is required as primary.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - GLMM with appropriate distribution/link.

## C2) Effect modification (interaction) analyses
- **Test name:** Add interaction terms in mixed model (e.g., treatment×time×sex; treatment×PAI)
- **Purpose:** Explore whether LLLT effect differs by baseline factors (sex, tooth type, PAI 2 vs 3).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Biological plausibility (pain perception differs by sex; disease severity may matter).
  2. Can guide future stratified trials.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Small sample (n=36) → high risk of spurious subgroup findings.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If not pre-specified and power is inadequate; do not make confirmatory claims.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Descriptive stratified plots and estimation with wide CIs; Bayesian shrinkage models.

## C3) Model diagnostics and robustness checks
- **Tests/tools:** Residual plots; Q–Q plots; influence diagnostics; compare covariance structures using AIC/BIC.
- **Purpose:** Validate model assumptions and stability.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Small trials are sensitive to outliers and mis-specification.
  2. Supports credibility of inference.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Some diagnostics have limited reliability in very small samples.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Never “not use”; rather, avoid over-interpreting formal normality tests.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Robust mixed models; transformation; GLMM/ordinal models.

## TASK 3 — INAPPROPRIATE TESTS (should NOT be used)

1. **Independent t-test at each time point without multiplicity adjustment**
   - **Why inappropriate:** Inflates Type I error across 7 timepoints; ignores within-subject correlation; produces fragmented inference.

2. **One-way ANOVA ignoring time (collapsing data)**
   - **Why inappropriate:** Discards longitudinal structure and timing; reduces power and can bias conclusions.

3. **Paired t-test within each group (pre/post)**
   - **Why inappropriate:** There is no single baseline pain timepoint specified as pre-treatment comparator; and it doesn’t answer between-group causal question.

4. **Simple linear regression treating repeated observations as independent**
   - **Why inappropriate:** Violates independence; underestimates SEs → false positives.

5. **Repeated-measures ANOVA (classical) as the primary method**
   - **Why inappropriate:** Requires stronger assumptions (sphericity), handles missing data poorly, and is less flexible than MMRM for unequal covariance/missing diaries.

6. **Correlation tests (Pearson/Spearman) between treatment group and pain**
   - **Why inappropriate:** Treatment is binary randomized exposure; correlation is not the right framework for group comparisons.

## TASK 4 — SUMMARY (overall analytical approach)

A two-arm sham-controlled RCT with **7 repeated VAS pain measurements** requires a **longitudinal model** that accounts for within-subject correlation and potential missing diary entries. The primary analysis should therefore be an **MMRM/linear mixed-effects model** testing the **treatment × time** effect, supplemented by **AUC (overall pain burden)** and **multiplicity-controlled time-specific contrasts**. Because the protocol excludes patients who take analgesics (a post-randomization event related to pain), the SAP should include **sensitivity analyses** addressing analgesic use to protect against biased estimates.
