## TASK 1 — Information extraction (study characteristics)

### Study identification and design
- **Title/topic:** Effect of *sole* endodontic treatment on periodontal healing of **grade 3 endo-periodontal lesions without root damage** in **periodontally compromised patients**.
- **Design:** **Retrospective pilot** observational study with **within-tooth pre–post** comparisons (no parallel control group described).
- **Setting:** University Hospital Heidelberg (Periodontology + Restorative Dentistry departments).
- **Ethics:** Approved (S-312/2020).

### Objective and hypothesis
- **Aim:** Evaluate the effect of **sole endodontic treatment** on **periodontal healing** in the target lesion type.
- **Hypothesis:** Sole endodontic treatment **facilitates periodontal healing**.

### Population / sample
- **Participants:** 13 **periodontally compromised** patients.
- **Units analyzed:** 19 **teeth** (clustered within patients; some patients contribute >1 tooth).
- **Key inclusion descriptor:** teeth initially diagnosed as **combined endo-periodontal lesions without root damage, grade 3**.
- **Follow-up timing:**
  - **Clinical reevaluation:** **≥ 6 months** after endodontic treatment (PPD/CAL assessed; additional assessments during supportive periodontal care).
  - **Radiographs:** baseline; during; and **6–17 months** after endodontic treatment.

### Interventions / exposure
- **Endodontic protocol (standardized):**
  - Rubber dam isolation
  - Hand + rotary files (Reciproc®)
  - Irrigation: ultrasonically activated **NaOCl 3%** and **CHX 2%**, with ethyl alcohol rinse between to avoid precipitate
  - Intracanal medicament: **Ca(OH)₂ + CHX** paste at end of first visit
  - Second visit **≥ 2 weeks** later: obturation (thermoplastic or lateral condensation) using gutta-percha
- **Periodontitis therapy (systemic):**
  - Systematic therapy including **full-mouth disinfection**
  - **Adjunctive antibiotics if necessary**: van Winkelhoff regimen (amoxicillin 375 mg + metronidazole 250 mg, TID x 7 days)
  - **Important protocol constraint:** the *affected teeth* were **exempted from subgingival debridement** and **never instrumented subgingivally** during the study interval (intended to isolate effect of endodontics on those teeth).

### Outcomes and variables

#### Primary outcome
- **Change in pocket probing depth (PPD)** (continuous; mm), measured clinically **before** and **after** (timing: before and after active periodontal treatment; lesion teeth probed at ≥6 months post-endo).

#### Secondary outcomes
- **Change in PAI** (Periapical Index; ordinal 1–5 by Ørstavik) at apex on standardized periapical radiographs.
- **Change in clinical attachment loss (CAL)** (continuous; mm).

#### “Success/failure” derived outcomes
- **Clinical success:** teeth with **PPD and CAL improvement ≥50%** classified “success”; otherwise “failure”.
- **Radiographic success:** **PAI improvement** classified “successful”; otherwise “failure”.

#### Additional recorded variables (potential predictors/confounders)
- **Tooth-level:** restorations status, caries, sensitivity prior to endodontic treatment, furcation involvement, tooth mobility.
- **Patient-level:** smoking status, whether **adjunctive antibiotics** were used during full-mouth disinfection.
- **Endodontic quality variables (radiographic):** per Farzaneh scheme
  - **Length:** adequate (0–2 mm short), short (≥2 mm short), long (beyond apex)
  - **Density:** good vs poor
  - **Missed canal:** ≥1 canal not filled
- **Measurement/blinding:**
  - **PAI** scored by **one calibrated investigator blinded to clinical outcome**.
  - **Root filling quality** rated by **two investigators independently**; disagreements resolved by **consensus**.
  - Radiographs evaluated with standardized conditions (parallel technique; same certified monitor; dark room; same software).

## TASK 2 — Statistical analysis plan (tests by category)

### General analysis principles (apply throughout)
- **Analysis unit:** tooth, but **teeth are clustered within patient** → use **cluster-robust methods** or **mixed models/GEE**.
- **Two outcome scales:** continuous (PPD, CAL change) and ordinal (PAI, PAI change), plus binary “success/failure”.
- **Effect sizes:** report mean/median change with **95% CI**; for binary outcomes report **risk/proportion** with CI; for models report β/OR with CI.
- **Pilot study:** emphasize estimation (CIs) over hypothesis testing; avoid overfitting (rule of thumb: very limited predictors).

---

### CATEGORY A — MANDATORY (core tests/models)

#### A1) Paired / within-tooth change in PPD and CAL using **linear mixed-effects model (LMM)** (or GEE)
- **Purpose:** Test/estimate mean **pre–post change** in PPD (primary) and CAL while accounting for **multiple teeth per patient**.
- **Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):**
  1. Handles **correlated observations** (teeth nested within patients) via random intercept (LMM) or working correlation (GEE).
  2. Directly models **change over time** (time indicator) and provides **CIs** for mean change.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1 reason):**
  - With only **13 patients**, random-effects variance estimation can be unstable; GEE small-sample SEs may also be biased.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If there is effectively **one tooth per patient** (no clustering) and data are approximately normal—simpler paired methods suffice.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - If residuals are strongly non-normal/outliers: **clustered bootstrap CI** for mean change; or **mixed-effects quantile regression**; or analyze **ranked changes** with cluster-robust SEs.

*(If authors only have one post timepoint per tooth, model as outcome = change; or as repeated measure with time factor.)*

#### A2) Change in PAI (ordinal) using **mixed-effects ordinal logistic regression** (cumulative link mixed model) *or* **GEE ordinal model**
- **Purpose:** Evaluate whether PAI scores **improve after treatment** (shift toward lower scores) accounting for clustering.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. PAI is a **5-level ordinal scale**; ordinal models respect ordering without assuming equal spacing.
  2. Mixed/clustered approach accounts for **patient-level clustering**.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - **Very small sample** can lead to separation or unstable threshold estimates; proportional odds assumption may fail.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If PAI is collapsed to binary (improved vs not) and the ordinal information is no longer available/desired.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - If proportional odds fails: **partial proportional odds** model.
  - If ordinal model unstable: analyze **binary improvement** with clustered methods (see A3) or use **Wilcoxon signed-rank with clustered bootstrap** for paired ordinal change.

#### A3) Proportion “success” (clinical and radiographic) with **cluster-adjusted estimation** (e.g., GEE logistic with intercept only, or cluster-robust binomial CI)
- **Purpose:** Provide valid estimates and CIs for **success rates** (PPD/CAL ≥50% improvement; PAI improvement).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Outcomes are **binary** and clinically interpretable.
  2. Clustering by patient affects variance; cluster-adjusted methods prevent overly narrow CIs.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - With few clusters (13 patients), standard sandwich SEs can be biased downward.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If you ignore clustering and treat 19 teeth as independent—this should not be done.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated / small-cluster issue:**
  - **Cluster bootstrap** CIs for proportions; or **mixed-effects logistic regression** with random patient intercept and penalization (Firth) if needed.

---

### CATEGORY B — RECOMMENDED (adds rigor/validity)

#### B1) **Inter-rater reliability** for root filling quality ratings (two raters): **Cohen’s kappa** (weighted if ordinal) and/or **percent agreement**
- **Purpose:** Quantify agreement between the two investigators rating radiographic quality (length, density, missed canal).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Ratings are **categorical/ordinal** and done by **two observers**.
  2. Reliability affects credibility of associations using these ratings.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Kappa can be misleading with very high/low prevalence of categories in small samples.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If final ratings were always made by consensus only and original independent ratings are unavailable.
- **Alternative:**
  - **Gwet’s AC1/AC2** (more stable with prevalence issues) or **bootstrap CI** for agreement.

#### B2) Association of endodontic quality (length/density/missed canal) with success using **mixed-effects logistic regression** (or GEE logistic)
- **Purpose:** Explore whether radiographic quality predicts **clinical success** and/or **PAI improvement**.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Predictor is categorical; outcome is binary; accounts for **clustering**.
  2. Clinically important to link technical quality to healing.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Very small dataset → high risk of **overfitting** and separation (especially with rare categories like “missed canal”).
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When there are too few failures/successes per predictor level (sparse cells).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Exact logistic regression** (limited with clustering), **Firth-penalized logistic** with cluster bootstrap, or simple **descriptive stratification** only.

#### B3) Patient-level factors (smoking, antibiotic therapy) vs success using **mixed-effects logistic regression** (parsimonious)
- **Purpose:** Assess whether smoking/antibiotics relate to outcomes (confounding/effect modification).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Variables were recorded and are biologically plausible modifiers of periodontal healing.
  2. Mixed model accounts for multiple teeth per patient.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - With only 13 patients, patient-level predictors can be underpowered and unstable.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If nearly all patients fall into one category (e.g., almost all non-smokers), model is not estimable.
- **Alternative:**
  - **Fisher’s exact test at patient level** (one tooth per patient selected or aggregate per patient), or **descriptive** only.

#### B4) **Sensitivity analysis for follow-up time** (6–17 months) using regression adjustment
- **Test/model:** LMM (continuous change) / mixed ordinal model (PAI) including **follow-up duration** as covariate.
- **Purpose:** Check whether heterogeneity in follow-up explains outcome variability.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Follow-up varies substantially; healing is time-dependent.
  2. Helps reduce bias due to informative timing.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Follow-up time may be correlated with unmeasured clinical decisions (confounding by indication).
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If follow-up timing is missing/too coarsely recorded.
- **Alternative:**
  - Stratify descriptively (e.g., 6–9 vs ≥10 months) or use **Spearman correlation** with clustered bootstrap.

---

### CATEGORY C — OPTIONAL (supplementary insight)

#### C1) Nonparametric paired change for PPD/CAL: **Wilcoxon signed-rank test** (with caution about clustering)
- **Purpose:** Provide a robustness check for pre–post change without normality assumption.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Suitable for skewed changes/outliers common in clinical measures.
  2. Works with small samples.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Assumes independent pairs; clustering (multiple teeth/patient) violates this.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When >1 tooth per patient unless you do patient-level aggregation or cluster-resampling.
- **Alternative:**
  - **Clustered permutation test** or **cluster bootstrap** of median change.

#### C2) Correlation between continuous changes and ordinal PAI change: **Spearman correlation** (cluster bootstrap CI)
- **Purpose:** Evaluate whether radiographic improvement aligns with clinical improvement.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Handles non-normal/ordinal data.
  2. Clinically interpretable concordance check.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Correlation ignores clustering unless adjusted.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If multiple teeth per patient and no cluster adjustment.
- **Alternative:**
  - Mixed-effects models relating PAI change to PPD/CAL change.

#### C3) Exploratory subgroup comparisons (e.g., furcation involvement/mobility categories): **descriptive + exact/penalized models**
- **Purpose:** Hypothesis-generation about prognostic clinical factors.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Variables were measured (furcation, mobility).
  2. May explain heterogeneity in response.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Sparse categories; very high false-positive risk.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If subgroup sizes are tiny (e.g., n<5).
- **Alternative:**
  - Report **case-series style** summaries only.

## TASK 3 — Tests that should NOT be used (and why)

1. **Independent-samples t-test / one-way ANOVA** comparing “before vs after” as if groups are independent  
   - **Why inappropriate:** measurements are **paired within tooth**; violates independence; wrong SEs/p-values.

2. **Chi-square test** for success rates treating 19 teeth as independent  
   - **Why inappropriate:** ignores **patient clustering**; expected counts may be small → invalid approximation.

3. **Pearson correlation** between outcomes without checking distribution and clustering  
   - **Why inappropriate:** assumes linear relationship, approximate normality, and independence.

4. **Linear regression on PAI as numeric (1–5) without ordinal methods**  
   - **Why inappropriate:** assumes equal spacing and normal residuals; PAI is **ordinal**.

5. **Repeated-measures ANOVA** at the tooth level without accommodating patient clustering (and with small N)  
   - **Why inappropriate:** would still miss the **second-level clustering** (teeth within patient) and is fragile with missing/unequal timing.

## TASK 4 — Summary justification of overall approach

A valid plan must (i) treat the study as a **retrospective pre–post** evaluation with no concurrent control, (ii) analyze **PPD change** as the primary endpoint and **PAI/CAL** as secondary, and—critically—(iii) account for **non-independence of teeth within patients**. Therefore, mixed-effects (or GEE with small-sample/cluster-bootstrap corrections) form the backbone for continuous (PPD/CAL) and ordinal (PAI) outcomes, complemented by cluster-adjusted estimates for clinically meaningful “success/failure” rates and reliability assessment of radiographic ratings.
