TASK 1 – INFORMATION EXTRACTION  
(Study characteristics, design, variables, measurements)

1. Study design
- Type: Retrospective pilot study.
- Setting: Department of Restorative Dentistry / Section of Periodontology, University Hospital Heidelberg, Germany.
- Unit of analysis: Tooth (19 teeth) nested within patients (13 patients).
- Time frame:
  - Endodontic treatment: standardized protocol, 2 appointments (2+ weeks apart).
  - Clinical and radiographic follow-up: earliest 6 months after endodontic treatment; radiographic follow-up 6–17 months after endodontic treatment.
- Ethical approval: Local ethics committee S-312/2020.

2. Study objective and hypothesis
- Aim: To evaluate the effect of sole endodontic treatment of teeth diagnosed with combined endo-periodontal lesions without root damage grade 3 (in periodontally compromised patients) on periodontal healing.
- Hypothesis: Sole endodontic treatment facilitates periodontal healing.

3. Population and sample
- Population: Periodontally compromised patients with combined endo-periodontal lesions without root damage, grade 3 (according to 2017 World Workshop classification).
- Sample:
  - 13 patients.
  - 19 teeth with diagnosis: “combined endo-periodontal lesions without root damage grade 3 in periodontally compromised patients.”
- Inclusion (explicit and implied):
  - Periodontally compromised patients undergoing systematic periodontitis therapy.
  - Teeth with combined endo-periodontal lesion without root damage, grade 3.
  - Teeth receiving standardized endodontic treatment protocol.
  - Available baseline and follow-up clinical and radiographic data (≥6 months).
- Exclusion (implied):
  - Teeth with root damage (fractures, perforations, external root resorptions).
  - Teeth that received subgingival debridement on the target tooth during the study interval.

4. Treatment / interventions
- Systematic periodontal therapy (patient-level):
  - Full-mouth disinfection.
  - Adjunctive antibiotic therapy when necessary (van Winkelhoff protocol: 375 mg amoxicillin + 250 mg metronidazole, TID for 7 days).
  - Crucially: The study teeth were exempted from subgingival debridement and were never instrumented subgingivally during the study interval.
- Endodontic treatment (tooth-level; standardized protocol):
  - Rubber dam isolation.
  - Instrumentation: hand files + rotary files (Reciproc®).
  - Irrigation:
    - Sodium hypochlorite 3% (ultrasonically activated).
    - Chlorhexidine 2% (ultrasonically activated).
    - Ethyl alcohol rinse between NaOCl and CHX to avoid parachloroaniline precipitate.
  - Intracanal medication: calcium hydroxide powder mixed with chlorhexidine (pasty consistency) placed in canals at first appointment.
  - Second appointment ≥2 weeks later:
    - Obturation with gutta-percha:
      - Either thermoplastic filling or lateral condensation.
  - Radiographs taken before, during, and 6–17 months after treatment (parallel technique).

5. Outcomes and variables

5.1 Primary outcome
- Pocket probing depth (PPD, mm) – change over time.
  - Measurement:
    - Periodontal assessment before and after active periodontal treatment (full mouth).
    - For study teeth, PPD was recorded at reevaluation ≥6 months after endodontic treatment; further assessments during supportive periodontal care (intervals individualized).
  - Tooth-level classification for “clinical success”:
    - Teeth showing improvement (reduction) of PPD ≥ 50% were classified as “success.”
    - Others as “failure.”

5.2 Secondary outcomes
a) Periapical Index (PAI) – change over time.
   - Scale: ordinal, 1–5 (Orstavik 1986):
     1 = normal periapical structures  
     2 = small change in bone structure  
     3 = changes in bone structure with some mineral loss  
     4 = periodontitis with well-defined radiolucent area  
     5 = severe periodontitis with exacerbating features
   - Measurement:
     - Periapical radiographs before treatment and ≥6 months after beginning endodontic treatment.
     - Parallel technique; evaluated on standardized monitor in dark room.
     - Evaluation at the apex by one calibrated investigator, blinded to clinical outcome.
   - Tooth-level classification for “radiographic success”:
     - Teeth with improvement (decrease) in PAI score classified as “successful.”
     - Others as “failure.”

b) Clinical attachment loss (CAL, mm) – change over time.
   - Measurement:
     - Recorded at dental checkup and periodontal assessment before and after active periodontal treatment.
     - For the study teeth, CAL evaluated at reevaluation ≥6 months after endodontic treatment and during supportive care.
   - Tooth-level classification (aligned with PPD):
     - Teeth showing improvement (reduction) of CAL ≥ 50% classified as “success.”
     - Others as “failure.”

5.3 Other clinical variables
- Furcation involvement (categorical/ordinal) – baseline and follow-up.
- Tooth mobility (ordinal, e.g., Miller classification) – baseline and follow-up.
- Sensitivity of included teeth before endodontic treatment (vitality tests: cold/electric) – baseline.
- Restorative status and caries (baseline):
  - Presence/type of restoration.
  - Caries presence/absence.
- Smoking status (patient-level: smoker/non-smoker, possibly number of cigarettes/day or pack-years if available).
- Antibiotic therapy during full mouth disinfection (patient-level: yes/no, type – van Winkelhoff regimen).

5.4 Radiographic treatment quality variables
- Quality of root canal filling (Farzaneh et al. 2004):
  - Length (categorical):
    - Adequate: filling 0–2 mm short of root end.
    - Short: >2 mm short of root end.
    - Long: beyond root end.
  - Density (categorical):
    - Good: homogeneous, without voids.
    - Poor: detectable voids.
  - Missed canal (binary):
    - ≥1 canal not filled vs all canals filled.
  - Rating procedure:
    - Two experienced investigators rated independently, blinded to each other.
    - Disagreements resolved by consensus.
- Software: Sidexis Next Generation 1.53 (Sirona).

5.5 Derived/composite variables
- Tooth-level combined “endodontic success” vs “failure” based on PAI change.
- Tooth-level “periodontal success” vs “failure” based on ≥50% improvement in both PPD and CAL (as described).
- Potential combination of success categories to explore agreement between radiographic and clinical success.

6. Data structure
- Hierarchical / clustered:
  - Level 1: Tooth (n = 19).
  - Level 2: Patient (n = 13; some patients contribute more than one tooth).
- Longitudinal (repeated measures):
  - Baseline vs follow-up (≥6 months) for PPD, CAL, PAI and possibly other clinical measures.
  - Follow-up durations vary (6–17 months radiographically; clinical intervals individualized).

7. Data collection methods
- Clinical:
  - Standard periodontal charting: PPD, CAL, furcation involvement, mobility.
  - Vitality testing (cold, electric) pre-treatment.
  - Documentation of restorations, caries, smoking status, antibiotic use.
- Radiographic:
  - Periapical radiographs with parallel technique.
  - Standardized viewing conditions (same monitor, dark room).
  - PAI scored by calibrated, blinded examiner.
  - Root filling quality scored by two independent examiners; consensus for discrepancies.

TASK 2 – STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

Because this is a small retrospective pilot with 19 teeth, emphasis should be on paired, within-tooth comparisons and descriptive analysis, with cautious inferential testing. Clustering within patients should be acknowledged; with only 13 patients, sophisticated multilevel models are exploratory at best.

Below, tests are structured into three categories (Mandatory, Recommended, Optional), with details as requested.

-----------------------------------
CATEGORY A – MANDATORY ANALYSES
-----------------------------------

A1. Descriptive statistics

Test / Method  
- Descriptive statistics (no inferential test): means, medians, SDs, interquartile ranges; counts and percentages.

Purpose  
- Summarize baseline characteristics and outcome distributions:
  - Patient-level: age (if available), sex, smoking status, antibiotic use.
  - Tooth-level: tooth type, baseline PPD, CAL, PAI, furcation, mobility, vitality, restorative status, quality of root filling (length, density, missed canals).
  - Outcome changes: ΔPPD, ΔCAL, ΔPAI; success/failure counts.

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) Essential to describe the sample and assess plausibility of further analyses, especially in a pilot with small N.  
2) Allows visual identification of outliers, skewness and potential ceiling/floor effects that inform choice of inferential tests.

Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)  
- Not a stand-alone approach for hypothesis testing; does not provide p-values or confidence intervals.

When not to use  
- Never as the only analysis if inferential conclusions about change or effect are desired.

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Not applicable (purely descriptive).

---

A2. Paired comparison of PPD (primary outcome)

Depending on distribution, one of:

A2a. Paired t-test (for mean PPD change per tooth)  
A2b. Wilcoxon signed-rank test (for median PPD change per tooth)

Purpose  
- Test whether PPD significantly decreases from baseline to follow-up in the treated teeth.

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) The same tooth is measured at two time points → paired/within-subject design.  
2) PPD is a continuous (interval) variable; paired t-test is standard when differences are approximately normally distributed.  
3) If normality of differences is questionable (small N, skew), Wilcoxon signed-rank is a robust non-parametric alternative for paired ordinal/continuous data.

Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)  
- Paired t-test:
  - May be inappropriate if the distribution of PPD change is highly skewed or heavily influenced by outliers in such a small sample.  
- Wilcoxon signed-rank:
  - Assumes symmetric distribution of differences around the median; performance can degrade if differences are extremely asymmetric.

When this test should NOT be used  
- Paired t-test:
  - When the normality of differences is grossly violated and sample size is too small to rely on the central limit theorem.  
- Both:
  - When time points or measurements are not clearly matched (e.g., missing baseline or follow-up per tooth).

Alternative if assumptions are violated  
- Use exact Wilcoxon signed-rank test (exact p-values) or permutation test for paired differences.  
- Report effect size with 95% CI using bootstrap methods.

Implementation details  
- Calculate PPD at tooth level as either:
  - Mean PPD across all measured sites for that tooth, or  
  - Maximum PPD per tooth.  
- Then compute difference: follow-up – baseline, and apply paired test to these tooth-level summaries.

---

A3. Paired comparison of CAL (secondary outcome)

Tests analogous to PPD:

A3a. Paired t-test (for mean CAL change per tooth)  
A3b. Wilcoxon signed-rank test (for median CAL change per tooth)

Purpose  
- Evaluate whether CAL significantly improves (decreases) from baseline to follow-up after sole endodontic treatment.

Why appropriate  
1) CAL is continuous, measured on same tooth at two time points → paired design.  
2) Tests directly address the secondary hypothesis about periodontal attachment gain.

Why it may not be appropriate  
- Same reasons as for PPD: small sample, non-normal change distribution, outliers.

When NOT to use  
- As for PPD: non-matched observations, gross deviations from underlying assumptions for parametric/non-parametric test.

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Exact Wilcoxon signed-rank or permutation-based paired test; bootstrap CI for median (or mean) change.

---

A4. Paired comparison of PAI score (ordinal secondary outcome)

Primary candidate: Wilcoxon signed-rank test (or exact version).  

Purpose  
- Assess whether PAI scores (ordinal 1–5) significantly improve (decrease) from baseline to follow-up.

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) PAI is an ordered categorical variable; Wilcoxon signed-rank tests median change for paired ordinal data.  
2) Same tooth evaluated at baseline and follow-up → paired design fits Wilcoxon framework.  
3) Small sample size; Wilcoxon is robust and doesn’t require normality.

Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)  
- Assumes at least ordinal scale and that differences between paired observations can be ranked; if there are many ties or extremely few levels, power is limited, and assumptions may be strained.

When NOT to use  
- When the variable is nominal without meaningful order (not the case here).  
- When changes are mostly ties (e.g. almost all PAI unchanged), limiting test informativeness.

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Sign test for paired data, which only uses direction of change, not magnitude.  
- Exact permutation tests for direction of change.

---

A5. Success vs failure proportions (pre-post change)

Test candidate: McNemar test (if defined as paired binary state per tooth).  

However, here success/failure is defined only at follow-up based on change from baseline, so the comparison is not pre vs post state but proportion of teeth meeting ≥50% improvement. For hypothesis testing:

- One-sample binomial test on observed success rate vs a null hypothesis (e.g., 0.5), or report descriptive with CI.

Purpose  
- Quantify and inferentially assess the proportion of teeth achieving “periodontal success” (PPD/CAL ≥ 50% improvement) and “radiographic success” (PAI improvement).

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) Outcome is binary at tooth-level (success/failure) → binomial framework is appropriate.  
2) The sample size is small; exact binomial tests and exact CIs are suitable and do not rely on normal approximations.

Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)  
- The choice of comparison value for the binomial test (e.g., 0.5) is somewhat arbitrary in absence of a control group; inferential testing may be of limited interpretability.

When NOT to use  
- When no meaningful reference proportion exists and the goal is descriptive; forcing a hypothesis test can be misleading.

Alternative if assumptions are violated  
- None needed (binomial assumptions are minimal: independent trials, identical success probability). Dependency of teeth within patients violates strict independence; this mainly affects precision, so interpret CIs cautiously.

Implementation  
- Provide success proportions with exact 95% CIs; optionally use binomial tests as exploratory.

---

A6. Agreement between clinical and radiographic success

Test: Cohen’s kappa (for binary success/failure).  

Purpose  
- Quantify the agreement beyond chance between:
  - Periodontal success (PPD/CAL-based) and  
  - Radiographic success (PAI-based).

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) Both variables are dichotomous (success/failure) for the same tooth.  
2) Kappa accounts for agreement expected by chance, giving more informative assessment than percent agreement.

Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)  
- Small sample and potentially imbalanced categories (e.g., many successes or many failures) can result in unstable or misleading kappa values.

When NOT to use  
- When one category is extremely rare (e.g., almost all successes), making kappa uninterpretable or very low despite high observed agreement.

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Report raw agreement and simple cross-tabulation; use prevalence-adjusted bias-adjusted kappa (PABAK) as a sensitivity measure.

---

A7. Inter-rater agreement for root filling quality

Test: Cohen’s kappa (binary categories) or weighted kappa (for ordinal categories) – prior to consensus.

Purpose  
- Quantify inter-examiner reliability for the quality ratings (length, density, missed canals).

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) Two raters provided categorical ratings on the same teeth.  
2) Kappa corrects for chance agreement and is standard in reliability assessment.

Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)  
- Since final analysis uses consensus ratings, kappa is mainly descriptive; low kappa in a small sample can be unstable and may not meaningfully change analytic conclusions.

When NOT to use  
- If only consensus ratings are retained and individual ratings are not recorded or are incomplete.

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- For ordinal data, use weighted kappa with appropriate weighting; or report % agreement plus simple statistics.

CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED ANALYSES
-----------------------------------

B1. Effect size estimation with confidence intervals

Method  
- For continuous outcomes (ΔPPD, ΔCAL):  
  - Mean change ± SD and 95% CI;  
  - Standardized effect size (Cohen’s d for paired data).  
- For ordinal/scale outcomes (PAI change):  
  - Median change with IQR and bootstrap 95% CI.  
- For binary outcomes (success proportions):  
  - Exact 95% CIs for proportions.

Purpose  
- Provide magnitude and precision of effects beyond p-values.

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) Pilot studies are better interpreted in terms of effect size and uncertainty.  
2) CIs are more informative than isolated p-values and align with best reporting practices.

Why it may not be appropriate  
- Small sample leads to wide CIs; they may be imprecise.

When NOT to use  
- Never strictly “not to use”; but interpretation should emphasize exploratory nature.

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Use bootstrap CIs for non-normal or small-sample distributions.

---

B2. Exploratory association between endodontic quality and outcomes

Tests  
- Association of root filling length/density/missed canal with:
  - ΔPPD, ΔCAL, ΔPAI (continuous/ordinal):  
    - Mann–Whitney U test (two groups, e.g., adequate vs inadequate length, good vs poor density).  
  - Success vs failure (binary):
    - Fisher’s exact test for small counts.

Purpose  
- Explore whether quality of filling is associated with periodontal and radiographic healing.

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) Root filling quality is categorical; success and healing measures are continuous/ordinal or binary, suitable for these tests.  
2) Non-parametric methods (Mann–Whitney, Fisher) accommodate small sample and non-normality.

Why it may not be appropriate  
- Multiple testing with small N risks spurious associations (type I error); results should be viewed as exploratory, not confirmatory.

When NOT to use  
- When group sizes are extremely unbalanced (e.g., 1–2 teeth in one category), making tests uninformative.

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Combine categories (e.g., “adequate or short” vs “long”) to ensure minimally adequate group sizes, or refrain from inferential testing and present descriptive comparisons only.

---

B3. Exploratory association with patient-level factors (smoking, antibiotic use)

Tests  
- Influence of smoking status and antibiotic therapy on:
  - ΔPPD, ΔCAL, ΔPAI (tooth-level):
    - Hierarchical/mixed models (exploratory, see B4), or  
    - Simple two-group comparison with cluster-robust standard errors:  
      - Mann–Whitney U or t-test with cluster-robust variance (if software allows).
  - Success vs failure:
    - Cluster-adjusted logistic regression (if feasible) or Fisher’s exact test with caution.

Purpose  
- Explore potential modifiers of healing at patient level.

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) Smoking and antibiotic use are clinically relevant confounders/moderators of periodontal healing.  
2) Even descriptive patterns can inform design and stratification in future larger studies.

Why it may not be appropriate  
- Very small number of patients and non-independent observations (multiple teeth per patient) make it easy to over-interpret associations.

When NOT to use  
- When group size is very small (e.g., only 1–2 patients in one level of a factor) or when such analysis would obviously overfit.

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Restrict to descriptive stratification (e.g., mean ΔPPD by smoking status) without formal testing.

---

B4. Exploratory mixed-effects models for longitudinal change

Model  
- Linear mixed-effects model (LMM) for continuous outcomes (PPD, CAL):  
  - Outcome: change (or follow-up value adjusted for baseline).  
  - Random effect: patient (to account for clustering).  
  - Fixed effects: baseline values, time (if multiple follow-up times), root filling quality, smoking, antibiotic use (very parsimonious!).

For PAI (ordinal), consider exploratory cumulative link mixed model (CLMM).

Purpose  
- Account for non-independence of teeth within patients and explore multivariable predictors of change.

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) Data are clustered; mixed models directly handle patient-level random effects.  
2) Allows simultaneous adjustment for several predictors in an exploratory way.

Why it may not be appropriate  
- With only 13 patients and 19 teeth, these models will be largely underpowered and risk overfitting. Estimates may be unstable and sensitive to modeling choices.

When NOT to use  
- As primary confirmatory analysis; when you cannot specify a parsimonious model with very few predictors.

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Use simpler approaches (paired tests, descriptive stratification); treat any regression modeling as sensitivity/exploratory analysis only.

---

B5. Time-to-event analyses (if variable follow-up times strongly differ)

Optional but recommended if end points are clearly definable (e.g., time to achieve PAI improvement):

Test  
- Kaplan–Meier curves and log-rank test, if event timing is known and there is censoring.

Given only 6–17 months follow-up with sparse time-point information, this may not be well-defined and is likely not feasible; therefore, treat as low-priority recommended only if date-level data exist.

B6. Graphical displays

Methods  
- Boxplots or violin plots for baseline vs follow-up PPD, CAL, PAI.  
- Spaghetti plots (change trajectories per tooth).  
- Scatter plots of baseline vs follow-up measures, with identity line.  
- Bar charts for success/failure and root filling quality.

Purpose  
- Visual inspection of patterns, outliers, and general trends.

Why appropriate  
1) Enhance interpretability of small-sample data.  
2) Provide intuitive evidence of improvement.

Why it may not be appropriate  
- With very small numbers, some graphs may be sparse or revealing of individuals; anonymization is required.

When NOT to use  
- If privacy cannot be preserved (e.g., more detailed identifiers or rare combinations).

Alternative  
- Summarize patterns in text or tables.

C1. Site-level analyses (within tooth)

If site-level PPD/CAL data are available (e.g., 6 sites per tooth), additional paired analyses can be conducted.

Test  
- Mixed-effects models with sites nested in teeth nested in patients; or

Purpose

Why appropriate

Why it may not be appropriate

When NOT to use  
- If site-level data are incomplete, inconsistent, or when sample size is too small for multi-level modeling.

Alternative

C2. Ordinal regression for PAI change predictors

Test  
- Ordinal logistic regression (proportional odds model) or CLMM (if random effects included).

Purpose  
- Model the probability of being in better vs worse PAI categories at follow-up as a function of baseline PAI, root filling quality, and other covariates.

Why appropriate  
1) PAI is ordinal; ordinal regression directly models ordered categories.  
2) Provides odds ratios for improving PAI with better root filling quality.

Why it may not be appropriate  
- Extremely limited sample and sparse distribution of PAI categories → unstable, possibly non-convergent models.

When NOT to use  
- When category counts are very small or if proportional odds assumption clearly fails.

Alternative  
- Collapse PAI into fewer categories (e.g., PAI ≤ 2 vs > 2; improvement yes/no) and use binary logistic regression or Fisher’s exact test descriptively.

---

C3. Sensitivity analyses for success thresholds

Method  
- Repeat success/failure classification using different improvement thresholds (e.g., ≥30% instead of ≥50% PPD/CAL improvement) to evaluate robustness of conclusions.

Purpose  
- Check how sensitive the findings are to the chosen 50% cut-off.

Why appropriate  
1) The 50% threshold is somewhat arbitrary; sensitivity analyses can show robustness.  
2) Helpful for planning future trials and defining clinically meaningful endpoints.

Why it may not be appropriate  
- Multiple comparisons; may cause confusion in interpretation.

When NOT to use  
- If sample size is too small to support several reclassifications meaningfully.

Alternative  
- Present continuous changes (ΔPPD, ΔCAL) alongside single binary classification to avoid multiple thresholds.

---

C4. Bayesian analyses

Method  
- Bayesian paired models (for ΔPPD, ΔCAL, ΔPAI) using weakly informative priors.

Purpose  
- Provide posterior distributions for effect sizes in a small pilot, which may be more informative than frequentist p-values.

Why appropriate  
1) Naturally accommodates small samples with explicit prior uncertainty.  
2) Yields probability statements about effects that may be more intuitive.

Why it may not be appropriate  
- Requires careful choice and justification of priors; may be unfamiliar to intended readership.

When NOT to use  
- When expertise, software, or space for explanation is limited.

Alternative  
- Stick with frequentist effect sizes and CIs.

TASK 3 – INAPPROPRIATE TESTS (AND WHY)

1. Independent-samples t-test for pre vs post comparisons  
- Why inappropriate: Pre and post measurements are on the same teeth; independence assumption is violated.  

2. One-way ANOVA for pre vs post measurements  
- Why inappropriate: Only two time points with paired measurements; ANOVA for independent groups is not suited; repeated-measures ANOVA would be more relevant (though overkill here).

3. Chi-square test of independence for small 2×2 tables (e.g., success vs failure vs quality) without exact correction  
- Why inappropriate: Very small cell counts and expected frequencies <5; Chi-square approximations are unreliable. Fisher’s exact test should be used instead.

4. Pearson correlation between baseline and follow-up PPD/CAL to infer treatment effect  
- Why inappropriate: High correlation between time points is expected regardless of treatment; correlation does not test change or treatment effect.

5. Simple linear regression ignoring clustering (multiple teeth per patient) for predictors of change  
- Why inappropriate: Assumes independent observations; multiple teeth in one patient introduce intra-patient correlation, leading to underestimated standard errors and inflated type I error.

6. Parametric repeated-measures ANOVA with multiple within-tooth time points if only two time points exist  
- Why inappropriate: Overly complex for two time points; paired t-test is the correct parametric method.

7. Treating PAI as continuous and applying paired t-test without checking  
- Why inappropriate: PAI is ordinal with 5 categories; equal-interval assumption is questionable. A non-parametric paired test (Wilcoxon) is more defensible.

8. Kaplan–Meier survival analysis for radiographic success if only two time points with no actual time-to-event data  
- Why inappropriate: Time-to-event methods require clear event times and censoring structure; with only baseline and single terminal follow-up, this is not true survival data.

9. Logistic regression with many predictors relative to sample size (e.g., success vs failure modeled with >2–3 covariates)  
- Why inappropriate: With 19 observations and limited events, such models will be overfitted and unreliable (rule of thumb: ≥10 events per predictor).

TASK 4 – SUMMARY / JUSTIFICATION OF OVERALL ANALYTICAL APPROACH

- The study is a small retrospective pilot with hierarchical (teeth within patients) and paired (pre/post) structure.  
- The primary scientific question concerns within-tooth change in periodontal parameters (PPD, CAL) and radiographic status (PAI) after standardized endodontic treatment, without concomitant instrumentation of the lesion teeth.  
- Therefore, the core of the analysis must focus on:
  - Paired comparisons of baseline vs follow-up outcomes (PPD, CAL, PAI), using non-parametric or simple parametric paired tests as appropriate;  
  - Clear descriptive statistics and effect size estimates with confidence intervals;  
  - Simple binary classifications of success/failure based on clinically meaningful thresholds, reported with proportions and exact CIs.  
- The clustering of teeth within patients is acknowledged; due to small numbers, adjustment via mixed models can only be used in an exploratory manner and not as definitive confirmatory analysis.  
- Additional exploratory analyses examine the influence of root canal treatment quality and patient-level factors (smoking, antibiotics) on outcomes, using robust small-sample tests (Mann–Whitney, Fisher’s exact) and carefully interpreting results as hypothesis-generating.  
- This strategy optimally balances methodological rigor with the practical constraints of a small pilot dataset, while providing clinically relevant and statistically sound insights to inform future, larger studies.
