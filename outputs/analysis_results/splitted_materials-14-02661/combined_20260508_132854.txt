## TASK 2: Statistical analysis plan (tests by category)

### General analysis principles (apply to all categories)
- **Analysis population:** Prefer **intention-to-treat (ITT)**; however, pain outcomes require follow-up—plan:
  - Primary: **modified ITT** including all randomized with ≥1 postoperative pain measurement.
  - Sensitivity: per-protocol (completed 7-day diary).
- **Primary estimand:** Difference in postoperative pain course between sealers, accounting for repeated measures and stratification by tooth type.
- **Alpha:** 0.05 (two-sided). Control multiplicity (see recommended tests).
- **Descriptives:** CONSORT flow; baseline comparability by arm within strata; report effect sizes with 95% CI.
- **Missing data:** repeated VAS likely incomplete; use mixed models (valid under MAR) and sensitivity analyses (pattern-mixture / MI).
- **Key design caveat:** **Sealer and obturation technique are perfectly confounded**, so inference is formally about the **treatment package** (sealer+technique), unless explicitly justified otherwise.

### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY (analysis would be incomplete without these)

#### A1) Linear mixed-effects model (LMM) for repeated VAS (or GLMM if non-normal)
- **Purpose:** Compare **postoperative pain intensity trajectories** over 7 days between arms, using all repeated VAS data.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Handles **correlated repeated measures** (daily pain within patient).
  2. Uses **incomplete follow-up** efficiently under MAR (less biased than complete-case).
  3. Allows inclusion of **time**, **treatment**, and **treatment×time** interaction (tests whether trajectories differ).
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - VAS distributions can be **zero-inflated / skewed**; LMM residual normality may be violated.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If outcome is better treated as categorical (e.g., none/mild/moderate/severe) or if residual diagnostics show extreme non-normality not fixable by transformation.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **GLMM** with appropriate family (e.g., Gamma with log link for positive VAS; or hurdle/zero-inflated models),
  - **Rank-based mixed model** / **GEE with robust SE**,
  - **Ordinal mixed model** if VAS categorized.

*Model specification (minimum):*
- Outcome: VAS (rest) and VAS (biting) analyzed **separately** (two primary endpoints) or jointly (recommended below).
- Fixed effects: treatment arm, day (categorical), treatment×day, tooth-type stratum, baseline pain (corresponding rest/biting), possibly day-of-week if relevant.
- Random effects: patient random intercept (and possibly random slope for day).
- Primary contrast: overall treatment effect across days (marginal means) and/or interaction (difference in trajectories).

#### A2) Baseline comparability and descriptive statistics (no hypothesis emphasis)
- **Test name:** Descriptive comparisons; if testing is done: **t-test/Mann–Whitney**, **chi-square/Fisher** as appropriate.
- **Purpose:** Describe baseline characteristics (age/sex/tooth type, baseline pain).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Confirms randomization plausibly balanced key factors (especially with attrition).
  2. Informs covariate adjustment needs (baseline pain strongly related to postoperative pain).
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Significance testing of baseline is often **misleading**; differences can occur by chance.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Do not use p-values to “prove” balance or decide inclusion of confounders.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Use **standardized mean differences (SMDs)** instead of tests.

#### A3) Primary binary endpoint analysis: incidence of postoperative pain
- **Test name:** **Logistic regression** (or mixed logistic if defined daily).
- **Purpose:** Compare **incidence of any postoperative pain** over 7 days between arms.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Incidence is a stated objective; logistic regression yields **odds ratio** with CI.
  2. Can adjust for **tooth type** and **baseline pain**, and handle unequal follow-up definitions.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - “Incidence” definition not specified; different thresholds change results.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If incidence is computed per-day per-subject (repeated binary), simple logistic ignoring within-subject correlation is invalid.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated / repeated structure:**
  - **GEE logistic** for repeated daily binary pain,
  - **Mixed-effects logistic regression** with patient random intercept.

*Operational definition to pre-specify (needed):*
- “Any pain” = VAS > 0 on any day post-obturation (or ≥10mm as minimal clinically important difference; choose and justify).

### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED (enhance rigor/quality)

#### B1) ANCOVA-style comparison of summary measures (AUC, peak pain)
- **Test name:** **Linear regression / ANCOVA** for:
  - **AUC(0–7)** of VAS,
  - **Peak VAS**, and/or **mean VAS** over 7 days.
- **Purpose:** Provide clinically interpretable single-number summaries of postoperative pain burden.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. AUC captures overall pain experience and avoids multiple day-wise tests.
  2. ANCOVA adjustment for baseline pain improves precision.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - AUC requires reasonably complete diaries; missingness can bias unless handled carefully.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If missing diary days are frequent and not handled by imputation/model-based estimation.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Quantile regression** (median AUC/peak),
  - **Permutation tests** for summary measures,
  - Model-based AUC estimated from mixed model.

#### B2) Multiplicity control across endpoints and time
- **Test name:** **Holm–Bonferroni** (familywise) or **Benjamini–Hochberg** (FDR), plus prespecified primary endpoint hierarchy.
- **Purpose:** Control inflated Type I error due to:
  - rest vs biting pain,
  - multiple days,
  - multiple contrasts.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. The study measures pain repeatedly and in two conditions → many potential comparisons.
  2. Prevents overstating “significant” isolated findings.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Overly conservative with small sample sizes may increase Type II error.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If only a single primary estimand is tested (e.g., treatment effect from mixed model) and everything else is explicitly exploratory.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Use a **single mixed-model primary test** (treatment or interaction) and limit secondary tests.

#### B3) Sensitivity analyses for missing data
- **Test name:** **Multiple imputation (MI)** with Rubin’s rules; **pattern-mixture** sensitivity.
- **Purpose:** Evaluate robustness to attrition and incomplete diaries.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Attrition occurred after randomization; ignoring it risks bias.
  2. MI can incorporate baseline predictors and partial outcomes.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - MI depends on model specification; may be unstable with small n.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If missingness is minimal (<5%) and mixed model already used with strong MAR plausibility.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Inverse probability weighting (IPW)**,
  - **Worst/best-case bounds** for binary incidence.

#### B4) Stratified/interaction analysis by tooth type (effect modification)
- **Test name:** In mixed model or regression: **treatment×tooth-type interaction**; report stratum-specific effects.
- **Purpose:** Assess whether treatment differs for molars vs anterior/premolars.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Randomization was **stratified** by tooth type; effect may plausibly differ.
  2. Helps interpret results clinically by tooth category.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Limited sample sizes per stratum → low power; interaction tests can be unstable.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If pre-specified as exploratory only; do not over-interpret.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Present **stratum-specific estimates with CIs** without formal interaction testing.

#### B5) Model diagnostics and assumption checks
- **Test name:** Residual diagnostics; **Shapiro–Wilk** (limited usefulness), **Levene/Brown–Forsythe** for variance, influence diagnostics.
- **Purpose:** Validate chosen parametric models.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. VAS data often violate normality; diagnostics guide transformations/robust methods.
  2. Identifies outliers/influential points.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Formal normality tests are oversensitive/undersensitive depending on n; should not be sole criterion.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Don’t use Shapiro–Wilk to automatically switch to nonparametric without considering model robustness.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Robust regression**, **bootstrap CIs**, **GEE robust SE**.

### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL (supplementary insights)

#### C1) Time-to-event analysis: time to pain resolution
- **Test name:** **Kaplan–Meier** curves + **Cox proportional hazards model**
- **Purpose:** Compare time until pain becomes 0 (or below threshold) and stays there.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Uses timing information; clinically intuitive (“how fast does pain resolve?”).
  2. Handles censoring if diaries incomplete.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Requires clear definition of “resolution” and diary compliance; daily measurement leads to interval censoring.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If pain fluctuates (0 then returns) and “resolution” is not well-defined.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Accelerated failure time** models; **discrete-time survival** (complementary log–log).

#### C2) Ordinal pain categories (none/mild/moderate/severe)
- **Test name:** **Ordinal logistic regression** / **ordinal mixed model**
- **Purpose:** Present results in clinically meaningful categories.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Avoids over-precision of VAS and accommodates skew/ceiling effects.
  2. Easier clinical interpretation.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Requires validated cutpoints; proportional odds assumption may fail.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If cutpoints are arbitrary and not justified.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Partial proportional odds** models; **multinomial regression**.

#### C3) Mediation-like exploration of obturation-related factors (not fully available)
- **Test name:** Not a formal test here; exploratory adjustment if variables exist (e.g., sealer extrusion on radiograph).
- **Purpose:** Explore mechanisms (e.g., extrusion → pain).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Mechanistically plausible.
  2. Could explain heterogeneity.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - These variables are not described as collected; and adjusting for post-randomization mediators can bias causal estimates.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If the goal is causal effect of assigned treatment; do not adjust for mediators.
- **Alternative:**
  - Report descriptively; pre-specify future studies.

## TASK 3: Inappropriate tests (should NOT be used)

1. **Multiple independent t-tests comparing arms at each day (7 days × 2 conditions) without correction**
   - Inflates Type I error; ignores within-subject correlation.

2. **Paired t-test between treatment arms**
   - Groups are independent; pairing is incorrect.

3. **One-way ANOVA treating all daily VAS observations as independent**
   - Violates independence (repeated measures within person).

4. **Simple chi-square test on “any pain” without accounting for stratification/covariates (if used as sole analysis)**
   - Loses precision; cannot adjust for baseline pain; stratified randomization suggests at least stratified/adjusted analysis.

5. **Linear regression on VAS with no handling of repeated measures and no diagnostics**
   - Standard errors and p-values invalid if repeated structure ignored; risk of model misspecification.

## TASK 4: Summary justification of overall approach

The study is a stratified parallel RCT with **daily repeated VAS pain measurements** (rest and biting) over 7 days plus baseline pain. The core analysis should therefore use **mixed-effects (or marginal) repeated-measures models** to (i) respect within-patient correlation, (ii) estimate treatment differences over time, and (iii) use partially observed diaries. Complementary analyses of **pain incidence** and **summary measures (AUC/peak)** provide clinically interpretable results, while multiplicity control and missing-data sensitivity analyses address the major validity threats (many comparisons and attrition).

### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS
1. **Test Name**: Independent Samples t-Test (or Mann-Whitney U Test if assumptions are violated)
   - **Purpose**: Compare mean VAS pain scores between Endoseal MTA and AH Plus groups.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     - The study aims to compare pain intensity between two independent groups.
     - VAS scores are continuous variables suitable for parametric or non-parametric comparison.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:
     - Assumes normality and homogeneity of variance, which may not hold for VAS scores.
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**:
     - If VAS scores are highly skewed or sample sizes are small.
   - **Alternative**: Mann-Whitney U Test (non-parametric).

2. **Test Name**: Chi-Square Test (or Fisher’s Exact Test)
   - **Purpose**: Compare the incidence of postoperative pain between groups.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     - Incidence is a categorical variable (presence/absence of pain).
     - Suitable for analyzing proportions in independent groups.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:
     - Requires sufficient sample size in each cell; may be inappropriate for small subgroups.
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**:
     - If expected frequencies in any cell are <5.
   - **Alternative**: Fisher’s Exact Test.

3. **Test Name**: Repeated Measures ANOVA (or Friedman Test if assumptions are violated)
   - **Purpose**: Analyze changes in VAS scores over time (days 1–7) within and between groups.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     - Accounts for repeated measurements of pain intensity over time.
     - Suitable for comparing trends between groups.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:
     - Assumes sphericity and normality of residuals.
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**:
     - If data violates sphericity or normality assumptions.
   - **Alternative**: Friedman Test (non-parametric).

#### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED TESTS
1. **Test Name**: Mixed-Effects Model
   - **Purpose**: Account for both fixed effects (group, time) and random effects (patient variability).
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     - Handles unbalanced data due to dropouts.
     - Accounts for repeated measures and inter-patient variability.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:
     - Requires advanced statistical software and expertise.
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**:
     - If sample size is insufficient for model convergence.
   - **Alternative**: Repeated Measures ANOVA.

2. **Test Name**: Logistic Regression
   - **Purpose**: Assess predictors of postoperative pain incidence (categorical outcome).
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     - Allows adjustment for covariates (e.g., preoperative pain, tooth type).
     - Suitable for binary outcomes.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:
     - Requires sufficient events per predictor variable.
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**:
     - If sample size is insufficient for reliable estimates.
   - **Alternative**: Chi-Square Test with stratification.

#### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL TESTS
1. **Test Name**: Subgroup Analysis (e.g., stratified t-tests or ANOVA)
   - **Purpose**: Explore differences within subgroups (e.g., anterior/premolar vs molar).
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     - Provides insights into subgroup-specific effects.
     - May reveal differential effects of sealers by tooth type.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:
     - Reduces statistical power due to smaller subgroup sample sizes.
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**:
     - If subgroup sample sizes are too small.
   - **Alternative**: Pooled analysis with interaction terms.

2. **Test Name**: Correlation Analysis (e.g., Spearman’s Rank Correlation)
   - **Purpose**: Assess relationship between preoperative pain and postoperative pain intensity.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     - Explores potential predictors of postoperative pain.
     - Suitable for ordinal or non-normally distributed data.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:
     - Does not account for confounding variables.
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**:
     - If data is dichotomized (presence/absence of pain).

CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS

A1. Descriptive statistics and baseline comparability

- Test name:  
  - Descriptive summaries + t-test / Mann–Whitney U test for continuous variables,  
  - Chi-square test / Fisher’s exact test for categorical variables.

- Purpose:  
  - Summarize baseline characteristics and check for balance between sealer groups (Endoseal vs AH Plus), overall and within tooth-type strata.

- Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):  
  1) This is an RCT with modest sample size; assessing baseline comparability is critical to support internal validity and interpret treatment effects.  
  2) VAS scores and age are continuous; sex, tooth type, and other categorical variables (if available) require different tests (t/Manh–Whitney for continuous; chi-square/Fisher for categorical).  

- Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason):  
  - With relatively small per-group sample sizes, parametric assumptions (normal distribution, equal variances) may be questionable; t-tests or chi-square may be underpowered or inaccurate.

- When this test should NOT be used:  
  - t-test: if distributions are heavily skewed or have extreme outliers and sample sizes are very small (e.g., <15 per group).  
  - Chi-square test: when expected cell counts are <5 in >20% of cells.

- Alternative if assumptions are violated:  
  - For continuous variables: use Mann–Whitney U test (Wilcoxon rank-sum) instead of t-test.  
  - For categorical variables with small counts: use Fisher’s exact test instead of chi-square.

A2. Primary endpoint: Comparison of postoperative pain incidence at each time point

- Test name:  
  - Chi-square test or Fisher’s exact test for proportion with pain (Endoseal vs AH Plus) at each day (1–7) and pain condition (rest, biting).

- Purpose:  
  - Compare the proportion of patients experiencing any postoperative pain between sealers at specified time points (e.g., day 1, day 3, day 7).

- Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):  
  1) Incidence is a binary outcome (pain present vs absent); chi-square/Fisher tests are standard for comparing proportions between two independent groups.  
  2) Groups are randomized and independent, and group sizes are moderate (n≈35 vs 32 overall), supporting use of these tests for daily incidence comparisons.

- Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason):  
  - Testing each day separately inflates type I error (multiple comparisons) and ignores the within-subject repeated structure over days.  
  - Expected frequencies may be small on days with low pain incidence, undermining chi-square validity.

- When this test should NOT be used:  
  - When sample size per group is very small or event rate is very low (e.g., <5 events per cell).  
  - As the sole analysis of incidence if a more holistic repeated-measures or longitudinal approach is planned (risk of multiplicity).

- Alternative if assumptions are violated:  
  - Fisher’s exact test for sparse data.  
  - A repeated-measures logistic regression or generalized estimating equation (GEE) model (see B2) to model incidence across all days simultaneously.

A3. Primary endpoint: Comparison of postoperative pain intensity as repeated VAS measures

- Test name:  
  - Linear mixed-effects model (LMM) for repeated measures, OR  
  - Repeated-measures ANOVA (RM-ANOVA) if assumptions are met.

- Purpose:  
  - Evaluate differences in mean pain intensity (VAS) over time between sealers, accounting for repeated measurements within individuals and the time course.

- Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):  
  1) Pain is measured daily for 7 days in the same subject, generating correlated data; LMM / RM-ANOVA accounts for within-subject correlation.  
  2) LMM can accommodate missing data under missing at random (e.g., missing some daily ratings) more flexibly than listwise deletion.  

- Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason):  
  - VAS scores may be skewed or show floor effects (many zeros); if distribution is strongly non-normal, linear models may be suboptimal.  
  - With relatively small sample size and multiple time points, RM-ANOVA sphericity assumptions may be violated and hard to assess.

- When this test should NOT be used:  
  - RM-ANOVA: should not be used with substantial missing data or when sphericity is severely violated and corrections (Greenhouse–Geisser) result in very low df.  
  - LMM: not appropriate if pain is best conceptualized as ordinal or zero-inflated and linearity is clearly violated.

- Alternative if assumptions are violated:  
  - Use a generalized linear mixed model (GLMM) with appropriate link (e.g., gamma or log-link for skewed distributions, or cumulative logit for ordinal categories).  
  - Use nonparametric repeated-measures approaches (e.g., Friedman test for within-group time comparisons and Mann–Whitney on change scores at key time points), though less powerful and less comprehensive.

A4. Comparison of summary pain intensity measures between groups

To supplement repeated measures, one or two clinically meaningful summary metrics should be compared.

- Test name:  
  - Mann–Whitney U test (or t-test if assumptions met) on:  
    - Maximum VAS over 7 days.  
    - AUC (area under the VAS–time curve) over 7 days.

- Purpose:  
  - Provide simple, interpretable group comparisons that summarize pain trajectory into single patient-level measures.

- Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):  
  1) Reduces multiple repeated observations to a single summary, simplifying analysis and avoiding complex modeling.  
  2) Mann–Whitney is robust for skewed distributions and small sample sizes; appropriate for VAS which may be non-normal and bounded.  

- Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason):  
  - Summaries (especially maximum VAS) can discard temporal information and obscure patterns (e.g., early vs late pain).  
  - If data are very heavily tied at 0, rank-based methods may have limited discriminatory power.

- When this test should NOT be used:  
  - If important clinical questions hinge on the time course of pain rather than overall intensity (in that case, rely on LMM/GLMM).  
  - If missing daily data preclude reliable computation of AUC or maximum.

- Alternative if assumptions are violated:  
  - For highly skewed AUC: a permutation test comparing medians or means between groups.  
  - Transform AUC (e.g., log(AUC+1)) and use t-test if approximate normality achieved.

A5. Adjustment for preoperative pain and tooth type (stratified or multivariable analysis)

- Test name:  
  - Linear mixed-effects model or ANCOVA with baseline covariates, including:  
    - Preoperative VAS at rest and biting.  
    - Tooth-type stratum (group 1 vs 2).  

- Purpose:  
  - Adjust for potential imbalances in preoperative pain and tooth type, which are known predictors of postoperative pain.

- Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):  
  1) Preoperative pain intensity is an established predictor of postoperative pain; including it improves precision and controls for residual confounding.  
  2) The design stratified by tooth type; adjusting for stratum or testing sealer*tooth-type interaction respects randomization structure.

- Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason):  
  - If baseline differences are minimal and sample size is modest, multivariable models can overfit and reduce power.  

- When this test should NOT be used:  
  - If sample size is too small to support models with several covariates (rule of thumb: ≥10–15 subjects per parameter).  
  - When there is extreme collinearity (e.g., if tooth type and another collected covariate are highly correlated).

- Alternative if assumptions are violated:  
  - Use stratified analyses: perform sealer comparisons separately within anterior/premolar and molar strata and possibly combine using a stratified test (e.g., Cochran–Mantel–Haenszel for binary endpoints, or stratified Wilcoxon tests for continuous outcomes).

  1) Time until pain resolution is clinically meaningful and uses all available information about duration of pain, not just intensity.

- Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason):

- When this test should NOT be used:  
  - If pain trajectories are too irregular or incomplete to define a clear resolution time.  
  - If nearly all patients are pain-free by day 1–2, providing minimal variation.

- Alternative if assumptions are violated:  
  - Compare proportion pain-free at key time points (e.g., day 3, day 7) using chi-square/Fisher tests.

TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS FOR THIS STUDY

T1. Independent-samples t-test on daily VAS without accounting for repeated measures (for all days simultaneously)

- Why inappropriate:  
  - Ignores the within-subject correlation across days; inflated type I error and incorrect standard errors.  
  - Treating all daily observations as independent heavily violates data structure.

- Prefer:  
  - Linear mixed-effects model or RM-ANOVA for repeated measures; or per-day tests with acknowledgment of multiplicity.

T2. Paired t-test between groups (Endoseal vs AH Plus)

- Why inappropriate:  
  - Groups are independent, not paired; paired t-tests assume within-subject pairing (e.g., split-mouth design), which this study does not have.

- Prefer:  
  - Independent t-test (or Mann–Whitney) for between-group comparisons.

T3. One-way ANOVA across multiple days treating “day” as independent group

- Why inappropriate:  
  - Days are repeated within the same patient, not independent groups; violates the independence assumption of standard one-way ANOVA.

- Prefer:  
  - RM-ANOVA or mixed models.

T4. Pearson correlation used to infer group differences

- Why inappropriate:  
  - Correlation is for association between two continuous variables, not for direct group comparison; using correlation coefficients to infer sealer differences would be conceptually incorrect.

- Prefer:  
  - Group comparison tests (t-test, Mann–Whitney, mixed models).

T5. Simple linear regression of postoperative pain on sealer type ignoring baseline and repeated measures

- Why inappropriate:  
  - Does not account for repeated measures or baseline pain; residuals will be correlated and model mis-specified.  

- Prefer:  
  - Mixed models (for repeated measures) and ANCOVA (with baseline pain) as specified above.

T6. Using parametric tests on VAS without checking assumptions in this small sample

- Why inappropriate:  
  - VAS may be skewed or heavily zero-inflated; blindly applying parametric tests can lead to misleading inferences.

- Prefer:  
  - At minimum, inspect distributions and use nonparametric or transformed methods if needed.
