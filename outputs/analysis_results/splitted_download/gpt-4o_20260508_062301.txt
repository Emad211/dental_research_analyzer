### TASK 1: INFORMATION EXTRACTION

#### Study Characteristics:
1. **Study Design**: 
   - Randomized clinical trial (RCT) with a parallel design.
   - Allocation ratio: 1:1.

2. **Objective**: 
   - To compare the effects of gel and solution forms of 5.25% sodium hypochlorite (NaOCl) on postoperative pain in mandibular molar teeth with symptomatic irreversible pulpitis.

3. **Groups**:
   - **Group 1**: 5.25% NaOCl solution.
   - **Group 2**: 5.25% NaOCl gel.

4. **Sample Size**:
   - Total: 114 participants (57 in each group).
   - Power: 95.1%.
   - Confidence level: 95%.
   - Effect size: 0.1121.

5. **Eligibility Criteria**:
   - **Inclusion**:
     - Healthy individuals aged 18–45 years.
     - Mandibular molar teeth diagnosed with symptomatic irreversible pulpitis.
     - Preoperative pain scores between moderate and severe (VAS: 4–10).
     - Teeth with specific anatomical characteristics (e.g., canal inclination ≤25°).
   - **Exclusion**:
     - Recent analgesic/anti-inflammatory use.
     - Pregnant or lactating patients.
     - Teeth with periapical lesions, resorption, calcification, or open apices.
     - Traumatic malocclusion or lack of occlusal contact.

6. **Variables**:
   - **Primary Outcome**: Postoperative pain levels measured using the Visual Analogue Scale (VAS) at 6, 24, 48, 72 hours, and 1 week post-treatment.
   - **Secondary Outcomes**: None explicitly mentioned.

7. **Measurements**:
   - Pain levels classified as:
     - No pain (VAS = 0).
     - Mild pain (VAS = 1–3).
     - Moderate pain (VAS = 4–6).
     - Severe pain (VAS = 7–10).
   - Pain scores collected via patient-reported forms and confirmed through follow-up phone calls.

8. **Randomization and Blinding**:
   - Randomization: Computer-generated sequence.
   - Blinding: Patients were blinded; clinicians were not due to the nature of the intervention.

9. **Interventions**:
   - Both groups underwent single-session root canal treatment with standardized procedures (e.g., ProTaper Next files, apex locator, irrigation with EDTA and saline).
   - Group-specific irrigants:
     - Group 1: 5 mL of 5.25% NaOCl solution.
     - Group 2: 5.25% NaOCl gel applied to files and canals, with saline irrigation.

10. **Data Collection**:
    - Pain scores recorded by patients on a detailed form at specified time points.
    - Follow-up phone calls to confirm and record data.

### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY
1. **Test Name**: Independent Samples t-test (or Mann-Whitney U test if non-parametric).
   - **Purpose**: Compare mean VAS pain scores between the two groups at each time point.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. The study involves two independent groups.
     2. The primary outcome (VAS scores) is continuous.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:
     - If VAS scores are not normally distributed.
   - **When NOT to Use**: If assumptions of normality and homogeneity of variance are violated.
   - **Alternative**: Mann-Whitney U test for non-parametric data.

2. **Test Name**: Repeated Measures ANOVA (or Friedman Test if non-parametric).
   - **Purpose**: Analyze changes in VAS pain scores over time within each group.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. The study involves repeated measurements of the same outcome (VAS) over time.
     2. It accounts for within-subject correlations.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:
     - If sphericity is violated or data is non-parametric.
   - **When NOT to Use**: If assumptions of normality or sphericity are violated.
   - **Alternative**: Friedman Test for non-parametric data.

3. **Test Name**: Chi-Square Test (or Fisher’s Exact Test).
   - **Purpose**: Compare categorical pain levels (no pain, mild, moderate, severe) between groups at each time point.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. The outcome is categorical.
     2. The groups are independent.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:
     - If expected cell counts are too low.
   - **When NOT to Use**: If >20% of cells have expected counts <5.
   - **Alternative**: Fisher’s Exact Test.

---

#### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED
1. **Test Name**: Mixed-Effects Model.
   - **Purpose**: Analyze VAS pain scores over time, accounting for both within- and between-group effects.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. It handles missing data more robustly than repeated measures ANOVA.
     2. It accounts for both fixed (group, time) and random effects (individual variability).
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:
     - Requires advanced statistical software and expertise.
   - **When NOT to Use**: If the dataset is too small for reliable model convergence.
   - **Alternative**: Repeated Measures ANOVA or Friedman Test.

2. **Test Name**: Effect Size Calculation (e.g., Cohen’s d).
   - **Purpose**: Quantify the magnitude of differences in VAS scores between groups.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Provides clinically meaningful interpretation of results.
     2. Complements p-values by assessing practical significance.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:
     - If the sample size is too small for reliable effect size estimation.
   - **When NOT to Use**: If data is highly skewed.
   - **Alternative**: Non-parametric effect size measures (e.g., rank-biserial correlation).

---

#### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL
1. **Test Name**: Kaplan-Meier Survival Analysis.
   - **Purpose**: Assess time to pain resolution (VAS = 0) for each group.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Provides insight into the duration of postoperative pain.
     2. Accounts for censored data (e.g., patients lost to follow-up).
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:
     - If time-to-event data is not well-defined or incomplete.
   - **When NOT to Use**: If the majority of patients do not achieve pain resolution within the study period.
   - **Alternative**: Descriptive analysis of time-to-resolution.

2. **Test Name**: Subgroup Analysis.
   - **Purpose**: Explore potential differences in outcomes based on baseline characteristics (e.g., age, gender).
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Identifies potential effect modifiers.
     2. Enhances understanding of subgroup-specific effects.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:
     - Risk of Type I error due to multiple comparisons.
   - **When NOT to Use**: If sample size is insufficient for reliable subgroup analysis.
   - **Alternative**: Pool data and analyze overall trends.
