TASK 1: INFORMATION EXTRACTION  
(Study characteristics, variables, measurements, data collection)

1. Study design
- Type: Prospective cohort study.
- Setting: Department of Endodontics, School of Dentistry, Ege University, İzmir, Turkey.
- Time frame:
  - Recruitment: February 2016–December 2018.
  - Follow-up: Minimum 2 years, up to 4 years, with scheduled recalls.
- Design standards: Conducted according to STROBE and Preferred Reporting Items for Observational studies in Endodontics (PRIO-Endo 2020).
- Ethical approval: Local Ethical Committee (no. 15-6.1/8), Helsinki 2008 compliant.

2. Study objectives
- Primary aim: To evaluate the influence of various predictors on the outcome of root canal retreatment in symptomatic mandibular first molars with periapical lesions after a minimum of 2 years.
- Implicit primary outcome:
  - Root canal retreatment outcome: success (healed + healing) vs failure (nonhealing).
- Implicit secondary outcomes (from text):
  - Distribution of healed/healing/failure categories.
  - Influence of preoperative and intraoperative factors (“predictors”) on outcome.

3. Population and sampling
- Source population: Patients referred for root canal retreatment of mandibular molars.
- Screening:
  - Assessed: 420 mandibular molars referred for root canal retreatment.
  - Eligible and included: 120 symptomatic mandibular first molars with periapical lesions (420 → 120).
- Follow-up:
  - Lost during follow-up:
    - 2 teeth with vertical root fracture diagnosed at first-year follow-up.
    - 15 patients not available for recall.
  - Analysed: 103 patients (103 teeth); recall rate 85.3%.

4. Inclusion criteria
- Patients with:
  - Noncontributory medical history.
  - Age ≥18 (implied by exclusion of <18).
- Teeth:
  - Mandibular first molars only.
  - Previously root canal treated, with mature roots.
  - Symptomatic with:
    - Preoperative pain and tenderness to percussion.
  - At least one root with periapical lesion:
    - PAI score 4 or 5 (periapical index, Ørstavik et al., 1986).
    - Largest lesion diameter 3–10 mm on preoperative digital radiographs (paralleling technique, film holder).
  - Diagnosed as:
    - Symptomatic apical periodontitis OR
    - Acute apical abscess with localized intraoral swelling.

5. Exclusion criteria
- Patient level:
  - Age <18 years.
  - Extra-oral swelling.
  - Antibiotic intake within 2 weeks before start of retreatment.
- Tooth level:
  - Second and third molars.
  - Teeth with asymptomatic apical periodontitis.
  - Two adjacent teeth requiring retreatment (to avoid clustering).
  - Advanced periodontal disease:
    - Periodontal pocket depth >5 mm and/or attachment loss ≥3 mm.
  - Nonrestorable loss of tooth structure.
  - Vertical root fracture.
  - External or internal root resorption.
  - Complications related to primary root canal treatment such as:
    - Broken instrument.
    - Perforation.

6. Treatment protocol and grouping
- All treatments:
  - Performed by one experienced endodontist (8 years experience).
  - Magnification: 3.5× loupes; microscope used when needed.
  - Anaesthesia: Ultracaine DS.
  - Rubber dam isolation.
  - Access cavity; removal of existing restoration/caries.
  - Removal of gutta-percha:
    - Mainly ProTaper Universal Retreatment files (PTU-R).
    - Hedström files when necessary.
    - No solvent used.
  - Negotiation of canals: small K-files (sizes 06, 08, 10) where needed.
  - Working length (WL): electronic apex locator (Raypex VI) + confirmatory periapical radiographs.
  - Irrigants (during instrumentation): NaOCl and EDTA with side-vented 30G needle.
  - Root canal preparation: ProTaper Universal (PTU) to sizes determined by canal anatomy.
  - Intracanal medicament: Calcium hydroxide (CH) in most cases, except first visit in teeth with persistent exudate.
  - Final irrigation protocol:
    - 17% EDTA, 2.5% NaOCl, distilled water, then 2% chlorhexidine (CHX); manual dynamic agitation with gutta-percha.
  - Obturation:
    - Modified single-cone technique with PTU matched cones + AH Plus sealer; accessory cones if needed.
  - Final restoration:
    - Direct composite resin or core build-up + single porcelain crown.
- Visit structure:
  - All retreatments completed in 2–4 appointments:
    - 2 appointments: 75 teeth without active exudate drainage.
    - 3 appointments: 20 teeth with active exudate drainage.
    - 4 appointments: 8 teeth (4 flare-ups, 4 exudate up to third appointment).

7. Key exposure / predictor variables

7.1 Preoperative predictors (recorded before retreatment)
- Demographic:
  - Age (continuous, in years).
  - Gender (male/female).
- Clinical:
  - Soft tissue tenderness: present/absent.
  - Intraoral swelling: present/absent.
- Radiographic:
  - Periapical lesion size: 3–10 mm (continuous; may also be categorized).
  - PAI score: 4 vs 5.
  - Apical level of pre-existing root canal filling: short/adequate/overfill.
  - Density of existing root canal filling:
    - Poor: visible voids within gutta-percha or between gutta-percha and canal walls.
    - Good: homogeneous, no visible voids.
    - Unfilled: no gutta-percha inside the canal.

7.2 Intraoperative predictors (recorded during retreatment)
- Active exudate drainage through the root canal: present/absent.
- Apical level of root canal filling after retreatment (short/adequate/overfill – implied symmetry with pre-op).
- Density/quality of root canal filling after retreatment: good/poor.
- Procedural complications:
  - Sealer and/or gutta-percha extrusion (yes/no).
  - Broken instrument (yes/no).
  - Perforation (yes/no).
  - Flare-up (yes/no).
- Restoration type after retreatment:
  - Single crown vs direct composite.

7.3 Follow-up / outcome variables

a) Primary clinical/radiographic outcome categories  
Definition based on modified PAI system (Holden et al., 2008):

- Healed:
  - Clinical: no signs or symptoms (no pain, no tenderness to percussion/palpation, no sinus tract, no swelling; normal mobility).
  - Radiographic: complete resolution of periapical lesion, PAI 1 or 2.
- Healing:
  - Clinical: no signs or symptoms.
  - Radiographic: reduction in lesion size; PAI 3 or 4 at follow-up.
- Nonhealing:
  - Clinical: symptomatic or asymptomatic.
  - Radiographic:
    - Lesion size unchanged or increased and PAI 4 or 5, or
    - Previously normal ligament space developed into lesion, compared with baseline.

b) Derived binary outcome
- Failure: Nonhealing.
- Success: Healed + Healing combined (primary binary outcome used for predictors).

c) Longitudinal measurements
- Repeated follow-up visits:
  - 3, 6, 9, 12 months (year 1).
  - Every 6 months in year 2.
  - Then annually until study end (up to 4 years).
- At each visit:
  - Clinical exam: pain, tenderness to percussion/palpation, sinus tract, swelling, mobility.
  - Quality of restoration: secondary caries, marginal integrity, discrepancy, decementation.
  - Radiographs:
    - Standardized long-cone paralleling technique with film holder, phosphor plates; beam alignment standardized by film holder and pre-op reference.

d) Radiographic scoring protocol
- PAI scoring:
  - Two independent calibrated endodontists, using PAI on digital periapical radiographs.
  - Calibration: 100 images unrelated to study.
  - Evaluation: initial and final radiographs for each tooth independently.
  - Disagreements:
    - Discussed until consensus; if persistent disagreement, worst (higher) PAI score recorded.
- Multiple lesions in same tooth:
  - Worst PAI score across roots taken as tooth-level PAI.

8. Sample size
- Initially included: 120 teeth.
- Analysed at final follow-up: 103 teeth.
- No explicit a priori sample size calculation reported in text provided.

9. Data structure
- Unit of analysis: Tooth (one tooth per patient).
- Design: Single cohort, no randomized groups; all treated with similar protocol, but differing in clinical/radiographic predictors.
- Key analytic structure:
  - Cross-sectional end point (final outcome vs predictors).
  - Longitudinal outcome category at multiple time points (if used).
  - Time-to-event (time to healing/success) is possible but not explicitly mentioned.

TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

I will assume:
- Primary outcome: binary success (healed + healing) vs failure at last follow-up.
- Secondary outcome: three-category status (healed, healing, nonhealing) at last follow-up; time to success if data on timing of healing is available.
- Predictors as listed above.

For clarity, I separate tests into Category A (mandatory), B (recommended), C (optional).

-------------------------------------------------------------
CATEGORY A – MANDATORY ANALYSES
-------------------------------------------------------------

A1. Descriptive statistics

- Test/Method:
  - Descriptive statistics (no formal “test”): means/SD or medians/IQR, frequencies and percentages.

- Purpose:
  - To summarize baseline characteristics and intraoperative variables.
  - To describe outcome distribution (healed, healing, nonhealing; success vs failure).

- Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):
  1) Essential to understand sample composition, prevalence of predictors, and outcome frequencies before any inferential analysis.
  2) Needed to check plausibility, ranges, missing data, and to determine the feasibility and power of regression analyses (e.g., events per variable).

- Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason):
  - Not inappropriate per se; the concern is only choice of summary: mean/SD may be misleading for skewed data (e.g., lesion size).

- When NOT to use:
  - Means and SDs should not be used for heavily skewed continuous variables or those with outliers; prefer median/IQR.

- Alternatives if assumptions violated:
  - For skewed continuous variables: report medians, IQR, and possibly range.
  - For small counts: report frequencies but consider collapsing categories.

---

A2. Comparison of baseline characteristics between included and lost-to-follow-up cases

- Test name:
  - Independent-samples t-test or Mann–Whitney U test for continuous variables.
  - Chi-squared test or Fisher’s exact test for categorical variables.

- Purpose:
  - To assess whether patients lost to follow-up differ systematically from those retained, i.e., evaluate potential attrition bias.

- Why appropriate:
  1) Prospective cohort with 15/120 lost to recall plus 2 excluded for fractures; comparison helps quantify risk of selection bias at analysis.
  2) Standard approach for assessing generalizability and whether missingness is likely MCAR/MAR vs MNAR.

- Why it may not be appropriate:
  - With very small number in the “lost” group, statistical tests may be underpowered and p-values misleading.

- When NOT to use:
  - When predictors are unknown for lost patients (if data not recorded at baseline) or when lost numbers are extremely small for any test to be meaningful.

- Alternatives if assumptions violated:
  - Descriptive comparison without formal testing.
  - Standardized differences (effect sizes) instead of p-values to compare groups.

---

A3. Primary analysis: Association between predictors and binary outcome (success vs failure)

- Test name:
  1) Univariable logistic regression.
  2) Multivariable logistic regression.

- Purpose:
  - To quantify the effect of preoperative and intraoperative predictors on odds of success vs failure at final follow-up, estimate adjusted odds ratios (ORs) with confidence intervals.

- Why appropriate:
  1) Outcome is binary (success/failure); logistic regression is the standard modelling approach.
  2) Allows simultaneous adjustment of multiple predictors (e.g., age, gender, lesion size, PAI, exudate presence, quality of filling, restoration type), addressing confounding.
  3) Can handle categorical predictors with multiple levels (e.g., apical level: short/adequate/overfill) via dummy variables.

- Why it may not be appropriate:
  - Number of failures is small (12 failures among 103 teeth), which limits the number of predictors in a multivariable model (events-per-variable rule). Overfitting and unstable estimates are risks.

- When NOT to use:
  - When outcome prevalence is extremely low with many predictors, and EPV < ~10 (or at least <5), standard logistic regression may be unreliable.
  - If complete or quasi-complete separation occurs (e.g., a predictor perfectly predicts success/failure).

- Alternatives if assumptions violated:
  - Penalized logistic regression:
    - Firth’s penalized likelihood.
    - Ridge or LASSO (if variable selection is desired).
  - Exact logistic regression for predictors with very sparse cells.
  - Grouped modelling: reduce number of predictors, combine categories, or use hierarchical modelling to preserve EPV.

---

A4. Secondary categorical analysis: Association between predictors and three-category outcome

- Test name:
  - Multinomial logistic regression (healed vs healing vs nonhealing).

- Purpose:
  - To explore whether predictors differentially affect being healed vs healing vs nonhealing, not just success vs failure.

- Why appropriate:
  1) The outcome is nominal with three ordered states, but healing vs healed may have different clinical meaning; multinomial allows this without forcing order.
  2) Provides more granular insight (e.g., certain factors might be associated with fully healed vs mid-term healing).

- Why it may not be appropriate:
  - Sample size: with 103 teeth, and only 12 failures, disaggregating into three categories further reduces events per cell, yielding unstable estimates.

- When NOT to use:
  - When any outcome category has very small counts (e.g., <10), leading to sparse cells and convergence issues.
  - When interpretation becomes too complex relative to sample size and clinical relevance.

- Alternatives if assumptions violated:
  - Collapsing categories:
    - Binary logistic regression (success vs failure) as primary.
  - Ordinal logistic regression (proportional odds model) if the proportional odds assumption holds and sample size is adequate.
  - Descriptive stratification (presenting percentages by predictor) without regression.

---

A5. Bivariate associations between categorical predictors and outcome

- Test name:
  - Chi-squared test of independence or Fisher’s exact test.

- Purpose:
  - To evaluate unadjusted associations between individual categorical predictors (e.g., apical level, exudate presence, restoration type, PAI 4 vs 5) and success vs failure.

- Why appropriate:
  1) Quick assessment of strong bivariate associations, helpful for model building and interpretation.
  2) Appropriate for categorical variables with moderate sample sizes.

- Why it may not be appropriate:
  - Expected cell counts may be small due to low number of failures, violating chi-squared assumptions.

- When NOT to use:
  - When any expected cell frequency <5 in 20% or more of cells; the chi-squared approximation becomes unreliable.

- Alternatives if assumptions violated:
  - Fisher’s exact test (2×2 tables or small multiway tables).
  - Collapsing categories (e.g., short + overfill vs adequate).

---

A6. Calibration/reliability of PAI scoring

- Test name:
  - Weighted kappa (for pre-study calibration).
  - Simple/weighted kappa or intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC) for PAI scoring before consensus.

- Purpose:
  - To quantify interobserver agreement in PAI scoring (ordinal scale) before consensus, ensuring reliability of radiographic outcome classification.

- Why appropriate:
  1) PAI is ordinal (scores 1–5); weighted kappa accounts for ordered categories.
  2) Inter-rater agreement is key given that outcome classification is based on PAI.

- Why it may not be appropriate:
  - If final analyses are conducted on consensus scores only, post-consensus kappa cannot be computed (no variability). Only pre-consensus scoring can be used.

- When NOT to use:
  - When ratings are not recorded separately per rater (only consensus score retained).
  - When there are too few observations per category to compute stable kappa.

- Alternatives if assumptions violated:
  - Report percent agreement with caution.
  - Use ICC for ordinal/continuous scoring if treating PAI as quasi-continuous (less ideal than weighted kappa).

CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED ANALYSES
(Enhance rigor and insight)

B1. Modelling time to healing/success

- Test name:
  - Kaplan–Meier survival analysis (time-to-success).
  - Log-rank test for comparing survival curves across predictor groups.
  - Cox proportional hazards regression (if dates of healing known).

- Purpose:
  - To evaluate how quickly teeth reach success (healed or healing) and analyze predictors of time to success (or time to failure if defined).
  - Use intermediate follow-up data (3, 6, 9, 12, 18, 24, etc.) rather than only last time point.

- Why appropriate:
  1) Follow-up schedule includes multiple time points and variable follow-up durations (2–4 years); time-to-event analysis appropriately incorporates censored observations.
  2) Provides more nuanced picture than a simple end-point yes/no success.

- Why it may not be appropriate:
  - The exact time of transition from “healing” to “healed” may be interval-censored (only known within recall intervals), complicating standard Cox/Kaplan–Meier assumptions.

- When NOT to use:
  - If dates of healing or failure cannot be approximated (e.g., if only final status at last visit is recorded, with no intermediate classification).
  - If the exact definition of event time is ambiguous (e.g., healing defined as a status category rather than a discrete event).

- Alternatives if assumptions violated:
  - Discrete-time survival analysis (e.g., complementary log–log model) treating follow-up intervals as discrete.
  - Use the last follow-up outcome only and logistic regression as primary, acknowledging loss of timing information.

---

B2. Assessment of lesion size change as a continuous outcome

- Test name:
  - Paired t-test or Wilcoxon signed-rank test for change in lesion size (mm) from baseline to final.
  - Linear regression modelling change in lesion size as dependent variable with predictors (or ANCOVA with final size adjusting for baseline).

- Purpose:
  - Quantify radiographic change in lesion size, not just categorical healing status.
  - Evaluate predictors of greater or lesser reduction in lesion size.

- Why appropriate:
  1) Lesion size (3–10 mm) is measured on radiographs; change in mm can be informative, especially for cases classified as healing.
  2) Continuous measures generally have more statistical power and can capture subtle improvements.

- Why it may not be appropriate:
  - Measurement error and radiographic distortion can limit precision; lesion size may be non-normal and bounded.

- When NOT to use:
  - If lesion size is not measured quantitatively (only categorized).
  - If measurement methods are inconsistent across time points.

- Alternatives if assumptions violated:
  - Nonparametric tests (Wilcoxon signed-rank) for paired comparisons.
  - Ordinal analysis of ordered categories of change (e.g., large reduction, small reduction, none).

---

B3. Model diagnostics and assumption checking for logistic regression

- Test name:
  - Hosmer–Lemeshow goodness-of-fit test, calibration plots.
  - Assessment of multicollinearity (VIF).
  - Influential observations (Cook’s distance, leverage).
  - Linearity in the logit for continuous predictors (e.g., age, lesion size).

- Purpose:
  - To ensure logistic models fit the data adequately and assumptions are reasonably met.

- Why appropriate:
  1) Multivariable models with multiple predictors require checking for calibration and influential points, especially with small sample size.
  2) Ensures transparency and robustness of inferential conclusions.

- Why it may not be appropriate:
  - Hosmer–Lemeshow test has low power with small n and can yield misleading p-values; VIFs might be unstable with small sample sizes.

- When NOT to use:
  - When sample size is too small for meaningful grouping in Hosmer–Lemeshow test (e.g., fewer than 8–10 groups or total N <100).

- Alternatives if assumptions violated:
  - Use AIC/BIC, pseudo R² and calibration plots instead of Hosmer–Lemeshow p-values.
  - Simplify model by reducing predictors or combining categories.
  - Use penalized regression or Bayesian approaches.

---

B4. Sensitivity analyses

- Test name:
  - Logistic regression restricted to teeth with complete follow-up ≥3 years vs ≥2 years.
  - Analysis excluding teeth with complications (e.g., flare-ups, extrusions).
  - Multiple imputation if small amount of missing predictor data (if present).

- Purpose:
  - Test robustness of primary results to different assumptions and subsets.

- Why appropriate:
  1) Prospective observational design is vulnerable to confounding and selection; sensitivity analysis helps gauge stability of results.
  2) Complications could act as mediators or confounders; excluding them can show whether predictors are acting primarily via complications.

- Why it may not be appropriate:
  - With small numbers of failures or complications, restriction may further reduce power and yield unstable estimates.

- When NOT to use:
  - When subgroups become too small for meaningful inference.

- Alternatives if assumptions violated:
  - Descriptive sensitivity (compare effect sizes qualitatively) rather than fully adjusted models.

---

B5. Analysis of restoration quality vs outcome

- Test name:
  - Chi-squared or Fisher’s exact test (restoration type/quality vs success).
  - Logistic regression including restoration type/quality as predictor.

- Purpose:
  - Evaluate whether restoration type (single crown vs composite) or quality at follow-up is associated with outcome.

- Why appropriate:
  1) Restoration quality is known to affect long-term endodontic outcomes.
  2) Data on restoration type and quality is collected at follow-ups.

- Why it may not be appropriate:
  - Timing: restoration quality assessed at follow-up may be influenced by the outcome itself (reverse causality).

- When NOT to use:
  - When restoration quality data is only available at the same time as outcome assessment, making temporal direction unclear.

- Alternatives if assumptions violated:
  - Restrict analysis to restoration type (assigned at baseline after retreatment) rather than quality at follow-up.

---

B6. Ordinal analysis of PAI scores

- Test name:
  - Wilcoxon signed-rank test for paired PAI baseline vs final.
  - Ordinal logistic regression (proportional odds) with final PAI as outcome, adjusting for baseline PAI and predictors.

- Purpose:
  - To exploit the full ordinal nature of PAI rather than collapsing into categories.

- Why appropriate:
  1) PAI 1–5 is ordered; ordinal models can efficiently use this structure.
  2) May reveal factors associated with more substantial radiographic improvement, even within the “success” category.

- Why it may not be appropriate:
  - Proportional odds assumption (same effect across cutpoints) may not hold.
  - Sparsity in some PAI categories, especially at follow-up (e.g., few PAI 5 after treatment).

- When NOT to use:
  - If there are too few observations per PAI level.
  - If tests show clear violation of proportional odds.

- Alternatives if assumptions violated:
  - Partial proportional odds models.
  - Collapsing PAI categories (e.g., 1–2 vs 3 vs 4–5) and using multinomial logistic regression.

TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS AND WHY

Below are tests that should NOT be used for this study (or only with extreme caution), with justification.

1. Student’s paired t-test for binary or ordinal outcomes
- Inappropriate because:
  - Outcome (success/failure or PAI categories) is categorical, not continuous; t-tests assume continuous, normally distributed variables.
- Better alternatives:
  - McNemar test (for paired binary, e.g., presence/absence of lesion at baseline vs follow-up).
  - Wilcoxon signed-rank (for ordinal scores if treated as continuous-like).

2. Independent-samples t-test for outcome vs categorical predictors when data are non-normal or ordinal
- Inappropriate when:
  - Outcome is binary (success/failure); t-tests are not designed for binary outcomes.
  - PAI or ordered categories are analysed as means without justification.
- Better alternatives:
  - Logistic regression for binary outcomes.
  - Ordinal logistic regression or nonparametric tests for ordered outcomes.

3. Pearson correlation to assess association between predictors and binary outcome
- Inappropriate because:
  - Pearson correlation assumes two continuous variables; one variable here is binary.
  - Interpretation is misleading; logistic regression or point-biserial correlation is more appropriate.

4. Simple linear regression with binary outcome
- Inappropriate because:
  - Violates assumptions of linear regression (normal residuals, homoscedasticity).
  - Predicted values can be outside [0,1]; logistic regression is the correct approach.

5. Repeated-measures ANOVA for longitudinal categorical outcomes
- Inappropriate because:
  - Outcome over time is categorical, not continuous.
  - Repeated-measures ANOVA assumes normally distributed continuous dependent variable and sphericity.

6. One-way ANOVA to compare PAI categories as “means” across groups
- Inappropriate when:
  - PAI scores are ordinal, not interval-scaled.
  - ANOVA assumes equal intervals between scores and normality of residuals.

7. Multiple uncorrected pairwise chi-squared tests across many predictors without multivariable adjustment
- Problematic because:
  - High risk of type I error inflation; without adjustment or modelling, conclusions may be misleading.
- Better alternative:
  - Logistic regression with limited key predictors and possibly penalization.

8. Stepwise variable selection (forward/backward stepwise logistic regression)
- Inappropriate (or strongly discouraged):
  - With small sample size and limited events, stepwise procedures are unstable, data-driven and produce biased coefficients and p-values.
- Better alternative:
  - Pre-specify predictors based on clinical reasoning; consider penalized regression.

9. Parametric survival models requiring precise event times if only interval data exist
- Inappropriate because:
  - If event time (healing) is only known within follow-up intervals, ordinary continuous-time models may misrepresent timing unless interval-censored methods are used.

TASK 4: SUMMARY OF OVERALL ANALYTICAL APPROACH

- Study type: Single-arm prospective cohort, 103 mandibular first molars followed for 2–4 years after standardized root canal retreatment.
- Primary focus: Identify preoperative and intraoperative predictors of treatment success (healed + healing) vs failure (nonhealing), using tooth-level data.

Proposed overall approach:

1) Describe the cohort comprehensively (demographic, preoperative, intraoperative, outcome distribution).
2) Check for attrition bias by comparing included vs lost-to-follow-up teeth.
3) Use logistic regression (preferably penalized if needed due to small number of failures) to assess associations between carefully selected predictors and binary success/failure at final follow-up.
4) Complement with bivariate analyses (chi-squared/Fisher, nonparametric tests) for exploratory understanding.
5) Where data permit, exploit longitudinal structure via time-to-event analysis for time to success and analyze continuous/ordinal radiographic measures (lesion size, PAI change) for finer-grained insight.
6) Document reliability of PAI scoring (weighted kappa) to support validity of radiographic outcomes.
7) Conduct limited sensitivity analyses to assess robustness (e.g., excluding complications, varying follow-up thresholds).

This approach aligns with the study’s prospective cohort design, respects the small number of failures by avoiding overparameterized models, and uses methods tailored to the binary/ordinal nature of the outcome and the predictors collected.
