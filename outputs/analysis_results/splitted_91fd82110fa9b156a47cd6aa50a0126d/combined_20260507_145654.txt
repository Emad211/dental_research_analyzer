## TASK 1: Information extraction (study characteristics)

### Study design, setting, timeline
- **Design:** Prospective cohort study (single-arm treatment cohort) with evaluation of predictors of outcome.
- **Setting:** University endodontic department (Ege University, Turkey).
- **Recruitment window:** Feb 2016–Dec 2018.
- **Follow-up schedule:** 3, 6, 9, 12 months (year 1); every 6 months (year 2); annually thereafter.
- **Follow-up duration:** minimum **2 years**, maximum **4 years**.
- **Reporting guidance:** STROBE; PRIDE 2020 (observational studies in endodontics).

### Objective
- Evaluate the **influence of various predictors** on the **outcome of root canal retreatment** in **symptomatic mandibular first molars with periapical lesions**, assessed after ≥2 years.

### Study population
- **Source:** 420 referred mandibular molars assessed for eligibility.
- **Enrolled:** 120 symptomatic mandibular first molars with periapical lesions.
- **Analyzed at last follow-up:** **103 teeth/patients** (recall 85.3%).
  - Losses: 2 vertical root fractures detected at 1-year follow-up; 15 unavailable for recall.

### Inclusion criteria (key)
- Adults (implied; exclusion <18).
- Previously root canal treated **mature mandibular first molars**.
- **Symptomatic**: preoperative pain and percussion tenderness.
- **Periapical lesion**: at least one root with **PAI 4 or 5**.
- Lesion diameter **3–10 mm** on standardized digital periapical radiographs (paralleling technique).
- Diagnoses: symptomatic apical periodontitis or acute apical abscess with localized intraoral swelling.

### Exclusion criteria (as provided/visible)
- <18 years; extra-oral swelling; antibiotics within 2 weeks; 2nd/3rd molars; asymptomatic AP; two adjacent teeth requiring retreatment; advanced periodontal disease (PD>5mm and/or CAL≥3mm); nonrestorable; vertical root fracture; internal/external resorption; iatrogenic complications from primary treatment (broken instrument/perforation).

### Treatment/exposure details
- All retreatments by one experienced endodontist, magnification used; microscope in selected cases.
- Multi-visit retreatment (2–4 visits).
- **Key “grouping” factor during treatment:** **active exudate drainage through the canal (present/absent)**.
  - Two-visit (mostly no exudate): 75 teeth.
  - Three-visit (exudate): 20 teeth.
  - Four-visit: 8 teeth (4 flare-up; 4 persistent exudate).

### Predictors/variables collected

**Preoperative predictors (recorded before retreatment):**
- Patient **age** (continuous)
- **Gender** (categorical)
- **Soft tissue tenderness** (present/absent)
- **Intraoral swelling** (present/absent)
- **Periapical lesion size** (3–10 mm; continuous or categorized)
- **PAI score** (4 vs 5)
- **Apical level of existing root filling**: short / adequate / overfill
- **Density of existing root filling**: poor / good / unfilled

**Intraoperative/postoperative predictors (recorded during visits):**
- **Active exudate drainage** (present/absent)
- **Apical level after retreatment** (not explicitly defined here, but recorded)
- **Density after retreatment** (good/poor)
- **Complications**: extrusion (sealer/gutta-percha), broken instrument, perforation, flare-up (binary each or combined)
- **Restoration type**: single crown vs composite

**Outcome variables**
- **Primary outcome (categorical, 3-level):** modified PAI-based outcome at last follow-up:
  - **Healed:** asymptomatic + PAI 1–2
  - **Healing:** asymptomatic + reduced lesion + PAI 3–4
  - **Nonhealing (Failure):** symptomatic or not improving/worse + PAI 4–5 or new lesion
- **Derived binary outcome:**
  - **Success = healed + healing**
  - **Failure = nonhealing**
- Clinical components assessed at follow-up: pain, percussion/palpation tenderness, sinus tract/swelling, mobility; restoration quality. (However, final outcome definition is primarily the combined clinical+radiographic categories above.)

### Measurement methods / data quality
- Standardized digital periapical radiographs with long-cone paralleling + film holder; efforts to reproduce geometry.
- **Two independent calibrated endodontists** scored PAI on initial and final radiographs.
  - If disagreement: consensus; if still not agree: **worst score recorded**.
  - If two lesions on different roots: **worst PAI used** as tooth-level score.
- No explicit statement of blinding to predictors/timing beyond “initial and final radiographs … to prevent bias” (suggests masking to timepoint, but not fully specified).

# CATEGORY A – MANDATORY tests/models

## A1) Binary logistic regression (multivariable)
- **Purpose:** Identify predictors associated with **Success (yes/no)**.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Primary endpoint is naturally **binary** (success vs failure) as defined by authors.
  2. Allows **simultaneous adjustment** for multiple clinical/radiographic predictors (reduces confounding).
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - Only **12 failures (11.7%)** among 103 → high risk of **sparse-data bias/overfitting** with many predictors.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If you cannot ensure an adequate events-per-parameter or cannot apply penalization; or if outcome separation occurs.
- **Alternative if assumptions/limitations violated:**
  - **Penalized logistic regression (Firth)** or **ridge/elastic-net**; or reduce model complexity via pre-specified limited predictors.

**Implementation notes (mandatory in SAP):**
- Prefer **penalized/Firth** a priori given 12 failures.
- Pre-specify a small predictor set (e.g., exudate, baseline PAI, lesion size, post-retreatment density, restoration type; plus age/sex as minimal adjusters).
- Report adjusted **ORs** with 95% CIs; assess calibration.

---

## A2) Ordinal regression for ordered outcome (proportional odds model)
- **Test/model:** **Ordinal logistic regression** on 3-level outcome (Failure < Healing < Healed).
- **Purpose:** Use the full ordering information rather than collapsing to success/failure.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Outcome is **ordered** and clinically meaningful (healed better than healing better than failure).
  2. Often improves power/efficiency vs dichotomization.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Proportional odds assumption may not hold; small cell counts (especially failures).
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If proportional odds is strongly violated and alternative ordinal models are unstable due to sparse data.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Partial proportional odds model**, or **multinomial logistic regression** (see Recommended) if feasible, or collapse outcome with justification.

---

## A3) Inter-rater agreement for PAI scoring
- **Test:** **Weighted Cohen’s kappa** (or ICC for ordinal) for the two raters’ initial independent scores.
- **Purpose:** Quantify reliability of radiographic scoring.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. PAI is **ordinal**; weighted kappa accounts for partial disagreement.
  2. Two independent evaluators were used; reliability is integral to validity.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - If only consensus/worst scores are stored and initial independent ratings are unavailable.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If ratings are not independent (e.g., raters discussed before recording).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated/unavailable:**
  - Report **percent agreement** and/or ensure collection of independent scores in future; use **Gwet’s AC2** if kappa paradox arises.

# CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED tests/models

## B1) Missing data / attrition bias assessment
- **Test:** Compare baseline predictors between **followed (n=103)** vs **lost (n=17)**.
  - Categorical: **chi-square** or **Fisher’s exact**
  - Continuous: **t-test** or **Wilcoxon rank-sum**
- **Purpose:** Evaluate whether loss to follow-up is plausibly random.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. 14.7% attrition could bias associations if related to prognosis.
  2. Supports choice of missing-data method (MAR vs MNAR concerns).
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Low power with small “lost” group; multiple comparisons risk.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When baseline data for dropouts are missing/unreliable.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Descriptive standardized differences; proceed to **multiple imputation** with sensitivity analyses.

---

## B2) Penalized logistic regression (explicitly recommended)
- **Model:** **Firth logistic regression** for Success
- **Purpose:** Reduce small-sample/separation bias.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Only 12 failures → classic MLE logistic may be biased/infinite.
  2. Common in clinical prognostic studies with rare outcomes.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Interpretation is similar but may differ slightly from standard ORs; not available in all software.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Large-sample setting with adequate events and no separation.
- **Alternative:**
  - **Bayesian logistic regression** with weakly informative priors; or ridge regression.

---

## B3) Time-to-event analysis (if failure time is known)
- **Test/model:** **Kaplan–Meier** curves + **Cox proportional hazards**
- **Purpose:** Incorporate **variable follow-up times (2–4 years)** and censoring; estimate hazard of failure.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Patients have different observation windows; dichotomizing at “last follow-up” ignores timing.
  2. Clinical failures (e.g., detected VRF, persistent symptoms) may occur at known visits.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Failure time may be interval-censored (only known between visits) and events are few.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If exact/interval failure times are not available or too few events for Cox.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Discrete-time survival model** (complementary log–log), or **parametric survival** with penalization; or keep primary binary endpoint and adjust for follow-up time as covariate.

---

## B4) Model diagnostics and performance
- **Tests/metrics:** 
  - **Calibration plot** + calibration intercept/slope
  - **Brier score**
  - **ROC AUC** (with optimism correction via bootstrap)
- **Purpose:** Evaluate predictive/prognostic model adequacy (not just significance).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Study aim is “influence of predictors”; model validity matters.
  2. Prevents overinterpretation of unstable associations.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - With small n and rare failures, AUC and calibration can be unstable.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When presenting only descriptive associations and no multivariable model.
- **Alternative:**
  - Bootstrap internal validation; report shrinkage; or focus on effect estimates with penalization.

---

## B5) Sensitivity analysis for outcome definition
- **Approach:** Re-run primary model with alternative endpoints:
  - (i) **Healed only** vs (Healing + Failure)
  - (ii) **Failure** vs (Healed + Healing) (primary)
- **Purpose:** Assess robustness to categorization (particularly “healing”).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. “Healing” is intermediate and may later heal or fail.
  2. Tests whether predictors differ for complete healing vs partial improvement.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Smaller effective event counts worsen power and stability.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If sample sizes become too sparse (e.g., healed-only modeling with too few non-healed).
- **Alternative:**
  - Ordinal modeling (A2) as primary/secondary to avoid dichotomies.

# CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL tests/models

## C1) Unadjusted bivariate comparisons (screening)
- **Tests:** 
  - Chi-square/Fisher for categorical predictors vs success
  - t-test/Wilcoxon for continuous predictors vs success
- **Purpose:** Simple association summaries.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Communicates crude relationships.
  2. Helps identify sparse categories and data issues.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - “Significance screening” is misleading and increases Type I error.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - As the sole basis for variable selection.
- **Alternative:**
  - Use clinical pre-specification + penalized multivariable modeling.

---

## C2) Interaction assessment (effect modification)
- **Model feature:** Add interaction terms (e.g., exudate×baseline PAI; restoration×post density).
- **Purpose:** Identify whether predictor effects differ by subgroup.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Clinically plausible (e.g., exudate may matter more with high baseline PAI).
  2. Generates hypotheses for future trials.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Very low power; high overfitting risk with 12 failures.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Unless pre-specified and limited to 1–2 interactions.
- **Alternative:**
  - Stratified descriptive analyses with caution; Bayesian hierarchical modeling.

---

## C3) Agreement on lesion size (if measured by >1 assessor)
- **Test:** ICC / Bland–Altman
- **Purpose:** Reliability of continuous lesion size measurement.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Lesion size is used as predictor/outcome component.
  2. Measurement error affects associations.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Lesion size measurement process/duplicate readings not described.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If only single measurement exists.
- **Alternative:**
  - Standardize measurement protocol; use repeated measures in future.

---

## C4) Multilevel modeling (if multiple roots/lesions per tooth were analyzed)
- **Model:** Mixed-effects models
- **Purpose:** Account for clustering (roots within tooth).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Would be necessary if root-level outcomes were used.
  2. Avoids underestimated SEs.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Current analysis is explicitly **tooth-level** using “worst PAI”.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When only one observation per patient/tooth is analyzed.
- **Alternative:**
  - Keep tooth-level endpoint as done; or redesign to root-level with adequate sample.

## TASK 3: Inappropriate tests (should NOT be used)

1. **Paired t-test / repeated-measures ANOVA on PAI scores**
   - PAI is **ordinal**, not continuous; and only baseline/final scores are not repeated continuous measurements with normality.
2. **Multiple independent t-tests comparing many predictor subgroups without adjustment**
   - Inflates Type I error; does not adjust for confounding; inappropriate for etiologic “predictor” aim.
3. **Linear regression with Success coded 0/1**
   - Violates homoscedasticity/normality; can predict outside [0,1]; logistic/GLM is required.
4. **Standard chi-square tests when expected cell counts are small (e.g., complications, failures)**
   - With only 12 failures, many contingency cells will be sparse; prefer **Fisher’s exact** or exact/penalized models.
5. **Stepwise variable selection based purely on p-values**
   - High overfitting risk given low number of failures; produces unstable/inflated effects.
6. **McNemar test**
   - Requires paired binary outcomes on the same unit pre/post; not aligned with this prognostic cohort question.

### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY
1. **Descriptive Statistics**:  
   - **Purpose**: Summarize baseline characteristics (e.g., age, gender, lesion size, PAI scores).  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Provides a clear understanding of the study population.  
     2. Helps identify potential confounders.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Not inferential; cannot establish relationships.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: N/A (always required).  
   - **Alternative**: None.

2. **Chi-Square Test (or Fisher’s Exact Test)**:  
   - **Purpose**: Compare categorical variables (e.g., success rates between exudate and non-exudate groups).  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Tests associations between categorical predictors and outcomes.  
     2. Suitable for binary or ordinal data (e.g., healed vs. non-healed).  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Requires sufficient sample size in each category.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: Small expected frequencies (<5).  
   - **Alternative**: Fisher’s Exact Test.

3. **Logistic Regression**:  
   - **Purpose**: Identify predictors of treatment success (healed/healing vs. failure).  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Accounts for multiple predictors simultaneously.  
     2. Provides odds ratios for risk factors.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Assumes linearity in log-odds.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: When sample size is too small for the number of predictors.  
   - **Alternative**: Penalized regression (e.g., LASSO).

#### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED
1. **Kaplan-Meier Survival Analysis**:  
   - **Purpose**: Assess time to healing or failure.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Accounts for varying follow-up durations.  
     2. Handles censored data (e.g., lost to follow-up).  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Assumes non-informative censoring.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: When time-to-event data is unavailable.  
   - **Alternative**: Cox proportional hazards model.

2. **Cox Proportional Hazards Regression**:  
   - **Purpose**: Identify predictors of time to healing or failure.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Adjusts for multiple covariates.  
     2. Provides hazard ratios for predictors.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Assumes proportional hazards.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: Violations of proportional hazards assumption.  
   - **Alternative**: Stratified Cox model.

#### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL
1. **Repeated Measures ANOVA (or Mixed-Effects Model)**:  
   - **Purpose**: Analyze changes in PAI scores over time.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Accounts for within-subject correlations.  
     2. Suitable for longitudinal data.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Assumes normality of residuals.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: Non-normal data or unequal variances.  
   - **Alternative**: Non-parametric tests (e.g., Friedman test).

2. **Subgroup Analysis**:  
   - **Purpose**: Explore differences in outcomes by subgroups (e.g., gender, lesion size).  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Identifies effect modifiers.  
     2. Provides additional clinical insights.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Risk of type I error due to multiple comparisons.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: Small sample sizes in subgroups.  
   - **Alternative**: Adjusted analyses with interaction terms.

TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS AND WHY

Below are tests that should NOT be used for this study (or only with extreme caution), with justification.

1. Student’s paired t-test for binary or ordinal outcomes
- Inappropriate because:
  - Outcome (success/failure or PAI categories) is categorical, not continuous; t-tests assume continuous, normally distributed variables.
- Better alternatives:
  - McNemar test (for paired binary, e.g., presence/absence of lesion at baseline vs follow-up).
  - Wilcoxon signed-rank (for ordinal scores if treated as continuous-like).

2. Independent-samples t-test for outcome vs categorical predictors when data are non-normal or ordinal
- Inappropriate when:
  - Outcome is binary (success/failure); t-tests are not designed for binary outcomes.
  - PAI or ordered categories are analysed as means without justification.
- Better alternatives:
  - Logistic regression for binary outcomes.
  - Ordinal logistic regression or nonparametric tests for ordered outcomes.

3. Pearson correlation to assess association between predictors and binary outcome
- Inappropriate because:
  - Pearson correlation assumes two continuous variables; one variable here is binary.
  - Interpretation is misleading; logistic regression or point-biserial correlation is more appropriate.

4. Simple linear regression with binary outcome
- Inappropriate because:
  - Violates assumptions of linear regression (normal residuals, homoscedasticity).
  - Predicted values can be outside [0,1]; logistic regression is the correct approach.

5. Repeated-measures ANOVA for longitudinal categorical outcomes
- Inappropriate because:
  - Outcome over time is categorical, not continuous.
  - Repeated-measures ANOVA assumes normally distributed continuous dependent variable and sphericity.

6. One-way ANOVA to compare PAI categories as “means” across groups
- Inappropriate when:
  - PAI scores are ordinal, not interval-scaled.
  - ANOVA assumes equal intervals between scores and normality of residuals.

7. Multiple uncorrected pairwise chi-squared tests across many predictors without multivariable adjustment
- Problematic because:
  - High risk of type I error inflation; without adjustment or modelling, conclusions may be misleading.
- Better alternative:
  - Logistic regression with limited key predictors and possibly penalization.

8. Stepwise variable selection (forward/backward stepwise logistic regression)
- Inappropriate (or strongly discouraged):
  - With small sample size and limited events, stepwise procedures are unstable, data-driven and produce biased coefficients and p-values.
- Better alternative:
  - Pre-specify predictors based on clinical reasoning; consider penalized regression.

9. Parametric survival models requiring precise event times if only interval data exist
- Inappropriate because:
  - If event time (healing) is only known within follow-up intervals, ordinary continuous-time models may misrepresent timing unless interval-censored methods are used.

### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS
*(Tests without which the analysis would be incomplete)*

**1. Cohen’s Kappa (or Weighted Kappa)**
*   **Purpose:** To measure inter-rater reliability between the two independent endodontists scoring the radiographs using the PAI system.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) The primary outcome relies entirely on subjective radiographic scoring by human observers; proving their agreement is vital for internal validity. 
    2) The PAI score is categorical/ordinal, making Kappa the standard metric for agreement beyond chance.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** If the distribution of PAI scores is highly skewed (e.g., almost all scores are exactly the same), Kappa can be artificially low (the "Kappa paradox").
*   **When NOT to use:** Do not use if the radiographic measurement was a continuous variable (e.g., exact lesion volume in mm³).
*   **Alternative:** Gwet’s AC1 statistic (if the Kappa paradox is observed) or Percentage Agreement.

**2. Chi-Square Test of Independence (or Fisher’s Exact Test)**
*   **Purpose:** To perform univariate analysis comparing categorical predictor variables (e.g., gender, exudate presence, restoration type) against the dichotomized outcome (Success vs. Failure).
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) Both the independent variables (e.g., swelling present/absent) and the dependent variable (Success/Failure) are categorical. 
    2) It allows for the initial identification of factors associated with treatment failure before building a complex model.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It does not account for confounding variables or the effect of time to follow-up.
*   **When NOT to use:** Do not use Chi-Square if more than 20% of the expected cell frequencies are less than 5.
*   **Alternative:** Fisher’s Exact Test (mandatory here, as the "Failure" group only has 12 cases, meaning expected cell counts in cross-tabulations will frequently drop below 5).

**3. Multiple Logistic Regression**
*   **Purpose:** To identify independent predictors of root canal retreatment success/failure while controlling for confounding variables.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) The primary outcome is dichotomous (Success vs. Failure). 
    2) It allows the researchers to evaluate the adjusted impact of multiple preoperative and intraoperative variables simultaneously.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** The low number of failure events (n=12) severely limits the number of predictors that can be included in the model without causing overfitting (the "rule of 10 events per variable" suggests only 1-2 predictors should be modeled).
*   **When NOT to use:** Do not use if the outcome is kept in its 3-tier ordinal format (Healed, Healing, Nonhealing).
*   **Alternative:** Penalized Logistic Regression (Firth's Logistic Regression) to handle the rare events/small sample size of the failure group.

**1. Shapiro-Wilk Test**
*   **Purpose:** To assess the normality of continuous variables (e.g., patient age, exact lesion size in mm).

*   **When NOT to use:** Do not use if comparing more than two groups (e.g., comparing age across Healed, Healing, and Nonhealing).
