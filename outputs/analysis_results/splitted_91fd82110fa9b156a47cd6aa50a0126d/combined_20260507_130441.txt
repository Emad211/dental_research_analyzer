## TASK 1 — INFORMATION EXTRACTION (study characteristics)

### Study design, setting, timeframe
- **Design:** Prospective cohort study (single-arm clinical cohort; observational predictors of outcome).
- **Setting:** Department of Endodontics, School of Dentistry, Ege University (Turkey).
- **Recruitment period:** February 2016–December 2018.
- **Follow-up schedule:** 3, 6, 9, 12 months (year 1); every 6 months (year 2); then annually.
- **Follow-up duration:** Minimum **2 years**, maximum **4 years**.

### Objective
- To evaluate the **influence of various predictors** on the **outcome of root canal retreatment** in **symptomatic mandibular first molars with periapical lesions** after ≥2 years.

### Participant flow / sample size
- **Assessed:** 420 mandibular molars referred for retreatment.
- **Included at baseline:** 120 symptomatic mandibular first molars with periapical lesions.
- **Losses/exclusions during follow-up:**
  - 2 teeth: **vertical root fracture** detected at 1-year follow-up
  - 15: not available for recall
- **Analyzed at last follow-up:** **103 teeth** (recall rate **85.3%**).

### Eligibility criteria (key analytic implications)
- **Inclusion:**
  - Adults (≥18)
  - **Previously root canal treated mature mandibular first molars**
  - **Symptomatic**: preoperative pain + tenderness to percussion
  - **Periapical lesion present**: at least one root with **PAI 4 or 5**
  - Lesion largest diameter **3–10 mm** on standardized digital periapical radiographs
  - Diagnoses: symptomatic apical periodontitis or acute apical abscess with localized intraoral swelling
- **Exclusion:**
  - <18 years
  - Extra-oral swelling
  - Antibiotics within previous 2 weeks
  - Second/third molars
  - Asymptomatic apical periodontitis
  - Two adjacent teeth requiring retreatment (avoids within-patient clustering)
  - Advanced periodontal disease (PD>5 mm and/or CAL ≥3 mm)
  - Nonrestorable tooth structure
  - Vertical root fracture, resorption
  - Primary RCT complications: broken instrument/perforation

### Treatment/exposure description (not randomized)
- All retreatments by **one endodontist** (8 years’ experience), using magnification; microscope selectively.
- **Two clinical pathways** based on **active exudate drainage** (present/absent), affecting number of visits and interim management.
- **Visits:** 2–4 visits total.
  - 2 visits: 75 teeth without active exudate drainage
  - 3 visits: 20 teeth with active exudate drainage
  - 4 visits: 8 teeth (4 flare-ups; 4 prolonged exudate)

### Predictors / covariates collected

**Preoperative predictors**
- **Age** (continuous)
- **Gender** (categorical)
- **Soft tissue tenderness** (present/absent)
- **Intraoral swelling** (present/absent)
- **Periapical lesion size** (3–10 mm; recorded from radiographs; likely continuous in mm or categorized)
- **Initial PAI score** (4 vs 5)
- **Preoperative apical level of root filling**: short / adequate / overfill
- **Preoperative density of root filling**: poor / good / unfilled

**Intraoperative/postoperative predictors**
- **Active exudate drainage through canal** (present/absent)
- **Post-retreatment apical level** (categories not fully restated but implied)
- **Post-retreatment density** (good/poor)
- **Complications:** sealer/gutta-percha extrusion; broken instrument; perforation; flare-up (binary indicators; possibly multiple)
- **Restoration type:** single crown vs composite (final restoration)

### Outcomes

**Primary outcome (final follow-up, tooth-level):**
- **Three-category outcome (modified PAI-based):**
  - **Healed:** asymptomatic + radiographic resolution; **PAI 1–2**
  - **Healing:** asymptomatic + lesion reduction; **PAI 3–4**
  - **Nonhealing (Failure):** symptomatic or asymptomatic with unchanged/increased lesion; **PAI 4–5** or new lesion development
- **Binary composite outcome:**  
  - **Success = healed + healing**
  - **Failure = nonhealing**

**Observed outcome counts (at last follow-up; n=103):**
- Healed: 66 (64.1%)
- Healing: 25 (24.2%)
- Failure: 12 (11.7%)
- Success total: 91 (88.3%)

### Measurement methods / reliability features
- **Radiographs:** digital periapical, paralleling technique with film holder; standardized beam positioning.
- **PAI scoring:** two independent calibrated endodontists; disagreements resolved by consensus; if persistent disagreement, **worst score recorded**.
- If two separate lesions on different roots: **worst PAI** used as tooth’s final PAI.

# CATEGORY A — MANDATORY (analysis incomplete without these)

## A1) Descriptive statistics (with confidence intervals)
- **Test/Method name:** Descriptive summaries + **95% CI** for proportions (Wilson/Clopper–Pearson) and means/medians.
- **Purpose:** Characterize cohort, predictors, and outcomes; quantify success/failure rates with uncertainty.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Required for transparent reporting (STROBE) and interpretation of model-based results.
  2. Outcome is categorical; proportions with CI are the natural summary.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):** If distributions are highly skewed, mean±SD can mislead.
- **When NOT to use:** Do not report mean±SD for clearly non-normal bounded measures (e.g., lesion size if heavily skewed) without checking.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:** Use median (IQR), and nonparametric summaries; bootstrap CI for medians.

## A2) Univariable association screening (categorical predictors vs binary success)
- **Test name:** **Fisher’s exact test** (preferred) or chi-square when adequate counts.
- **Purpose:** Compare success proportions across predictor categories (e.g., swelling yes/no).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Many predictors are binary/nominal; outcome is binary.
  2. **Small failure cell counts** (n=12) make Fisher’s exact more valid than chi-square.
- **Why may not be appropriate:** Multiple testing inflates type I error; also ignores confounding.
- **When NOT to use:** Avoid chi-square if expected counts <5 in any cell; avoid interpreting univariable p-values as causal.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:** Fisher’s exact (exact inference) or exact logistic regression.

## A3) Univariable comparisons for continuous predictors (e.g., age, lesion size) by outcome
- **Test name:** **Mann–Whitney U** (success vs failure); optionally t-test if normality holds.
- **Purpose:** Assess whether distributions differ by outcome.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Continuous predictors likely non-normal (lesion size bounded 3–10 mm).
  2. Robust with small samples and skewness.
- **Why may not be appropriate:** Does not adjust for confounders; tests stochastic dominance not mean difference.
- **When NOT to use:** When you need adjusted inference or interpretation in original units.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:** Permutation test; robust regression; use logistic regression with continuous predictors.

## A4) Primary multivariable model for predictors of success
- **Test/Model name:** **Penalized logistic regression** (e.g., Firth correction) with success (yes/no).
- **Purpose:** Estimate adjusted associations (odds ratios) between predictors and success.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Binary primary endpoint (success/failure).
  2. **Rare failure (12/103)** → standard logistic regression risks separation and biased estimates; Firth reduces small-sample bias.
- **Why may not be appropriate:** ORs can be hard to interpret clinically; penalization choices affect estimates.
- **When NOT to use:** If outcome is not binary (use multinomial/ordinal models) or if time-to-event is available and relevant.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - If strong nonlinearity: logistic regression with restricted cubic splines (penalized).
  - If repeated measures/clustering existed: mixed-effects logistic (not indicated here).

**Implementation notes (mandatory):**
- Predefine a **limited predictor set** due to only 12 failures (e.g., 3–5 key predictors chosen a priori: lesion size, initial PAI, active exudate, post-retreatment filling density, restoration type).
- Report **adjusted OR**, 95% CI, and predicted probabilities.

## A5) Inter-examiner reliability for PAI scoring (baseline and/or final)
- **Test name:** **Weighted Cohen’s kappa** (ordinal PAI) or ICC for ordinal treated as continuous (kappa preferred).
- **Purpose:** Quantify agreement between the two evaluators before consensus.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. PAI is **ordinal**; weighted kappa accounts for partial agreement.
  2. Validates outcome measurement quality; critical when radiographic scoring drives endpoint.
- **Why may not be appropriate:** If only consensus scores are stored and raw independent scores are unavailable.
- **When NOT to use:** Don’t compute kappa on consensus-only data.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:** Gwet’s AC2 (more stable under prevalence effects), or report % agreement with caution.

# CATEGORY B — RECOMMENDED (improves rigor)

## B1) Multinomial or ordinal modeling of 3-level outcome
- **Test/Model name:** **Ordinal logistic regression** (proportional odds) or **multinomial logistic regression**.
- **Purpose:** Model healed/healing/failure without collapsing information.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Outcome has ordered clinical meaning (failure < healing < healed).
  2. Avoids information loss from dichotomizing.
- **Why may not be appropriate:** Proportional odds assumption may fail; small failure group limits stability.
- **When NOT to use:** If proportional odds is clearly violated and multinomial is too underpowered.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:** Partial proportional odds model; multinomial with penalization; binary success model as fallback.

## B2) Check and model nonlinearity for continuous predictors
- **Test/Method name:** **Restricted cubic splines** in (penalized) logistic regression; likelihood ratio test for nonlinearity.
- **Purpose:** Avoid incorrect linearity assumption for age/lesion size.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Lesion size is bounded and may relate nonlinearly to healing.
  2. Improves calibration and predictive validity.
- **Why may not be appropriate:** Needs adequate sample size; risk of overfitting with few failures.
- **When NOT to use:** When events are too few to support spline terms.
- **Alternative:** Categorize into clinically meaningful bins (predefined) or use linear term with sensitivity checks.

## B3) Model diagnostics and performance (binary model)
- **Tests/Methods:**  
  - **Calibration plot** + calibration intercept/slope  
  - **Brier score**  
  - **c-statistic (AUC)** with optimism correction (bootstrap)
- **Purpose:** Assess how well the model predicts success and whether probabilities are reliable.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Predictor analysis often implicitly suggests prediction; diagnostics prevent misleading inferences.
  2. Penalized models still require calibration assessment.
- **Why may not be appropriate:** With small n and rare failure, AUC can be unstable.
- **When NOT to use:** If the goal is purely etiologic inference and not prediction (still useful but secondary).
- **Alternative:** Use bootstrap internal validation; report optimism-corrected metrics.

## B4) Sensitivity analysis for loss to follow-up (attrition bias)
- **Test/Method name:** **Inverse probability of censoring weighting (IPCW)** or **best/worst-case bounds** for success rate.
- **Purpose:** Evaluate robustness to missing outcome data (17/120 not evaluated).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Prospective cohort with nontrivial attrition; missingness may relate to prognosis.
  2. Success proportion and predictor effects may be biased if missing not completely at random.
- **Why may not be appropriate:** IPCW requires modeling missingness; can be unstable with small n.
- **When NOT to use:** If no data exist on predictors for those lost (cannot model missingness).
- **Alternative:** Multiple imputation for missing outcomes is generally not advised; use bounds/sensitivity scenarios.

## B5) Compare baseline characteristics of followed vs lost participants
- **Test name:** Fisher’s exact / Mann–Whitney U (as appropriate).
- **Purpose:** Assess whether attrition is differential.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Identifies potential selection bias.
  2. Informs interpretation of generalizability.
- **Why may not be appropriate:** Low power; “no significant difference” doesn’t prove no bias.

## TASK 3 — INAPPROPRIATE TESTS (should NOT be used)

1. **Paired t-test / Wilcoxon signed-rank test (pre vs post PAI) as the main analysis**
   - Why inappropriate: endpoint is **categorical clinical outcome** at final follow-up; repeated PAI across time is not analyzed as paired continuous change here, and “worst PAI per tooth” complicates change-score interpretation.

2. **One-way ANOVA for outcome groups (healed/healing/failure) without addressing ordinal nature and small failure n**
   - Why inappropriate: assumes normal residuals/variance for continuous outcomes; here outcome is categorical and group sizes are imbalanced (failure n=12).

3. **Standard (unpenalized) multivariable logistic regression with many predictors**
   - Why inappropriate: **events are too few** (12 failures) → separation, inflated ORs, unreliable p-values/CI.

4. **Chi-square tests when expected cell counts are small**
   - Why inappropriate: violation of chi-square approximation; Fisher’s exact is preferred.

5. **Linear regression treating success (0/1) as continuous (linear probability model)**
   - Why inappropriate: heteroskedasticity, predicted probabilities outside [0,1], inefficient and misleading with rare events.

6. **Repeated-measures ANOVA**
   - Why inappropriate: outcome is not a continuous repeatedly measured variable with normality; follow-up visits produce interval assessments and missingness.

- If event times are interval-censored (only known to occur between visits): use models for interval-censored data; or, more simply, use discrete-time logistic models.

7. Sensitivity analysis for classification of “healing” cases  

Test / method:  
- Sensitivity analyses reclassifying “healing” as:  
  - (a) success (primary definition),  
  - (b) failure (exploratory), and  
  - (c) excluded from analysis.  
- For each definition, recompute success rates, univariable tests and logistic models.  

Purpose:  
- To assess robustness of conclusions to how “healing” is handled.  

Why appropriate (2+ reasons):  
1) Healing (PAI 3–4, reduced lesion) is intermediate; clinically debatable whether it should be labeled as success.  
2) The proportion of healing cases is non-negligible (~24%), so classification choice may influence inferences.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- May be viewed as post hoc and not prespecified in original protocol; can produce multiplicity of analyses.  

When NOT to use:  
- If authors intend to adhere strictly to a predefined outcome definition without exploring alternatives.  

Alternative:  
- Use ordinal models (see point 4) that treat healed, healing, failure separately rather than aggregating.

8. Analysis of change in lesion size and PAI score  

Test / method:  
- Paired analyses for continuous lesion size (mm):  
  - Paired t-test or Wilcoxon signed-rank test for pre vs final lesion size (overall; also within subgroups).  
- For PAI (ordinal):  
  - Wilcoxon signed-rank test for paired ordinal data, or sign test.  

Purpose:  
- To quantify radiographic improvement beyond binary outcome classifications; to show degree of lesion reduction.  

Why appropriate (2+ reasons):  
1) Pre- and post-treatment measures are from the same tooth (paired data).  
2) Lesion size and PAI are primary radiographic markers of healing.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Post-treatment lesion size is not recorded numerically in the article; may require measurement from stored images.  

When NOT to use:  
- If numeric lesion size or raw PAI scores are unavailable or unreliable.  

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- For non-normal changes: Wilcoxon signed-rank instead of paired t-test.  
- For PAI: use non-parametric trend tests or transition matrices (present % of teeth moving from PAI 5→3 etc.).

9. Analysis of effect of restoration quality on outcome  

Test / method:  
- Univariable and multivariable logistic regression including restoration type (crown vs composite) and restoration quality at follow-up (adequate vs inadequate).  
- Chi-square/Fisher’s exact for cross-tabulations.  

Purpose:  
- To examine whether the type and quality of final restoration influence long-term retreatment success.  

Why appropriate (2+ reasons):  
1) Coronal seal is a known factor in endodontic prognosis.  
2) Restoration type is clearly defined and recorded; clinically modifiable.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Restoration type may be confounded by initial tooth damage (more severely compromised teeth may receive crowns).  

When NOT to use:  
- If restoration quality data are incomplete or missing for many patients.  

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- If sample size too small for multivariable modeling: use descriptive comparisons and exact tests; interpret cautiously.

10. Handling missing data and attrition  

Method:  
- Compare baseline characteristics of patients included (n=103) vs not retained (n=17) using chi-square/Fisher’s exact and t-test/Mann–Whitney.  
- If differences appear substantial and missingness is likely not completely at random, consider multiple imputation (MI) for missing outcomes, although events post-dropout are unknown → in practice, MI is limited here.  

Purpose:  
- To assess potential attrition bias and its impact on estimated success rates and predictor-outcome associations.  

Why appropriate (2+ reasons):  
1) 14% attrition (17/120) may bias results if differential by baseline factors.  
2) Transparency about missingness strengthens validity.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Information on those lost to follow-up may be too limited to support MI; imputation of outcome without any follow-up signal is speculative.  

When NOT to use:  
- Do not perform MI for outcome if no follow-up information; better to perform complete-case analysis plus a conservative best-case/worst-case sensitivity analysis.  

Alternative:  
- Present sensitivity bounds: assume all lost-to-follow-up are successes, then assume all are failures, and show how rates change.

C. OPTIONAL ANALYSES  

11. Exploratory interaction analyses  

Test / method:  
- Logistic regression including interaction terms (e.g., preoperative lesion size × active exudate; crown × restoration quality).  

Purpose:  
- To explore whether the association between certain predictors and outcome varies across levels of other variables.  

Why appropriate (2+ reasons):  
1) Biological plausibility (e.g., large lesions might have worse prognosis particularly when exudate is present).  
2) May generate hypotheses for future studies.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Very limited power due to small number of failures; high risk of overfitting and spurious findings.  

When NOT to use:  
- In small datasets with low event counts, especially if multiple interactions are tested.  

Alternative:  
- Stratified descriptive analyses without formal interaction tests.

12. Predictive performance and model validation  

Test / method:  
- Internal validation of logistic model via bootstrapping (e.g., 1000 resamples):  
  - Optimism-corrected C-statistic (AUC).  
  - Calibration slope and intercept.  
- Hosmer–Lemeshow test (with caveats) and calibration plot.  

Purpose:  
- To assess discrimination and calibration of predictive models for clinical decision-making.  

Why appropriate (2+ reasons):  
1) Prospective cohort design is well-suited to developing prognostic models.  
2) Internal validation avoids overoptimistic estimates from the same data used to fit the model.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Sample size (103, 12 events) is marginal for reliable prediction model development; bootstrapped estimates may still be unstable.  

When NOT to use:  
- When the primary goal is inference about individual predictors, not prediction; or when event count is insufficient for robust validation.  

Alternative:  
- Present simplified risk stratification based on a few key predictors without formal scoring system; emphasize exploratory nature.

13. Subgroup analyses  

Potential subgroups:  
- Exudate present vs absent.  
- Initial PAI 4 vs 5.  
- Lesion size small (3–5 mm) vs large (>5–10 mm).  
- Restoration: crown vs composite.  

Test / method:  
- Within each subgroup, descriptive success rates; chi-square/Fisher’s exact; simple logistic models if feasible.  

Purpose:  
- To understand whether specific clinical scenarios have notably different outcomes.  

Why appropriate (2+ reasons):  
1) Clinically relevant subpopulations (e.g., with exudate) may have different prognosis.  
2) Helps tailor clinical recommendations.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Multiple subgroup analyses inflate type I error; small sample sizes in subgroups yield unstable estimates.  

When NOT to use:  
- When sample sizes per subgroup are very small (<20) or when there is no strong a priori rationale.  

Alternative:  
- Report subgroup results descriptively, without formal significance testing.

TASK 3 – INAPPROPRIATE TESTS FOR THIS STUDY  

1. Student’s paired t-test comparing independent groups (e.g., exudate vs non-exudate)  

Why inappropriate:  
- Paired t-test is for paired/within-subject comparisons; exudate vs non-exudate groups are independent teeth/patients.

Use instead:  
- Independent samples t-test or Mann–Whitney U.

2. Repeated measures ANOVA for outcome over time  

Why inappropriate:  
- The primary endpoint is the final status after 2–4 years; outcome categories at intermediate visits are not treated as continuous repeated measurements.  
- Data are categorical (healed/healing/failure), not continuous.

Use instead:  
- Categorical longitudinal methods (e.g., generalized estimating equations) if repeated outcome modeling were planned; but not necessary for the primary question.

3. Parametric tests treating PAI scores as continuous without justification (e.g., t-test on mean PAI)  

Why inappropriate:  
- PAI is ordinal (1–5), not interval; the difference between 1 and 2 is not necessarily equal to that between 4 and 5.  
- Using mean PAI and t-tests assumes interval-level measurement.

Use instead:  
- Non-parametric tests (Wilcoxon, Mann–Whitney), or ordinal regression, or contingency-table analyses.

4. Linear regression with binary outcome (success/failure) as dependent variable  

Why inappropriate:  
- Linear regression assumes continuous, normally distributed residuals; binary outcome violates these assumptions, leading to predicted probabilities outside [0,1] and incorrect inferences.

Use instead:  
- Logistic regression (or probit models).

5. Unadjusted multiple comparisons without correction when conducting numerous hypothesis tests  

Why inappropriate:  
- Many univariable tests across many predictors increase risk of type I error; significant findings may be due to chance.

Better approach:  
- Present p-values as exploratory; focus on effect sizes and CIs; limit the number of primary hypotheses.  
- Optionally, consider false discovery rate control if many tests are considered confirmatory.

6. Complex machine learning models (e.g., random forests, neural networks)  

Why inappropriate:  
- Sample size (n=103, 12 failures) is too small for such flexible models; high risk of severe overfitting, non-interpretability, and unstable predictions.

Use instead:  
- Simple logistic regression with a small number of predictors.

7. Clustered data methods (e.g., mixed models with multiple teeth per patient)  

Why inappropriate:  
- The design includes one tooth per patient; there is no clustering at patient level for the primary outcome.

Use instead:  
- Standard single-level analyses as outlined.

TASK 4 – SUMMARY / JUSTIFICATION OF ANALYTICAL APPROACH  

- The study is a single-arm prospective cohort with a binary (success/failure) and ordered (healed/healing/failure) outcome measured at 2–4 years.  
- Primary analytic goal: assess which preoperative and intraoperative factors predict treatment outcome.  
- The core approach:  
  - Descriptive statistics to characterize the cohort and outcomes.  
  - Univariable tests to explore crude associations between each predictor and success/failure.  
  - A parsimonious multivariable logistic regression model (or penalized variant) to estimate adjusted effects of key predictors.  
  - Reliability analysis (kappa) for PAI assessments to support validity of the outcome measure.  
- Recommended complementary analyses include sensitivity analyses for handling “healing” cases, assessment of lesion size/PAI change, limited survival analysis to account for variable follow-up time, and exploration of restoration-related effects.  
- Given the small number of failures, emphasis should be on careful modeling with few predictors, transparent reporting of effect sizes and uncertainty, and clear separation of confirmatory vs exploratory analyses. This approach yields statistically coherent, clinically interpretable, and methodologically rigorous insight into the prognostic factors for root canal retreatment in symptomatic mandibular first molars with periapical lesions.
