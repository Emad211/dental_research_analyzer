### TASK 1: INFORMATION EXTRACTION

**Study Characteristics:**
*   **Study Design:** Prospective cohort study (Observational), adhering to STROBE and PRILE 2020 guidelines.
*   **Objective:** To evaluate the influence of various preoperative and intraoperative predictors on the outcome of root canal retreatment in symptomatic mandibular first molars with periapical lesions after a minimum of 2 years.
*   **Population & Sample Size:** 
    *   *Initial assessment:* 420 patients.
    *   *Eligible/Enrolled:* 120 symptomatic mandibular first molars.
    *   *Lost to follow-up:* 17 (2 vertical root fractures, 15 unavailable).
    *   *Final Analyzed Sample:* 103 teeth/patients (85.3% recall rate).
*   **Follow-up Duration:** Minimum 2 years, up to 4 years (evaluated at 3, 6, 9, 12 months, then every 6 months in year 2, then annually).
*   **Dependent Variable (Outcome):** Treatment outcome based on clinical and radiographic (modified PAI) criteria. 
    *   *Original categories:* Healed (PAI 1-2, no symptoms), Healing (PAI 3-4 with reduced size, no symptoms), Nonhealing (PAI 4-5 unchanged/increased, or symptoms).
    *   *Analyzed categories (Dichotomized):* Success (Healed + Healing) vs. Failure (Nonhealing).
*   **Independent Variables (Predictors):**
    *   *Preoperative:* Age, gender, soft tissue tenderness, intraoral swelling, periapical lesion size (3–10 mm), PAI score (4 or 5), initial apical level (short/adequate/overfill), initial filling density (poor/good/unfilled).
    *   *Intraoperative:* Active exudate drainage, final apical level, final filling density, complications (extrusion, broken instrument, perforation, flare-up), restoration type (single crown/composite), number of visits (2, 3, or 4).
*   **Data Collection/Measurements:** Clinical examination (pain, tenderness, swelling, mobility, restoration quality) and radiographic assessment (PAI scoring by two independent, calibrated endodontists; worst score recorded in case of disagreement).

### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY (Essential for a complete analysis)

**1. Cohen’s Kappa (or Weighted Kappa)**
*   **Purpose:** To measure inter-rater reliability between the two independent endodontists for the PAI radiographic scoring.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) Radiographic evaluation using the PAI scale is subjective; proving that the two raters agreed validates the reliability of the primary outcome measure. 
    2) Weighted Kappa specifically accounts for the ordinal nature of the PAI scale (1 to 5), penalizing large disagreements more than small ones.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** If one rater scores exactly the same for every single patient (zero variance), the Kappa calculation will fail or be misleading.
*   **When NOT to use:** Do not use for continuous variables (e.g., exact lesion size in millimeters).
*   **Alternative:** Intraclass Correlation Coefficient (ICC) if the PAI scores are treated as continuous/interval data.

**2. Chi-Square Test of Independence (or Fisher’s Exact Test)**
*   **Purpose:** To evaluate bivariate associations between categorical predictor variables (e.g., gender, presence of exudate, restoration type) and the dichotomized outcome (Success vs. Failure).
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) The primary outcome is categorical (binary). 
    2) The majority of the extracted predictors (swelling, density, apical level) are categorical.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** Chi-square loses statistical power if the sample size in specific sub-categories is very small.
*   **When NOT to use:** When more than 20% of the expected cell counts in the contingency table are less than 5.
*   **Alternative:** Fisher’s Exact Test (mandatory when expected cell counts are < 5, which is highly likely given the small number of failures, $n=12$).

**3. Multiple Logistic Regression**
*   **Purpose:** To identify independent predictors of treatment success while controlling for confounding variables.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) The dependent variable is binary (Success vs. Failure). 
    2) It allows for the simultaneous evaluation of multiple preoperative and intraoperative predictors to see which ones truly drive the outcome.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** With only 12 "Failure" events, the model is at high risk of overfitting. A standard rule of thumb is 10 events per predictor variable; thus, putting all variables into the model simultaneously will cause mathematical instability.
*   **When NOT to use:** When the outcome is continuous, or if there is perfect separation (e.g., if *all* teeth with single crowns succeeded and *all* with composite failed).
*   **Alternative:** Penalized Logistic Regression (Firth's Logistic Regression) to handle the rare events/small sample size of the failure group.

#### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED (Enhances quality and rigor)

**1. Shapiro-Wilk Test**
*   **Purpose:** To assess the normality of continuous variables (e.g., patient age, lesion size in mm).
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) It is highly accurate for sample sizes around $N=100$. 
    2) It dictates whether parametric or non-parametric tests should be used for subsequent continuous data analysis.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It can be overly sensitive to minor deviations from normality, though this is less of an issue at $N=103$.
*   **When NOT to use:** Do not use for categorical or ordinal data (like PAI scores).
*   **Alternative:** Kolmogorov-Smirnov test, or visual inspection using Q-Q plots.

**2. Independent Samples t-test (or Mann-Whitney U test)**
*   **Purpose:** To compare continuous predictors (like age or exact lesion size) between the Success and Failure groups.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) Allows researchers to determine if the mean/median age or lesion size differs significantly between teeth that healed versus those that failed. 
    2) Provides a foundation for deciding whether to include these continuous variables in the logistic regression model.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** If the continuous data is highly skewed and the t-test is used, the results may be invalid.
*   **When NOT to use:** When comparing categorical variables.
*   **Alternative:** Mann-Whitney U test (if the Shapiro-Wilk test shows the data violates the assumption of normality).

**3. Variance Inflation Factor (VIF) Analysis**
*   **Purpose:** To check for multicollinearity among the predictor variables before running the logistic regression.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) Many endodontic variables are correlated (e.g., "number of visits" is directly tied to "presence of exudate"). 
    2) High multicollinearity inflates standard errors and makes regression coefficients unreliable.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It only measures linear relationships between predictors; it does not solve the problem of small sample size.
*   **When NOT to use:** When conducting simple bivariate analyses (it is strictly a pre-regression diagnostic).
*   **Alternative:** Correlation matrices (Spearman/Pearson) to manually check for highly correlated pairs of predictors.

#### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL (Provides supplementary insights)

**1. Kaplan-Meier Survival Analysis & Log-Rank Test**
*   **Purpose:** To analyze the *time* it takes for a tooth to achieve "Healed" status, or the *time* to "Failure".
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) The study has variable follow-up times (2 to 4 years) and multiple recall intervals (3, 6, 9, 12 months, etc.). 
    2) It accounts for censored data (patients who dropped out or haven't failed yet by the end of the study).
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** The exact time of healing or failure is interval-censored (it happens between appointments, not on an exact day), which standard Kaplan-Meier doesn't handle perfectly.
*   **When NOT to use:** If all patients were evaluated at exactly one uniform time point (e.g., exactly at 24 months with no intermediate checks).
*   **Alternative:** Cox Proportional Hazards Regression (if adjusting for multiple covariates over time).

**2. Wilcoxon Signed-Rank Test**
*   **Purpose:** To compare the preoperative PAI scores (4 or 5) with the postoperative PAI scores (1 through 5) within the same patients.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) It utilizes the paired nature of the data (same tooth, before and after). 
    2) It proves that the treatment had a statistically significant effect on reducing lesion size overall, regardless of the binary success/failure cutoff.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** The study's primary aim is predicting *outcome categories*, not just proving that retreatment reduces PAI scores generally.
*   **When NOT to use:** When comparing independent groups (e.g., exudate vs. no exudate).
*   **Alternative:** Paired t-test (only if PAI scores were somehow treated as continuous and normally distributed, which is statistically incorrect for a 5-point ordinal scale).

### TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS

*   **Analysis of Variance (ANOVA) / ANCOVA:** Inappropriate because the primary outcome variable (Success vs. Failure) is binary, not continuous. ANOVA requires a continuous dependent variable.
*   **Multiple Linear Regression:** Inappropriate for the same reason as ANOVA. Predicting a binary outcome with linear regression violates the assumption of linearity and can yield predicted probabilities outside the 0-1 range.
*   **Pearson Correlation Coefficient:** Inappropriate for the main analysis because it measures the linear relationship between two *continuous* variables. Most variables in this study (and the outcome) are categorical or ordinal.
*   **Paired t-test for the main outcome:** Inappropriate because the outcome is categorical. Furthermore, you cannot pair "preoperative predictors" with "postoperative success" in a t-test format.

### TASK 4: SUMMARY

The analytical approach for this prospective cohort study must be anchored in the fact that the primary outcome is a dichotomized categorical variable (Success vs. Failure). Therefore, the core methodology relies on **categorical data analysis**. 

The analysis should begin with descriptive statistics and validation of the radiographic scoring using **Weighted Kappa** to ensure inter-rater reliability. Following this, bivariate screening using **Fisher's Exact Tests** (due to the low number of failures, $n=12$) and **Mann-Whitney U tests** will identify which individual predictors are associated with the outcome. Finally, a carefully constructed **Multiple Logistic Regression** (potentially penalized due to the low event-per-variable ratio) is required to isolate the independent predictors of retreatment success. Optional survival analysis could leverage the longitudinal recall data to provide deeper insights into the *timeline* of healing, adding significant clinical value to the findings.
