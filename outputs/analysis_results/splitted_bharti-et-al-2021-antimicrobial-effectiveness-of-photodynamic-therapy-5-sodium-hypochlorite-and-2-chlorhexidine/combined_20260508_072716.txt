## TASK 2: Statistical Analysis Plan (SAP)

### A. Core analysis framework (endpoints and estimands)
**Primary endpoint (recommended to define explicitly):**
- **Log-reduction in CFU:**  
  \( \Delta = \log_{10}(CFU_{S1}+c) - \log_{10}(CFU_{S2}+c) \) with small constant \(c\) (e.g., 1) to handle zeros.
  - This directly quantifies antimicrobial effect and is typically more statistically well-behaved than raw CFU or percent change.

**Key comparison (primary estimand):**
- Difference in mean (or median) **log-reduction** among **three groups**.

**Secondary endpoints (as described / supportive):**
- **Percent reduction**: \(100 \times (CFU_{S1}-CFU_{S2})/CFU_{S1}\) (requires handling CFU\(_{S1}=0\) and heavy skew)
- **Post-treatment CFU (S2)** adjusted for baseline (S1)

**Analysis populations:**
- **Intention-to-treat (ITT)**: all randomized participants with any microbiological data (preferred)
- **Per-protocol (PP)**: those completing assigned irrigation/PDT and sampling as planned (supportive)

**Missing data:**
- If S2 missing: avoid ad-hoc imputation; prefer **multiple imputation** (if missingness nontrivial) or analyze with mixed models if repeated-measures framework used and missing-at-random is plausible.

## CATEGORY A – MANDATORY tests

### 1) One-way ANOVA on log-reduction (or ANCOVA on S2 with baseline S1)
**Test name:** One-way ANOVA (primary) *or* ANCOVA (preferred if using S2 as outcome)  
**Purpose:** Compare antimicrobial effectiveness across 3 groups.  
**Why appropriate:**
1. **Three independent groups** (parallel RCT) → ANOVA/ANCOVA matches design.
2. Log-transformed CFU change is usually closer to **normality/homoscedasticity** than raw CFU or percent reduction.
3. ANCOVA (S2 ~ group + S1) improves **precision** by adjusting for baseline bacterial load.  
**Why may not be appropriate:**
- CFU outcomes may remain **non-normal** or have **many zeros**, violating assumptions.  
**When NOT to use:**
- Strong non-normality after transformation, extreme outliers/zero-inflation that cannot be reasonably modeled, or major heteroscedasticity not correctable.  
**Alternative if assumptions violated:**
- **Kruskal–Wallis** test on log-reduction (or on rank-transformed change)
- **Generalized linear model** for counts (negative binomial), possibly **zero-inflated**; or **robust regression** / Welch ANOVA if heteroscedasticity only.

### 2) Paired within-group pre–post comparison (S1 vs S2)
**Test name:** Paired t-test on log(CFU+1) (within each group) *or* Wilcoxon signed-rank test  
**Purpose:** Confirm that each disinfection method significantly reduces microbial load.  
**Why appropriate:**
1. S1 and S2 are **paired** measures within the same tooth/patient.
2. Clinically relevant to show **within-group reduction** in addition to between-group differences.  
**Why may not be appropriate:**
- Multiple within-group tests inflate **type I error**; also, significance within groups is not the primary RCT question.  
**When NOT to use:**
- If the primary analysis is a single mixed/ANCOVA model and you want to avoid redundant hypothesis testing; or if multiplicity isn’t controlled.  
**Alternative if assumptions violated:**
- Use **Wilcoxon signed-rank** (for non-normal differences) or a **mixed model** with time×group interaction (single unified analysis).

### 3) Post-hoc pairwise comparisons with multiplicity control
**Test name:** Tukey HSD (after ANOVA) / Bonferroni-Holm pairwise comparisons (after Kruskal–Wallis: Dunn test with adjustment)  
**Purpose:** Identify which specific groups differ (PDT vs NaOCl, PDT vs CHX, NaOCl vs CHX).  
**Why appropriate:**
1. Three-arm trial requires **pairwise** interpretation after global test.
2. Controls **family-wise error** across multiple comparisons.  
**Why may not be appropriate:**
- If the study has a **single pre-specified primary contrast** only (not stated here), full pairwise testing may be unnecessary.  
**When NOT to use:**
- When global test is not significant and strict gatekeeping is used (depending on protocol).  
**Alternative if assumptions violated:**
- **Dunn’s test** (nonparametric) with Holm adjustment; or **GLM contrasts** with robust SEs.

## CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED tests (rigor/quality)

### 4) Linear mixed-effects model (LMM) for repeated measures (time: S1/S2)
**Test name:** LMM with fixed effects for group, time, and **group×time interaction**; random intercept for subject  
**Purpose:** Unified inference on whether reduction over time differs by group (interaction).  
**Why appropriate:**
1. Explicitly models **paired structure** (S1/S2) and tests differential change via **interaction**.
2. Handles **missing S2** more principled than complete-case paired tests (under MAR).
3. Provides estimated marginal means and CIs for changes.  
**Why may not be appropriate:**
- With only two time points, ANCOVA/change-score models may be simpler; also LMM assumes approximate normal residuals on the chosen scale.  
**When NOT to use:**
- When outcome distribution is severely non-normal even after transformation and count models are more suitable.  
**Alternative if assumptions violated:**
- **Generalized linear mixed model (GLMM)** with negative binomial distribution for CFU counts; or nonparametric methods on change scores.

### 5) Assumption checks (diagnostics) and variance-robust options
**Tests/Procedures:** Shapiro–Wilk (residuals), Levene/Brown–Forsythe (homogeneity), influence/outlier diagnostics  
**Purpose:** Verify suitability of parametric inference and guide model choice.  
**Why appropriate:**
1. CFU data often violate assumptions; diagnostics prevent invalid p-values/CIs.
2. Supports transparent reporting and justifies transformations/robust methods.  
**Why may not be appropriate:**
- In moderate samples, normality tests can be underpowered/overly sensitive; focus should be on residual plots.  
**When NOT to use:**
- Do not use normality tests alone to decide; avoid mechanistic “p>0.05 means normal.”  
**Alternative if assumptions violated:**
- **Welch ANOVA**, robust SEs (HC3), transformation, or switch to **rank/GLM** approach.

### 6) Effect size estimation with confidence intervals
**Test/Metric:** Mean difference in log-reduction (and back-transformed ratios), partial eta-squared (ANOVA), or standardized effect (Hedges g)  
**Purpose:** Quantify magnitude of antimicrobial differences beyond p-values.  
**Why appropriate:**
1. Aligns with clinical relevance (e.g., fold-reduction).
2. Enables comparison with prior studies and supports meta-analysis.  
**Why may not be appropriate:**
- Standardized effects (g) can be hard to interpret clinically for skewed counts.  
**When NOT to use:**
- Avoid overemphasis on standardized measures if clinical scale is clearer.  
**Alternative if assumptions violated:**
- **Cliff’s delta** (nonparametric effect size) with bootstrap CIs.

### 7) Baseline comparability across randomized groups (descriptive + limited testing)
**Test name:** ANOVA/Kruskal–Wallis for age; chi-square/Fisher for sex; and importantly baseline **S1 CFU** comparisons  
**Purpose:** Describe and assess potential imbalance.  
**Why appropriate:**
1. Ensures transparency about baseline equivalence and potential confounding.
2. Baseline microbial load imbalance can affect S2 and percent reduction; informs ANCOVA.  
**Why may not be appropriate:**
- Significance testing of baseline variables is often discouraged; randomization already addresses expectation of balance.  
**When NOT to use:**
- Do not use baseline p-values to decide whether to adjust; pre-specify adjustment (at least for S1).  
**Alternative if assumptions violated:**
- Use **standardized mean differences (SMDs)** instead of p-values.

## CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL analyses (supplementary insights)

### 8) Responder / eradication analysis (binary)
**Test name:** Chi-square or Fisher’s exact test; logistic regression (adjusted)  
**Endpoint:** e.g., **culture negative at S2** (CFU=0) or achieving ≥X log-reduction  
**Purpose:** Provide clinically intuitive success rates.  
**Why appropriate:**
1. Binary outcomes are easy to interpret clinically.
2. Addresses zero-inflation and highlights eradication.  
**Why may not be appropriate:**
- Dichotomization loses information and depends on threshold; culture methods have detection limits.  
**When NOT to use:**
- As the sole primary endpoint unless pre-specified and powered for it.  
**Alternative if assumptions violated:**
- Exact methods (Fisher), or use **Poisson/negative binomial** models without dichotomizing.

### 9) Negative binomial regression (count model) for S2 with offset/baseline adjustment
**Test name:** Negative binomial GLM (or zero-inflated NB)  
**Purpose:** Model CFU counts directly while handling overdispersion.  
**Why appropriate:**
1. CFU are **counts**; NB handles **overdispersion** common in microbiology.
2. Can incorporate baseline S1 as covariate and estimate **rate ratios**.  
**Why may not be appropriate:**
- With small sample size per group (18), complex models (especially zero-inflated) may be unstable.  
**When NOT to use:**
- When convergence issues arise or too many parameters relative to sample size.  
**Alternative if assumptions violated:**
- Simpler log-transform + ANCOVA; or **quasi-Poisson** with robust SEs.

### 10) Sensitivity analyses for transformation constant and handling zeros
**Method:** Repeat primary analysis with c=0.5 vs 1, or using log10(CFU+1) vs ln(CFU+1)  
**Purpose:** Check robustness of conclusions.  
**Why appropriate:**
1. CFU zeros materially affect log-based analyses.
2. Demonstrates stability of inference.  
**Why may not be appropriate:**
- May be seen as multiplicity if over-interpreted; keep as sensitivity, not fishing.  
**When NOT to use:**
- If protocol pre-specified a single approach and journal space is limited.  
**Alternative:**
- Use count models that naturally handle zeros.

## TASK 3: Inappropriate tests (should NOT be used)

1. **Independent (unpaired) t-test for S1 vs S2 within a group**  
   - Wrong because S1 and S2 are **paired** within the same tooth/patient.

2. **Multiple separate t-tests for all pairwise group comparisons without adjustment**  
   - Inflates **type I error** (multiplicity problem) in a 3-arm trial.

3. **Chi-square test on raw CFU counts**  
   - CFU is not categorical; chi-square would be a misuse unless CFU is meaningfully categorized (and even then information loss occurs).

4. **Correlation (Pearson) between treatment group and CFU reduction**  
   - Treatment group is categorical; correlation is not an appropriate primary inferential tool for group comparisons.

5. **Simple “percent reduction” ANOVA without addressing non-normality/undefined values**  
   - Percent reduction is often highly skewed and problematic when CFU\(_{S1}\)=0 or very small; would risk invalid inference unless carefully handled.
