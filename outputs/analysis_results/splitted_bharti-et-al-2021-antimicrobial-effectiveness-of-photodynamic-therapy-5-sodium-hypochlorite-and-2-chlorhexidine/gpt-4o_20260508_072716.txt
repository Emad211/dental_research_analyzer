### TASK 1: INFORMATION EXTRACTION

#### Study Characteristics
1. **Study Design**: Triple-blind randomized clinical trial (RCT).
2. **Objective**: To compare the antimicrobial effectiveness of photodynamic therapy (PDT), 5% sodium hypochlorite (NaOCl), and 2% chlorhexidine gluconate (CHO) in disinfecting previously root canal-treated teeth with apical periodontitis.
3. **Null Hypothesis**: There is no difference in microbial elimination between PDT, NaOCl, and CHO.
4. **Groups**:
   - **Group 1**: 5% sodium hypochlorite (NaOCl) disinfection.
   - **Group 2**: 2% chlorhexidine gluconate (CHO) disinfection.
   - **Group 3**: Photodynamic therapy (PDT) disinfection.
5. **Sample Size**: 
   - Total: 54 patients (18 per group).
   - Power = 80%, Confidence Interval = 95%.
6. **Population**:
   - Inclusion: Patients with previously root canal-treated teeth with apical periodontitis (periapical index ≥ 3), intact coronal restorations, no gingival recession, and no periodontal pocket > 4 mm.
   - Exclusion: Patients with immunocompromising conditions, diabetes mellitus, chronic infections, rheumatoid arthritis, immunosuppressive therapy, pregnancy, or nursing.
7. **Randomization**: Block randomization.
8. **Blinding**: Triple-blind (patients, microbiological examiners, and data analysts were blinded).
9. **Variables**:
   - **Independent Variable**: Disinfection technique (PDT, NaOCl, CHO).
   - **Dependent Variable**: Reduction in microbial load (measured as colony-forming units [CFU]).
10. **Measurements**:
    - Microbiological samples (S1 and S2) collected using sterile paper points.
    - Reduction in CFU after disinfection.
11. **Data Collection Methods**:
    - Pre-treatment microbial sample (S1) collected before disinfection.
    - Post-treatment microbial sample (S2) collected after disinfection.
    - Microbial samples incubated at 37°C for 48 hours, and CFU counted using light microscopy (400× magnification).
12. **Statistical Software**: OpenEpi used for sample size calculation.

### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS
1. **Test Name**: Paired t-test (or Wilcoxon signed-rank test if data is non-parametric).
   - **Purpose**: To compare pre- and post-treatment microbial counts (S1 vs. S2) within each group.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. The study design involves comparing two related samples (S1 and S2) for each group.
     2. The outcome variable (CFU reduction) is continuous.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Assumes normality of the differences between S1 and S2.
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**: If the data is not normally distributed.
   - **Alternative if Assumptions Are Violated**: Wilcoxon signed-rank test (non-parametric).

2. **Test Name**: One-way ANOVA (or Kruskal-Wallis test if data is non-parametric).
   - **Purpose**: To compare the mean microbial reduction (CFU) across the three groups (PDT, NaOCl, CHO).
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. The study involves comparing three independent groups.
     2. The outcome variable (CFU reduction) is continuous.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Assumes homogeneity of variances and normality.
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**: If data is not normally distributed or variances are unequal.
   - **Alternative if Assumptions Are Violated**: Kruskal-Wallis test (non-parametric).

3. **Test Name**: Post-hoc tests (e.g., Tukey’s HSD or Dunn’s test).
   - **Purpose**: To identify which pairs of groups differ significantly after ANOVA or Kruskal-Wallis test.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Post-hoc tests are necessary to determine specific group differences.
     2. They control for Type I error in multiple comparisons.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Requires a significant result from ANOVA or Kruskal-Wallis test.
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**: If the overall test (ANOVA or Kruskal-Wallis) is not significant.
   - **Alternative if Assumptions Are Violated**: Non-parametric pairwise comparisons (e.g., Dunn’s test).

#### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED TESTS
1. **Test Name**: Chi-square test of independence (or Fisher’s exact test for small sample sizes).
   - **Purpose**: To compare categorical outcomes (e.g., proportion of patients with complete microbial eradication) across groups.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. The test is suitable for categorical data.
     2. It allows comparison of proportions between groups.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Requires a sufficient sample size for each category.
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**: If expected cell counts are less than 5.
   - **Alternative if Assumptions Are Violated**: Fisher’s exact test.

2. **Test Name**: Repeated measures ANOVA (or Friedman test if data is non-parametric).
   - **Purpose**: To analyze the change in microbial counts (S1 vs. S2) across all three groups simultaneously.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Accounts for within-subject variability.
     2. Suitable for comparing repeated measures across multiple groups.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Assumes normality and sphericity.
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**: If data is not normally distributed or sphericity is violated.
   - **Alternative if Assumptions Are Violated**: Friedman test (non-parametric).

     1. Provides additional context to statistical significance.

   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Does not test for statistical significance.
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**: If the primary focus is hypothesis testing.
   - **Alternative if Assumptions Are Violated**: Non-parametric effect size measures.

2. **Test Name**: Logistic regression.
   - **Purpose**: To model the probability of complete microbial eradication as a function of treatment group.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Suitable for binary outcomes (e.g., eradication: yes/no).
     2. Adjusts for potential confounders.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Requires a larger sample size for reliable estimates.
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**: If the sample size is insufficient.
   - **Alternative if Assumptions Are Violated**: Exact logistic regression.
