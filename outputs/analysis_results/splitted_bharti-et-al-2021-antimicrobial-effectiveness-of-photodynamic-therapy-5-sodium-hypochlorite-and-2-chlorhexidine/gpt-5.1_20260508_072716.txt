CATEGORY A – MANDATORY ANALYSES  
--------------------------------  

A1. Descriptive statistics and baseline comparability  
-----------------------------------------------------  

**Test / Method:**  
- Descriptive statistics + baseline comparability checks:  
  - Continuous variables (age, baseline CFU_S1): means ± SD or medians (IQR);  
  - Categorical variables (sex, tooth type, PAI): counts and percentages.  
  - Tests: ANOVA / Kruskal–Wallis for continuous variables, chi-square or Fisher’s exact test for categorical variables.  

**Purpose:**  
- Summarize baseline characteristics and verify that randomization achieved balance across groups.  

**Why appropriate:**  
1. RCTs require demonstration that groups are comparable at baseline to support internal validity.  
2. Age, sex, baseline CFU and tooth characteristics may influence microbial load or treatment effect, so imbalances should be detected and reported.  

**Why it may not be appropriate:**  
- With small n per group (18), formal hypothesis testing for baseline equivalence can be underpowered and misleading; descriptive comparisons may suffice.  

**When it should NOT be used:**  
- For very small subgroups (e.g., tooth subtype with n<5 per group), chi-square is inappropriate (expected counts <5).  

**Alternatives / modifications:**  
- For continuous baseline variables with strong skewness or outliers: use Kruskal–Wallis instead of ANOVA.  
- For sparse categorical data: Fisher’s exact test instead of chi-square.  

---

A2. Within-group change in CFU (paired analysis)  
-----------------------------------------------  

**Test / Method:**  
- Primary within-group change:  
  - For each group, comparison of CFU_S1 vs CFU_S2 using:  
    - Paired t-test if log-transformed CFU approximates normality; or  
    - Wilcoxon signed-rank test on raw CFU or log-transformed values.  
  - Alternatively, analysis on percent reduction within group vs 0 using one-sample t-test or one-sample Wilcoxon signed-rank.  

**Purpose:**  
- Demonstrate that each disinfection method significantly reduces bacterial load from baseline.  

**Why appropriate:**  
1. Two repeated measures (S1, S2) per subject; paired tests are specifically designed for dependent samples.  
2. CFU (after suitable transformation) or percent reduction is continuous, allowing powerful parametric methods if assumptions are met.  

**Why it may not be appropriate:**  
- CFU data often highly skewed, with zero truncation at S2; parametric paired t-test on untransformed CFU may violate normality and homoscedasticity assumptions.  

**When not to use:**  
- Do not use paired t-test on raw CFU if distributions remain markedly non-normal after log transformation or if there are heavy ties / many zeros.  

**Alternatives if assumptions violated:**  
- Wilcoxon signed-rank test.  
- For count data modeling, use a within-group Poisson or negative binomial mixed model (see A4).

A3. Between-group comparison of antimicrobial effect (primary endpoint)  
----------------------------------------------------------------------  

The key endpoint should be defined clearly before analysis:

- **Preferred primary endpoint:** Percent reduction in CFU between S1 and S2 for each tooth.  
- Alternative endpoints:  
  - Log-transformed CFU_S2 with baseline CFU_S1 as covariate (ANCOVA).  
  - Absolute reduction CFU (S1 − S2).  

**Test / Method option 1 (continuous outcome):**  
- One-way ANOVA comparing mean percent reduction across three groups.  
- If baseline CFU differs by group, use ANCOVA with:  
  - Outcome: Percent reduction or log(CFU_S2 + 1).  
  - Covariate: Baseline log(CFU_S1 + 1).  
  - Factor: Group (3 levels).  

**Purpose:**  
- Provide overall comparison of antimicrobial effectiveness across the three interventions.  
- Adjust for baseline bacterial load if needed.  

**Why appropriate:**  
1. Randomized parallel-group design with a continuous outcome allows use of ANOVA/ANCOVA, which is standard for comparing means across >2 groups.  
2. Percent reduction or log CFU often approximates normal distributions, especially after transformation; ANCOVA improves precision by adjusting for baseline differences.  

**Why it may not be appropriate:**  
- Percent reduction is a proportion (0–100%) and can be severely skewed, especially if many values approach 100% (ceiling effect). Normality and equal variance may be violated.  

**When not to use:**  
- If residuals from ANOVA/ANCOVA show gross non-normality, heteroscedasticity, or heavy outliers even after transformations.  
- If group sample sizes differ markedly due to dropouts (not apparent here, but possible).  

**Alternatives if assumptions violated:**  
- Kruskal–Wallis test comparing distributions of percent reduction across groups (non-parametric analog).  
- For binary outcome (bacteria present vs absent at S2), use chi-square or Fisher’s exact test (see A4).  
- For count data, use generalized linear models (GLM) with Poisson or negative binomial distributions and log link (A4).  

---

A4. Between-group comparison using count data models (CFU-based, preferred if raw CFU are used)  
----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------  

**Test / Method:**  
- Generalized linear model (GLM) with:  
  - Outcome: CFU_S2 (count).  
  - Distribution: Poisson or negative binomial (if overdispersion).  
  - Link: Log.  
  - Predictors: Group (3 levels), baseline CFU_S1 (as covariate, maybe log-transformed).  
- Or generalized linear mixed model (GLMM) for S1 and S2 jointly:  
  - Outcome: CFU (S1 & S2 repeated within subject).  
  - Fixed effects: Group, Time (S1 vs S2), Group × Time interaction.  
  - Random effect: Subject ID.  

**Purpose:**  
- Direct modeling of count data respecting their discrete, skewed nature.  
- Assess interaction between time and group (is reduction over time different between treatments?).  

**Why appropriate:**  
1. CFU are counts with possible overdispersion; GLM/GLMM with appropriate distributions accurately model such data without requiring normality.  
2. The Group × Time interaction in a GLMM is a natural formal test of differential effectiveness of treatments.  

**Why it may not be appropriate:**  
- Sample size (18 per group, 54 total) may be modest for stable estimation of more complex GLMMs, especially with overdispersion and random effects.  
- Implementation complexity is higher, and some clinical investigators may find results harder to interpret than simple percent reduction or ANOVA.  

**When not to use:**  
- If CFU counts are not reliably measured (e.g., strong heaping, truncated at high ranges) or if many S2 values are zero leading to convergence issues in Poisson models.  

**Alternatives if assumptions violated:**  
- Negative binomial instead of Poisson if overdispersion.  
- Zero-inflated Poisson/negative binomial if many zeros at S2 and model fit is poor.  
- If modeling proves unstable, revert to transformed continuous analyses (ANOVA/ANCOVA) or non-parametric tests.

A5. Between-group comparison of “sterilization success” (binary outcome)  
-----------------------------------------------------------------------  

**Test / Method:**  
- Define sterilization success as CFU_S2 = 0 (no culturable bacteria) vs CFU_S2 > 0.  
- Compare proportions of success across groups using:  
  - Chi-square test of independence.  
  - Fisher’s exact test if any expected cell count <5.  

**Purpose:**  
- Provide a clinically interpretable binary outcome (complete elimination of culturable bacteria).  
- Compare success rates of PDT vs NaOCl vs CHO.  

**Why appropriate:**  
1. Important clinical interpretation: percent of cases with complete disinfection.  
2. With 18 per group, chi-square/Fisher comparisons across 3×2 table are feasible.  

**Why it may not be appropriate:**  
- Loss of information: reduces continuous CFU data to binary, reducing statistical power.  
- If bacterial culture detection limit is not fully reliable, classification may misrepresent slight residual colonization.  

**When not to use:**  
- If only a very small number of cases achieve CFU_S2 = 0 or if nearly all do; extreme imbalance will make chi-square invalid or uninformative.  

**Alternatives if assumptions violated:**  
- Exact multinomial tests or exact logistic regression.  
- Logistic regression with Group as predictor and adjusting for baseline CFU_S1.

B4. Multiple comparison adjustments  
-----------------------------------  

**Method:**  
- If ANOVA/ANCOVA shows a significant overall group effect, perform post hoc pairwise comparisons:  
  - Methods: Tukey’s HSD, Bonferroni, or Holm adjustment.  

**Purpose:**  
- Identify which pairs of treatments differ while controlling family-wise error rate.  

**Why appropriate:**  
1. Three arms means 3 pairwise comparisons; adjustment is needed to avoid inflated type I error.  
2. Supports more precise interpretation: e.g., is PDT significantly better than both CHO and NaOCl, or only one?  

**Why it may not be appropriate:**  
- With small sample size, adjusted tests may be underpowered and fail to detect clinically relevant differences.  

**When not to use:**  
- If the primary plan pre-specifies only a single primary contrast (e.g., PDT vs NaOCl) and others are exploratory; in that case, adjust only for planned comparisons or clearly label exploratory analyses.  

**Alternatives if assumptions violated:**  
- Non-parametric post hoc tests with Bonferroni adjustment (e.g., pairwise Mann–Whitney tests).

TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS (FOR THIS STUDY)  
============================================  

Below are tests/methods that should NOT be used or would generally be inappropriate for the data and design:

1. Independent-samples t-test for within-group S1 vs S2 comparisons  
-------------------------------------------------------------------  
- Reason: S1 and S2 are paired measurements on the same tooth; independence assumption violated.  
- Using independent t-test would ignore pairing, reducing power and biasing SEs.

2. Simple unpaired analysis of CFU_S2 without accounting for baseline CFU differences  
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------  
- Reason: Baseline CFU_S1 may differ across groups by chance; analyzing only CFU_S2 ignoring baseline can bias comparisons or reduce power.  
- Prefer percent change, ANCOVA, or GLMM with baseline.

3. Repeated-measures ANOVA treating each group’s S1 and S2 as independent repetitions  
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------  
- If implemented inappropriately without correct specification of the within-subject factor, it misrepresents the 2-level repeated measure and the parallel-group design.  
- Proper approach would be mixed models or repeated-measures ANOVA with Time as within-subject factor and Group as between-subject factor; anything else is incorrect.

4. Parametric ANOVA on raw (untransformed) CFU counts without checking assumptions  
-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------  
- CFU counts are typically heavily skewed; ANOVA on raw CFU is rarely appropriate.  
- This can violate normality and homogeneity of variance assumptions, yielding invalid p-values.

5. Non-paired non-parametric tests for paired data (e.g., Mann–Whitney for S1 vs S2 in same group)  
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------  
- S1 and S2 are paired; non-paired tests waste information and are incorrect for repeated measures.  
- Appropriate paired non-parametric test is Wilcoxon signed-rank.

6. Correlation coefficients (e.g., Pearson) to compare groups  
-------------------------------------------------------------  
- Pearson’s correlation is not designed to compare treatment groups; it assesses linear association between two continuous variables.  
- Not relevant as primary tool here.

7. Survival analysis (Kaplan–Meier, Cox regression)  
---------------------------------------------------  
- No time-to-event outcome; this is a single-session disinfection trial with immediate post-treatment CFU measures.  
- Survival methods are not applicable.

8. Multiple linear regression with a large number of covariates (e.g., 5–6+) on n=54  
------------------------------------------------------------------------------------  
- Overparameterized model would be unstable and overfitted, especially if using binary outcomes; violates the events-per-variable rules of thumb.  

9. Using p-values for baseline variables as proof of randomization success  
-------------------------------------------------------------------------  
- Formal baseline hypothesis tests are discouraged; randomization ensures comparability in expectation, not necessarily equal observed means; interpretation can be misleading.
