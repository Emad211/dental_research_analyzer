## A1) Chi-square test of independence (or Fisher’s exact)
- **Purpose:** Compare **IANB success proportion** between randomized groups.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Primary endpoint is a **binary success/failure rate**.
  2. RCT with **independent groups** → direct comparison of proportions is core.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - If **expected cell counts are small** (e.g., <5), chi-square approximation may be inaccurate.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Use of chi-square specifically should be avoided when sparse data lead to small expected counts.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Fisher’s exact test** (exact p-value) for sparse tables.
  - Report **RR/RD** with exact or Newcombe CIs.

## A2) Effect size estimation for binary outcome (RR/RD/OR with 95% CI)
- **Purpose:** Quantify treatment effect magnitude beyond p-values.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. RCT interpretation requires **clinical effect size** (not just significance).
  2. Binary outcomes are naturally expressed as **RR** or **RD** (often more interpretable than OR).
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - OR can be misleading when outcome is common; RD may vary by baseline risk.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Avoid interpreting **OR as RR** when success is common.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Use **log-binomial regression** (RR) or **Poisson with robust SE** (RR) if direct RR estimation has convergence issues.

## A3) Baseline comparability checks (descriptive + standardized differences; no hypothesis testing preferred)
- **Purpose:** Assess balance in baseline covariates (e.g., baseline VAS, age, sex if recorded).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Confirms randomization likely achieved balance.
  2. Identifies covariates for potential **precision adjustment**.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Formal hypothesis tests on baseline variables can be misleading (random imbalances can be “significant”).
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Do not use baseline p-values to decide “whether randomization worked.”
- **Alternative:**
  - **Standardized mean differences (SMDs)**; graphical balance plots.
