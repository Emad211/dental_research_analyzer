## TASK 2: Statistical Analysis Plan (SAP)

### Overview of analysis principles
- **Analysis population (mandatory):**
  - **Intention-to-treat (ITT):** analyze all randomized participants in assigned group.
  - **Per-protocol (PP) (recommended):** exclude major protocol deviations (e.g., took analgesic <12h, wrong medication taken), used as sensitivity.
- **Descriptive statistics:**
  - Continuous (e.g., baseline VAS): mean (SD) if ~normal; median (IQR) if skewed.
  - Categorical: counts and percentages.
- **Alpha:** 0.05 (two-sided).
- **Effect sizes:** emphasize **risk difference (RD)**, **risk ratio (RR)**, and/or **odds ratio (OR)** with **95% CI** for binary outcomes.
- **Missing data:** document extent/reasons; for binary primary outcome consider **multiple imputation** or **best/worst case** sensitivity if missingness is non-trivial; if minimal, complete-case.

# CATEGORY A — MANDATORY tests

## A1) Chi-square test of independence (or Fisher’s exact)
- **Purpose:** Compare **IANB success proportion** between randomized groups.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Primary endpoint is a **binary success/failure rate**.
  2. RCT with **independent groups** → direct comparison of proportions is core.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - If **expected cell counts are small** (e.g., <5), chi-square approximation may be inaccurate.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Use of chi-square specifically should be avoided when sparse data lead to small expected counts.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Fisher’s exact test** (exact p-value) for sparse tables.
  - Report **RR/RD** with exact or Newcombe CIs.

## A2) Effect size estimation for binary outcome (RR/RD/OR with 95% CI)
- **Purpose:** Quantify treatment effect magnitude beyond p-values.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. RCT interpretation requires **clinical effect size** (not just significance).
  2. Binary outcomes are naturally expressed as **RR** or **RD** (often more interpretable than OR).
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - OR can be misleading when outcome is common; RD may vary by baseline risk.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Avoid interpreting **OR as RR** when success is common.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Use **log-binomial regression** (RR) or **Poisson with robust SE** (RR) if direct RR estimation has convergence issues.

## A3) Baseline comparability checks (descriptive + standardized differences; no hypothesis testing preferred)
- **Purpose:** Assess balance in baseline covariates (e.g., baseline VAS, age, sex if recorded).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Confirms randomization likely achieved balance.
  2. Identifies covariates for potential **precision adjustment**.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Formal hypothesis tests on baseline variables can be misleading (random imbalances can be “significant”).
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Do not use baseline p-values to decide “whether randomization worked.”
- **Alternative:**
  - **Standardized mean differences (SMDs)**; graphical balance plots.

# CATEGORY B — RECOMMENDED tests

## B1) Multivariable logistic regression (adjusted analysis of IANB success)
- **Purpose:** Estimate **adjusted OR** (and optionally adjusted RD/RR) for success, controlling for baseline predictors (e.g., baseline pain severity on VAS; other available covariates).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Outcome is **binary**; logistic model is standard.
  2. Adjustment can **increase precision** and handle chance imbalances.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - With small samples/few events, model may be unstable/overfit.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If events per parameter are too low (rule-of-thumb: ~10 events/parameter; use caution).
- **Alternatives if assumptions/limitations arise:**
  - **Penalized logistic regression** (Firth correction) for separation/small-sample bias.
  - **Poisson regression with robust SE** to estimate adjusted RR.

## B2) Comparison of baseline pain (VAS) between groups: t-test or Mann–Whitney U
- **Purpose:** Describe/confirm similarity of **baseline pain intensity**.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Baseline VAS is continuous; two-group comparison is straightforward.
  2. Helps contextualize results and guides covariate adjustment strategy.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - VAS distributions can be skewed or bounded; t-test may be sensitive to non-normality with small n.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Don’t rely on baseline p-values for inclusion/exclusion of covariates.
- **Alternatives if assumptions violated:**
  - If non-normal/outliers: **Mann–Whitney U** and report median (IQR).
  - Consider **robust t-test** (e.g., Welch) if variances differ.

## B3) Interim/assumption checks for modeling: goodness-of-fit + influential points (logistic model)
- **Purpose:** Validate adequacy of regression model.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Ensures conclusions are not driven by model misfit.
  2. Identifies separation/influential observations in small RCTs.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Some GOF tests (e.g., Hosmer–Lemeshow) have low power in small samples.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Do not over-interpret non-significant GOF as “model is correct.”
- **Alternatives:**
  - Use **calibration plots**, **Brier score**, and **penalized methods** if unstable.

## B4) Sensitivity analysis: Per-protocol (PP) vs ITT
- **Purpose:** Assess robustness of findings to adherence/protocol deviations.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. ITT is primary for unbiased causal inference; PP may show efficacy under adherence.
  2. Helps interpret inconsistencies due to noncompliance.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - PP can be biased if nonadherence relates to prognosis.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Do not replace ITT with PP as the main inference.
- **Alternatives:**
  - **Complier average causal effect (CACE)** methods if adherence data are strong (optional/advanced).

# CATEGORY C — OPTIONAL tests (supplementary insights)

## C1) Subgroup analysis with interaction term (e.g., baseline pain severity strata)
- **Purpose:** Explore whether treatment effect differs by baseline pain level (e.g., moderate vs severe within 55–170 mm range).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Biological plausibility: higher inflammation/pain may reduce block success.
  2. Interaction modeling is preferable to separate within-subgroup tests.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Underpowered; high false-positive risk if many subgroups.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Avoid post-hoc subgroup fishing; avoid multiple unplanned interactions.
- **Alternatives:**
  - Pre-specify limited subgroups; adjust for multiplicity or treat as exploratory.

## C2) Number needed to treat (NNT) with CI
- **Purpose:** Translate RD into clinically interpretable metric: patients needed to premedicate for one additional successful IANB.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Enhances clinical interpretability.
  2. Directly derived from RD.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - NNT unstable when RD near 0; CI can be wide or cross infinity.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When RD not meaningfully estimated or CI includes 0 extensively.
- **Alternatives:**
  - Report **RD and CI** only.

## C3) Bayesian analysis of success probability (optional)
- **Purpose:** Provide posterior probability that combination improves success by clinically meaningful margin.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Small-to-moderate samples: Bayesian estimation can be more informative than dichotomous p-values.
  2. Can incorporate skeptical/neutral priors based on prior RCTs.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Requires priors; results may be sensitive and less familiar to readership.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If priors cannot be justified or transparency cannot be ensured.
- **Alternatives:**
  - Frequentist CI-based inference (primary).

## TASK 3: Tests that should NOT be used (and why)

1. **Paired t-test / Wilcoxon signed-rank test**
   - **Why not:** Groups are randomized **independent samples**; not paired/repeated within-subject (per excerpt).
2. **One-way ANOVA (for primary endpoint)**
   - **Why not:** Primary outcome is **binary**; ANOVA assumes continuous normally distributed residuals.
3. **Pearson correlation (as primary analysis)**
   - **Why not:** Does not compare randomized groups or estimate causal treatment effect; also unsuitable for binary primary endpoint.
4. **Multiple unadjusted subgroup-specific chi-square tests**
   - **Why not:** Inflates type I error; interaction approach is superior.
5. **Stepwise regression solely based on p-values**
   - **Why not:** Unstable in small datasets; increases bias and false discoveries; covariate choice should be pre-specified.
