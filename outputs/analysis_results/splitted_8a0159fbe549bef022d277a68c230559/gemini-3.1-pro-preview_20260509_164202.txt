### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS
*(Tests without which the analysis would be incomplete)*

**1. Chi-Square Test of Independence (or Fisher’s Exact Test)**
*   **Purpose:** To compare the primary outcome (proportion of IANB success vs. failure) between the study groups, and to compare baseline categorical variables (e.g., Sex distribution).
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) The primary outcome (anesthetic success) is a categorical, binary variable. 
    2) The study compares independent groups (randomized arms).
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It does not account for potential confounding variables (e.g., differences in baseline pain) that might influence the outcome.
*   **When NOT to use:** When expected cell counts in the contingency table are less than 5.
*   **Alternative:** Fisher’s Exact Test (if expected cell counts are < 5).

**2. Shapiro-Wilk Test**
*   **Purpose:** To assess the normality of continuous data (e.g., Age, baseline VAS scores).
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) It is highly sensitive and accurate for sample sizes under 100 (N=94). 
    2) It dictates whether parametric or non-parametric tests should be used for continuous variables.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It can be overly sensitive to minor deviations from normality, though this is less of an issue at N=94 than in massive datasets.
*   **When NOT to use:** When the data is strictly categorical or binary.
*   **Alternative:** Kolmogorov-Smirnov test, or visual inspection of Q-Q plots.

**3. Independent Samples t-test OR Mann-Whitney U test**
*   **Purpose:** To compare baseline continuous variables (Age, initial VAS pain scores) between groups to ensure randomization was successful. *(Note: If >2 groups, use One-Way ANOVA or Kruskal-Wallis).*
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) Essential to prove baseline equivalence, which validates the RCT design. 
    2) Directly compares the central tendency of continuous variables across independent groups.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** If the data is highly skewed (common with pain VAS scores), the mean (used in t-tests) is not a reliable measure of central tendency.
*   **When NOT to use:** Do not use the t-test if the data violates the assumption of normality (Shapiro-Wilk $p < 0.05$) or homogeneity of variance.
*   **Alternative:** Mann-Whitney U test (for non-normal data) or Welch’s t-test (if variances are unequal).

#### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED TESTS
*(Tests that enhance the quality and rigor of the analysis)*

**4. Binary Logistic Regression**
*   **Purpose:** To evaluate the effect of the premedication on IANB success while controlling for baseline covariates (e.g., initial pain score, age, tooth type).
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) Baseline pain severity is a known strong predictor of IANB failure; regression adjusts for any accidental baseline imbalances. 
    2) It allows for the calculation of Adjusted Odds Ratios (aOR).
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** If the sample size is too small relative to the number of predictors, the model may overfit.
*   **When NOT to use:** When the dependent variable is continuous, or if there is perfect separation in the data (e.g., one group has 100% success).
*   **Alternative:** Mantel-Haenszel test (for stratified categorical analysis).

**5. Relative Risk (RR) and 95% Confidence Intervals**
*   **Purpose:** To quantify the clinical magnitude of the treatment effect.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) Provides a highly interpretable metric for clinicians (e.g., "Patients taking the combo are 2 times more likely to achieve profound anesthesia"). 
    2) Confidence intervals provide a measure of precision for the estimate.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** RR can sometimes be misinterpreted by laypersons compared to absolute risk reduction.
*   **When NOT to use:** In case-control studies (where Odds Ratios must be used instead).
*   **Alternative:** Odds Ratio (OR) or Absolute Risk Reduction (ARR).

#### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL TESTS
*(Tests that provide supplementary insights)*

**6. Number Needed to Treat (NNT)**
*   **Purpose:** To determine how many patients need to receive the ibuprofen/dexamethasone combination to achieve one additional successful IANB compared to the control.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) It is the ultimate metric for clinical decision-making and cost-benefit analysis. 
    2) Easily derived from the Absolute Risk Reduction.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It does not provide statistical significance, only clinical relevance.
*   **When NOT to use:** When the primary outcome result is not statistically significant (NNT is meaningless if the difference is due to chance).
*   **Alternative:** Number Needed to Harm (NNH) if evaluating adverse effects.

**7. Kaplan-Meier Analysis / Log-Rank Test**
*   **Purpose:** If the researchers recorded the *time to onset* of pulpal anesthesia (via EPT/cold test at intervals), this tests the speed of anesthesia onset between groups.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) Time-to-event analysis handles censored data (patients who never achieve anesthesia). 
    2) Provides a visual survival curve of anesthesia onset.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** The text does not explicitly state that time-to-onset was measured as an outcome.
*   **When NOT to use:** When the exact time of the event is unknown or not recorded.
*   **Alternative:** Cox Proportional Hazards Regression (if adjusting for covariates).

*   **Pearson Correlation Coefficient:** 
    *   *Why:* Correlation evaluates the linear relationship between two continuous variables. The primary outcome here (Success/Failure) is binary, and the independent variable (Group) is categorical.
*   **Linear Regression:** 
    *   *Why:* Linear regression requires a continuous dependent variable. Because the primary outcome is binary (IANB success: Yes/No), linear regression violates core assumptions and would yield invalid predictions.
