TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

The plan is constructed under conservative assumptions: at least two parallel groups (combination vs control), binary primary outcome (IANB success), and continuous VAS secondary outcomes.

CATEGORY A – MANDATORY ANALYSES

A1. Descriptive statistics and baseline comparability

Test/Approach  
- Descriptive statistics for baseline characteristics:
  - Means ± SD or medians (IQR) for continuous variables (age, baseline VAS).
  - Frequencies and percentages for categorical variables (sex, ASA class, tooth, group).

Purpose  
- To describe the study sample and assess the success of randomization (baseline balance across groups).

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) RCTs require demonstration that groups are comparable at baseline; descriptive summaries and simple tests (e.g., t-test/χ²) help detect major imbalances.  
2) Understanding distributions (central tendency, spread, outliers) is essential to select appropriate inferential tests and transformations.

Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)  
- Overemphasis on statistical tests for baseline comparability in RCTs can be misleading; randomization does not guarantee perfect balance, and formal hypothesis tests for baseline imbalance are often discouraged.

When NOT to use  
- Avoid inferential hypothesis testing for baseline differences as a primary focus; descriptive emphasis is sufficient.

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- For non-normal continuous baseline variables, use medians (IQR) and nonparametric comparisons (Mann–Whitney, Kruskal–Wallis) only if clinically needed, but interpret cautiously.

A2. Primary analysis of IANB success (binary outcome)

Recommended primary test:  
- Pearson chi-square test or Fisher’s exact test for comparison of proportions.

Purpose  
- To test whether the proportion of IANB success differs between the combination (ibuprofen + dexamethasone) group and comparator group(s).

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) Outcome is binary (success vs failure), and exposure (premedication group) is categorical; chi-square is the standard test for association between two categorical variables.  
2) The trial is randomized with independent participants; thus, independence assumption is satisfied and group sizes are similar, which suits chi-square.

Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)  
- If expected cell counts are small (e.g., some groups have <5 successes/failures), the chi-square approximation may be invalid.

When NOT to use  
- Do not use Pearson chi-square if any expected cell count is <5 or if sample size per group is very small.

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Fisher’s exact test for 2×2 tables (or exact methods for larger tables) should be used instead of chi-square.

Analytical details  
- Compare success rates between:
  - Combination vs placebo (or standard care).
  - If multiple premedication groups exist (e.g., ibuprofen alone, dexamethasone alone), use:
    - Overall chi-square for k×2 table, followed by pairwise comparisons with appropriate multiple-comparison control (e.g., Bonferroni-adjusted p-values).

Effect size  
- Report:
  - Risk difference (RD) with 95% CI.
  - Relative risk (RR) with 95% CI.
  - Possibly odds ratio (OR) with 95% CI (especially if logistic regression is used in Category B).

A3. Secondary analysis: comparison of continuous pain scores (VAS) between groups

Recommended test:  
- Independent samples t-test (2 groups) or one-way ANOVA (≥3 groups), conditional on approximate normality and homogeneous variances.  
- Alternatively, pre-specified transformation (e.g., log or rank) if needed.

Purpose  
- To compare mean pain intensity (baseline-adjusted or raw) between treatment groups at key time points (e.g., pain during access cavity preparation, instrumentation).

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) VAS is typically treated as continuous, near-interval-level data; t-tests/ANOVA are standard tools to compare mean pain scores.  
2) With ~94 participants and typical VAS distributions, the central limit theorem often supports parametric tests, providing efficient and interpretable estimates.

Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)  
- VAS scores may be skewed, have ceiling/floor effects, or be heavily clustered at 0 or extremes, violating normality and equal variance assumptions.

When NOT to use  
- Do not use t-test/ANOVA if:
  - Sample size per group is small and distributions are markedly non-normal/outlier-prone.
  - Variances are highly unequal and transformations do not stabilize them.

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Mann–Whitney U test (2 groups) or Kruskal–Wallis test (≥3 groups) for continuous pain scores.  
- Consider analyzing dichotomized pain (e.g., “no/mild vs moderate/severe”) with chi-square as a supportive analysis.

A4. Handling multiple groups (if >2 arms)

If the trial includes more than two arms (e.g., ibuprofen alone, dexamethasone alone, combination, placebo):

Primary binary outcome:  
- Global comparison with chi-square (k×2) followed by:
  - Adjusted pairwise tests with Bonferroni or Holm correction.

Continuous VAS outcomes:  
- One-way ANOVA with:
  - Post hoc pairwise comparisons (Tukey or Bonferroni) if overall p < 0.05.

Same assumptions and alternative tests as above.

A5. Intention-to-treat (ITT) and per-protocol analyses

Purpose  
- ITT: Analyze all randomized patients in the groups to which they were assigned, regardless of protocol deviations.  
- Per-protocol (PP): Sensitivity analysis including only those who adhered to the assigned intervention and completed key procedures.

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) ITT preserves the benefits of randomization and reflects real-world clinical effectiveness.  
2) PP analysis can assess efficacy under ideal adherence and check robustness of ITT findings.

Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)  
- PP analysis can introduce bias if exclusions differ systematically by group; ITT should remain primary.

When NOT to use  
- Do not interpret PP as primary if it differs substantially from ITT or if missingness is high and nonrandom.

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- If missing outcome data are substantial, use multiple imputation or sensitivity analyses with best-/worst-case scenarios for the binary success outcome.

CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED ANALYSES (ENHANCE RIGOR)

B1. Logistic regression for primary outcome (IANB success)

Test  
- Binary logistic regression.

Purpose  
- To estimate adjusted effects of treatment on IANB success, accounting for potential covariates (age, sex, baseline pain, tooth type, ASA class).

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) Primary endpoint is binary; logistic regression is the standard multivariable method for modeling such outcomes.  
2) Adjusting for baseline covariates improves precision and can check whether results are robust to any residual imbalance after randomization.

Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)  
- With a limited number of events (successes or failures), including too many covariates can lead to overfitting and unstable estimates.

When NOT to use  
- Avoid logistic regression with many predictors if events per variable (EPV) < 10; overparameterization can distort the treatment effect.

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Fit a simpler model with fewer prespecified covariates or use Firth penalized logistic regression in small-sample or separation scenarios.  
- Use unadjusted chi-square/Fisher test as primary if regression is untenable.

B2. Repeated-measures analysis for VAS at multiple time points

Test  
- Linear mixed-effects model (preferred) or repeated-measures ANOVA, assuming approximate normality.

Purpose  
- To model the trajectory of pain scores over time (e.g., baseline, during procedure, post-procedure), capturing within-subject correlation and between-group differences over time.

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) Pain is often measured at multiple time points; repeated measures need to account for correlation within subjects.  
2) Mixed models can handle missing time points under missing-at-random assumptions and allow flexible covariance structures.

Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)  
- If only two time points exist (baseline and one post-intervention), a simpler ANCOVA may suffice; full mixed modeling could overcomplicate interpretation.

When NOT to use  
- Do not use repeated-measures ANOVA if many patients have missing follow-up pain scores or if sphericity assumptions are clearly violated and cannot be adjusted without severely reducing power.

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Use nonparametric repeated-measures methods (e.g., Friedman test for 3+ time points) or analyze change scores per subject using Mann–Whitney / t-tests, depending on distribution.

B3. ANCOVA for continuous outcomes (e.g., intraoperative pain controlling for baseline)

Test  
- Analysis of covariance (ANCOVA).

Purpose  
- To compare post-intervention pain (e.g., during access) between groups while adjusting for baseline pain VAS.

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) Adjusting for baseline improves power and reduces residual variance, especially important when baseline pain varies within inclusion range 55–170 mm.  
2) ANCOVA provides unbiased estimates of treatment differences under RCT randomization if assumptions hold (linear relationship between baseline and outcome).

Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)  
- Violations of linearity between baseline pain and outcome or heterogeneity of slopes (interaction of baseline-by-treatment) can make ANCOVA inappropriate.

When NOT to use  
- Do not use ANCOVA if there is substantial baseline imprecision (e.g., VAS measured inconsistently) or if baseline-outcome relationship differs markedly across groups.

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Use change scores (post – baseline) and compare via t-test/Mann–Whitney, acknowledging generally lower power but fewer modeling assumptions.

B4. Subgroup and interaction analyses

Test  
- Logistic regression (for binary success) with interaction terms:
  - Treatment × baseline pain category.
  - Treatment × tooth type, etc.

Purpose  
- To explore whether treatment effect differs across clinically relevant subgroups (e.g., high vs very high baseline pain, first vs second molar).

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) Clinical heterogeneity in SIP (different teeth, pain levels) may influence anesthetic response; exploring interactions can generate hypotheses for future research.  
2) Interaction tests in regression provide more reliable inference than separate subgroup analyses.

Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)  
- The trial is likely underpowered for definitive interaction analyses; multiple testing increases type I error.

When NOT to use  
- Do not present subgroup findings as definitive or primary unless pre-specified and adequately powered.

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Restrict to descriptive subgroup summaries without formal hypothesis testing; interpret qualitatively.

B5. Sensitivity analyses for missing data and definition of success

Test  
- Sensitivity logistic/chi-square analyses under different assumptions:
  - E.g., treat all missing outcomes as failures; then as successes; compare results.

Purpose  
- To assess robustness of the primary conclusion to missing data and to variations in the threshold defining “success” (e.g., VAS cut-offs).

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) Helps ensure that conclusions about treatment efficacy are not artifacts of arbitrary decisions on missingness or categorization.  
2) Increases transparency and credibility of results.

Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)  
- If missing data are trivial (<5% of primary outcome), extensive sensitivity analyses may not change interpretation and could clutter the presentation.

When NOT to use  
- Do not overinterpret small differences between sensitivity scenarios when main ITT results are stable and sample is nearly complete.

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- For more extensive missingness, consider multiple imputation for primary outcome and repeat primary analyses.

Test

Purpose  
- To respect the ordinal nature of VAS categories and examine overall shifts in pain distribution, not just mean or dichotomized outcomes.

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) VAS often maps to clinically meaningful verbal categories; ordinal models can capture distributional changes.  
2) Provides richer clinical interpretation (e.g., shift from severe to moderate pain) beyond binary success/failure.

Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)  
- Proportional odds assumption may be violated; with limited sample size, model fit and stability can be problematic.

When NOT to use  
- Do not use ordinal regression if category frequencies are sparse or if assumption checks (e.g., score tests) show clear violation.

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Collapse categories into binary outcome (e.g., none/mild vs moderate/severe) and analyze via logistic regression or chi-square.

C2. Time-to-event analysis (if time to complete access without significant pain is recorded)

Test

Purpose

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)

Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)

When NOT to use

Alternative if assumptions violated

TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS (For this study)

T3.1 Paired t-test or McNemar’s test between groups

Why inappropriate  
- These tests are designed for paired or matched data within the same individuals (pre vs post, or matched pairs). This RCT uses independent parallel groups; there is no pairing between patients in different groups.

T3.2 Repeated-measures ANOVA assuming sphericity for many time points without checking

Why inappropriate  
- If there are multiple pain measurements over time, repeated-measures ANOVA requires the sphericity assumption, which is often violated with pain data. Using it without tests or corrections (Greenhouse–Geisser) can inflate type I error.

Preferred alternative  
- Linear mixed-effects models (as in Category B), or nonparametric repeated-measures methods.

T3.3 Parametric tests ignoring binary nature of primary outcome (e.g., t-test on proportions)

Why inappropriate  
- Proportions are bounded between 0 and 1 and not normally distributed; it is invalid to apply t-tests directly to proportions at the individual level for a binary outcome.

Preferred alternative  
- Chi-square/Fisher or logistic regression.

T3.4 Multiple unadjusted post hoc comparisons without correction

Why inappropriate  
- In multi-arm trials or multiple outcomes, unadjusted multiple comparisons increase the chance of false-positive findings.

Preferred alternative  
- Adjust for multiple comparisons (Bonferroni, Holm, Tukey, etc.) or clearly label exploratory analyses as such.

T3.5 Use of nonparametric tests (e.g., Mann–Whitney) followed by parametric confidence intervals for means

Why inappropriate  
- Inconsistency between test and estimand: nonparametric tests target differences in distributions/medians, while parametric CI for means assumes normality.

Preferred alternative  
- If nonparametric methods are used, present medians (IQR) and nonparametric effect measures (e.g., Hodges–Lehmann estimate with CI).

T3.6 Complex modeling not justified by sample size (e.g., high-dimensional machine learning models, multi-level models with many random effects)

Why inappropriate  
- N=94 is small for complex models; such methods would overfit and obscure interpretation in a straightforward two-group RCT.

Preferred alternative  
- Focus on simple, pre-specified models: chi-square/Fisher, logistic regression with limited covariates, t-tests/ANOVA or rank-based tests for VAS.
