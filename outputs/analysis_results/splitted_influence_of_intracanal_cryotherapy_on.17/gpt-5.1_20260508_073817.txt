A1. DESCRIPTIVE AND BASELINE BALANCE

Test/Method: Descriptive statistics + group comparison at baseline

Purpose
- Summarize baseline characteristics by group.
- Check for any strong imbalances that might require covariate adjustment.

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)
1) RCT with small sample (n=12/group) – randomization should balance, but formal checking confirms no substantive differences.
2) Baseline pain (VAS), age, and sex are important predictors of postoperative pain and IL‑6; imbalances may confound group comparisons.

Why it may not be appropriate
- Formal hypothesis testing of baseline differences can be misleading in RCTs (randomization ensures validity; baseline p-values have limited interpretive value).

When NOT to use
- Do not interpret baseline p-values as evidence of “need” to adjust; use clinical judgment and pre-specified covariates.

Alternatives if assumptions violated
- For continuous variables: use nonparametric tests instead of t-tests (e.g., Mann–Whitney U, Kruskal–Wallis) if needed.
- For categorical variables: use Fisher’s exact test instead of chi-square when expected cell counts are small.

Concretely:
- Age: One-way ANOVA (or Kruskal–Wallis) across 4 groups.
- Baseline VAS: One-way ANOVA (or Kruskal–Wallis).
- Sex and other categorical variables: Chi-square or Fisher’s exact test.

A2. PRIMARY PAIN ANALYSIS – BETWEEN-GROUP COMPARISON

Assuming VAS measured at multiple time points (e.g., 6, 24, 48 h):

Test name: Repeated-measures ANOVA (mixed-design ANOVA) or Linear Mixed-Effects Model (LMM)

Purpose
- Compare the time course of postendodontic pain between the four irrigation groups.
- Assess:
  - Main effect of group.
  - Main effect of time.
  - Group × time interaction.

Why appropriate
1) Pain is a continuous repeated-measures outcome; mixed models or repeated-measures ANOVA correctly account for within-patient correlation.
2) Balanced design (n=12 per group) with multiple time points is well-suited to a mixed-design analysis, allowing simultaneous inference on trajectories.

Why it may not be appropriate
- Assumptions: normality of residuals, sphericity (for classical repeated-measures ANOVA), homoscedasticity, and complete data.
- Small sample size and skewed pain scores (VAS often non-normal) can compromise parametric RM-ANOVA.

When NOT to use
- If there is substantial missing data or unequal follow-up times between patients.
- If VAS distributions are highly skewed or bounded with many zeros (e.g., at later time points).

Alternative if assumptions violated
- Linear Mixed-Effects Model with appropriate covariance structure (e.g., unstructured, AR(1)); more robust to missing data and sphericity violations.
- Nonparametric alternative:
  - For repeated measures: Friedman test within each group plus pairwise comparisons across groups at each time point using Kruskal–Wallis or Mann–Whitney tests with multiplicity correction.
- Transformation of VAS (e.g., Box–Cox or log for positive values) if necessary.

If only a single post-treatment VAS time point is used as primary:
- Replace repeated-measures ANOVA with one-way ANOVA across 4 groups (A3 below) or Kruskal–Wallis if non-normal.

A3. PRIMARY PAIN ANALYSIS – SINGLE TIME POINT (e.g., 24 h VAS)

Test name: One-way ANOVA

Purpose
- Compare mean VAS pain score at a clinically important time (e.g., 24 h post-treatment) across the four groups.

Why appropriate
1) Continuous outcome (VAS) across more than two independent groups; one-way ANOVA is the standard.
2) Balanced design (equal n) improves robustness to minor normality violations and supports simple post hoc testing (e.g., Tukey).

Why it may not be appropriate
- VAS may be non-normal, especially with ceiling or floor effects and small n (n=12 per group).
- Homogeneity of variances may be violated.

When NOT to use
- If Shapiro–Wilk shows severe non-normality and transformation does not fix it.
- If Levene’s test suggests strongly unequal variances.

Alternative if assumptions violated
- Kruskal–Wallis test followed by pairwise Dunn–Bonferroni or similar post hoc tests.
- Welch ANOVA if variances are unequal but normality is approximately satisfied.

A4. PRIMARY IL‑6 ANALYSIS – BETWEEN-GROUP COMPARISON

Case 1: Single post-treatment IL‑6 measurement (e.g., relative expression at one time point).

Test name: One-way ANOVA on IL‑6 (or log-transformed IL‑6 / ΔΔCt)

Purpose
- Compare mean IL‑6 expression across the four irrigation groups.

Why appropriate
1) IL‑6 expression typically treated as continuous; PCR-derived measures (ΔCt, ΔΔCt, or log fold-change) lend themselves to parametric analysis.
2) Balanced groups with N=12 allow straightforward ANOVA and post hoc comparisons to identify which cryotherapy protocol yields lower IL‑6.

Why it may not be appropriate
- Gene expression data often skewed and may violate normality.
- Small group size increases sensitivity to outliers.

When NOT to use
- If IL‑6 data are strongly skewed or zero-inflated and normality cannot be achieved through transformation.
- If variance heterogeneity is substantial.

Alternative if assumptions violated
- Transform IL‑6 (e.g., log-transformation) and re-run ANOVA.
- Use Welch ANOVA if variances differ.
- Use Kruskal–Wallis test if parametric assumptions remain violated.

Case 2: Pre- and post-treatment IL‑6 paired data:

Test name: Repeated-measures ANOVA or LMM (2 time points × 4 groups)

Purpose
- Evaluate change in IL‑6 over time across groups, incorporating baseline values.

Why appropriate
1) Accounts for within-patient pairing (baseline vs post).
2) Provides test of group × time interaction (differential change).

Why it may not be appropriate
- Same issues as pain repeated measures: non-normality, small sample, missing values.

Alternative if assumptions violated
- LMM with random intercepts.
- For change scores (ΔIL‑6 = post – pre), one-way ANOVA across groups, or Kruskal–Wallis if non-normal.

A5. IL‑6 – COVARIATE-ADJUSTED ANALYSIS

Test name: ANCOVA (Analysis of Covariance)

Purpose
- Compare IL‑6 between groups while adjusting for baseline covariates (e.g., age, sex, baseline pain, baseline IL‑6 if available).
- Improve precision and account for potential small imbalances.

Why appropriate
1) RCT with modest sample size; ANCOVA reduces residual variance by adjusting for strong predictors (baseline measures).
2) Baseline IL‑6 and pain may influence postoperative IL‑6; ANCOVA separates treatment effect from these influences.

Why it may not be appropriate
- Requires linear relationship between covariates and IL‑6, and homogeneity of regression slopes.
- Limited degrees of freedom with small N may cause overfitting if too many covariates included.

When NOT to use
- When covariate data are missing for many patients.
- When group × covariate interaction indicates non-parallel slopes.

Alternative if assumptions violated
- Use stratified analysis (e.g., stratify by high vs low baseline pain) and compare within strata.
- Use nonparametric ANCOVA-like approaches (e.g., rank ANCOVA) [more complex and less standard].

B1. POST HOC PAIRWISE COMPARISONS

Test name: Tukey’s HSD (for ANOVA) or Dunn’s test (for Kruskal–Wallis)

Purpose
- Identify which specific groups differ in pain and IL‑6 after omnibus tests show significant group effects.

Why appropriate
1) Four-level factor requires clarification of which irrigation protocols are superior/inferior.
2) Tukey’s HSD controls familywise error; Dunn’s with Bonferroni/Holm correction appropriate in nonparametric setting.

Why it may not be appropriate
- Sample size per group is small; power for pairwise differences may be limited.
- Multiple comparisons increase risk of false positives if correction not robustly applied.

When NOT to use
- If omnibus test is non-significant (no evidence of overall group differences).
- If sample size or distribution issues make interpretation unstable (e.g., many zero VAS scores).

Alternative if assumptions violated
- Use Games–Howell test (for unequal variances).
- For nonparametric data: Conover–Iman or Dwass–Steel–Critchlow–Fligner tests.

TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS FOR THIS STUDY

1. Independent-samples t-test for primary outcomes
- Why not: There are four groups, not two. Multiple pairwise t-tests inflate Type I error and ignore overall group structure.
- Appropriate alternative: One-way ANOVA or Kruskal–Wallis, followed by corrected post hoc tests.

2. Paired t-test for between-group comparisons
- Why not: Paired t-test requires paired observations on the same individuals (or matched pairs). Here groups are independent.
- Appropriate alternative: Independent-group methods (ANOVA, mixed models).

3. Chi-square test for continuous pain or IL‑6 outcomes (after arbitrary categorization)
- Why not:
  - Dichotomizing continuous outcomes (e.g., high vs low pain) loses information and power.
  - Chi-square on categorized outcomes is a step backward when continuous methods are available.
- Appropriate alternative: Direct analysis of continuous VAS and IL‑6 via ANOVA/LMM.

4. Multiple unadjusted pairwise t-tests without multiplicity correction
- Why not:
  - Increases Type I error probability.
  - Violates good statistical practice in multi-arm RCT.
- Alternative: Tukey’s HSD, Bonferroni-Holm, or other multiplicity-adjusted comparisons.

5. Simple pre–post within-group tests only (e.g., paired t-tests within each group, ignoring between-group comparison)
- Why not:
  - RCT’s primary question is between-group differences, not within-group pre–post changes.
  - Within-group tests can be misleading even if between-group differences are null.
- Appropriate alternative: Group × time analyses (ANOVA/LMM).

6. Nonparametric tests with no account for repetition (e.g., Wilcoxon rank-sum on each time point without accounting for correlation or multiplicity)
- Why not:
  - Ignores within-subject correlation and repeated-measures nature.
  - Multiplicity issues if many separate tests are done.
- Alternative: Repeated-measures friendly methods (LMM, RM-ANOVA, Friedman).

7. Logistic regression treating continuous VAS as binary outcome (e.g., pain yes/no) without strong rationale
- Why not:
  - Dichotomizing VAS (e.g., ≥4 vs <4) wastes information.
  - Sample size likely too small for reliable logistic regression with multiple covariates.
- Appropriate alternative: Linear models for continuous outcomes; logistic only if clinical question explicitly focuses on probability of “clinically significant pain.”
