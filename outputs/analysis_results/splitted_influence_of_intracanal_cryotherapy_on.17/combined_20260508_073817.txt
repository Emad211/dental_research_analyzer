# CATEGORY A — MANDATORY tests

## A1) One-way ANOVA (or Welch ANOVA) for between-group comparisons of a single continuous endpoint at one timepoint
- **Purpose:** Test whether mean outcome differs across 4 groups (e.g., IL‑6 at a specified timepoint; pain at a single specified postop timepoint or pain change score).
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1) Study has **4 independent randomized groups** → standard omnibus comparison.
  2) Outcomes (VAS, IL‑6) are typically treated as **continuous**; ANOVA yields an overall test before pairwise comparisons.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - If outcome distribution is markedly non-normal (common for **ΔΔCt/2^-ΔΔCt**) or variances differ substantially.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When there are **repeated measures** over time and correlation must be modeled (then use mixed models).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Welch ANOVA** (unequal variances) or **Kruskal–Wallis** (nonparametric), or analyze **log-transformed** IL‑6.

## A2) Post-hoc multiple comparisons with multiplicity control (Tukey / Games–Howell)
- **Purpose:** Identify which groups differ after significant omnibus test.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1) Four groups imply multiple pairwise tests; control family-wise error.
  2) Provides interpretable clinical contrasts (e.g., “final cold NaOCl” vs control).
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - If the omnibus test is not meaningful due to non-normality/outliers or if a priori contrasts were the sole interest.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When you have **pre-specified primary contrasts only** (then use planned contrasts with adjusted alpha).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Games–Howell** (heteroscedasticity), **Dunn test with Holm adjustment** after Kruskal–Wallis.

## A3) Linear mixed-effects model (LMM) or repeated-measures ANOVA for postoperative pain measured at multiple timepoints
*(Needed if pain is recorded at multiple postoperative times, which is typical in this topic; the excerpt doesn’t list times, but the trial objective “postendodontic pain” almost always implies repeated follow-up.)*
- **Purpose:** Compare **pain trajectories over time** between groups; estimate group, time, and group×time interaction.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1) Handles **within-subject correlation** across repeated VAS measurements.
  2) LMM accommodates **missing follow-up** under MAR assumptions better than RM-ANOVA.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - If only one postoperative pain measurement exists (no repeated structure).
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When outcome is not approximately continuous and residual diagnostics fail badly (e.g., severe floor effects with many zeros).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Generalized linear mixed model (GLMM)** (e.g., ordinal mixed model), or **rank-based mixed models**, or analyze **AUC pain** with Kruskal–Wallis/ANOVA.

## A4) Missing data handling and reporting (not a “test,” but mandatory analytical component)
- **Purpose:** Prevent biased estimates due to attrition.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1) RCT validity depends on transparent handling of missing outcomes.
  2) Pain follow-ups commonly have missingness.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Complete-case only can be acceptable if missingness is negligible and balanced, but that must be demonstrated.
- **When NOT to use (for simple imputation approaches):**
  - Avoid last-observation-carried-forward for pain trajectories (can bias).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Multiple imputation** (continuous) / **MI with predictive mean matching**, plus sensitivity analyses.

# CATEGORY B — RECOMMENDED tests

## B1) Baseline comparability across groups (ANOVA/Kruskal–Wallis for continuous; Chi-square/Fisher for categorical)
- **Purpose:** Describe (not “test”) balance in baseline characteristics (age, baseline VAS, etc. if collected).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1) Ensures transparency about randomization success and potential prognostic imbalance.
  2) Identifies variables for **precision adjustment** (not for “fixing” randomization).
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Significance testing of baseline can be misleading; emphasis should be on **clinical importance**.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Do not use baseline p-values to decide covariate inclusion mechanically.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Use nonparametric counterparts; or present standardized mean differences (SMDs) instead of p-values.

## B2) ANCOVA (or LMM with baseline as covariate) for pain outcomes
- **Purpose:** Compare groups while adjusting for **baseline pain (VAS)** to improve precision.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1) Baseline VAS is available (eligibility used it) and is prognostic of postoperative pain.
  2) ANCOVA generally increases power and reduces residual variance in RCTs.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - If baseline measure is truncated by design (here **VAS > 7**), which can reduce linearity and normality.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When baseline is not measured consistently or baseline is post-randomization.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Robust regression, rank ANCOVA, or analyze change scores with caution.

## B3) Effect sizes with confidence intervals (mean difference, standardized effect, partial eta²)
- **Purpose:** Quantify magnitude and precision beyond p-values.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1) Clinically interpretable (e.g., pain reduction in VAS units).
  2) Facilitates meta-analysis and clinical decision-making.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - If outcome scale is highly non-normal and effect size chosen is not robust.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Avoid standardized effects alone when raw units are clinically meaningful.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Median differences with bootstrap CIs; Hodges–Lehmann estimate.

## B4) Correlation/association between IL‑6 expression and pain (Spearman / Pearson; or regression)
- **Purpose:** Assess whether higher IL‑6 is associated with higher pain.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1) Mechanistic coherence: IL‑6 described as marker of tissue damage and linked to pain.
  2) Adds biological plausibility to clinical findings.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - If IL‑6 and pain are measured at mismatched times or IL‑6 is only measured in a subset.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When association could be distorted by group effects without adjustment.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Spearman correlation; or multivariable regression adjusting for group.

- **Purpose:** Translate continuous pain into clinically meaningful success rates.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1) Clinicians often prefer “clinically acceptable pain” thresholds.
  2) Complements mean differences (captures distributional shifts).
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Dichotomization loses information and reduces power.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - As the sole primary analysis.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Exact methods; or ordinal models with multiple categories.

## C3) Nonparametric omnibus for skewed endpoints: Kruskal–Wallis with Dunn–Holm
- **Purpose:** Compare groups when distributions are non-normal/outlier-prone.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1) Robust to non-normality common in gene expression.
  2) Works with small n (12/group), where normality is harder to verify.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Tests differences in distributions/medians, not means; less efficient when normality holds.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When modeling covariates/time is essential (use regression/mixed models).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated (ties/heavy discreteness):**
  - Permutation tests; rank-based ANOVA methods.

## C4) Permutation test for group differences
- **Purpose:** Distribution-free inference for small samples.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1) Small sample size per arm (n=12) makes asymptotic approximations weaker.
  2) Provides exact/near-exact p-values under exchangeability.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - More complex with missing data and repeated measures.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When data are not exchangeable (e.g., strong heteroscedasticity without stratification).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Robust regression / bootstrap.

## TASK 3 — INAPPROPRIATE TESTS (should NOT be used)

1. **Paired t-test between groups**  
   - Wrong because groups are **independent**; pairing does not exist.

2. **Multiple independent t-tests for all pairwise group comparisons without correction**  
   - Inflates Type I error substantially with 4 groups (6 pairwise tests).

3. **Simple chi-square test on continuous VAS or continuous IL‑6 values**  
   - Chi-square is for categorical counts; would require arbitrary binning (information loss).

4. **One-way ANOVA on repeated pain measurements ignoring within-subject correlation**  
   - Violates independence; underestimates SE → false positives.

5. **Linear regression on raw 2^(-ΔΔCt) IL‑6 values without considering skewness/outliers**  
   - Gene expression ratios are often highly right-skewed; may break model assumptions unless transformed/robust methods used.

6. **Last Observation Carried Forward (LOCF) as primary missing data method**  
   - Can bias pain trajectory estimates; not principled for longitudinal RCTs.
