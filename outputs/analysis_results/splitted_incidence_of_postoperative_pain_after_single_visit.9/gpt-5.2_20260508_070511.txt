## TASK 1 — INFORMATION EXTRACTION (study characteristics)

### Study identification
- **Title:** Incidence of postoperative pain after single-visit and multiple-visit root canal therapy: A randomized controlled trial  
- **Setting:** Department of Conservative Dentistry and Endodontics, Hitkarini Dental College, India  
- **Ethics/registration:** IEC approval; registered in CTRI (**CTRI/2019/05/019067**)  

### Objectives
- **Primary objective:** Compare **post-obturation pain prevalence** at **6 h, 12 h, 1 day, and 2 days** after treatment between:
  - **Single-visit RCT (SV)** vs **Two-visit RCT (MV)**
- **Secondary objective:** Compare **tenderness on percussion** **1 week after obturation** between groups.

### Study design
- **Design:** **Parallel-group randomized controlled trial (RCT)**.
- **Unit of randomization/analysis:** **Patient/tooth** (mandibular first molar).
- **Operator:** All RCTs performed by **principal investigator** (single operator).

### Participants
- **Screened:** 90  
- **Excluded pre-randomization:** 15 not meeting inclusion + 5 not consenting  
- **Randomized/recruited:** **70** (35 per group)

#### Inclusion criteria (as reported)
- Consent for planned SV or two-visit treatment
- **Moderately curved root canals**
- Tooth type implied by aim: **mandibular first molar** requiring RCT

#### Exclusion criteria (as reported)
- Any systemic disease
- Pregnancy
- Calcified canals, unusual canal morphology, extra root, or procedural error during treatment
- Periapical radiolucency **> 0.5 cm**
- Took antibiotics and/or analgesics right after first appointment
- **Post-randomization exclusion:** if rescue medication taken within **48 h** after treatment → excluded from study (introduces attrition/non-ITT concerns)

### Interventions / Groups
- **Group 1 (SV):** Cleaning/shaping + **obturated same visit**
  - Obturation: gutta-percha + **Sealapex** sealer, **lateral compaction**
  - Temporary restoration; post-obturation radiograph
- **Group 2 (Two-visit):**
  - Visit 1: cleaning/shaping + **calcium hydroxide** intracanal medicament; provisional seal
  - Visit 2 (after **1 week**): obturation same method/materials as Group 1
- **Rescue medication:** **Ibuprofen** prescribed; advised only for severe pain

### Clinical protocol (key standardizations)
- Anesthesia: inferior alveolar nerve block with 2% lidocaine + 1:80,000 epinephrine
- Isolation: rubber dam
- WL: apex locator (Root ZX) + radiographic confirmation
- Instrumentation: hybrid hand K-files + S-one NiTi rotary
- Irrigation: 3% NaOCl + saline; 17% EDTA; final saline
- Apical preparation: ≥ 3 sizes larger than initial apical file
- Restoration: composite after 1 week of obturation

### Outcomes, variables, measurement schedule
1. **Pain**
   - **Preoperative pain:** recorded in clinic using **Heft-Parker VAS (HP VAS)**
   - **Postoperative pain:** recorded by patient on HP VAS at:
     - **6 hours**, **12 hours**, **1 day**, **2 days** (post-obturation)
   - Data collection: patient takes form home; **telephone reminder**
   - Variable type: HP VAS is often treated as continuous, but is ordinal/interval-like; distribution frequently skewed with many zeros.
2. **Tenderness on percussion**
   - Assessed at **1 week after obturation**
   - Scale not described (likely binary present/absent or ordinal grading)

### Sample size planning (as reported)
- Formula for comparing two means:
  \[
  n=\frac{(Z_{\alpha}+Z_{\beta})^{2}\,2s^{2}}{(\bar{x}_{1}-\bar{x}_{2})^{2}}
  \]
- **α=0.05**, **power=80%**
- Computed **30/group**, inflated to **35/group** for dropouts

# CATEGORY A — MANDATORY (without these the analysis is incomplete)

## A1) Linear Mixed-Effects Model (LMM) or Generalized Linear Mixed Model (GLMM) for repeated HP VAS pain
**Test/model name:** Repeated-measures **mixed-effects model** (time, group, group×time; subject random intercept).  
- Use **LMM** if residuals acceptable after transformation; use **GLMM** (e.g., Gamma/log, Tweedie, or ordinal mixed model) if pain is skewed/zero-inflated.

**Purpose**
- Compare **post-obturation pain trajectories** across time between SV vs two-visit groups.

**Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)**
1. Pain is measured **repeatedly within the same patient**, so observations are correlated; mixed models account for this.
2. Tests the clinically relevant question: **does pain differ by group over time** (group×time interaction) rather than isolated time-point tests.
3. Can accommodate **unequal variances** and **incomplete follow-up** (under MAR) better than complete-case ANOVA.

**Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)**
- HP VAS may be **non-normal, bounded, and zero-inflated**, making a standard LMM with normal errors poorly fitting.

**When this test should NOT be used**
- When pain is clearly **ordinal with heavy floor effects** and you cannot justify treating it as continuous, and no suitable transformation resolves this.

**Alternative if assumptions are violated**
- **Ordinal mixed-effects model** (cumulative logit) treating HP VAS in ordered categories.
- **GLMM with Gamma (log link)** for positive pain plus a **two-part (hurdle) model** if many zeros.
- **Nonparametric longitudinal methods** (e.g., rank-based methods) as a fallback.

**Implementation details**
- Fixed effects: group (SV vs MV), time (categorical: 6h/12h/1d/2d), group×time.
- Covariate adjustment (if collected): **preoperative pain** (strong predictor), age/sex, periapical status.
- Report: estimated marginal means at each time, differences with 95% CI, interaction P value.

## A2) Between-group comparison of tenderness on percussion at 1 week
**Test name:** **Chi-square test of independence** (or **Fisher’s exact** if expected counts small).

**Purpose**
- Compare **proportion with tenderness** at 1 week between SV and MV groups.

**Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)**
1. Outcome is categorical (likely **present/absent**).
2. Groups are **independent** due to randomization.

**Why it may not be appropriate**
- If tenderness is recorded on an **ordinal scale** (none/mild/moderate/severe), chi-square loses ordering information.

**When this test should NOT be used**
- When expected cell counts are too small for chi-square and Fisher’s exact is not used; or when outcome is ordinal and you want an effect estimate respecting order.

**Alternative if assumptions are violated**
- **Fisher’s exact test** (small counts).
- **Ordinal logistic regression** (if ordinal tenderness grading).
- **Log-binomial/Poisson regression with robust SE** to estimate risk ratio.

---

## A3) Primary estimand reporting: effect sizes with confidence intervals
**Test name:** Not a “test” per se, but **mandatory reporting** of effect sizes:
- Pain: mean/median differences at each time, and/or overall trajectory difference
- Tenderness: risk difference, risk ratio, or odds ratio with 95% CI

**Purpose**
- Provide clinically interpretable magnitude of effect.

**Why appropriate**
1. P-values alone don’t quantify clinical relevance.
2. Required for transparent RCT reporting (CONSORT-aligned).

**Why it may not be appropriate**
- Some effect measures (e.g., OR) can be misleading when outcomes are common.

**When it should NOT be used**
- Don’t preferentially report odds ratios if tenderness prevalence is high and RR/RD is more interpretable.

**Alternatives**
- Use **risk ratio** (Poisson robust) or **risk difference** (binomial identity model).

# CATEGORY B — RECOMMENDED (improves rigor)

## B1) Baseline comparability checks (descriptive + limited inference)
**Test name:** 
- Continuous baseline variables: **t-test** or **Mann–Whitney U**
- Categorical: **Chi-square/Fisher**

**Purpose**
- Describe balance after randomization; identify large imbalances that may warrant covariate adjustment.

**Why appropriate**
1. Even with randomization, small samples can show imbalance by chance.
2. Helps justify adjusted analyses (e.g., controlling for preop pain).

**Why it may not be appropriate**
- Significance testing of baseline variables is often discouraged because imbalance testing is not the goal of randomization.

**When it should NOT be used**
- Do not use baseline P-values to decide “success” of randomization or to decide inclusion/exclusion of covariates mechanically.

**Alternative**
- Prefer **standardized mean differences (SMDs)** and clinical judgment.

---

## B2) ANCOVA-style adjustment for preoperative pain (as covariate)
**Test/model name:** Mixed-effects model including **preoperative pain** as covariate.

**Purpose**
- Increase precision and control for an important prognostic factor.

**Why appropriate**
1. Preoperative pain strongly predicts postoperative pain in endodontics.
2. Adjustment improves power/precision even in RCTs.

**Why it may not be appropriate**
- If preoperative pain is missing for many participants or measured inconsistently.

**When it should NOT be used**
- If covariate is post-randomization (not the case here) or affected by treatment.

**Alternative**
- Stratified analysis by baseline pain category; multiple imputation for missing baseline pain.

---

## B3) Multiple-comparisons control for 4 time points
**Test name:** **Holm correction** (family-wise) or **Benjamini–Hochberg** (FDR) for pairwise time-point contrasts.

**Purpose**
- Limit false positives when comparing groups at multiple times.

**Why appropriate**
1. Four post-op time points increase type I error if tested separately.
2. Encourages pre-specification of primary contrast(s) (e.g., 24h peak pain).

**Why it may not be appropriate**
- If the primary inference is based on the **global group×time interaction** from the mixed model, additional correction may be unnecessary for that global test.

**When it should NOT be used**
- Avoid blanket correction if you pre-specify one primary time point/contrast and treat others as secondary/exploratory.

**Alternative**
- Hierarchical testing sequence (interaction first; then time-specific contrasts only if interaction significant).

---

## B4) Sensitivity analysis for rescue medication use (post-randomization)
**Test/model name:** 
- Include a binary indicator “rescue medication within 48h” as outcome/mediator; or
- **Two-part approach**: (i) pain outcome, (ii) rescue-med use as separate endpoint.

**Purpose**
- Address bias introduced by excluding those who took analgesics; quantify whether groups differ in rescue-med need.

**Why appropriate**
1. Rescue medication is clinically meaningful and related to pain severity.
2. Excluding these patients biases toward lower pain and breaks randomization.

**Why it may not be appropriate**
- Rescue medication may be rare; low event counts limit inference.

**When it should NOT be used**
- Don’t adjust for rescue medication as a covariate in the primary model if it lies on the causal pathway (post-treatment mediator), unless doing mediation-specific analyses.

**Alternative**
- Treat rescue medication as part of a **composite endpoint** (“moderate/severe pain or rescue med”) as sensitivity.

# CATEGORY C — OPTIONAL (supplementary insights)

## C1) Area Under the Curve (AUC) for pain over 0–48h
**Test name:** Compare **AUC** between groups (t-test/Mann–Whitney or regression).

**Purpose**
- Summarize overall pain burden across repeated measures into one metric.

**Why appropriate**
1. Clinically intuitive “total pain experience.”
2. Avoids multiple time-point testing.

**Why it may not be appropriate**
- AUC requires reasonably complete pain series; missing points complicate calculation.

**When it should NOT be used**
- When missingness is substantial and not handled appropriately.

**Alternative**
- Model-based estimated AUC from mixed model predictions; multiple imputation.

---

## C2) Time-to-no-pain analysis
**Test name:** **Kaplan–Meier** curves + **log-rank test**; **Cox regression**.

**Purpose**
- Compare how quickly patients become pain-free between groups.

**Why appropriate**
1. Uses timing information (6h/12h/1d/2d) in a clinically interpretable way.
2. Handles censoring if pain persists beyond follow-up.

**Why it may not be appropriate**
- Pain is only assessed at discrete time points; event time is interval-censored.

**When it should NOT be used**
- If you cannot handle interval censoring and simply assign event times naively.

**Alternative**
- **Discrete-time survival model** or methods for **interval-censored** data.

---

## C3) Subgroup / effect-modification (pre-specified only)
**Test name:** Interaction terms (e.g., group×baseline pain category; group×sex).

**Purpose**
- Explore whether treatment effect differs by baseline pain or other factors.

**Why appropriate**
1. Could identify clinically relevant heterogeneity (e.g., symptomatic vs asymptomatic).
2. Supports hypothesis generation for future trials.

**Why it may not be appropriate**
- Small sample (70) → underpowered; high risk of false positives.

**When it should NOT be used**
- Post hoc subgrouping without pre-specification and without interaction testing.

**Alternative**
- Report descriptively; meta-analytic follow-up in larger datasets.

## TASK 3 — INAPPROPRIATE TESTS (should NOT be used)

1. **Separate independent t-tests at each time point without multiplicity control**
   - Inflates type I error; ignores within-subject correlation; inefficient vs mixed model.

2. **One-way ANOVA treating the four time points as independent groups**
   - Violates independence (same patients repeated measures).

3. **Paired t-test within each group over time as primary analysis**
   - Doesn’t answer between-group comparative question; multiple testing; ignores interaction.

4. **Correlation (Pearson/Spearman) between group code and pain**
   - Group is not a continuous variable; correlation is not an appropriate comparative framework.

5. **Per-protocol-only analysis excluding rescue-med users as the sole primary analysis**
   - Post-randomization exclusion introduces selection bias and undermines randomization.

6. **Linear regression on pooled repeated observations without clustering/robust SE**
   - Underestimates SE due to within-subject correlation.
