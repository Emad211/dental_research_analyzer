## TASK 2 – STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

Assume standard good practice: define α = 0.05 (two-sided) and report 95% confidence intervals (CI). Because it is a retrospective cohort with survival-time outcome, survival analysis methods are central.

### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS

#### A1. Descriptive statistics

- **Test / Approach**: Descriptive summaries (not a test but necessary).
- **Purpose**:
  - Describe baseline characteristics of teeth and patients overall and by restoration group.
- **Why appropriate**:
  1. Essential to understand the sample: distributions of age, sex, tooth type, location, follow-up time, etc.
  2. Helps identify imbalances between crown and composite groups and informs adjustment needs.
- **Why it may not be appropriate**:
  - Not a hypothesis test, so cannot provide inferential comparisons.
- **When NOT to use**:
  - Never omitted; but avoid relying solely on descriptives to draw causal inferences.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated**:
  - For non-normal continuous variables, use medians and IQR instead of means and SD, and graphical displays.

#### A2. Kaplan–Meier survival analysis

- **Test name**: Kaplan–Meier estimator with log-rank comparison.
- **Purpose**:
  - Estimate survival function (probability of **survival from unrestorable fracture** over time) for:
    - Crown vs composite restoration groups.
  - Produce survival curves and survival probabilities at specific time points/intervals (e.g., 12–24 months, 24–60 months).
- **Why appropriate**:
  1. Outcome is **time-to-unrestorable fracture** with right censoring (teeth without event at last recall), which is the standard setting for Kaplan–Meier analysis.
  2. Allows visualization of survival curves and comparison across groups with different follow-up durations.
- **Why it may not be appropriate**:
  - Assumes independent censoring and does not adjust for covariates (unadjusted comparison can be confounded).
- **When NOT to use**:
  - If exact times to event/censoring are not available and only aggregate intervals are known.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated**:
  - If independence of censoring is strongly violated, consider sensitivity analyses or parametric survival models that can incorporate informative censoring (more complex).
  - If only interval-censored data are available, use interval-censored survival methods (Turnbull estimator; parametric models for interval censoring).

#### A3. Log-rank test for group comparison

- **Test name**: Log-rank (Mantel–Cox) test.
- **Purpose**:
  - Compare survival distributions between:
    - Crown vs composite.
    - Potentially other categorical factors (e.g., post type, amount of tooth surface loss categories).
- **Why appropriate**:
  1. Designed to test equality of survival functions in the presence of censoring.
  2. Non-parametric; does not require specifying a functional form of the hazard.
- **Why it may not be appropriate**:
  - Most powerful when hazards are proportional; if hazards cross heavily (e.g., large difference early, none later), interpretation can be difficult, and test may lose power or mislead.
- **When NOT to use**:
  - For covariate-adjusted comparisons (cannot handle multiple covariates).
  - When the sample size within subgroups is very small (few events).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated**:
  - Use **Wilcoxon (Breslow) test** or **Tarone–Ware** test if early vs late differences are of particular interest.
  - Use **Cox proportional hazards model** (adjusted and can handle non-proportional hazards with time-varying effects).

#### A4. Cox proportional hazards regression

- **Test name**: Cox proportional hazards (PH) model.
- **Purpose**:
  - Assess the effect of **restoration type** (crown vs composite) on the hazard of **unrestorable fracture**, adjusting for other risk factors:
    - Age, sex, tooth type, tooth location, opposing dentition, number of adjacent teeth, post type (crown group), amount of tooth surface loss (composite group, if modeled appropriately), etc.
- **Why appropriate**:
  1. Gold-standard semi-parametric model for time-to-event data with censoring; yields hazard ratios (HRs) with 95% CI.
  2. Allows simultaneous adjustment for multiple covariates to control confounding and to identify independent risk factors.
- **Why it may not be appropriate**:
  - Relies on the proportional hazards assumption (log hazard ratio constant over time) for each covariate.
- **When NOT to use**:
  - If proportional hazards assumption is grossly violated and cannot be remedied (e.g., time-varying effects) or if the number of events is too low relative to the number of predictors (overfitting).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated**:
  - If PH violated:
    - Use **time-varying covariates** within Cox (include interaction with time).
    - Use **stratified Cox models** for covariates violating PH.
    - Use **parametric survival models** (e.g., Weibull, log-logistic) or **flexible parametric models**.
  - If events are few:
    - Use **penalized Cox** (Firth correction, ridge/LASSO) or reduce the model to fewer key covariates.

#### A5. Group comparisons of baseline characteristics

- **Test names**:
  - For continuous variables (e.g., age, recall period): 
    - **t-test** (if normal) or **Mann–Whitney U**.
  - For categorical variables (e.g., sex, tooth type, tooth location, opposing dentition):
    - **Chi-square test** or **Fisher’s exact test** (if small counts).
- **Purpose**:
  - Assess whether there are significant baseline differences between crown and composite groups that could act as confounders.
- **Why appropriate**:
  1. Helps interpret observed group differences in survival (imbalance may indicate confounding, affecting causal interpretation).
  2. Guides choice of covariates to adjust for in multivariable models.
- **Why it may not be appropriate**:
  - These tests assume independence of observations; if multiple teeth per patient, clustering may violate this assumption.
- **When NOT to use**:
  - If there is substantial clustering and cluster-level correlation must be accounted for (e.g., many teeth per patient) and you are strictly interested in patient-level comparability; however, for descriptive purposes they are usually still reported.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated**:
  - Use **mixed models** or **GEE** for continuous outcomes to account for clustering when comparing baseline characteristics.
  - Use cluster-adjusted chi-square or **Rao–Scott** test for survey-like clustered data.

### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED TESTS

#### B1. Assessment of proportional hazards assumption

- **Test name**: Schoenfeld residuals test and graphical diagnostics.
- **Purpose**:
  - Verify whether the proportional hazards assumption holds for the Cox model, particularly for the key exposure (crown vs composite).
- **Why appropriate**:
  1. PH assumption is critical for valid Cox model interpretation; must be checked rather than assumed.
  2. Aids selection of appropriate model (PH vs time-varying, stratified, or parametric).
- **Why it may not be appropriate**:
  - With small sample size or few events, residual-based tests may have low power and produce unstable results.
- **When NOT to use**:
  - With extremely few events (<10–15); rely more on visual inspection and simplified models.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated**:
  - Implement **time-varying effects** for exposures (interaction with log(time)).
  - Use **stratified Cox** for variables violating PH.
  - Switch to **parametric models** that can model non-proportional hazards (e.g., accelerated failure time models).

#### B2. Multivariable modeling with clustering (if multiple teeth per patient)

- **Test name**:
  - **Cox proportional hazards model with robust sandwich variance** (clustered by patient), or
  - **Frailty model** (shared frailty for patient).
- **Purpose**:
  - Account for potential **intra-patient correlation** if patients have more than one included molar.
- **Why appropriate**:
  1. Retrospective tooth-level data often include multiple teeth per patient; ignoring correlation may underestimate standard errors and inflate type I error.
  2. Cluster-robust SE or frailty models correct inference while preserving the main estimates (HRs).
- **Why it may not be appropriate**:
  - If almost all patients contribute only one tooth, clustering may be minimal and robust methods add little benefit.
- **When NOT to use**:
  - When data cannot be linked at patient level (no patient IDs) or when it is clear that each patient contributes only one tooth.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated**:
  - If frailty distribution assumptions (e.g., gamma) are questionable, revert to **robust variance** approach.
  - If patient IDs are unknown, sensitivity analysis cannot be cluster-adjusted; report this as a limitation.

#### B3. Subgroup survival analyses

- **Test name**: Stratified Kaplan–Meier curves and stratified log-rank tests.
- **Purpose**:
  - Explore survival differences within subgroups, such as:
    - Amount of tooth surface loss (composite group only).
    - Type of post (crown group only).
    - Tooth location (upper vs lower), tooth type (first vs second molar).
- **Why appropriate**:
  1. Helps identify specific situations where composite vs crown performs differently (e.g., occlusal-surface-only vs more extensive tooth loss).
  2. Clinically relevant to refine treatment recommendations.
- **Why it may not be appropriate**:
  - Multiple subgroup tests raise multiplicity issues (inflated type I error).
- **When NOT to use**:
  - For very small subgroups with few events (unstable survival estimates).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated**:
  - Incorporate interactions in the **multivariable Cox model** (e.g., treatment × tooth surface loss) instead of multiple unadjusted subgroup comparisons.

#### B4. Competing risk considerations (if other failure causes are recorded)

- **Test name**: Competing risks analysis using **Fine–Gray subdistribution hazard model**.
- **Purpose**:
  - If other competing reasons for tooth loss (e.g., caries, periodontal reasons) or other definitive endpoints are recorded, analyze **unrestorable fracture** as a specific cause in the presence of competing events.
- **Why appropriate**:
  1. Standard Kaplan–Meier may overestimate cause-specific failure if competing events are common.
  2. Fine–Gray subdistribution hazards directly model the cumulative incidence of unrestorable fracture in the presence of competing events.
- **Why it may not be appropriate**:
  - If the only endpoint is defined as unrestorable fracture and other causes of extraction are excluded as per inclusion/exclusion criteria (which is largely the case here), competing risks might be minimal.
- **When NOT to use**:
  - When there are essentially no recorded competing events or competing outcomes are excluded by design.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated**:
  - Use **cause-specific Cox models** as an alternative perspective (hazard of fracture in the presence of competing risk).

#### B5. Sensitivity analyses by follow-up period

- **Test name**: Stratified survival analysis (e.g., separate Cox models or restricted follow-up).
- **Purpose**:
  - Examine whether hazard ratios for crown vs composite differ between:
    - 12–24 months
    - 24–60 months (as mentioned in conclusions).
- **Why appropriate**:
  1. Authors report different significance by follow-up period; modeling this explicitly clarifies whether treatment effect is time-dependent.
  2. Restricting follow-up helps to test early vs late effect hypotheses.
- **Why it may not be appropriate**:
  - Reduces the number of events within each interval, decreasing power.
- **When NOT to use**:
  - When event numbers are insufficient within the intervals to allow stable models.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated**:
  - Use a **Cox model with time-varying treatment effect** (treatment × time interaction) over the full follow-up period.

- **Test name**: Multivariable logistic regression.
- **Purpose**:

- **Why it may not be appropriate**:

- **When NOT to use**:

- **Alternative if assumptions violated**:

#### C2. Time-dependent ROC / discrimination metrics

- **Test name**: Time-dependent ROC curves, C-statistic for survival models.
- **Purpose**:
  - Assess discrimination of multivariable survival model (how well it distinguishes teeth that fracture vs survive over time).
- **Why appropriate**:
  1. Provides a measure of predictive performance of the risk model.
  2. Useful for evaluating clinical utility of risk factors.
- **Why it may not be appropriate**:
  - Requires adequate number of events; can be unstable with small datasets.
- **When NOT to use**:
  - Very low event rates or very small sample size.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated**:
  - Use **Harrell’s C-index** for survival data; simpler but still subject to data limitations.

#### C3. Model calibration tests

- **Test name**: Calibration plots or Hosmer–Lemeshow-type tests adapted to survival models.
- **Purpose**:
  - Evaluate how well predicted survival probabilities agree with observed outcomes.
- **Why appropriate**:
  1. Complements discrimination; a model could have good discrimination but poor calibration.
  2. Important if the goal is to use the model for risk prediction.
- **Why it may not be appropriate**:
  - Requires sufficiently large sample to create meaningful deciles of risk.
- **When NOT to use**:
  - Small datasets where calibration curves become noisy.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated**:
  - Bootstrap calibration or internal validation techniques.

#### C4. Multiple imputation (if missing data present)

- **Test name**: Multiple imputation by chained equations (MICE).
- **Purpose**:
  - Handle missing covariate data (e.g., missing opposing dentition, number of adjacent teeth).
- **Why appropriate**:
  1. Reduces bias and loss of power from complete-case analysis if missing data are non-trivial and plausibly missing at random.
  2. Allows inclusion of all available observations in multivariable models.
- **Why it may not be appropriate**:
  - Complex, requires assumption of missing at random; may be overkill if missingness is minimal.
- **When NOT to use**:
  - Missingness is rare (<5%) or clearly not at random with no auxiliary variables to support MAR.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated**:
  - Sensitivity analysis comparing complete-case analysis with simple imputation (e.g., missing category for categorical variables).

## TASK 3 – INAPPROPRIATE TESTS

Below are commonly misused tests for this type of data and why they should not be used.

### I1. Simple chi-square test on survival status ignoring time

- **Test**: Chi-square comparing proportion of survival/non-survival between crown and composite, **without** accounting for time-to-event or censoring.
- **Why inappropriate**:
  - Follow-up times differ between teeth and between groups; ignoring time and censoring biases comparison.
  - Kaplan–Meier/Cox are standard for such data and provide more informative estimates.

### I2. Independent samples t-test on recall period as “survival analysis”

- **Test**: Comparing mean recall period between fractured vs non-fractured teeth as a proxy for survival.
- **Why inappropriate**:
  - Censored teeth (no fracture at last recall) are not properly handled by a t-test; it assumes complete data.
  - Time-to-event distributions are typically skewed and require survival methods.

### I3. ANOVA on number of events per group without time

- **Test**: ANOVA comparing counts or rates of fractures between groups without accounting for varying exposure time.
- **Why inappropriate**:
  - Does not account for person-time (tooth-time) at risk and censoring.
  - Poisson or survival models are preferable if one wants incidence rates.

### I4. Paired tests (paired t-test, McNemar) for between-group comparison

- **Why inappropriate**:
  - Data are not matched pairs; assignments to crown vs composite are not paired or cross-over; using paired tests would misrepresent the design.

### I5. Standard linear regression on survival time (without transformation or censoring handling)

- **Why inappropriate**:
  - Survival time is right-censored and often non-normal; ordinary least squares regression cannot incorporate censored observations properly.
  - Leads to biased estimates and invalid inference; survival models are required.

### I6. Repeated-measures ANOVA for multiple follow-up times

- **Why inappropriate**:
  - Primary outcome is **time until first event**; repeated measures of fracture status at different times are not the object of interest.
  - Event-time data with censoring are not suited to repeated-measures ANOVA.
