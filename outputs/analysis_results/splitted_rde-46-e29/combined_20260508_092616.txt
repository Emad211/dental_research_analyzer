## CATEGORY A – MANDATORY tests/models

### A1. Kaplan–Meier (KM) survival estimation + log-rank test
- **Purpose:** Estimate and compare **fracture survival curves** between crown vs composite.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Outcome is **time-to-event** with likely **right censoring** at last recall.
  2. Provides **nonparametric** survival estimates and visual comparison over time.
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - If exact **event times** are not available (only “fractured vs not by recall”), KM/log-rank can be biased/misleading.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When follow-up is only interval-censored without reliable event timing and you cannot approximate event times (then use interval-censored methods).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated / data limitations:**
  - If interval-censored: **Turnbull estimator** and **log-rank–type tests for interval-censored data**; or discrete-time survival models.

### A2. Cox proportional hazards regression (multivariable)
- **Purpose:** Estimate adjusted association between restoration type and hazard of **unrestorable fracture**, controlling for covariates.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Standard for **time-to-event** outcomes with censoring; yields interpretable **HRs**.
  2. Allows adjustment for measured confounders (age, sex, tooth type/location, opposing dentition, adjacent teeth).
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - **Proportional hazards (PH) assumption** may not hold (the paper’s conclusion suggests differences mainly at 12–24 months → possible non-PH).
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If strong non-PH exists and you cannot model it (or if event times are unreliable).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Time-varying coefficients** (restoration × time interaction), **stratified Cox**, or **accelerated failure time (AFT)** models; or **restricted mean survival time (RMST)** comparison.

### A3. PH assumption diagnostics (e.g., Schoenfeld residual test + plots)
- **Purpose:** Verify whether Cox PH assumption is reasonable; guide modeling (time-varying effects/stratification).
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Cox model validity depends on PH; diagnostics prevent incorrect inference.
  2. The reported time-specific difference (12–24 months only) suggests potential non-PH.
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - Low event counts reduce power and stability of PH diagnostics.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When not fitting a Cox-type PH model.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Fit models that do not require PH (AFT, RMST, flexible parametric survival).

### A4. Descriptive/baseline group comparisons (not “hypothesis tests” as primary, but required reporting)
- **Tests:**  
  - Categorical: **Chi-square** or **Fisher’s exact**  
  - Continuous: **t-test** or **Wilcoxon rank-sum** (as appropriate)
- **Purpose:** Describe imbalance between crown vs composite groups (confounding by indication).
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Retrospective, non-randomized design: need to document baseline differences.
  2. Helps justify covariate adjustment/propensity modeling.
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - Overemphasis on p-values for baseline balance is discouraged; standardized differences often preferred.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Do not use baseline p-values as evidence of “successful matching” or “no confounding.”
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Use **standardized mean differences (SMDs)** and graphical balance diagnostics.

## CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED tests/models

### B1. Cox model with clustering/within-patient correlation handling (robust SE or frailty)
- **Purpose:** Correct inference if multiple teeth per patient are included.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Teeth within the same patient share behaviors/occlusion/biology → correlated outcomes.
  2. Ignoring clustering underestimates SEs and inflates type I error.
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - If dataset truly has one tooth per patient, adds complexity without benefit.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When clustering is absent or cannot be identified (no patient ID).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Shared frailty Cox** (random effect), or **marginal Cox with robust sandwich SE**.

### B2. Competing risks analysis (Fine–Gray) if extractions/deaths are treated as competing events
- **Purpose:** If teeth can be lost for reasons other than unrestorable fracture (or patient-level loss to follow-up related to tooth condition), estimate subdistribution effects.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. In dental survival, **extraction** can preclude observation of fracture.
  2. Cox censoring assumes non-informative censoring; competing risks relax this.
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - The study **excluded** teeth extracted for other reasons, which may reduce applicability; also event definition is specifically “unrestorable fracture.”
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When competing events are not observed/recorded or were removed by design in a way that makes interpretation unclear.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Cause-specific Cox models; sensitivity analysis treating excluded extractions as competing events if data available.

### B3. Propensity score methods (matching, weighting, or covariate adjustment)
- **Purpose:** Reduce confounding by indication for crown vs composite assignment.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Treatment choice is influenced by prognosis/cost/waitlist → strong confounding.
  2. PS methods improve covariate balance and can complement multivariable Cox.
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - Unmeasured confounding remains; small sample sizes or poor overlap can make PS unstable.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When there is severe lack of common support (positivity violation) or too few events.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Penalized regression with key confounders; trimming non-overlap regions; instrumental variable methods (rarely feasible).

### B4. Interaction (effect modification) testing in survival model
- **Candidates (based on collected variables):**
  - Restoration type × tooth location (upper/lower)
  - Restoration type × tooth type (1st/2nd molar)
  - Restoration type × opposing dentition
  - Restoration type × adjacent teeth count
  - For composites: restoration type × tooth surface loss category (if harmonizable)
- **Purpose:** Assess whether crown benefit differs by clinical context.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Biological plausibility (occlusal forces differ by arch/opponent/contacts).
  2. The paper’s conclusion suggests subgroup relevance (e.g., “occlusal-only loss”).
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - Multiple testing and low power; risk of spurious findings.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When sample size/events per parameter are insufficient.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Pre-specified limited interactions; use hierarchical modeling; present stratified descriptive survival.

### B5. Flexible parametric survival modeling / RMST
- **Purpose:** Address potential non-PH and provide absolute, time-specific effects.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. If hazard difference is time-limited (12–24 months), RMST is robust and interpretable.
  2. Flexible parametric models (Royston–Parmar) can model changing hazards smoothly.
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - Requires more modeling choices; may be unstable with sparse events.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Very small datasets with few events.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Stratified Cox + time-window analyses; discrete-time survival.

## TASK 3: Inappropriate tests (should NOT be used)

1. **Simple independent-samples t-test/chi-square on “survived vs not” ignoring time**
   - **Why not:** Discards censoring and varying follow-up; biased effect estimates.
2. **Binary logistic regression of failure without incorporating follow-up time**
   - **Why not:** Same issue—mixes 12-month and 60-month teeth as equivalent exposure time; odds ratio can be misleading.
   - **If used at all:** only in a **fixed-time** analysis (e.g., 24-month failure among those with ≥24 months follow-up), clearly defined as a secondary/landmark analysis.
3. **Paired tests (paired t-test, McNemar)**
   - **Why not:** Groups are not paired/matched by design (unless an explicit matched PS design is implemented).
4. **Repeated-measures ANOVA / linear regression for the primary endpoint**
   - **Why not:** Outcome is not continuous and is time-to-event; assumptions violated.
5. **Pearson correlation between “recall months” and fracture**
   - **Why not:** Nonlinear/time-to-event structure and censoring make correlation uninterpretable.

#### A3. Log-rank test for group comparison

- **Test name**: Log-rank (Mantel–Cox) test.
- **Purpose**:
  - Compare survival distributions between:
    - Crown vs composite.
    - Potentially other categorical factors (e.g., post type, amount of tooth surface loss categories).
- **Why appropriate**:
  1. Designed to test equality of survival functions in the presence of censoring.
  2. Non-parametric; does not require specifying a functional form of the hazard.
- **Why it may not be appropriate**:
  - Most powerful when hazards are proportional; if hazards cross heavily (e.g., large difference early, none later), interpretation can be difficult, and test may lose power or mislead.
- **When NOT to use**:
  - For covariate-adjusted comparisons (cannot handle multiple covariates).
  - When the sample size within subgroups is very small (few events).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated**:
  - Use **Wilcoxon (Breslow) test** or **Tarone–Ware** test if early vs late differences are of particular interest.
  - Use **Cox proportional hazards model** (adjusted and can handle non-proportional hazards with time-varying effects).

#### A4. Cox proportional hazards regression

- **Test name**: Cox proportional hazards (PH) model.
- **Purpose**:
  - Assess the effect of **restoration type** (crown vs composite) on the hazard of **unrestorable fracture**, adjusting for other risk factors:
    - Age, sex, tooth type, tooth location, opposing dentition, number of adjacent teeth, post type (crown group), amount of tooth surface loss (composite group, if modeled appropriately), etc.
- **Why appropriate**:
  1. Gold-standard semi-parametric model for time-to-event data with censoring; yields hazard ratios (HRs) with 95% CI.
  2. Allows simultaneous adjustment for multiple covariates to control confounding and to identify independent risk factors.
- **Why it may not be appropriate**:
  - Relies on the proportional hazards assumption (log hazard ratio constant over time) for each covariate.
- **When NOT to use**:
  - If proportional hazards assumption is grossly violated and cannot be remedied (e.g., time-varying effects) or if the number of events is too low relative to the number of predictors (overfitting).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated**:
  - If PH violated:
    - Use **time-varying covariates** within Cox (include interaction with time).
    - Use **stratified Cox models** for covariates violating PH.
    - Use **parametric survival models** (e.g., Weibull, log-logistic) or **flexible parametric models**.
  - If events are few:
    - Use **penalized Cox** (Firth correction, ridge/LASSO) or reduce the model to fewer key covariates.

- **Purpose**:

- **Why appropriate**:
  1. Helps interpret observed group differences in survival (imbalance may indicate confounding, affecting causal interpretation).
  2. Guides choice of covariates to adjust for in multivariable models.
- **Why it may not be appropriate**:
  - These tests assume independence of observations; if multiple teeth per patient, clustering may violate this assumption.
- **When NOT to use**:
  - If there is substantial clustering and cluster-level correlation must be accounted for (e.g., many teeth per patient) and you are strictly interested in patient-level comparability; however, for descriptive purposes they are usually still reported.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated**:
  - Use **mixed models** or **GEE** for continuous outcomes to account for clustering when comparing baseline characteristics.

## TASK 3 – INAPPROPRIATE TESTS

Below are commonly misused tests for this type of data and why they should not be used.

### I1. Simple chi-square test on survival status ignoring time

- **Test**: Chi-square comparing proportion of survival/non-survival between crown and composite, **without** accounting for time-to-event or censoring.
- **Why inappropriate**:
  - Follow-up times differ between teeth and between groups; ignoring time and censoring biases comparison.
  - Kaplan–Meier/Cox are standard for such data and provide more informative estimates.

### I2. Independent samples t-test on recall period as “survival analysis”

- **Test**: Comparing mean recall period between fractured vs non-fractured teeth as a proxy for survival.
- **Why inappropriate**:
  - Censored teeth (no fracture at last recall) are not properly handled by a t-test; it assumes complete data.
  - Time-to-event distributions are typically skewed and require survival methods.

### I3. ANOVA on number of events per group without time

- **Test**: ANOVA comparing counts or rates of fractures between groups without accounting for varying exposure time.
- **Why inappropriate**:
  - Does not account for person-time (tooth-time) at risk and censoring.
  - Poisson or survival models are preferable if one wants incidence rates.

### I4. Paired tests (paired t-test, McNemar) for between-group comparison

- **Why inappropriate**:
  - Data are not matched pairs; assignments to crown vs composite are not paired or cross-over; using paired tests would misrepresent the design.

### I5. Standard linear regression on survival time (without transformation or censoring handling)

- **Why inappropriate**:
  - Survival time is right-censored and often non-normal; ordinary least squares regression cannot incorporate censored observations properly.
  - Leads to biased estimates and invalid inference; survival models are required.

### I6. Repeated-measures ANOVA for multiple follow-up times

- **Why inappropriate**:
  - Primary outcome is **time until first event**; repeated measures of fracture status at different times are not the object of interest.
  - Event-time data with censoring are not suited to repeated-measures ANOVA.
