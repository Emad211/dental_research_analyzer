### TASK 1: INFORMATION EXTRACTION

#### Study Characteristics
1. **Study Design**: Retrospective cohort study.
2. **Objective**: To compare the fracture survival rates of endodontically treated molars (ETT) restored with single-unit crowns versus direct resin composite restorations and identify potential risk factors associated with fracture survival.
3. **Groups**:
   - Group 1: ETT restored with single-unit full-coverage crowns.
   - Group 2: ETT restored with direct resin composite restorations.
4. **Sample Size**: Not explicitly provided in the text but derived from dental records spanning 2015–2019.
5. **Variables**:
   - **Independent Variables**:
     - Type of restoration (crown vs. resin composite).
     - Tooth structure loss (occlusal surface only vs. additional loss).
     - Tooth type (first vs. second molars).
     - Tooth location (upper vs. lower molars).
     - Opposing dentition (natural tooth, fixed prosthesis, removable prosthesis, or none).
     - Number of adjacent teeth.
     - Type of post (no post, fiber post, metal post).
   - **Dependent Variable**: Fracture survival (survival vs. non-survival).
6. **Measurements**:
   - Survival criteria:
     - Survival: No fracture or restorable fracture.
     - Non-survival: Unrestorable fracture.
   - Recall period: Minimum of 12 months, with some cases extending up to 60 months.
   - Fracture classification: Restorable vs. unrestorable.
7. **Data Collection Methods**:
   - Clinical and radiographic records from 2015–2019.
   - Information extracted: Age, sex, recall period, tooth type/location, restoration type, opposing dentition, adjacent teeth, tooth structure loss, and post type.

### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY
1. **Test Name**: Chi-Square Test
   - **Purpose**: To assess the association between categorical variables (e.g., restoration type and fracture survival).
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     - Both variables (restoration type and survival) are categorical.
     - Suitable for comparing proportions between groups.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:
     - Assumes independence of observations, which may be violated if clustering exists (e.g., multiple teeth per patient).
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**: If expected frequencies in any cell are <5.
   - **Alternative**: Fisher’s Exact Test (if sample size is small).

2. **Test Name**: Kaplan-Meier Survival Analysis
   - **Purpose**: To estimate survival probabilities over time for molars restored with crowns vs. resin composites.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     - Survival data includes time-to-event information (fracture occurrence).
     - Accounts for censored data (e.g., teeth lost due to other reasons).
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:
     - Assumes proportional hazards, which may not hold.
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**: If survival times are not available or improperly recorded.
   - **Alternative**: Life Table Analysis.

3. **Test Name**: Log-Rank Test
   - **Purpose**: To compare survival distributions between groups (crown vs. resin composite).
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     - Designed for comparing survival curves.
     - Accounts for censored data.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:
     - Sensitive to violations of proportional hazards assumption.
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**: If survival curves cross significantly.
   - **Alternative**: Breslow (Generalized Wilcoxon) Test.

#### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED
1. **Test Name**: Cox Proportional Hazards Regression
   - **Purpose**: To identify risk factors associated with fracture survival while adjusting for covariates (e.g., tooth type, location, opposing dentition).
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     - Handles time-to-event data.
     - Allows inclusion of multiple predictors.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:
     - Assumes proportional hazards, which may not hold.
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**: If proportional hazards assumption is violated.
   - **Alternative**: Stratified Cox Regression.

2. **Test Name**: Logistic Regression
   - **Purpose**: To model the probability of fracture survival (binary outcome) based on predictors like restoration type and tooth structure loss.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     - Suitable for binary outcomes.
     - Can include multiple predictors.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:
     - Does not account for time-to-event data.
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**: If time-to-event data is critical.
   - **Alternative**: Cox Regression.

---

#### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL
1. **Test Name**: Multilevel (Hierarchical) Modeling
   - **Purpose**: To account for clustering effects (e.g., multiple teeth per patient).
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     - Accounts for non-independence of observations.
     - Suitable for nested data structures.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:
     - Requires complex modeling and larger sample sizes.
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**: If sample size is insufficient or clustering is negligible.
   - **Alternative**: Generalized Estimating Equations (GEE).

2. **Test Name**: Subgroup Analysis
   - **Purpose**: To explore survival rates within specific subgroups (e.g., upper vs. lower molars).
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     - Provides detailed insights into subgroup-specific effects.
     - Useful for hypothesis generation.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:
     - Risk of overfitting or false positives due to multiple comparisons.
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**: If sample size within subgroups is too small.
   - **Alternative**: Pooling subgroups for broader analysis.
