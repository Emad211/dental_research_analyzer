CATEGORY A – MANDATORY ANALYSES  

A1. Descriptive statistics and baseline comparability  

- Test name  
  - Descriptive summaries; ANOVA or Kruskal–Wallis (for continuous baseline variables), Chi‑square or Fisher’s exact test (for categorical baseline variables).  

- Purpose  
  - Summarize and assess balance of baseline demographic and clinical characteristics across the four randomized groups.  

- Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
  1) Randomization should create comparable groups; presenting baseline comparability is essential to support internal validity and to interpret treatment effects.  
  2) Identifying any baseline imbalances (even by chance) informs whether adjusted analyses may be needed (e.g., using baseline as covariate).  

- Why it may not be appropriate  
  - Formal hypothesis tests for baseline balance are sometimes discouraged because any observed differences are due to randomization; they can be misinterpreted.  

- When this test should NOT be used  
  - Not to “decide” if randomization was successful or to selectively adjust only imbalanced covariates (this can bias inference).  

- Alternative if assumptions are violated  
  - If normality or homoscedasticity assumptions do not hold for continuous variables, use non‑parametric tests (Kruskal–Wallis) or simply show descriptive summaries (means/SDs, medians/IQRs) without formal p‑values.

A2. Primary clinical outcome analysis: change in PD and rCAL over time  

Given repeated measures at baseline, 3, 6, 12 months in a 4‑group trial with factorial structure, the proper approach is a linear mixed‑effects model.

- Test name  
  - Linear mixed‑effects model (LMM) for repeated measures (or, alternatively, repeated‑measures ANOVA with Greenhouse–Geisser correction).  

- Purpose  
  - Compare trajectories of periodontal parameters (especially PD and rCAL) across groups over time, accounting for within‑subject correlation and baseline values.  

- Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
  1) Handles correlated repeated measurements on the same tooth/patient, using random intercepts (and optionally random slopes), providing correct standard errors and p‑values.  
  2) Accommodates unbalanced data and dropouts under missing‑at‑random (MAR), which is realistic in a 12‑month clinical trial.  
  3) Allows explicit modelling of treatment main effects (GP vs MTA; graft vs no graft) and time × treatment interactions (differential change over time).  

- Why it may not be appropriate  
  - Requires assumptions of approximate normality of residuals and linearity in time; also requires MAR for valid inference if there is attrition.  

- When this test should NOT be used  
  - If outcome distributions are highly skewed with severe ceiling/floor effects not correctable by transformation.  
  - If missingness is clearly not MAR (e.g., subjects with poor response systematically drop out) and cannot be appropriately modelled.  

- Alternative if assumptions are violated  
  - For non‑normal continuous outcomes: apply transformations (log, square‑root) or use robust LMM (e.g., with sandwich estimators) or generalized estimating equations (GEE) with appropriate working correlation and robust standard errors.  
  - If linearity in time is not appropriate: model time as categorical in LMM or use non‑linear mixed models.  

Implementation details:  
- Model PD and rCAL separately as primary outcomes.  
- Fixed effects:  
  - Time (categorical: baseline, 3, 6, 12 months).  
  - Obturation material (GP vs MTA).  
  - Bone graft (no vs yes).  
  - Time × material interaction, time × graft interaction; optionally material × graft and three‑way interaction.  
  - Baseline value can be included explicitly (ANCOVA‑type LMM) or time factor centered at baseline.  
- Random effects: patient‑specific intercept (and optionally slope).  
- Outcomes reported:  
  - Estimated marginal means (EMMs) and 95% CIs at each time point by treatment, and contrasts for change from baseline to 12 months.  
  - p‑values for main treatment effects and interactions.

A3. Between‑group comparisons at key timepoints (e.g., 12‑month PD and rCAL)  

Even with LMM, regulators and clinicians often want a simple comparison at the final timepoint.

- Test name  
  - ANCOVA at 12 months for PD and rCAL (continuous outcomes).  

- Purpose  
  - Compare groups on 12‑month PD and rCAL, adjusting for baseline values; provide clinically intuitive estimates of final status and treatment effect.  

- Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
  1) ANCOVA controls for baseline differences and is more powerful and less biased than using change scores alone.  
  2) Many clinicians understand effect sizes at the final visit; this serves as a key primary/secondary endpoint summary.  

- Why it may not be appropriate  
  - If the relationship between baseline and follow‑up is highly non‑linear or interactions between baseline and treatment are substantial, the simple ANCOVA might misrepresent treatment effect.  

- When this test should NOT be used  
  - If baseline measures are missing for many subjects or measured inconsistently.  
  - If substantial deviation from normality and homoscedasticity persists after reasonable transformations.  

- Alternative if assumptions are violated  
  - Non‑parametric ANCOVA approaches (e.g., Quade’s rank ANCOVA).  
  - Compare change scores using non‑parametric tests (Kruskal–Wallis or Mann–Whitney) as a secondary approach.  

Note: This can be done as 4‑group ANCOVA or as 2×2 factorial ANCOVA (GP vs MTA; graft vs no graft).

A4. Radiographic success analysis at 6 and 12 months  

Radiographic success is defined as CBCTPAI 0 or 1 (success vs failure).

- Test name  
  - Chi‑square test (or Fisher’s exact test) across 4 groups.  
  - Logistic regression for factorial contrasts.  

- Purpose  
  - Compare success proportions between treatment arms; evaluate effects of obturation material, bone grafting, and their interaction on radiographic outcomes.  

- Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
  1) Radiographic success is binary; chi‑square provides a simple unadjusted comparison; logistic regression allows adjustment for covariates (e.g., age, baseline CBCTPAI, defect size).  
  2) Logistic regression can estimate odds ratios with 95% CIs for GP vs MTA and graft vs no graft, which are clinically interpretable.  

- Why it may not be appropriate  
  - Chi‑square requires expected cell counts ≥5; if groups are small or success/failure is rare, the approximation may be poor.  
  - Logistic regression can be unstable with small sample size or complete separation (e.g., one group with 100% success).  

- When this test should NOT be used  
  - Chi‑square: not for sparse data; use Fisher’s exact instead.  
  - Logistic regression: not when events per variable are very low (risk of overfitting).  

- Alternative if assumptions are violated  
  - Fisher’s exact test for 2×2 comparisons.  
  - Penalized logistic regression (Firth correction) if separation occurs.  
  - Non‑parametric trend tests for ordered groups (if an ordered hypothesis is meaningful, e.g., GP < MTA < GP+graft < MTA+graft).

A5. Analysis of CBCTPAI as an ordinal outcome  

- Test name  
  - Ordinal logistic regression (proportional odds model).  

- Purpose  
  - Use the full ordinal information of CBCTPAI (not only dichotomized success) to compare the distribution of periapical healing across treatments.  

- Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
  1) CBCTPAI is an ordered categorical scale; ordinal logistic regression respects the ordinality and typically has more power than binary dichotomization.  
  2) Can adjust for baseline CBCTPAI and other covariates, providing adjusted proportional odds ratios.  

- Why it may not be appropriate  
  - Proportional odds assumption (same treatment effect across all cutpoints) may be violated.  

- When this test should NOT be used  
  - If sample size is too small in some CBCTPAI categories, leading to unstable estimates.  
  - If the proportional odds assumption is clearly violated and cannot be reasonably relaxed.  

- Alternative if assumptions are violated  
  - Partial proportional odds model.  
  - Collapsing categories (e.g., 0–1 vs 2–3 vs 4–5) and using multinomial logistic regression.  

---

A6. Handling missing data  

- Approach  
  - Primary analysis: Linear mixed‑effects models inherently handle missing outcome data under MAR.  
  - Radiographic outcomes: Multiple imputation (MI) if missingness is >5–10%, imputing CBCTPAI and success status using baseline and available clinical data.  

- Rationale  
  1) MAR is plausible: dropout related to observed previous responses rather than unobserved future responses.  
  2) MI avoids loss of power and bias from complete‑case analysis.  

- When NOT to rely on MAR  
  - If strong evidence suggests missing not at random (MNAR), e.g., patients with worsening PD drop out; then sensitivity analyses with MNAR models (pattern mixture models) are warranted.

TASK 3 – INAPPROPRIATE TESTS  

Below are tests that should generally NOT be used for this study and why.

1. Multiple independent t‑tests without adjustment  
- Why inappropriate  
  - Comparing each pair of groups separately for each timepoint and outcome with unadjusted t‑tests would severely inflate Type I error and ignore factorial design and repeated‑measures structure.  

2. Simple pre–post paired t‑tests within each group without between‑group comparison  
- Why inappropriate  
  - Demonstrates improvement within groups (expected due to treatment) but does not answer the trial question: are groups different in their changes?  
  - Can misleadingly suggest all treatments are “effective” without showing relative efficacy.  

3. Ignoring repeated measures and using ordinary least squares (OLS) regression treating all observations as independent  
- Why inappropriate  
  - Violates independence assumption; underestimates standard errors; yields anti‑conservative p‑values.  

4. Using one‑way ANOVA on change scores at multiple timepoints without accounting for baseline and within‑patient correlation  
- Why inappropriate  
  - Loses efficiency compared to ANCOVA; repeated measures tested separately at each time earn multiple testing issues.  

5. Parametric tests on ordinal CBCTPAI treating it as continuous without justification  
- Why inappropriate  
  - The CBCTPAI categories are ordinal with potentially unequal spacing; standard linear models assume equal distance between categories, which may not hold.  

6. Use of non‑parametric tests (e.g., Friedman, Wilcoxon) as the primary analytical framework for all continuous outcomes in this randomized trial  
- Why inappropriate  
  - They cannot easily model factorial structure, interactions, or covariates; less powerful than LMM/ANCOVA when normality is approximately met.  

7. Stepwise regression model building based on p‑values  
- Why inappropriate  
  - Data‑driven variable selection leads to biased estimates and overfitting in modest samples. Randomization already balances covariates; extensive adjustment is unnecessary.  

8. Over‑stratified subgroup analyses with separate models per subgroup  
- Why inappropriate  
  - With n=30 per group, splitting into multiple subgroups yields very small cell sizes and unstable estimates.  

9. Using survival analysis for PD or rCAL directly (as if they were time‑to‑event)  
- Why inappropriate  
  - PD and rCAL are continuous clinical measures, not event times; survival methods do not apply.
