# CATEGORY A — MANDATORY tests/analyses

## A1. Cohen’s kappa (weighted where appropriate)
**Purpose**
- Quantify agreement **beyond chance** between each observer and the **reference standard** for **categorical** outcomes.

**Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)**
- Agreement vs reference is the core study aim.
- Corrects for agreement expected by chance; suitable for binary outcomes like **periapical lesion present/absent** (at tooth-level) or categorized counts (if categorized).

**Why may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)**
- Kappa is sensitive to **prevalence** and **marginal imbalance** (“kappa paradox”), especially if lesions are rare.

**When NOT to use**
- When the variable is truly **continuous/count** with many values and you want agreement on the numeric scale (use ICC/Bland–Altman instead).
- When categories are extremely imbalanced and kappa becomes misleading without additional indices.

**Alternative if assumptions/limitations**
- Report **Gwet’s AC1/AC2** or **prevalence-adjusted bias-adjusted kappa (PABAK)** as a sensitivity analysis.
- Also report **percent agreement** and positive/negative agreement.

---

## A2. Intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC) for counts/scores (two-way, absolute agreement)
**Purpose**
- Quantify agreement between each observer and reference for **numeric** variables: **remaining teeth count, root-filled teeth count, DMFT, number of teeth with periapical lesions (count)**.

**Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)**
- These outcomes are numeric and assessed on the same set of PRs.
- ICC (absolute agreement) captures both correlation and systematic differences (bias) between observer and reference.

**Why may not be appropriate**
- ICC can be distorted if there is **restricted range** of values across PRs (low between-PR variability).
- Not ideal if distributions are highly skewed with many zeros (e.g., periapical lesion counts).

**When NOT to use**
- For binary outcomes (use kappa/AC1).
- When agreement is better expressed as **measurement error** and bias across the range (use Bland–Altman).

**Alternative if assumptions/limitations**
- Use **Lin’s concordance correlation coefficient (CCC)**.
- Use **Bland–Altman** with appropriate transformation or count-based modeling for heavy skew.

---

## A3. Bland–Altman analysis (observer vs reference) for numeric outcomes
**Purpose**
- Evaluate **systematic bias** (mean difference) and **limits of agreement** for numeric variables (DMFT, counts).

**Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)**
- Reliability studies should show not only a single coefficient but also the **magnitude of disagreement** in clinically interpretable units (e.g., ± teeth).
- Detects proportional bias (differences increasing with higher scores).

**Why may not be appropriate**
- Standard Bland–Altman assumes approximately **constant variance** of differences; counts often show heteroscedasticity.

**When NOT to use**
- If the outcome is categorical/binary.
- If differences are strongly heteroscedastic and not addressable via transformation (unless using advanced BA methods).

**Alternative if assumptions violated**
- **Log/variance-stabilizing transformation** (e.g., log(x+1)).
- **Regression-based Bland–Altman** (model difference vs mean) or **Poisson/negative binomial mixed models** for count disagreement.

---

## A4. Paired comparison of agreement metrics pre- vs post-calibration (and 3 months)
**Test name**
- For binary/tooth-level agreement: **McNemar test** on discordant pairs (observer vs reference) *within the same units* to test change in misclassification rate across timepoints.
- For numeric absolute error (e.g., |observer−reference|): **Wilcoxon signed-rank test** (or paired t-test if approximately normal) comparing baseline vs post-calibration and baseline vs 3 months.

**Purpose**
- Establish whether calibration **improved** agreement (not just describe it).

**Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)**
- Same PRs are rated repeatedly ⇒ paired design.
- Directly addresses the study aim of improvement due to calibration and persistence at 3 months.

**Why may not be appropriate**
- McNemar requires paired binary outcomes on the same unit; if only radiograph-level counts are available, McNemar is not applicable.
- Multiple comparisons across outcomes/observers inflate type I error if unaddressed.

**When NOT to use**
- When assessments are not paired (e.g., different PRs).
- When outcome is not binary for McNemar or not suitable for paired rank/t tests.

**Alternative if assumptions violated**
- Use **generalized estimating equations (GEE)** or **mixed-effects logistic/Poisson models** with timepoint as predictor and clustering by PR (and tooth, if tooth-level).

## TASK 3: Inappropriate tests (should NOT be used) and why

1. **Independent-samples t-test / one-way ANOVA** comparing pre vs post
   - Wrong because measurements are **paired/repeated** on the same PRs/observers; ignores correlation and misstates SEs.

2. **Pearson correlation alone** (observer vs reference)
   - Correlation measures association, not agreement; can be high with systematic bias (e.g., consistently overcounting).

3. **Chi-square test of independence** for pre vs post agreement tables
   - Treats observations as independent; should be **McNemar** (paired) or a repeated-measures model.

4. **Simple linear regression of observer on reference** as the primary reliability analysis
   - Again assesses association; does not directly quantify agreement or limits of disagreement; residual structure violates assumptions due to repeated measures.

5. **Using only percent agreement** without chance-corrected metrics
   - Can be misleading under class imbalance (likely for periapical lesions).

## TASK 3 – INAPPROPRIATE TESTS

Below are tests that **should not** be used for this study’s primary aims, with justification.

### I1. Simple Pearson correlation (r) between observers and reference for counts

- **Why inappropriate:**
  - Correlation measures **linear association**, not agreement. Two observers can correlate highly while having large systematic bias.
  - The main question is reliability (agreement), not just whether high or low values go together.
- **Prefer:** ICC, Bland–Altman plots, and bias tests.

### I2. Independent samples t-tests or chi-square tests comparing observers

- **Why inappropriate:**
  - Observers evaluate the **same radiographs**; measurements are paired, not independent.
  - Independent-samples tests ignore pairing and within-subject correlation, leading to incorrect inference.
- **Prefer:** Paired tests (McNemar, paired t-test/Wilcoxon), ICC, kappa.

### I3. One-way ANOVA for comparing mean counts across observers

- **Why inappropriate:**
  - Same radiographs are assessed repeatedly → repeated measures design; one-way ANOVA assumes independent groups.
  - A single observer’s repeated counts over time are also within-subject, violating independence.
- **Prefer:** Repeated measures ANOVA or mixed-effects models if needed; more commonly ICC and BA analysis.

### I4. Linear regression treating observer as predictor of “true” count

- **Why inappropriate:**
  - Regression of reference counts on observer counts does not measure reliability; slope and intercept reflect calibration but not agreement metrics.
  - Errors-in-variables issues: both measurements have error.
- **Prefer:** ICC, Bland–Altman, calibration plots if needed.

### I5. Survival analysis methods (Kaplan–Meier, Cox regression)

- **Why inappropriate:**
  - No time-to-event outcomes; study is cross-sectional with repeated ratings, not longitudinal survival.
- **Prefer:** Not applicable.

### I6. Standard diagnostic test meta-analysis methods

- **Why inappropriate:**
  - Single study, not multiple independent studies; meta-analytic pooling is unnecessary.
- **Prefer:** Direct reporting of diagnostic measures per observer and time.

### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

Given the mix of categorical (lesions, root fillings) and continuous/count data (DMFT, remaining teeth), as well as the repeated-measures design, the following statistical tests are recommended.

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY (Essential for completing the analysis)

**1. Cohen’s Kappa (κ)**
*   **Purpose:** To measure the exact agreement between each individual observer and the reference standard for categorical variables (periapical lesions, root-filled teeth) at each time point.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) It accounts for agreement occurring by chance, which is critical in diagnostic imaging where disease prevalence may be low. 
    2) It is the gold standard for evaluating binary rater agreement against a known reference.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It can be artificially lowered by the "Kappa paradox" if the prevalence of the condition (e.g., periapical lesions) is extremely high or extremely low in the sample.
*   **When NOT to use:** Do not use for continuous variables like the DMFT score.
*   **Alternative:** Gwet’s AC1 statistic (more robust to prevalence imbalances).

**2. Intraclass Correlation Coefficient (ICC) - Two-Way Mixed Effects, Absolute Agreement**
*   **Purpose:** To evaluate the reliability between each observer and the reference standard for continuous/count variables (DMFT score, number of remaining teeth).
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) It measures absolute agreement, not just correlation, penalizing for systematic bias (e.g., if an observer consistently undercounts teeth). 
    2) It is specifically designed for continuous data assessed by multiple raters.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It assumes the data is normally distributed; DMFT scores in a specific cohort (like myocardial infarction patients) might be heavily skewed.
*   **When NOT to use:** Do not use for binary categorical data (presence/absence of lesions).
*   **Alternative:** Lin’s Concordance Correlation Coefficient (CCC) or non-parametric alternatives for reliability.

**3. Z-test for Comparing Kappa Coefficients (or evaluation of 95% Confidence Intervals)**
*   **Purpose:** To statistically test if the *change* in agreement (Kappa) from T1 to T2, and T2 to T3, is statistically significant.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) The primary objective is to prove that calibration *improves* reliability; simply showing a higher Kappa number is insufficient without proving statistical significance. 
    2) It allows for direct comparison of observer performance across time.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** Standard Z-tests for Kappa assume independent samples, but these are the *same* radiographs evaluated by the *same* observers over time (paired data).
*   **When NOT to use:** When comparing completely different, non-overlapping datasets where variance cannot be pooled.
*   **Alternative:** Bootstrapping to generate empirical 95% confidence intervals for the *difference* in Kappas.

#### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED (Enhances quality and rigor)

**4. Diagnostic Accuracy Metrics (Sensitivity, Specificity, PPV, NPV)**
*   **Purpose:** To determine *how* the observers improved. Did calibration reduce false positives (improving specificity) or reduce false negatives (improving sensitivity)?
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) The study utilizes a "reference standard," allowing the analysis to move beyond mere agreement into actual diagnostic performance. 
    2) The methodology notes that observers were told to adjust their thresholds (e.g., Observer 1 was told to lower the threshold, Observers 2 & 3 to raise it). Sensitivity/specificity will prove if these specific instructions worked.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** The "reference standard" is a consensus of two experts on a panoramic radiograph, which the authors admit has low sensitivity compared to CBCT. It is an imperfect gold standard.
*   **When NOT to use:** If a true reference standard (like histology or CBCT) is completely absent and consensus cannot be trusted.
*   **Alternative:** Latent Class Analysis (LCA) to estimate diagnostic accuracy without a perfect gold standard.

**5. Bland-Altman Analysis**
*   **Purpose:** To visually and quantitatively assess the agreement between observers and the reference standard for the DMFT score.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) It clearly illustrates the mean bias (e.g., if students systematically score DMFT higher than experts). 
    2) It establishes 95% limits of agreement, providing clinical context to the errors.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** DMFT scores are discrete counts (integers), which can cause data points on a Bland-Altman plot to overlap heavily, making visual interpretation difficult without jittering.
*   **When NOT to use:** For binary variables (periapical lesions).
*   **Alternative:** Mountain plots (folded empirical cumulative distribution plots).

#### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL (Supplementary insights)

**6. Fleiss’ Kappa**
*   **Purpose:** To measure the inter-observer agreement *among the three test observers* (excluding the reference standard).
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) It answers a secondary question: Did calibration make the observers agree more with *each other*, standardizing the diagnostic process? 
    2) It handles multiple (>2) raters simultaneously.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** The primary aim is agreement with the *reference standard*, not peer-to-peer agreement.
*   **When NOT to use:** When comparing only two raters (use Cohen's Kappa instead).
*   **Alternative:** Light’s Kappa (average of all pairwise Cohen's Kappas).

**7. Generalized Estimating Equations (GEE) or Generalized Linear Mixed Models (GLMM)**
*   **Purpose:** To model the probability of a correct diagnosis (matching the reference) over time, accounting for the clustering of teeth within patients.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) Periapical lesions are assessed per tooth. A single panoramic radiograph contains up to 32 teeth. Teeth within the same patient's mouth are not statistically independent. 
    2) GEE accounts for this intra-subject correlation.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It is highly complex and may overcomplicate a study primarily aimed at a clinical dental audience accustomed to basic Kappa statistics.
*   **When NOT to use:** If the sample size of clusters (patients) is too small (not an issue here with N=100).
*   **Alternative:** Standard logistic regression with robust standard errors.
