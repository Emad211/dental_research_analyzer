## TASK 2 – STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

### Overarching approach

- Treat the **reference standard** as the “truth” for purposes of reliability.
- Assess:
  1. Intra‑observer reliability (same person, different time).
  2. Inter‑observer reliability (between each observer and reference; between observers).
  3. Effect of calibration on reliability (pre vs post vs 3‑month follow-up).
- Use:
  - Agreement measures suitable for **categorical / binary** data at tooth level and count / continuous data at PR level.
  - Paired comparisons across time points.
  - Graphical and descriptive summaries.

Below, tests are grouped into mandatory, recommended, and optional.

---

### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS

#### A1. Cohen’s kappa (κ) for binary tooth-level variables

- **Test name:** Cohen’s kappa (for 2 raters); weighted κ not necessary for binary.
- **Purpose:**  
  To quantify agreement beyond chance between two raters for binary classifications:
  - Tooth present vs absent.
  - Root-filled vs not.
  - Periapical lesion vs none.
  - Decayed/missing/filled for DMFT components (if analyzed at tooth level).

- **Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):**
  1. Primary data at tooth level are **categorical (binary)**, and kappa is standard for inter- and intra‑observer agreement for such data.
  2. Study explicitly concerns **observer reliability**, where κ is commonly used and interpretable with existing benchmarks (e.g. Landis & Koch, ref. 15).
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason):**
  - If prevalence is very high or very low (e.g., very few periapical lesions), κ can be low despite high raw agreement (prevalence paradox).
- **When this test should NOT be used:**
  - When categories are ordinal with >2 levels where partial agreement should be differentially weighted.
  - When counts per category are extremely unbalanced leading to unstable κ.
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
  - Use **prevalence-adjusted bias-adjusted kappa (PABAK)** or **Gwet’s AC1** to mitigate prevalence/bias effects.
  - Report **percent agreement** alongside κ to give context.

Apply separately:
- Observer vs reference standard at each time point.
- Observer at time 1 vs time 2 vs time 3 (intra‑observer).
- Observer 1 vs observer 2, etc. (inter‑observer).

---

#### A2. Intraclass Correlation Coefficient (ICC) for PR-level counts (e.g., number of root-filled teeth, DMFT)

- **Test name:** Intraclass Correlation Coefficient (ICC), two-way mixed effects, absolute agreement, single rater (ICC(3,1)).
- **Purpose:**  
  To quantify the degree of absolute agreement between numerical counts obtained by different raters or by the same rater over time:
  - Number of remaining teeth per PR.
  - Number of root-filled teeth per PR.
  - Number of teeth with periapical lesions per PR.
  - DMFT score per PR.

- **Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):**
  1. Outcomes at PR level are **continuous/count** measures; ICC is the standard reliability metric for such data.
  2. Multiple raters with a fixed set of raters (observers and reference) fit a **two-way mixed model**, and “absolute agreement” is the relevant form because exact match is desired.
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason):**
  - Count data may be skewed, overdispersed, and potentially zero-inflated; ICC assumes approximately normally distributed measurement errors and homoscedasticity.
- **When this test should NOT be used:**
  - When data are heavily skewed with many zeros and small sample size such that distributional assumptions are badly violated.
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
  - Transform the data (e.g., square root or log+1) and recompute ICC.
  - Use **Spearman’s rank correlation** or **Kendall’s tau** as non-parametric measures of association, with the understanding they measure association, not strict agreement.
  - For discrete counts with small ranges, use **weighted kappa** with categories grouped.

---

#### A3. McNemar’s test for pre–post calibration comparison at tooth level

- **Test name:** McNemar’s test for paired binary data.
- **Purpose:**  
  To test whether the proportion of correct classifications (agreement with reference standard) changed significantly **before vs after calibration** (as well as vs 3‑month follow-up) for each observer.

  Example: For each tooth, classify as “correct vs incorrect” compared to reference at time 1 vs time 2.

- **Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):**
  1. The same teeth are assessed by the same observer at two time points → paired binary outcome (correct/incorrect).
  2. McNemar’s test is designed for testing changes in paired proportions, exactly matching this design (pre–post).
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason):**
  - If tooth-level observations are **clustered within PRs/patients**, the assumption of independent pairs is violated (within-patient correlation).
- **When this test should NOT be used:**
  - When there are multiple dependent observations per subject and clustering is substantial, making simple McNemar invalid.
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
  - Use **generalized estimating equations (GEE)** with a logit link for repeated measures clustered within patient/PR.
  - Use **clustered (subject-level) McNemar’s test** or permutation approaches that respect clustering.

#### A4. Descriptive statistics and graphical summaries

(Not a “test” per se but mandatory components)

- **Purpose:**
  - To characterize distributions of counts and agreement measures across observers and time points.
  - Provide context for inferential tests and clinical interpretation.

- **Methods:**
  - Mean, SD, median, IQR for count outcomes per observer and time.
  - Proportions and 95% CIs for correct classification vs reference.
  - Bland–Altman plots for PR‑level counts (e.g., difference between observer and reference vs mean).
  - Plots of κ and ICC over time to show calibration effect.

- **Why appropriate:**
  - Essential to interpret the magnitude and variability of disagreement.
  - Help detect systematic bias (over- or underestimation) not visible from single statistics.

### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED TESTS

#### B1. Fleiss’ kappa or multi-rater kappa (for >2 raters)

- **Test name:** Fleiss’ kappa (or other multi-rater kappa variants).
- **Purpose:**  
  To quantify agreement beyond chance among **multiple raters simultaneously** for categorical outcomes (e.g., periapical lesion present/absent at tooth level).

- **Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):**
  1. The study involves multiple raters at some stages (three observers + reference standard), where a single global measure of agreement is informative.
  2. Allows comparison of overall agreement pre- vs post‑calibration at the group level.
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason):**
  - Requires all raters to evaluate the same set of units; if some raters did not assess the same PRs or teeth, the method becomes more complex or infeasible.
- **When this test should NOT be used:**
  - When there is incomplete or unbalanced data (not all raters rate all items).
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
  - Restrict analysis to subsets with complete ratings.
  - Use **Gwet’s AC1/AC2** for multiple raters (more robust to prevalence).

---

#### B2. Bland–Altman analysis for continuous/count outcomes vs reference

- **Test name:** Bland–Altman method for agreement.
- **Purpose:**  
  To examine bias and limits of agreement between observers and the reference standard for PR‑level counts (remaining teeth, root-filled teeth, DMFT, etc.).

- **Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):**
  1. Agreement in **numerical counts** is central; BA plots show mean difference and spread, helping distinguish systematic from random error.
  2. Applicable regardless of distribution shape (though interpretation is easier with roughly symmetric differences).
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason):**
  - When counts have narrow range and many ties, limits of agreement may be difficult to interpret.
- **When this test should NOT be used:**
  - For strictly categorical data (e.g., tooth-level lesion yes/no).
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
  - Use **ICC** only, supplemented by histograms of differences.
  - For skewed differences, consider **log transformation** before BA analysis.

---

#### B3. Generalized linear mixed models (GLMM) or GEE for clustered binary outcomes

- **Test name:** GLMM with logit link or GEE with exchangeable correlation.
- **Purpose:**  
  To model the probability of correct classification (observer vs reference) and/or lesion detection while taking into account:
  - Repeated measures on teeth nested within PR/patient.
  - Repeated assessments over time within observers.
  - Effect of calibration (time as factor).

- **Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):**
  1. Tooth-level data are **clustered** within PRs; GLMM/GEE appropriately handle intracluster correlation.
  2. Calibration is an **intervention over time**; GLMM/GEE can formally test the effect of time (pre vs post vs follow-up) on accuracy.
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason):**
  - Complexity and need for larger effective sample size; may be overkill if the primary objective is descriptive reliability rather than causal inference.
- **When this test should NOT be used:**
  - When the number of clusters (PRs/patients) is too small to reliably estimate random effects or robust variances.
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
  - Use **cluster-robust standard errors** with simpler logistic regression.
  - Use aggregated data (per PR: number of correctly vs incorrectly classified teeth) with binomial models.

---

#### B4. Paired t-test or Wilcoxon signed-rank test for systematic bias in counts

- **Test name:** Paired t-test (if approximately normal) or Wilcoxon signed-rank test (non-parametric).
- **Purpose:**  
  To test if there is a systematic difference between observer and reference standard in counts (e.g., DMFT) pre vs post calibration, or between time points.

- **Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):**
  1. For each PR, we can compute difference (observer – reference); paired tests check whether mean/median difference is zero.
  2. Helps quantify whether calibration eliminates systematic over‑ or underestimation.
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason):**
  - For highly skewed distributions or non-independent observations (same observer on same cases multiple times).
- **When this test should NOT be used:**
  - When the distribution of differences is markedly non-symmetric and sample size is very small.
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
  - Use **sign test** for median difference.
  - Use **mixed-effects models** for repeated measures over time.

### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL TESTS

#### C1. Sensitivity, specificity, PPV, NPV vs reference standard

- **Test name:** Diagnostic accuracy measures (sensitivity, specificity, positive/negative predictive values) with confidence intervals.
- **Purpose:**  
  To quantify diagnostic performance of each observer at each time point using the reference standard as “truth” for periapical lesion presence.

- **Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):**
  1. Directly addresses the clinical meaning of calibration: proportion of true lesions detected (sensitivity) and non-lesions correctly classified (specificity).
  2. Enables comparison of diagnostic performance pre vs post calibration.
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason):**
  - The reference standard is itself based on expert consensus, not a biological gold standard; so these measures describe agreement rather than true diagnostic accuracy.
- **When this test should NOT be used:**
  - When misinterpreted as absolute diagnostic accuracy against histopathology.
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
  - Use **likelihood ratios** or **Youden’s index** as complementary measures.

---

#### C2. Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) analysis

- **Test name:** ROC curve, Area Under the Curve (AUC).
- **Purpose:**  
  If observers applied any graded confidence (e.g., borderline lesions), ROC analysis could assess discrimination; even with binary calls, one can conceptually compare thresholds among observers.

- **Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):**
  1. Useful if observers can be instructed to apply varying thresholds (e.g., “liberal” vs “strict”) in future work.
  2. AUC summarizes discrimination independent of specific cut-point.
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason):**
  - In the present study, assessment appears binary without explicit confidence scores, limiting ROC’s utility.
- **When this test should NOT be used:**
  - When only a single binary decision rule exists per observer and no continuous or ordinal scores are recorded.
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
  - Omit ROC and focus on sensitivity/specificity at the implemented threshold.

---

#### C3. Stratified analyses by radiograph type (film vs digital) or other covariates

- **Test name:** Stratified κ/ICC calculations; interaction tests in GLMM/GEE.
- **Purpose:**  
  To explore whether reliability differs by imaging modality (film vs digital), MI vs control status, or other subgroups.

- **Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):**
  1. Different technical quality may affect lesion detection; stratified analyses can reveal such effects.
  2. Helps assess generalizability of calibration across contexts.
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason):**
  - May be underpowered if subgroup sizes are small.
- **When this test should NOT be used:**
  - When subgroup sample sizes are insufficient for stable estimates.
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
  - Pool groups but include type as a covariate in mixed models, or report descriptively only.

## TASK 3 – INAPPROPRIATE TESTS

Below are tests that **should not** be used for this study’s primary aims, with justification.

### I1. Simple Pearson correlation (r) between observers and reference for counts

- **Why inappropriate:**
  - Correlation measures **linear association**, not agreement. Two observers can correlate highly while having large systematic bias.
  - The main question is reliability (agreement), not just whether high or low values go together.
- **Prefer:** ICC, Bland–Altman plots, and bias tests.

### I2. Independent samples t-tests or chi-square tests comparing observers

- **Why inappropriate:**
  - Observers evaluate the **same radiographs**; measurements are paired, not independent.
  - Independent-samples tests ignore pairing and within-subject correlation, leading to incorrect inference.
- **Prefer:** Paired tests (McNemar, paired t-test/Wilcoxon), ICC, kappa.

### I3. One-way ANOVA for comparing mean counts across observers

- **Why inappropriate:**
  - Same radiographs are assessed repeatedly → repeated measures design; one-way ANOVA assumes independent groups.
  - A single observer’s repeated counts over time are also within-subject, violating independence.
- **Prefer:** Repeated measures ANOVA or mixed-effects models if needed; more commonly ICC and BA analysis.

### I4. Linear regression treating observer as predictor of “true” count

- **Why inappropriate:**
  - Regression of reference counts on observer counts does not measure reliability; slope and intercept reflect calibration but not agreement metrics.
  - Errors-in-variables issues: both measurements have error.
- **Prefer:** ICC, Bland–Altman, calibration plots if needed.

### I5. Survival analysis methods (Kaplan–Meier, Cox regression)

- **Why inappropriate:**
  - No time-to-event outcomes; study is cross-sectional with repeated ratings, not longitudinal survival.
- **Prefer:** Not applicable.

### I6. Standard diagnostic test meta-analysis methods

- **Why inappropriate:**
  - Single study, not multiple independent studies; meta-analytic pooling is unnecessary.
- **Prefer:** Direct reporting of diagnostic measures per observer and time.
