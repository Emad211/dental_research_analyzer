A. MICROBIOLOGICAL COUNTS (CFU/ml)  

Nature of data:  
- CFU/ml, highly skewed, spanning several orders of magnitude.  
- Repeated measures (S1, S2, S3) within each root/tooth.  
- Group comparisons over time.  

Transformations / preprocessing  
- Apply log₁₀(CFU + c) transformation (e.g., c=1 or 0.5) to normalize distributions and stabilize variance.  
- Alternatively, model counts via generalized linear models (GLMs) with Poisson or negative binomial distribution if raw counts are used.  

Primary microbiology analysis  

1) Change in bacterial load over time within and between groups  

Mandatory test A1: Repeated-measures ANOVA or linear mixed model on log₁₀ CFU  

- Test name:  
  - Prefer: Linear mixed-effects model (LMM) with random intercept for subject (and possibly for root within subject), and fixed effects for group, time (S1, S2, S3), and group×time interaction.  
  - Less ideal alternative if assumptions met and design simpler: Repeated-measures ANOVA (RM-ANOVA) with time as within-subject factor and group as between-subject factor.  

- Purpose:  
  - Assess whether the trajectory of bacterial load over the three timepoints differs between depotphoresis, DL, and control.  

- Why appropriate (LMM):  
  1) Accounts for correlation between repeated measures within the same subject/root.  
  2) Flexible with unbalanced data (missing S2 or S3 for some subjects) and unequal group sizes.  
  3) Can include covariates (e.g., baseline CFU, tooth type, lesion size).  

- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - Requires approximate normality and homoscedasticity of residuals when using log-transformed CFU; if log transform still yields highly skewed residuals, this assumption may fail.  

- When this test should NOT be used:  
  - Severe non-normality persists after transformation.  
  - Very small sample size per group/time combination (not the case here).  
  - If analytic software or expertise for mixed models is not available.  

- Alternative if assumptions violated:  
  - Nonparametric approach: Friedman test within each group for changes over time, combined with Kruskal–Wallis tests at each timepoint; OR  
  - Generalized linear mixed model (GLMM) with count distribution (negative binomial) and log link.  

Mandatory test A2: Between-group comparison of CFU reduction (S1→S2, S1→S3)  

- Test name:  
  - If log-transformed CFU: One-way ANOVA on Δlog CFU (e.g., S1–S2) across three groups; or LMM as above focusing on contrasts of change.  
  - If non-normal: Kruskal–Wallis test on change scores.  

- Purpose:  
  - Compare the magnitude of bacterial reduction among depotphoresis, DL, and control, especially immediate (S1–S2) and after medicament (S1–S3).  

- Why appropriate:  
  1) Directly addresses the clinical question of which method achieves greater bacterial reduction.  
  2) Change scores naturally adjust for baseline differences in CFU.  

- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - Change scores can be noisy if baseline variability is high; ANCOVA on S2 or S3 adjusting for S1 may be more efficient.  

- When this test should NOT be used:  
  - If baseline CFU differs substantially between groups; then ANCOVA is preferred.  

- Alternative if assumptions violated:  
  - ANCOVA / linear regression on S2 (or S3) log CFU with predictors: group and baseline log CFU; or robust regression / rank-based ANCOVA (e.g., Quade test).  

Recommended test B1: ANCOVA on S2 and S3 CFU adjusting for S1  

- Purpose:  
  - Improve precision and control for any baseline imbalances in microbial counts.  

- Why appropriate:  
  1) Strongly reduces residual variance when baseline CFU is predictive of follow-up CFU.  
  2) Maintains interpretability: estimated mean difference in S2/S3 CFU between groups at a common baseline CFU.  

- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - Assumes linear relationship between baseline and follow-up CFU and homogeneity of regression slopes; may be violated in heavily skewed data.  

- When not to use:  
  - If group-by-baseline interactions are strong and non-linear.  

- Alternative:  
  - GLMM with baseline CFU as covariate; or stratified analysis by baseline CFU tertiles.  

Optional exploratory B2/C1: Proportion achieving “complete disinfection”  

- Define a threshold, e.g., CFU below detection limit or below a clinically meaningful level at S3.  
- Test:  
  - Chi-square or Fisher’s exact test comparing proportions of “success” across three groups.  
- Alternative:  
  - Logistic regression adjusting for baseline CFU and tooth type.  

B. POSTOPERATIVE PAIN (VAS)  

Data structure  
- Continuous VAS scores (0–10) at multiple timepoints: 6h, 12h, 24h, 48h, 72h, and possibly 7 days.  
- Repeated measures, within-patient.  

Preprocessing  
- Inspect VAS distributions; may be moderately skewed. Usually treated as continuous.  

Primary pain analysis  

Mandatory test A3: Linear mixed-effects model (or RM-ANOVA) for VAS over time  

- Test name:  
  - Preferred: LMM with random intercept for subject, fixed effects for group, time, and group×time interaction.  
  - Simpler: RM-ANOVA with Greenhouse–Geisser correction if sphericity violated.  

- Purpose:  
  - Assess whether pain trajectories over time differ among depotphoresis, DL, and control.  

- Why appropriate:  
  1) Handles repeated VAS measures and within-subject correlation.  
  2) Allows unbalanced data (some missed timepoints).  

- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - If VAS distributions are heavily non-normal or highly skewed, parametric assumptions may be questionable.  

- When not to use:  
  - If some groups have very few patients, especially at later timepoints.  

- Alternative if assumptions violated:  
  - Nonparametric longitudinal methods (e.g., Brunner–Langer rank-based ANOVA-type statistics) or analysis at each timepoint using Kruskal–Wallis.  

Mandatory test A4: Between-group comparison at key timepoints  

- Test name:  
  - At each timepoint (e.g., 24h, 48h), one-way ANOVA comparing mean VAS among groups; or Kruskal–Wallis test if non-normal.  
- Purpose:  
  - Identify specific timepoints where pain differs significantly between interventions (e.g., whether DL reduces early pain more than others).  

- Why appropriate:  
  1) Clinically, time-specific differences matter (e.g., 24h peak pain).  
  2) Straightforward interpretation.  

- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - Multiple testing across several timepoints inflates type I error if not adjusted.  

- When not to use:  
  - Without multiplicity control; or if sample size is too small at later times.  

- Alternative:  
  - Pre-specify primary timepoint (e.g., 24h) and control FDR or Bonferroni for secondary timepoints.  

Recommended test B3: Area under the curve (AUC) for pain  

- Compute pain AUC over 0–72h for each patient (e.g., trapezoidal rule).  
- Compare AUC among groups using ANOVA or Kruskal–Wallis.  

- Purpose:  
  - Provide a single summary of overall pain burden.  

- Why appropriate:  
  1) Integrates pain intensity and duration.  
  2) Reduces multiple testing problem.  

- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - Requires relatively complete pain curves; missing timepoints complicate AUC computation.  

- When not to use:  
  - High proportion of missing VAS entries.  

- Alternative:  
  - Mixed model estimated marginal means over time.  

Optional C2: Ordinal models / clinically meaningful categories  

- Categorize VAS (e.g., 0–1=none, 2–3=mild, 4–6=moderate, ≥7=severe).  
- Use proportional odds model or chi-square at key times.

TASK 3 – INAPPROPRIATE TESTS  

Below are tests that should NOT be used in this study, with reasons.

1. Simple independent-samples t-tests at each timepoint without adjustment for repeated measures  

- Why inappropriate:  
  - Ignoring within-subject correlation for repeated measures (microbial counts and VAS) increases type I error and wastes information.  
  - Leads to multiple unadjusted comparisons and inflated false positives.  

2. Multiple paired t-tests within each group (e.g., S1 vs S2, S2 vs S3) without overall model  

- Why inappropriate:  
  - Fragmented analysis; does not formally compare groups on trajectories.  
  - Inflates type I error due to multiple testing; lacks overall test of group differences.  

3. Parametric tests on raw CFU without transformation or count model  

- Why inappropriate:  
  - CFU distributions are typically heavily skewed and overdispersed.  
  - Violates normality and homoscedasticity, undermining validity of t-tests/ANOVA.  

4. Treating each root as an independent subject in simple ANOVA/t-tests without accounting for clustering  

- Why inappropriate:  
  - Roots belonging to the same patient are correlated; independence assumption violated.  
  - Leads to underestimated standard errors and inflated significance.  

5. Using Pearson correlation or simple linear regression on non-normal CFU without transformation  

- Why inappropriate:  
  - Severe non-normality and outliers can distort Pearson-based analyses.  
  - Spearman rank correlation or transformed data would be more appropriate.  

6. Using parametric tests for VAS when sample sizes per group/time are small and distributions extremely skewed  

- Why inappropriate:  
  - With small n, non-normal distributions violate assumptions; nonparametric tests or transformations are safer.  

7. Logistic regression or chi-square without adjusting for multiple testing across many timepoints and outcomes  

- Why inappropriate:  
  - Conducting many separate tests without multiplicity control exaggerates evidence of differences; particularly problematic in pain analyses.  

8. Survival analysis (Kaplan–Meier, Cox) for pain duration without clear time-to-event definition  

- Why inappropriate:  
  - Pain here is measured as continuous intensity at fixed times, not as time until pain resolution. Retrofitting survival models would be artificial and misleading.  

9. Using parametric ANCOVA for CBCT lesion volume if volume is constructed as product of axes and strongly skewed, without transformation or robust methods  

- Why inappropriate:  
  - Volumes can be highly skewed; parametric ANCOVA on raw volumes would violate assumptions.  

10. Ignoring missing data mechanisms and using last observation carried forward (LOCF) for 6‑month radiographic data  

- Why inappropriate:  
  - LOCF assumes no change after last measurement; biased especially when outcome is expected to improve.  
  - Particularly problematic given large and differential attrition in the control group.
