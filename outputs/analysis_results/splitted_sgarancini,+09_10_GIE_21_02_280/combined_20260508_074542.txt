## TASK 1 — INFORMATION EXTRACTION (study characteristics)

### Study design and setting
- **Design:** Prospective, **3-arm parallel-group randomized clinical trial**, **single-center**, **single-blind** (patients blinded; VAS evaluators blinded; operator not blinded).
- **Setting:** Endodontics Department, Faculty of Dentistry, Zahedan University of Medical Sciences (Iran).
- **Recruitment period:** December 2018–June 2020.
- **Registration/ethics:** IRCT20180718040517N1; Ethics code IR.ZAUMS.REC.1397.278; CONSORT followed.

### Objective(s)
- Compare **depotphoresis vs diode laser vs control** regarding:
  1. **Microbial count reduction** in root canals (CFU/mL),
  2. **Postoperative pain** over 72 hours,
  3. **Healing of periapical lesions** after **6 months** measured on **CBCT**.

### Participants
- **Population:** Patients requiring endodontic treatment of teeth with **pulp necrosis + chronic apical periodontitis**.
- **Age:** 18–60 years.
- **Diagnosis confirmation:** Radiographic exam + negative cold test and EPT + no bleeding on pulp exposure.

#### Key inclusion criteria
- 18–60 years; tooth with confirmed necrotic pulp and chronic apical periodontitis; rubber dam isolation possible.

#### Key exclusion criteria
- Diabetes, immunocompromised, pregnancy
- Analgesic intake in past 3 days; antibiotic intake past month
- Teeth with periodontal disease, previous access cavity, not isolatable with rubber dam

### Randomization and concealment
- **Simple randomization** via online system immediately before treatment.
- **Allocation concealment:** computer-generated assignment after demographics entered; unique ID codes; codes not reused.
- **Blinding procedures (patient blinding via sham):**
  - Control: sham laser (fiber placed without activation) for some; sham depotphoresis clips without current for others.
  - Laser group: depotphoresis clips placed **without current** (sham depotphoresis).
  - Depotphoresis group: sham laser with power off.

### Groups / interventions
All groups received standard chemomechanical preparation and irrigation:
- Instrumentation: Reciproc files (25/8 or 40/6) + Reciproc Silver motor
- Irrigation: 2.5% NaOCl + 17% EDTA; then saline; agitation with #30 Hedstrom; sampling with paper points.

**Arm 1: Depotphoresis**
- Cupral paste (Cupral:Ca(OH)₂ = 1:9) placed in coronal third.
- Electrodes applied; current increased to manufacturer range **0.8–1.5 mA for 5 min**.
- Temporary Cavit; **calcium hydroxide-Cupral** intracanal medicament between sessions.

**Arm 2: Diode laser**
- 940 nm diode laser, 200 µm tip, inserted to 1 mm from apex and withdrawn helically (~2 mm/s)
- **Pulse mode**, **2 W**, 20 s irradiation + 10 s rest; repeated **3 cycles**
- Then **calcium hydroxide + saline** as medicament; Cavit.

**Arm 3: Control**
- No active adjunctive disinfection beyond standard care
- **Calcium hydroxide + saline** medicament; Cavit.

### Timing / repeated measures
- **Pain (VAS 0–10):**
  - Baseline (pre-treatment, session 1)
  - Post-treatment at **6, 12, 24, 48, 72 hours**
  - Recall at 7 days (visit mentioned; outcomes not clearly specified beyond VAS form collection)

- **Microbial sampling (CFU/mL):**
  - **S1:** after access/caries removal, **before** canal preparation (paper points 1 min)
  - **S2:** after chemomechanical preparation (paper points 60 s).

  - **S3:** at second session, after removing intracanal medicament (paper points; same method)

- **Radiographic healing (CBCT):**

    - **Mesiodistal (Lx)**
    - **Buccolingual (Lz)**
  - Additional CBCT “codes” based on Patel et al. (modified; codes 1 and 6 not applicable).
  - **Readers:** 2 endodontists; third adjudicated disagreements.

### Outcomes (as implied from intro/methods)
- **Primary/Key outcomes** (not explicitly labeled, but central):
  - Microbial load (CFU/mL) change across S1/S2/S3 and between groups
  - Postoperative pain trajectory (VAS over time) between groups
  - CBCT lesion size change (Lx, Ly, Lz) from baseline to 6 months; possibly categorical CBCT healing code

## TASK 2 — STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

### General principles (apply to all outcomes)
- **Analysis populations**
  - **Intention-to-treat (ITT)**: all randomized patients, analyzed by assigned group (recommended as primary for RCT).
  - **Per-protocol (PP)**: those completing assigned protocol and follow-up (sensitivity).
- **Unit of analysis**
  - Prefer **patient-level** primary analyses.  
  - If multiple teeth/roots per patient are included, use **mixed-effects models / GEE** to account for within-patient clustering.
- **Multiplicity**
  - Three groups → control family-wise error for pairwise comparisons (e.g., Tukey, Holm).
  - Multiple endpoints and repeated timepoints → pre-specify primary endpoint(s) and control secondary multiplicity (e.g., hierarchical testing or FDR).
- **Distribution handling**
  - CFU/mL usually right-skewed/zero-inflated → analyze **log10(CFU+1)** or use count models if appropriate.
  - VAS often non-normal and bounded → mixed model with robust SE; or ordinal methods as sensitivity.
- **Missing data**
  - 6-month attrition is substantial (especially control). Plan:
    - Explore missingness patterns and predictors.
    - Primary: mixed models (valid under MAR) + **multiple imputation** as sensitivity.
    - Consider worst/best-case sensitivity for key outcomes.
- **Reporting**
  - Effect sizes with 95% CI (mean differences, standardized differences, ratios, odds ratios), not only p-values.

### CATEGORY A — MANDATORY tests/models

#### A1) Linear Mixed-Effects Model (LMM) / Mixed Model for Repeated Measures (MMRM) — Pain (VAS over time)
- **Purpose:** Compare **pain trajectories** across groups over repeated timepoints (baseline, 6–72h), accounting for within-subject correlation.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Handles **repeated measures** and uneven spacing (6/12/24/48/72h).
  2. Can include **baseline pain** and estimate **group × time interaction** (treatment effect over time).
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - VAS may be markedly non-normal/heavy-tailed or many zeros, making Gaussian assumptions poor.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If pain is best treated as **ordinal** with strong ceiling/floor effects and residual diagnostics show severe violations not correctable.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Robust LMM** (sandwich SE), **GEE with robust SE**, or **ordinal mixed model** (cumulative logit) treating VAS as ordered categories; or analyze area-under-curve (AUC) with nonparametric methods.

**Model details to pre-specify:**
- Fixed effects: group, time (categorical), group×time, baseline pain (if not part of outcome vector), tooth type/lesion baseline size (optional covariates).
- Random effect: subject (patient); add random slope for time if justified.
- Covariance: unstructured or AR(1) (choose via AIC/BIC).

---

#### A2) Mixed-Effects Model (or GEE) for Microbial Load across S1/S2/S3
- **Test/model name:** LMM/MMRM on **log10(CFU/mL + 1)** with group×sampling-time interaction; or **GEE** with robust SE.
- **Purpose:** Compare **bacterial reduction patterns** among groups across three sampling points.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Accounts for **within-tooth/patient correlation** across repeated samples.
  2. Log-transform stabilizes variance and makes skewed CFU distributions more analyzable; interaction tests whether reductions differ by group.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - If CFU data are **zero-inflated** or counts are unreliable due to lab processing rules (“outer numbers thrown out”) causing nonstandard measurement error.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If CFU are analyzed as raw counts with extreme overdispersion/zero inflation and log-transform does not resolve model fit.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Negative binomial mixed model**; **zero-inflated negative binomial**; or nonparametric approaches on change scores (e.g., aligned ranks) as sensitivity.

**Key derived endpoints to consider (pre-specify):**
- ΔlogCFU S1→S2, S2→S3, and S1→S3 (overall disinfection).

---

#### A3) ANCOVA / Mixed Model for CBCT Lesion Size Change (baseline vs 6 months)
- **Test/model name:** **ANCOVA** (follow-up lesion size as outcome, baseline lesion size as covariate, group as factor) for each dimension (Lx, Ly, Lz); or multivariate extension (see B2).
- **Purpose:** Compare **6-month healing** across groups while adjusting for baseline lesion size.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. ANCOVA is more efficient than change-score tests and controls **baseline imbalance**.
  2. Straightforward interpretation: adjusted mean follow-up difference by group.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - If lesion measurements are highly non-normal, contain many zeros, or if missing follow-up is differential and not MAR.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If baseline and follow-up are not measured comparably (e.g., inconsistent measurement protocol) or if clustering (multiple roots/patient) is ignored.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Rank ANCOVA** (e.g., Quade), **robust regression**, or **mixed-effects model** with patient random effect; bootstrap CIs.

---

#### A4) Baseline comparability (descriptive + targeted tests)
- **Test name:** One-way **ANOVA** or **Kruskal–Wallis** for continuous baseline variables; **Chi-square** or **Fisher’s exact** for categorical.
- **Purpose:** Describe baseline characteristics and identify large imbalances to consider as covariates (not for “proof” of randomization).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Helps interpret results in presence of attrition and possible chance imbalances.
  2. Guides covariate adjustment choices for sensitivity analyses.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Overemphasis on p-values at baseline can be misleading in RCTs.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Do not use baseline p-values to decide inclusion/exclusion or to claim groups are “equivalent”.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Use **standardized mean differences (SMDs)** instead of hypothesis tests; Fisher instead of Chi-square for sparse cells.

### CATEGORY B — RECOMMENDED tests/models

#### B1) Cluster-adjusted modeling for “roots” (if multiple roots/teeth per patient)
- **Model:** **Mixed-effects** models with random intercept for patient; or **GEE** with patient as clustering unit.
- **Purpose:** Correct standard errors and inference when unit analyzed is tooth/root but randomization is at patient level.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Prevents **inflated type I error** due to within-patient correlation.
  2. Aligns analysis with the actual data hierarchy reported (patients and roots).
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - If truly only one tooth per patient is included (unclear), clustering may be unnecessary.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When each patient contributes exactly one independent observation.
- **Alternative:**
  - Analyze **one pre-specified tooth per patient** (patient-level endpoint), or average within patient (with caution).

---

#### B2) Multivariate approach to lesion dimensions (Lx, Ly, Lz)
- **Test/model name:** **MANOVA** on follow-up adjusted for baseline (or multivariate mixed model).
- **Purpose:** Jointly test whether groups differ in overall 3D lesion healing profile.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Accounts for correlation among lesion dimensions.
  2. Controls type I error vs three separate univariate tests.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Sensitive to multivariate normality; may be underpowered with small n at follow-up (especially control).
- **When NOT to use:**
  - With very small group sizes or severe non-normality/outliers.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Permutation MANOVA**, or control multiplicity across univariate ANCOVAs (Holm).

---

#### B3) Inter-rater reliability for CBCT categorical codes and/or continuous measures
- **Test name:** **Cohen’s kappa / weighted kappa** (categorical codes), **ICC (intraclass correlation)** for continuous lesion dimensions.
- **Purpose:** Quantify agreement between the two endodontist readers (and impact of adjudication).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Imaging outcomes depend on observer interpretation; reliability supports validity.
  2. Helps justify combining/using adjudicated readings.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - If only adjudicated final values are stored and raw paired ratings are unavailable.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When there is only one rating per scan with no duplicate reads.
- **Alternative:**
  - Bland–Altman plots for continuous measures; Gwet’s AC1 for kappa paradox scenarios.

---

#### B4) Sensitivity analyses for missing 6-month CBCT outcomes
- **Test/model:** **Multiple imputation (MI)** + analysis model; and/or **inverse probability weighting (IPW)**.
- **Purpose:** Assess robustness to differential dropout (notably high in control).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Attrition can bias healing comparisons; MI/IPW addresses missingness under MAR.
  2. Provides more defensible inference than complete-case only.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - If missingness is MNAR and not explainable by observed covariates; MI may still be biased.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When missingness is extreme with little auxiliary information (unstable imputations).
- **Alternative:**
  - Pattern-mixture / tipping-point analyses; bounded/worst-case sensitivity.

## TASK 3 — INAPPROPRIATE TESTS (should NOT be used)

1. **Multiple independent t-tests at each timepoint without correction** (pain or CFU)
   - Inflates type I error; ignores within-subject correlation; wastes repeated-measures structure.

2. **Paired t-test within each group only (pre/post) with no between-group comparison**
   - Can show “significant improvement” in all groups yet fail to answer the RCT question (comparative efficacy).

3. **Chi-square test on raw CFU counts categorized arbitrarily**
   - CFU is quantitative/count; categorization is information-losing and thresholds are arbitrary.

4. **Ordinary least squares regression treating each root as independent** (if multiple roots per patient)
   - Violates independence; underestimates SE; false positives.

5. **Using baseline significance tests to decide whether to adjust for covariates**
   - Data-driven and not principled; adjustment should be pre-specified (e.g., baseline lesion size).

6. **Repeated-measures ANOVA requiring complete cases only (listwise deletion)**
   - With notable dropout/missingness, biases and reduces power; mixed models are preferable.

## TASK 4 — SUMMARY (overall analytical approach)

The study is a 3-arm RCT with **longitudinal outcomes** (pain over 5 post-op timepoints; microbial load over 3 samplings) and a **baseline-to-6-month imaging endpoint**. The core approach should therefore use **mixed-effects/GEE frameworks** to (i) correctly model within-subject correlation, (ii) test **group differences over time** via interaction terms, (iii) adjust for key baselines (especially baseline CBCT lesion size), and (iv) appropriately address **attrition and possible clustering by patient** when multiple roots are analyzed. This yields valid comparative inference aligned with the trial’s objectives.

TASK 3 – INAPPROPRIATE TESTS  

Below are tests that should NOT be used in this study, with reasons.

1. Simple independent-samples t-tests at each timepoint without adjustment for repeated measures  

- Why inappropriate:  
  - Ignoring within-subject correlation for repeated measures (microbial counts and VAS) increases type I error and wastes information.  
  - Leads to multiple unadjusted comparisons and inflated false positives.  

2. Multiple paired t-tests within each group (e.g., S1 vs S2, S2 vs S3) without overall model  

- Why inappropriate:  
  - Fragmented analysis; does not formally compare groups on trajectories.  
  - Inflates type I error due to multiple testing; lacks overall test of group differences.  

3. Parametric tests on raw CFU without transformation or count model  

- Why inappropriate:  
  - CFU distributions are typically heavily skewed and overdispersed.  
  - Violates normality and homoscedasticity, undermining validity of t-tests/ANOVA.  

4. Treating each root as an independent subject in simple ANOVA/t-tests without accounting for clustering  

- Why inappropriate:  
  - Roots belonging to the same patient are correlated; independence assumption violated.  
  - Leads to underestimated standard errors and inflated significance.  

5. Using Pearson correlation or simple linear regression on non-normal CFU without transformation  

- Why inappropriate:  
  - Severe non-normality and outliers can distort Pearson-based analyses.  
  - Spearman rank correlation or transformed data would be more appropriate.  

6. Using parametric tests for VAS when sample sizes per group/time are small and distributions extremely skewed  

- Why inappropriate:  
  - With small n, non-normal distributions violate assumptions; nonparametric tests or transformations are safer.  

7. Logistic regression or chi-square without adjusting for multiple testing across many timepoints and outcomes  

- Why inappropriate:  
  - Conducting many separate tests without multiplicity control exaggerates evidence of differences; particularly problematic in pain analyses.  

8. Survival analysis (Kaplan–Meier, Cox) for pain duration without clear time-to-event definition  

- Why inappropriate:  
  - Pain here is measured as continuous intensity at fixed times, not as time until pain resolution. Retrofitting survival models would be artificial and misleading.  

9. Using parametric ANCOVA for CBCT lesion volume if volume is constructed as product of axes and strongly skewed, without transformation or robust methods  

- Why inappropriate:  
  - Volumes can be highly skewed; parametric ANCOVA on raw volumes would violate assumptions.  

10. Ignoring missing data mechanisms and using last observation carried forward (LOCF) for 6‑month radiographic data  

- Why inappropriate:  
  - LOCF assumes no change after last measurement; biased especially when outcome is expected to improve.

### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

*Note: Microbial CFU data is inherently highly skewed and must be $\log_{10}$ transformed prior to any parametric analysis.*

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS (Core Analysis)

**1. Shapiro-Wilk Test**
*   **Purpose:** To assess the normality of continuous data (CFU counts, VAS scores, lesion dimensions).
*   **Why appropriate:** (1) Essential for determining whether to use parametric or non-parametric tests. (2) Highly accurate for sample sizes under 50 per group.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** Can be overly sensitive to minor deviations from normality in larger samples (though less of an issue with n=16 to 26).
*   **When NOT to use:** When analyzing strictly categorical data (e.g., gender).
*   **Alternative:** Kolmogorov-Smirnov test.

**2. Kruskal-Wallis H Test (or One-Way ANOVA if data is normal)**
*   **Purpose:** To compare baseline characteristics (age, baseline CFU, baseline lesion size) and between-group differences at specific time points (e.g., CFU at S2, VAS at 24h).
*   **Why appropriate:** (1) Accommodates the comparison of three independent groups. (2) Kruskal-Wallis does not require the assumption of normal distribution, which is ideal for ordinal VAS scores and raw CFU data.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** Less statistically powerful than ANOVA if the data actually perfectly meets parametric assumptions.
*   **When NOT to use:** When comparing only two groups, or when comparing repeated measures within the same group.
*   **Alternative:** One-Way ANOVA (if data is normally distributed and variances are equal).

**3. Linear Mixed-Effects Models (LMM) or Generalized Estimating Equations (GEE)**
*   **Purpose:** To analyze repeated measures data (VAS over 6 time points; CFU over 3 time points).
*   **Why appropriate:** (1) Excellently handles missing data without dropping the entire patient (crucial here due to the loss of 14 control patients). (2) Accounts for the correlation of repeated measures within the same subject over time.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** Computationally complex and requires careful selection of the covariance structure.
*   **When NOT to use:** When analyzing data collected at only a single time point.
*   **Alternative:** Friedman Test (non-parametric) or Repeated Measures ANOVA (parametric, but requires imputation for missing data).

**4. Paired t-test (or Wilcoxon Signed-Rank Test)**
*   **Purpose:** To evaluate within-group healing by comparing CBCT lesion size at baseline versus 6 months.
*   **Why appropriate:** (1) Specifically designed to compare two related/paired continuous variables. (2) Directly answers the objective of whether healing occurred within each specific treatment arm.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** Does not compare the treatments *against* each other, only against their own baselines.
*   **When NOT to use:** When comparing independent groups (e.g., Laser vs. Control).
*   **Alternative:** Wilcoxon Signed-Rank Test (if differences are not normally distributed).

#### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED TESTS (Enhance Quality)

**1. Intraclass Correlation Coefficient (ICC)**
*   **Purpose:** To measure inter-rater reliability between the two endodontists evaluating the CBCT images.
*   **Why appropriate:** (1) Validates the reliability and reproducibility of the primary radiographic outcome. (2) Reduces the risk of measurement bias.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** Assumes that the raters are evaluating the exact same subjects under the exact same conditions.
*   **When NOT to use:** For objective, machine-generated data that requires no human interpretation (e.g., automated apex locator readings).
*   **Alternative:** Cohen’s Kappa (if lesion healing was categorized as success/failure rather than continuous mm).

**2. Analysis of Covariance (ANCOVA)**
*   **Purpose:** To compare the 6-month lesion sizes between the three groups while controlling for baseline lesion size.
*   **Why appropriate:** (1) Adjusts for any accidental baseline imbalances in lesion size between groups. (2) Increases statistical power by reducing within-group error variance.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** Requires the assumption of homogeneity of regression slopes (the relationship between baseline and 6-month size must be similar across all groups).
*   **When NOT to use:** If the covariate (baseline size) is not linearly related to the dependent variable (6-month size).
*   **Alternative:** Kruskal-Wallis test on the *change scores* (Baseline minus 6-month size).

**3. Multiple Imputation (MI)**
*   **Purpose:** To handle the missing data resulting from patient dropouts, particularly the 46% dropout in the control group.
*   **Why appropriate:** (1) Preserves the Intention-to-Treat (ITT) principle, which is the gold standard for RCTs. (2) Prevents the bias that occurs if dropouts are related to treatment efficacy (e.g., control patients dropping out due to lack of healing).
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** Assumes data is Missing at Random (MAR); if data is Missing Not At Random (MNAR), MI may introduce bias.
*   **When NOT to use:** When missing data is negligible (e.g., <5%) or when analyzing Per-Protocol populations exclusively.
*   **Alternative:** Last Observation Carried Forward (LOCF) - though generally discouraged in modern biostatistics.

#### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL TESTS (Supplementary Insights)

**1. Kaplan-Meier Survival Analysis**
*   **Purpose:** To analyze the "time to complete pain resolution" (time until VAS = 0).
*   **Why appropriate:** (1) Provides a dynamic view of patient comfort rather than just mean pain scores. (2) Easily handles censored data (patients who drop out before pain resolves).
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** Pain resolution might happen very quickly (within 24-48 hours), making survival curves look stepped and less informative.
*   **When NOT to use:** For cross-sectional data or data without a clear "event" time.
*   **Alternative:** Cox Proportional-Hazards Model (if adjusting for covariates like age or baseline pain).

**2. Effect Size Calculations (e.g., Partial Eta-Squared or Cohen’s d)**
*   **Purpose:** To quantify the magnitude of the difference between treatments, independent of sample size.
*   **Why appropriate:** (1) A p-value only tells if a difference exists; effect size tells if the difference is clinically meaningful. (2) Highly recommended by CONSORT guidelines.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** Can be difficult to interpret for complex mixed models or non-parametric data.
*   **When NOT to use:** When the primary study objective is purely equivalence or non-inferiority without establishing superiority.
*   **Alternative:** Rank-biserial correlation (for non-parametric effect sizes).

### TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS

1.  **Independent Samples t-test:** Should NOT be used to compare the three groups. Running multiple t-tests (A vs B, B vs C, A vs C) inflates the Type I error rate (false positives). An omnibus test like ANOVA or Kruskal-Wallis must be used first.
2.  **Standard Repeated Measures ANOVA (Complete Case Analysis):** Should NOT be used due to the massive dropout in the control group (from 30 to 16). Standard RM-ANOVA uses listwise deletion, meaning any patient missing even one VAS score or the S3 CFU count will be entirely deleted from the analysis, destroying statistical power and introducing severe bias.
3.  **Chi-Square Test for CFU or VAS:** Should NOT be used because CFU and VAS are continuous/ordinal variables, not categorical frequencies. Converting them into arbitrary categories (e.g., "High Pain" vs "Low Pain") results in a massive loss of data resolution.

### TASK 4: SUMMARY

The overall analytical approach must address three distinct types of data: highly skewed continuous data (CFU), ordinal repeated-measures data (VAS), and continuous pre-post data (CBCT lesion size). 

The most critical methodological challenge in this study is the severe attrition in the control group (nearly 50% dropout). Therefore, the statistical plan heavily relies on **Linear Mixed Models (LMM)** or **Generalized Estimating Equations (GEE)** for longitudinal data (VAS and CFU), as these models utilize all available data points without requiring listwise deletion. 

Furthermore, microbial counts must be $\log_{10}$ transformed prior to analysis to satisfy parametric assumptions. For radiographic healing, an **ANCOVA** is recommended to compare 6-month outcomes while controlling for baseline lesion size, ensuring that any initial disparities do not confound the final results. Finally, calculating the **Intraclass Correlation Coefficient (ICC)** is vital to validate the reliability of the two endodontists measuring the CBCT scans, ensuring the primary long-term outcome is robust and reproducible.
