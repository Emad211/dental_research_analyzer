# CATEGORY A — MANDATORY (core analyses)

## A1) Descriptive statistics (with paired reporting)
- **Purpose:** Summarize baseline and follow-up distributions of PAI and quality scores; quantify improvement/worsening.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Necessary for transparency and clinical interpretation (e.g., % adequate at baseline and 6 months).
  2. Informs choice of inferential tests (ordinal vs binary; skewness).
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):** If the dataset lacks paired IDs, “paired change” summaries cannot be computed.
- **When NOT to use:** Never omit descriptive statistics; but do not present means/SD for clearly ordinal scores as the only summary.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:** Use medians/IQRs and distribution plots instead of means/SD.

Minimum recommended outputs:
- PAI (ordinal): median (IQR) at baseline and 6 months; and % above lesion threshold.
- Adequate RCT (%).
- Change table: improved/unchanged/worsened.

---

## A2) McNemar test (paired binary outcome)
- **Test name:** McNemar’s test (exact version if sparse)
- **Purpose:** Test whether the **proportion** with a condition changes from baseline to 6 months:
  - e.g., **PAI lesion present vs absent** (based on pre-specified cutoff), and/or **adequate vs inadequate RCT**.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Designed for **paired dichotomous** data (same tooth measured twice).
  2. Tests change using only **discordant pairs**, matching the clinical “conversion” question (e.g., lesion resolved).
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):** If observations are not paired (different teeth at baseline and follow-up) or if clustering by patient is substantial and unaddressed.
- **When NOT to use:** When outcome has >2 categories (use ordinal methods) or when pairing is absent.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - If clustering or covariate adjustment is needed: **GEE logistic regression** with time indicator.
  - If very small discordant counts: **Exact McNemar**.

Report:
- Discordant pair counts, **paired risk difference** and/or **paired odds ratio** (with CI).

---

## A3) Wilcoxon signed-rank test (paired ordinal score)
- **Test name:** Wilcoxon signed-rank test
- **Purpose:** Compare **paired ordinal** scores (baseline vs 6 months), e.g.:
  - PAI score (ordinal)
  - Quality score 1–6 (ordinal)
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Suitable for **paired** measurements without assuming normality.
  2. Appropriate for **ordinal** scales where equal-interval assumptions are doubtful.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):** If there are many ties/zero differences (common with ordinal scales), power and interpretation can be limited.
- **When NOT to use:** When data are unpaired; or when you need to model covariates, clustering, or non-monotone change patterns.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - For many ties: **sign test** (less power but fewer assumptions).
  - For covariate adjustment/clustering: **ordinal mixed model** or **GEE ordinal model**.

Report:
- Median paired change and Hodges–Lehmann estimate (if computed) with CI.

# CATEGORY B — RECOMMENDED (improves rigor/interpretability)

## B1) Inter-/intra-examiner reliability for scoring (kappa / weighted kappa / ICC)
- **Test name:** Cohen’s kappa (binary), **weighted kappa** (ordinal), or ICC (if treated quasi-continuous)
- **Purpose:** Quantify reliability of PAI and quality scoring from radiographs.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Outcomes are **rater-dependent**; reliability affects validity.
  2. Strengthens credibility of retrospective radiographic assessments.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):** If only one scorer and no re-reading subset exists; then reliability cannot be estimated.
- **When NOT to use:** Do not report kappa if categories are extremely imbalanced without also reporting prevalence-adjusted interpretation (kappa paradox).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:** Use **Gwet’s AC1/AC2** when kappa is unstable under imbalance.

---

## B2) Paired effect sizes with confidence intervals
- **Test name:** Estimation-focused analysis (e.g., paired risk difference/ratio; Hodges–Lehmann median difference)
- **Purpose:** Provide magnitude of improvement, not only significance.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Clinically meaningful interpretation (e.g., % lesions resolved).
  2. CI conveys precision and is robust to sample size issues.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):** If the sample is not truly paired or follow-up is incomplete and biased.
- **When NOT to use:** Avoid naive CI calculations that ignore pairing.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:** **Bootstrap CIs** (cluster bootstrap if multiple teeth per patient).

---

## B3) Regression modeling to adjust for confounding and clustering (GEE or mixed effects)
- **Test name:**  
  - **GEE logistic regression** for binary outcomes (lesion present, adequate RCT)  
  - **Ordinal mixed-effects model** (cumulative logit) for PAI/quality scores  
- **Purpose:** Estimate the association of **time (baseline vs 6 months)** with outcomes while accounting for:
  - repeated measures (time)
  - possible **multiple teeth per patient** (clustering)
  - additional covariates (if available)
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Handles **correlated observations** (within tooth over time; within patient if multiple teeth).
  2. Allows **adjustment** and yields interpretable parameters (ORs) with CIs.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):** If covariates and patient/tooth identifiers are unavailable; model may be over-parameterized given available information.
- **When NOT to use:** If sample size is too small for model complexity or if the proportional odds assumption is badly violated (for ordinal models).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - For proportional odds violation: **partial proportional odds model** or treat as binary (pre-specified clinically meaningful cut).
  - For small samples/separation: **Firth penalized logistic regression**.

## B4) Sensitivity analysis for the PAI cutoff definition
- **Test name:** Sensitivity analysis using alternative dichotomizations (e.g., PAI ≥3 vs <3; PAI ≥4 vs ≤3), plus ordinal analysis
- **Purpose:** Address ambiguity/inconsistency in the text regarding PAI scale and threshold.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Cutoff choice can materially change “lesion present” prevalence and McNemar results.
  2. Improves robustness and transparency.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):** If the original dataset coding cannot support reclassification (e.g., only stored as binary).
- **When NOT to use:** If it becomes data-driven fishing; must be pre-specified as sensitivity, not primary.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:** Use **ordinal-only** approach (Wilcoxon/ordinal model) as primary.

## TASK 3 — Inappropriate tests (should NOT be used)

1. **Independent-samples t-test / one-way ANOVA** (baseline vs follow-up)
   - **Why inappropriate:** Measurements are **paired** on the same tooth; violates independence. Also outcomes are ordinal.
2. **Paired t-test** on PAI or 1–6 quality score (as primary)
   - **Why inappropriate:** Treats ordinal scores as continuous with equal intervals and normal differences; often unjustified.
3. **Pearson correlation** between baseline and follow-up as evidence of “improvement”
   - **Why inappropriate:** Correlation measures association, not change; high correlation can occur despite no improvement.
4. **Simple chi-square test of proportions** ignoring pairing (baseline vs follow-up lesion prevalence)
   - **Why inappropriate:** Ignores within-tooth pairing; p-values and CIs will be wrong.
5. **Linear regression** on ordinal outcomes without justification
   - **Why inappropriate:** Assumes interval scale and normal residuals; may produce misleading estimates.

---

## TASK 4 — Summary justification of the analytical approach

The study compares **radiographic outcomes before and after re-treatment** at a **6-month paired follow-up**. Therefore, the core analysis should use **paired methods**: (i) **McNemar** for dichotomized clinical endpoints (lesion present/absent; adequate/inadequate), and (ii) **Wilcoxon signed-rank** (or ordinal models) for the original **ordinal scores** (PAI; 1–6 quality). Rigor is improved by reporting **effect sizes with CIs**, evaluating **scoring reliability**, and—if multiple teeth per patient are included—using **GEE/mixed models** to account for **clustering** and allow adjustment.

  2) Directly addresses a clinically meaningful endpoint: adequacy improvement.
- Why it may not be appropriate:
  - Same considerations as with AP status: very small numbers of discordant pairs.
- When this test should NOT be used:
  - Independent groups.
  - When adequacy cannot be meaningfully dichotomized (e.g., if scoring scheme is more complex).
- Alternative if assumptions are violated:
  - Exact McNemar test.
  - For ordinal, multi‑category quality measures: Wilcoxon signed‑rank or marginal homogeneity tests.

6. Cross‑sectional association between post‑re‑treatment quality (adequate vs inadequate) and periapical status (AP vs no AP) at 6 months  
   (Chi‑square or Fisher’s exact test)
- Purpose:
  - To examine whether better technical quality of re‑treatment is associated with improved periapical health at 6 months.
- Why appropriate:
  1) Both variables are categorical (binary); association can be summarized in a 2×2 table.
  2) This explores the main clinical hypothesis that adequate RCT quality relates to healing.
- Why it may not be appropriate:
  - If some cells have very small counts (<5), chi‑square approximations may be inaccurate.
- When this test should NOT be used:
  - When sample sizes per cell are extremely small; or when there is severe zero inflation.
- Alternative if assumptions are violated:
  - Fisher’s exact test for small samples.
  - If quality is ordinal with more than two levels: Mantel–Haenszel trend test (if treating quality as ordered) or exact tests.

7. Estimation of effect sizes with confidence intervals
- Purpose:
  - To quantify the magnitude of changes and associations (not just p‑values).
- Examples:
  - Risk difference, risk ratio, and odds ratio for:
    - AP prevalence baseline vs follow‑up (paired OR via McNemar).
    - Adequacy prevalence baseline vs follow‑up.
    - Association between post‑re‑treatment adequacy and AP at 6 months (OR).
  - Median change in PAI and quality scores with confidence intervals (e.g., Hodges–Lehmann estimator).
- Why appropriate:
  1) Provides clinically interpretable measures of effect size.
  2) CIs highlight precision and are standard in clinical research.
- Why it may not be appropriate:
  - If data are extremely sparse, CI estimation may be unstable or very wide.
- When this should NOT be used:
  - There is no reason not to estimate effect sizes; but one should avoid over‑interpreting very imprecise estimates.
- Alternative if assumptions are violated:
  - Exact confidence intervals (e.g., exact binomial or exact OR CIs) for small samples.

CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED ANALYSES  
----------------------------------

These analyses improve depth and rigor but are not strictly necessary to address the core objective.

8. Ordinal logistic regression (proportional odds model) for PAI at 6 months  
   with post‑treatment quality as predictor
- Purpose:
  - To model periapical status (graded PAI categories) at 6 months as a function of filling quality (ordinal or dichotomous) and potentially adjust for confounders (e.g., baseline PAI, tooth type).
- Why appropriate:
  1) PAI is ordinal; proportional odds models use the full order information rather than dichotomizing.
  2) Allows adjustment for baseline PAI score and other covariates (tooth type, age, etc.), providing more robust estimates of the association between quality and outcome.
- Why it may not be appropriate:
  - Proportional odds assumption may be violated (the effect of quality may differ across thresholds).
- When this test should NOT be used:
  - When sample size is small relative to number of covariates (risk of overfitting and unstable estimates).
  - When PAI is heavily skewed (e.g., almost all scores in 1–2 or 4–5) leading to sparse categories.
- Alternative if assumptions are violated:
  - Partial proportional odds models (relaxing proportionality).
  - Binary logistic regression on dichotomized PAI (AP yes/no).
  - Collapsing categories (e.g., 1–2 vs 3 vs 4–5) and using multinomial logistic regression.

9. Logistic regression for AP status at 6 months (binary)  
   with quality of re‑treatment and baseline status as predictors
- Purpose:
  - To quantify the adjusted association between post‑re‑treatment adequacy and presence/absence of AP at 6 months, controlling for baseline AP and other covariates.
- Why appropriate:
  1) Binary outcome fits logistic regression framework well.
  2) Incorporates multiple predictors (baseline PAI, quality, tooth type, patient age) to control for confounding and provide adjusted odds ratios.
- Why it may not be appropriate:
  - If the number of outcome events (e.g., healed cases) is small, the model may be unstable (low events‑per‑variable).
- When this test should NOT be used:
  - With very limited sample size or rare outcome, especially if multiple predictors are included.
  - If there is serious multicollinearity among predictors.
- Alternative if assumptions are violated:
  - Exact logistic regression for small samples.
  - Penalized logistic regression (e.g., Firth’s correction) for sparse data.
  - Unadjusted analyses (stratified risk ratios/ORs) when regression is not feasible.

10. Multilevel / mixed‑effects modeling to account for clustering by patient  
    (e.g., mixed‑effects logistic regression)
- Purpose:
  - To account for potential non‑independence when multiple teeth per patient are included.
- Why appropriate:
  1) Teeth in the same patient may share biological and treatment characteristics; random intercept models can adjust for this.
  2) Provides correct standard errors and avoids inflated type I error due to clustering.
- Why it may not be appropriate:
  - The method requires information on patient identifiers and sufficient numbers of patients with more than one tooth to estimate random effects.
- When this test should NOT be used:
  - If sample size is small or clustering negligible (e.g., nearly all patients have only one tooth included).
  - When patient IDs are not available or anonymized in a way that precludes clustering.
- Alternative if assumptions are violated:
  - Use robust sandwich standard errors clustered by patient.
  - Restrict analysis to one tooth per patient (e.g., randomly select one).

11. Agreement/reliability analysis for scoring (if multiple raters)  
    (Cohen’s kappa, weighted kappa)
- Purpose:
  - To assess inter‑rater or intra‑rater reliability of PAI and quality scoring.
- Why appropriate:
  1) PAI and quality scores are subjectively interpreted; reliability metrics quantify consistency of scoring.
  2) Weighted kappa is suitable for ordinal scales where near‑miss disagreements are less serious than far‑miss.
- Why it may not be appropriate:
  - If only one examiner performed all ratings and no repeat ratings were done, reliability cannot be assessed.
- When this test should NOT be used:
  - When the same rater scored all radiographs once and there is no repeated scoring.
- Alternative if assumptions are violated:
  - Intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC) if numeric/continuous scoring is used by multiple raters.
  - Simply describe training and calibration if quantitative reliability data are unavailable.

12. Sensitivity analyses for different cut‑offs of PAI and quality
- Purpose:
  - To explore robustness of results to how PAI and quality scores are dichotomized or categorized (e.g., PAI ≤2 vs ≥3; quality 1–3 vs 4–6).
- Why appropriate:
  1) Cut‑offs for “AP present” and “adequate quality” can be somewhat arbitrary; sensitivity analyses show whether conclusions depend on these choices.
  2) Helps interpret clinical relevance: small shifts near cut‑off points may affect classification but not real clinical change.
- Why it may not be appropriate:
  - Multiple explorations can inflate type I error if treated as confirmatory without adjustment.
- When this should NOT be used:
  - As primary analyses; they should be clearly labeled as exploratory.
- Alternative if assumptions are violated:
  - Use models that directly use ordinal values (e.g., ordinal regression) rather than dichotomizing.

TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS AND WHY  
===================================

1. Independent‑samples t‑test for comparing baseline vs follow‑up PAI or quality scores
- Why inappropriate:
  - The data are paired (repeated measures on the same tooth); using an independent‑samples test ignores pairing and reduces power and validity.
  - PAI/quality are ordinal, not continuous, violating t‑test assumptions.
- Better alternative:
  - Wilcoxon signed‑rank test; or paired t‑test only if very strong justification for treating as continuous and normality is met.

2. One‑way ANOVA comparing baseline vs follow‑up PAI or quality scores as “groups”
- Why inappropriate:
  - Baseline and follow‑up are repeated measurements for the same subjects; ANOVA for independent groups is unsuitable.
  - Ordinal outcome; ANOVA assumes interval‑scale and normally distributed residuals.
- Better alternative:
  - Repeated measures methods; in this case non‑parametric paired tests (Wilcoxon).

3. Pearson correlation between baseline and follow‑up PAI as the main test of change
- Why inappropriate:
  - Correlation measures association, not change; high correlation can coexist with substantial systematic change.
  - PAI is ordinal; Pearson correlation assumes continuous, normally distributed variables.
- Better alternative:
  - Wilcoxon signed‑rank test and descriptive change summaries.

4. Simple linear regression with PAI as continuous outcome without checking assumptions
- Why inappropriate:
  - PAI is ordinal and bounded (1–5), violating linear regression assumptions (normal residuals, homoscedasticity).
  - The relationship between predictors and outcome may not be linear.
- Better alternative:
  - Ordinal logistic regression or logistic regression with dichotomized PAI.

5. Unpaired chi‑square test comparing baseline vs follow‑up prevalence of AP without accounting for pairing
- Why inappropriate:
  - Treating baseline and follow‑up as independent groups neglects the matched nature of the sample.
  - This can distort standard errors and p‑values.
- Better alternative:
  - McNemar’s test for paired proportions.

6. Parametric repeated‑measures ANOVA on PAI/quality scores over two time points
- Why inappropriate:
  - Ordinal outcome and only two time points (pre/post), making repeated measures ANOVA unnecessary and assumption‑heavy.
- Better alternative:
  - Wilcoxon signed‑rank test.

7. Multiple unadjusted subgroup tests without consideration of multiplicity
- Why inappropriate:
  - Increases risk of false positives; not ideal for a relatively small observational dataset.
- Better alternative:
  - Focus on a limited number of pre‑specified comparisons; consider adjustments (e.g., Bonferroni) if multiple primary tests.

TASK 4: SUMMARY OF OVERALL ANALYTICAL APPROACH  
===============================================

The study involves paired ordinal and binary outcomes measured on the same teeth before and after endodontic re‑treatment. The core analytical strategy should:

- Use appropriate paired analyses:
  - McNemar’s test for binary outcomes (AP yes/no, adequacy yes/no).
  - Wilcoxon signed‑rank test for ordinal scores (PAI, quality).
- Emphasize estimation:
  - Report change in prevalence, median score changes, and effect sizes (odds ratios, risk differences) with confidence intervals.
- Explore relationships between technical quality and clinical outcome:
  - Cross‑sectional association at 6 months (chi‑square/Fisher’s exact).
  - Regression models (logistic or ordinal) for adjusted analyses, if sample size and data structure permit.
- Consider clustering by patient and reliability of scoring if data are available.

This approach respects the paired, ordinal nature of the data, addresses the main clinical questions (improvement in quality and periapical healing), and provides interpretable, clinically relevant estimates while avoiding inappropriate parametric or independence‑based methods.
