## TASK 1 — Information extraction (study characteristics)

### Study design and setting
- **Design:** Retrospective radiographic record review with **paired before–after** comparison.
- **Setting:** Department of Endodontics (University of Sharjah, UAE).
- **Ethics:** Institutional ethical approval obtained.

### Objective
- To **assess the quality of root canal (RC) filling before and after endodontic re-treatment** and evaluate **changes in periapical status** over follow-up.

### Study material / sample
- **Sampling unit:** Radiographs of **failed endodontic treatment** cases.
- **Number of radiographs:** **238** (“238 radiographs of failed endodontic treatment”).
- **Timepoints:** 
  - **Baseline** (pre–re-treatment)
  - **Follow-up at 6 months** (post–re-treatment)
- **Exclusions:** Radiographs of **poor quality**, **non-diagnostic**, or with **radiographic errors**.

> Ambiguity to resolve in analysis: whether 238 refers to baseline only or complete paired baseline+6-month sets for the same teeth/patients. The methods imply paired comparison (“Baseline and follow-up radiographs at 6 months were compared”), so the analysis should assume **paired data** and report how many had complete follow-up.

### Imaging / data collection
- **Radiograph type:** Digital intraoral periapical radiographs (**IOPARs**).
- **Sensor:** Schick sensor.
- **X-ray unit:** Gnatus intraoral radiographic unit.
  - 72 kVp, 8 mA, exposure time 0.30–0.50 sec
- **Positioning:** Rinn XCP paralleling holder.

### Outcomes / variables (as described)
1. **Periapical status**
   - Assessed via **Periapical Index (PAI)** scoring system.
   - Stated as “scoring ranged from 1 to 5,” but also references **0/1** for absence/presence; and uses cutoffs:
     - **PAI < 3:** absence of periapical lesion
     - **PAI > 3:** presence of periapical lesion  
   - Therefore, PAI is intended as an **ordinal** score, plus a derived **binary** outcome (lesion present/absent).

2. **Quality of root canal filling**
   - Based on:
     - **Density/voids/homogeneity** (“absence of voids” and “condensation”)
     - **Filling length**: distance between end of filling and radiographic apex
   - **Scoring:** **1 to 6** using criteria from Unal et al.
   - Derived classification:
     - **Adequate RCT:** acceptable filling length **and** homogeneous filling (binary adequate vs inadequate).

### Other variables not explicitly provided but typically needed (not in text)
- Tooth type, number of roots/canals, operator level, retreatment technique, patient demographics, clustering by patient (multiple teeth), etc. None are described in the provided methods.

# CATEGORY A — MANDATORY (core analyses)

## A1) Descriptive statistics (with paired reporting)
- **Purpose:** Summarize baseline and follow-up distributions of PAI and quality scores; quantify improvement/worsening.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Necessary for transparency and clinical interpretation (e.g., % adequate at baseline and 6 months).
  2. Informs choice of inferential tests (ordinal vs binary; skewness).
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):** If the dataset lacks paired IDs, “paired change” summaries cannot be computed.
- **When NOT to use:** Never omit descriptive statistics; but do not present means/SD for clearly ordinal scores as the only summary.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:** Use medians/IQRs and distribution plots instead of means/SD.

Minimum recommended outputs:
- PAI (ordinal): median (IQR) at baseline and 6 months; and % above lesion threshold.
- Adequate RCT (%).
- Change table: improved/unchanged/worsened.

---

## A2) McNemar test (paired binary outcome)
- **Test name:** McNemar’s test (exact version if sparse)
- **Purpose:** Test whether the **proportion** with a condition changes from baseline to 6 months:
  - e.g., **PAI lesion present vs absent** (based on pre-specified cutoff), and/or **adequate vs inadequate RCT**.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Designed for **paired dichotomous** data (same tooth measured twice).
  2. Tests change using only **discordant pairs**, matching the clinical “conversion” question (e.g., lesion resolved).
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):** If observations are not paired (different teeth at baseline and follow-up) or if clustering by patient is substantial and unaddressed.
- **When NOT to use:** When outcome has >2 categories (use ordinal methods) or when pairing is absent.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - If clustering or covariate adjustment is needed: **GEE logistic regression** with time indicator.
  - If very small discordant counts: **Exact McNemar**.

Report:
- Discordant pair counts, **paired risk difference** and/or **paired odds ratio** (with CI).

---

## A3) Wilcoxon signed-rank test (paired ordinal score)
- **Test name:** Wilcoxon signed-rank test
- **Purpose:** Compare **paired ordinal** scores (baseline vs 6 months), e.g.:
  - PAI score (ordinal)
  - Quality score 1–6 (ordinal)
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Suitable for **paired** measurements without assuming normality.
  2. Appropriate for **ordinal** scales where equal-interval assumptions are doubtful.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):** If there are many ties/zero differences (common with ordinal scales), power and interpretation can be limited.
- **When NOT to use:** When data are unpaired; or when you need to model covariates, clustering, or non-monotone change patterns.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - For many ties: **sign test** (less power but fewer assumptions).
  - For covariate adjustment/clustering: **ordinal mixed model** or **GEE ordinal model**.

Report:
- Median paired change and Hodges–Lehmann estimate (if computed) with CI.

# CATEGORY B — RECOMMENDED (improves rigor/interpretability)

## B1) Inter-/intra-examiner reliability for scoring (kappa / weighted kappa / ICC)
- **Test name:** Cohen’s kappa (binary), **weighted kappa** (ordinal), or ICC (if treated quasi-continuous)
- **Purpose:** Quantify reliability of PAI and quality scoring from radiographs.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Outcomes are **rater-dependent**; reliability affects validity.
  2. Strengthens credibility of retrospective radiographic assessments.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):** If only one scorer and no re-reading subset exists; then reliability cannot be estimated.
- **When NOT to use:** Do not report kappa if categories are extremely imbalanced without also reporting prevalence-adjusted interpretation (kappa paradox).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:** Use **Gwet’s AC1/AC2** when kappa is unstable under imbalance.

---

## B2) Paired effect sizes with confidence intervals
- **Test name:** Estimation-focused analysis (e.g., paired risk difference/ratio; Hodges–Lehmann median difference)
- **Purpose:** Provide magnitude of improvement, not only significance.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Clinically meaningful interpretation (e.g., % lesions resolved).
  2. CI conveys precision and is robust to sample size issues.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):** If the sample is not truly paired or follow-up is incomplete and biased.
- **When NOT to use:** Avoid naive CI calculations that ignore pairing.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:** **Bootstrap CIs** (cluster bootstrap if multiple teeth per patient).

---

## B3) Regression modeling to adjust for confounding and clustering (GEE or mixed effects)
- **Test name:**  
  - **GEE logistic regression** for binary outcomes (lesion present, adequate RCT)  
  - **Ordinal mixed-effects model** (cumulative logit) for PAI/quality scores  
- **Purpose:** Estimate the association of **time (baseline vs 6 months)** with outcomes while accounting for:
  - repeated measures (time)
  - possible **multiple teeth per patient** (clustering)
  - additional covariates (if available)
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Handles **correlated observations** (within tooth over time; within patient if multiple teeth).
  2. Allows **adjustment** and yields interpretable parameters (ORs) with CIs.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):** If covariates and patient/tooth identifiers are unavailable; model may be over-parameterized given available information.
- **When NOT to use:** If sample size is too small for model complexity or if the proportional odds assumption is badly violated (for ordinal models).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - For proportional odds violation: **partial proportional odds model** or treat as binary (pre-specified clinically meaningful cut).
  - For small samples/separation: **Firth penalized logistic regression**.

---

## B4) Sensitivity analysis for the PAI cutoff definition
- **Test name:** Sensitivity analysis using alternative dichotomizations (e.g., PAI ≥3 vs <3; PAI ≥4 vs ≤3), plus ordinal analysis
- **Purpose:** Address ambiguity/inconsistency in the text regarding PAI scale and threshold.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Cutoff choice can materially change “lesion present” prevalence and McNemar results.
  2. Improves robustness and transparency.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):** If the original dataset coding cannot support reclassification (e.g., only stored as binary).
- **When NOT to use:** If it becomes data-driven fishing; must be pre-specified as sensitivity, not primary.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:** Use **ordinal-only** approach (Wilcoxon/ordinal model) as primary.

# CATEGORY C — OPTIONAL (supplementary insights)

## C1) Transition matrices and Bowker’s test of symmetry (multi-category paired)
- **Test name:** Bowker’s test of symmetry (generalized McNemar)
- **Purpose:** Evaluate whether changes across **multiple ordinal categories** are symmetric (e.g., 1–5 PAI transitions).
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Uses the full categorical scale rather than dichotomizing.
  2. Highlights where changes occur (e.g., mostly from 4→2).
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):** Requires adequate counts in off-diagonal cells; sparse tables reduce validity.
- **When NOT to use:** When categories are sparse or heavily tied.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:** Collapse categories or use **ordinal mixed models**.

---

## C2) Time-to-event analysis (only if additional follow-ups exist)
- **Test name:** Kaplan–Meier / Cox model
- **Purpose:** Evaluate time to healing/failure if multiple follow-up times are present.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Accounts for variable follow-up duration and censoring.
  2. Clinically relevant for healing trajectory.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):** Provided text includes only **one** follow-up at 6 months, so time-to-event is not supported.
- **When NOT to use:** When there are only two timepoints and no event times.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:** Use paired binary/ordinal analyses at 6 months.

---

## C3) Agreement between “adequate RCT” and periapical healing at 6 months
- **Test name:** Chi-square/Fisher (cross-sectional) or logistic regression at follow-up
- **Purpose:** Explore whether adequate filling quality is associated with lesion absence at 6 months.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Addresses clinically meaningful linkage between technical quality and outcome.
  2. Can guide hypotheses for future prospective work.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):** Retrospective design and confounding; causality cannot be inferred.
- **When NOT to use:** If both are derived from the same scoring process with shared rater bias, interpret cautiously.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:** Fisher’s exact test or penalized logistic regression.

## TASK 3 — Inappropriate tests (should NOT be used)

1. **Independent-samples t-test / one-way ANOVA** (baseline vs follow-up)
   - **Why inappropriate:** Measurements are **paired** on the same tooth; violates independence. Also outcomes are ordinal.
2. **Paired t-test** on PAI or 1–6 quality score (as primary)
   - **Why inappropriate:** Treats ordinal scores as continuous with equal intervals and normal differences; often unjustified.
3. **Pearson correlation** between baseline and follow-up as evidence of “improvement”
   - **Why inappropriate:** Correlation measures association, not change; high correlation can occur despite no improvement.
4. **Simple chi-square test of proportions** ignoring pairing (baseline vs follow-up lesion prevalence)
   - **Why inappropriate:** Ignores within-tooth pairing; p-values and CIs will be wrong.
5. **Linear regression** on ordinal outcomes without justification
   - **Why inappropriate:** Assumes interval scale and normal residuals; may produce misleading estimates.

## TASK 4 — Summary justification of the analytical approach

The study compares **radiographic outcomes before and after re-treatment** at a **6-month paired follow-up**. Therefore, the core analysis should use **paired methods**: (i) **McNemar** for dichotomized clinical endpoints (lesion present/absent; adequate/inadequate), and (ii) **Wilcoxon signed-rank** (or ordinal models) for the original **ordinal scores** (PAI; 1–6 quality). Rigor is improved by reporting **effect sizes with CIs**, evaluating **scoring reliability**, and—if multiple teeth per patient are included—using **GEE/mixed models** to account for **clustering** and allow adjustment.
