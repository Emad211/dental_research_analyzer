## TASK 1 — Information extraction (study characteristics)

### Study design and setting
- **Design:** Randomized, prospective, parallel-group **randomized controlled trial (RCT)**.
- **Centres:** Two university dental hospitals in Turkey (Erciyes University; Aydın Adnan Menderes University).
- **Recruitment period:** February 2018 to July 2019.
- **Registration/ethics:** ClinicalTrials.gov **NCT03919318**; ethics approval Erciyes University (2018/08).

### Objective(s) / hypotheses
- **Primary objective:** Compare the **incidence and intensity of postoperative pain** after single-visit root canal treatment among three sealers.
- **Secondary objective:** Compare **analgesic intake** among groups.
- **Null hypotheses:**
  1) Sealer type does not change pain incidence/intensity.  
  2) Analgesic intake does not differ among groups.

### Participants
- **Population:** Adults needing RCT of **mandibular 1st/2nd molars** diagnosed with **asymptomatic irreversible pulpitis** due to deep caries; healthy periapical tissues radiographically.
- **Key inclusion:** age 18–60; good oral hygiene; no analgesic/antibiotic use in last 7 days; positive cold/EPT; profuse bleeding on exposure with no hemostasis in 2–3 min; healthy periapical tissues.

- **Post-randomization/quality exclusions (important analytically):** **Overfilling/extrusion** beyond apex or **short fill >2 mm** short of radiographic apex (listed as exclusion criterion—implies these cases should be excluded from analysis if occur).

### Interventions / groups
Single-visit endodontic treatment with standardized protocol; random allocation to sealer:
1. **AH Plus** (epoxy/amine resin-based)
2. **Endoseal MTA** (calcium silicate/pozzolan-based MTA sealer)
3. **EndoSequence BC Sealer** (calcium silicate-based)

### Randomization and blinding
- **Allocation method:** Computer algorithm (random.org), equal allocation.
- **Allocation concealment:** A trained dental assistant **blinded to study procedures** performed allocation.
- **Blinding of operators/participants/outcome assessors:** Not clearly stated; pain outcomes were **self-reported** with daily phone call follow-up (risk of performance/detection bias if participants/operators not blinded).

### Sample size
- **A priori calculation:** G*Power; α=0.05, power=0.85; minimum **24 per group**.
- **Planned enrollment:** 32/group (96 total) anticipating dropouts.
- **Actual analyzed/enrolled after withdrawal:** 6 withdrew at start of obturation; final **n=90**, **30 per group**.

### Outcome variables and measurement schedule
**Primary outcome (pain):**
- **Instrument:** **VAS 0–100** (0=no pain; 100=unbearable).
- **Time points:** **6, 12, 24, 48 hours**, and **days 3, 4, 5, 6, 7** post-treatment.

**Secondary outcome (analgesic intake/pain impact category):**

# CATEGORY A — Mandatory tests (analysis incomplete without these)

## A1) Linear mixed-effects model (LMM) for repeated-measures VAS
- **Purpose:** Compare **postoperative pain intensity trajectories** over time among 3 sealers while accounting for within-patient correlation.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1) VAS measured **repeatedly** (9 time points); LMM models correlation via random effects.
  2) Handles **unbalanced/missing** observations without discarding subjects (under MAR).
  3) Allows direct testing of **Group**, **Time**, and **Group×Time interaction** (whether sealers differ at specific times or overall pattern differs).
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - If VAS has strong **non-normality**, heavy skew/ceiling at 0, or heteroscedasticity that is not well addressed by transformations/robust SEs.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When outcome is fundamentally non-continuous with extreme zero-inflation where continuous assumptions are untenable and alternative models fit clearly better (e.g., two-part models).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Robust LMM** (sandwich/Huber-White SE), **transformed VAS** (e.g., log(VAS+1)), or **generalized linear mixed model (GLMM)** with appropriate distribution; or **rank-based mixed models**.

**Implementation notes:**
- Fixed effects: Group (3), Time (categorical), Group×Time; consider baseline pain if measured (not described).
- Random effects: patient-level random intercept; consider random slope for time if supported.
- Planned contrasts: pairwise group comparisons at each time point with multiplicity control.

---

## A2) Ordinal mixed-effects model (cumulative logit GLMM) for analgesic/pain-impact score (0/1/2)
- **Purpose:** Assess whether sealers differ in the **severity/analgesic category** across time.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1) Outcome is **ordinal (3 levels)**; ordinal model respects ordering without forcing equal spacing.
  2) Repeated measures over time require within-subject correlation handling; mixed model does this.
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - **Proportional odds assumption** may be violated; with sparse “2” category counts, estimation may be unstable.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When categories are too sparse (e.g., almost all 0/1) causing separation or nonconvergence.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Partial proportional odds** model; **multinomial mixed model**; or collapse to binary (e.g., any analgesic use vs none) and use **binary mixed model (logit GLMM/GEE)**.

---

## A3) Baseline comparability (descriptive + appropriate tests)
Even though randomization targets balance, reporting is standard in RCTs.
- **Test name:** One-way **ANOVA** or **Kruskal–Wallis** (continuous baseline variables), and **Chi-square/Fisher’s exact** (categorical).
- **Purpose:** Describe whether groups are similar at baseline (site, age, sex, tooth type, etc. if collected).
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1) Helps identify chance imbalances that may warrant **adjusted analyses**.
  2) Provides transparency/CONSORT-style reporting.
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - Significance testing of baseline characteristics is sometimes discouraged because differences arise by chance under randomization.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Do not use baseline p-values to decide inclusion/exclusion of covariates mechanistically.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - For ANOVA → **Welch ANOVA** or **Kruskal–Wallis**; for Chi-square with small counts → **Fisher’s exact**.

# CATEGORY B — Recommended tests (improve rigor)

## B1) Generalized estimating equations (GEE) as sensitivity analysis (VAS and/or binary analgesic)
- **Purpose:** Provide **population-averaged** estimates robust to random-effects distribution assumptions.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1) Robust to misspecification of random-effects distribution; focuses on marginal effects.
  2) Useful sensitivity check against LMM/GLMM conclusions.
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - Requires enough subjects for robust sandwich SEs; with n=90, still usually acceptable but can be less stable with complex correlation structures.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When subject-specific effects are needed (e.g., prediction), or when missing data mechanism is likely MNAR and not addressed.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Return to **mixed models** or use **multiple imputation + LMM/GLMM**.

---

## B2) Multiplicity control for repeated time-point comparisons
- **Test/procedure:** **Holm correction** (family-wise) or **Benjamini–Hochberg (FDR)**; or pre-specify primary time point (e.g., 24h) and treat others as secondary.
- **Purpose:** Avoid inflated type I error from multiple pairwise/time comparisons.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1) 3 groups × many time points → many contrasts.
  2) Protects inference credibility.
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - Can reduce power, especially with small sample sizes.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If the primary inference is based on a **global interaction** test within a mixed model with limited planned contrasts.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Use **model-based global tests** (Group, Group×Time) with a few pre-planned contrasts.

---

## B3) Effect size estimation with confidence intervals
- **Measures:**  
  - VAS: **mean difference** (or standardized mean difference) at clinically relevant times; **area under the curve (AUC)** difference over 7 days.
  - Ordinal: **common odds ratio** (from ordinal model).
- **Purpose:** Quantify clinical magnitude, not only p-values.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1) RCT interpretation should emphasize **effect sizes** with CIs.
  2) Enables clinical relevance judgments (e.g., minimal clinically important difference for VAS).
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - If distribution is highly skewed with many zeros, mean difference may be less interpretable.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Do not overinterpret standardized effects without clinical context.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Use **median difference** via quantile methods, or **AUC** / **probability of superiority**.

---

## B4) Model adjustment for key design factors (covariate-adjusted mixed model)
- **Test/model:** LMM/GLMM including covariates: **centre**, **operator**, **tooth type**, and possibly **final file size (R25/R40)** if recorded.
- **Purpose:** Increase precision and address potential clustering/heterogeneity.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1) Two centres and two operators could influence pain outcomes.
  2) Adjusting can reduce residual variance and improve estimate precision.
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - Overfitting risk if many covariates with modest sample size and sparse outcomes.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If covariates are not reliably measured or have near-zero variability.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Include **centre/operator as random effects** if sufficient levels; otherwise fixed effects with parsimonious selection.

# CATEGORY C — Optional (supplementary insights)

## C1) Time-to-event analysis: time to “pain-free” (VAS=0) or time to no-analgesic category (0)
- **Test:** **Kaplan–Meier** curves + **log-rank test**; **Cox regression** for adjusted comparison.
- **Purpose:** Compare how quickly groups resolve pain.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1) Captures dynamic recovery and clinically intuitive endpoint.
  2) Uses partial information when pain resolves at different times.
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - Pain assessed at discrete times; event time is **interval-censored** (exact time unknown).
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If most participants have pain=0 from earliest time points (few events) or interval censoring is substantial without proper methods.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Interval-censored survival models** or use **AUC** / mixed models instead.

## C2) Nonparametric repeated-measures approach (if VAS severely non-normal)
- **Test:** **Friedman test** (within-group over time) plus between-group comparisons at each time with **Kruskal–Wallis**.
- **Purpose:** Distribution-free check of patterns.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1) Does not assume normality.
  2) Simple to report as sensitivity analysis.
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - Cannot easily model **Group×Time** interaction with missing data; limited inferential flexibility.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - As the primary analysis for this multi-timepoint RCT with missingness and covariates.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Rank-based mixed models** or robust LMM.

  2) Identifies reporting inconsistencies.

  - Actual pill consumption data not described; may not exist.

## TASK 3 — Inappropriate tests (should NOT be used)

1) **Multiple independent t-tests at each time point without correction**
- **Why not:** Inflates type I error; ignores within-subject correlation; wastes repeated-measures structure.

2) **One-way ANOVA on a single “pooled” dataset treating all repeated VAS measurements as independent**
- **Why not:** Violates independence; underestimates SEs and overstates significance.

3) **Paired t-test between groups**
- **Why not:** Groups are independent (parallel RCT), not paired/matched.

4) **Pearson correlation between sealer group (coded 1/2/3) and VAS**
- **Why not:** Group is categorical; numeric coding imposes artificial ordering/linearity; wrong estimand.

5) **Linear regression on ordinal analgesic score treating it as continuous without checking**
- **Why not:** 0/1/2 is ordinal with limited levels; linear model can give nonsensical fitted values and assumes equal spacing.

6) **Simple Chi-square test on analgesic categories pooled across all time points**
- **Why not:** Repeated measures violate independence; pooling discards time pattern and within-subject correlation.

## TASK 4 — Summary justification of the overall approach

This is a 3-arm parallel RCT with **repeated post-treatment measurements** of pain (VAS) and an **ordinal analgesic/pain-impact outcome** over 7 days. The core analysis should therefore use **mixed-effects (or marginal GEE) longitudinal models** that (i) respect within-subject correlation, (ii) allow **group, time, and group×time** comparisons, (iii) handle missing follow-up appropriately, and (iv) yield interpretable effect sizes with multiplicity-aware inference across multiple time points and pairwise group contrasts.

### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY
1. **Test Name**: Repeated Measures ANOVA or Mixed-Effects Model  
   - **Purpose**: To compare VAS pain scores over time across the three groups.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. The outcome (VAS score) is continuous and measured repeatedly over time.  
     2. It accounts for within-subject correlations due to repeated measures.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Assumes normality of residuals and sphericity (for ANOVA).  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If data are non-normal or sphericity is violated.  
   - **Alternative**: Non-parametric test (Friedman test) or Generalized Estimating Equations (GEE).

2. **Test Name**: Chi-Square Test or Fisher’s Exact Test  
   - **Purpose**: To compare the frequency of analgesic intake categories across groups.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. The outcome (analgesic intake) is categorical.  
     2. It evaluates differences in proportions among groups.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Small sample sizes in some categories may lead to inaccurate results.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If expected cell counts are <5.  
   - **Alternative**: Fisher’s Exact Test.

3. **Test Name**: One-Way ANOVA  
   - **Purpose**: To compare mean VAS scores at specific time points across groups.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. The outcome (VAS score) is continuous.  
     2. It evaluates group differences at individual time points.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Assumes normality and homogeneity of variance.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If assumptions are violated.  
   - **Alternative**: Kruskal-Wallis test.

---

#### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED
1. **Test Name**: Post-Hoc Pairwise Comparisons (e.g., Tukey’s HSD)  
   - **Purpose**: To identify which groups differ significantly in pain intensity or analgesic intake.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Provides detailed insights into pairwise group differences.  
     2. Controls for Type I error in multiple comparisons.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Increased risk of Type II error if sample size is small.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If overall group differences are not significant.  
   - **Alternative**: Bonferroni correction.

2. **Test Name**: Kaplan-Meier Survival Analysis  
   - **Purpose**: To evaluate time to pain resolution across groups.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Accounts for censored data (e.g., patients dropping out).  
     2. Suitable for time-to-event data.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Assumes proportional hazards, which may not hold.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If time-to-event data are not available.  
   - **Alternative**: Cox Proportional Hazards Model.

---

#### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL
1. **Test Name**: Multivariable Regression Analysis  
   - **Purpose**: To adjust for potential confounders (e.g., age, gender).  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Identifies independent predictors of postoperative pain.  
     2. Adjusts for baseline imbalances.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Requires larger sample size for multiple predictors.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If sample size is insufficient.  
   - **Alternative**: Stratified analysis.

2. **Test Name**: Effect Size Calculations (e.g., Cohen’s d)  
   - **Purpose**: To quantify the magnitude of differences between groups.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Provides clinically meaningful interpretation.

   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Does not test statistical significance.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If group differences are negligible.  
   - **Alternative**: Confidence intervals for mean differences.

### A2. Comparison of pain intensity (VAS) between sealer groups over time

Given repeated VAS measurements, there are two main options:

#### A2a. Mixed-effects linear model (preferred)

**Test:**  
- Linear mixed-effects model (LMM) with:
  - Outcome: VAS score.
  - Fixed effects: group (3 levels), time (9 levels), group × time interaction.
  - Random effect: patient-specific random intercept (and optional random slope for time).
  - Consider appropriate link/transform if needed (e.g., logit transform of scaled VAS).

**Purpose:**  
- Compare pain trajectories over time among groups, accounting for within-patient correlation and repeated measures.

**Why appropriate:**  
1. Repeated VAS measures within subjects violate independence; LMM explicitly models this correlation.  
2. Allows inclusion of all available data under missing-at-random, increasing efficiency and handling occasional missing time points.

**Why it may not be appropriate:**  
- VAS data are bounded (0–100) and potentially skewed; a simple linear model may violate normality and homoscedasticity assumptions if distributions are very skewed or many zeros.

**When NOT to use:**  
- When the VAS outcome is extremely non-normal (e.g., heavy ceiling/floor effects, many zeros) such that residual diagnostics show severe violations.  
- When the statistical expertise or software is not available to fit and interpret LMM correctly.

**Alternative if assumptions violated:**  
- Generalized linear mixed model (GLMM) with:
  - For continuous but bounded: Beta regression (on 0–1 scaled VAS, adjusting for zeros with a zero-inflated model if needed).  
  - For categorized VAS (e.g., no/mild/moderate/severe): ordinal mixed-effects model.

#### A2b. Repeated-measures ANOVA (traditional, less flexible)

**Test:**  
- Repeated-measures ANOVA with within-subject factor “time” and between-subject factor “group”.

**Purpose:**  
- Same as above: compare mean VAS over time between groups.

**Why appropriate:**  
1. Classical approach widely used in clinical dentistry literature; relatively straightforward to implement.  
2. Accounts for within-subject correlation via within-subject factor structure.

**Why it may not be appropriate:**  
- Requires sphericity assumption; violations are likely with 9 time points.  
- Less robust to missing data (listwise deletion usually required).

**When NOT to use:**  
- When there is considerable missing data at some time points.  
- When sphericity is badly violated and corrections (Greenhouse–Geisser) lead to very low effective df.

**Alternative if assumptions violated:**  
- Use LMM as above (A2a).  
- If wanting nonparametric: Friedman test per group, but that does not handle between-group comparisons well; better is mixed-model/GLMM.

**Why appropriate:**

**Why it may not be appropriate:**

**When NOT to use:**

**Alternative if assumptions violated:**

### A4. Comparison of analgesic intake (ordinal outcome) between groups over time

#### A4a. Ordinal logistic mixed-effects model (preferred)

**Test:**  
- Ordinal mixed-effects model (cumulative logit GLMM) with:
  - Outcome: analgesic category (0, 1, 2).
  - Fixed effects: group, time, group × time.
  - Random intercept for patient.

**Purpose:**  
- Compare distribution and trajectory of analgesic use severity categories between sealer groups.

**Why appropriate:**  
1. Outcome is clearly ordinal; ordinal models respect the ordering and avoid collapsing into binary.  
2. Mixed-effects structure captures repeated measures and correlation within patients, while allowing flexible time effects.

**Why it may not be appropriate:**  
- Requires proportional odds assumption; if violated, interpretation becomes difficult without more complex modeling.  
- Statistical expertise/software may be limited.

**When NOT to use:**  
- When very few patients reach category 2, leading to sparse data in upper category; the model may not converge.  
- When there is strong evidence that proportional odds assumption is violated and sample size is insufficient for a partial proportional odds model.

**Alternative if assumptions violated:**  
- Dichotomize outcome (e.g., 0 vs 1–2) and use binary logistic mixed-effects model.  
- Use nonparametric tests at individual time points (e.g., Kruskal–Wallis) if repeated-measures modeling is not feasible.

#### A4b. Nonparametric comparison at each time point (simple approach)

**Test:**  
- Kruskal–Wallis test for analgesic score at each time point.

**Purpose:**  
- Compare distributions of analgesic categories between groups without assuming normality.

**Why appropriate:**  
1. Ordinal outcome; Kruskal–Wallis respects rank ordering and does not require normality.  
2. Easy to implement and report.

**Why it may not be appropriate:**  
- Ignores the repeated-measures structure; multiple tests across time inflate type I error and fail to model within-subject correlation.

**When NOT to use:**  
- When a full multilevel analysis is planned or mandated.  
- When missing data pattern is nontrivial; Kruskal–Wallis at each time leads to different subsets of patients per time.

**Alternative if assumptions violated:**

TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS
===========================

Tests that should NOT be used for this study, with reasons.

1. **Independent samples t-tests at every time point (pairwise, unadjusted)**  
   - Why inappropriate:
     - Three groups and nine time points: multiple pairwise t-tests would massively inflate type I error.  
     - Ignores correlation across repeated measurements and multiplicity.
   - Better: ANOVA/mixed models with multiplicity adjustments.

2. **Paired t-tests comparing pre- and post-treatment VAS within groups**  
   - There is no pre-treatment VAS reported here; even if baseline pain were recorded, the trial question is between groups, not within.  
   - Paired tests within groups do not address comparative effectiveness and can mislead.

3. **Simple linear regression of “group” as continuous predictor**  
   - Group is categorical with three unordered levels; treating it as continuous imposes an artificial ordering and linearity that is not meaningful.

4. **Correlation coefficients (Pearson/Spearman) between group and pain**  
   - Group is categorical; correlation coefficient is not meaningful. Proper method is ANOVA/GLM/GLMM.

5. **Using parametric tests that ignore repeated measures (e.g., ANOVA on mean VAS per group without accounting for within-patient correlation)**  
   - Collapsing repeated measures without a clear summary (AUC) or ignoring correlation is statistically inefficient and may mis-estimate variability.

6. **Nonparametric tests for repeated measures across groups like multiple Friedman's tests**  
   - Friedman test is for within-subject comparisons of treatments, not for comparing treatment groups between subjects. Mis-application (e.g., per group separately) doesn’t answer between-group questions.

7. **Multiple chi-square tests on every combination of time point and group without adjustment**  
   - Severe multiplicity problem; p-values become uninterpretable.

8. **MANOVA on time points as multiple dependent variables without considering the repeated nature**  
   - Treating repeated measures as independent multivariate outcomes via MANOVA is uncommon and not well suited here; mixed models are more appropriate.

9. **Student’s t-test for proportions (instead of chi-square / logistic regression)**  
   - Proportions (e.g., incidence of pain) are not continuous; using t-tests would violate distribution assumptions.
