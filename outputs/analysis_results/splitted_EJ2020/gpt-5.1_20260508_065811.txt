A. CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS
-------------------------------

These are core analyses necessary to answer the main trial questions.

### A1. Baseline comparability across groups

**Test:**  
- For continuous baseline variables (e.g., age): One-way ANOVA or Kruskal–Wallis test.  
- For categorical variables (e.g., sex, tooth type): Chi-square test or Fisher’s exact test.

**Purpose:**  
- Assess whether randomization produced comparable groups at baseline regarding key prognostic factors.

**Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):**  
1. Study design is a three-arm RCT; baseline comparability supports valid attribution of differences to the intervention.  
2. Variables like age are likely approximately continuous and roughly normal in this sample; ANOVA is standard. Categorical demographics suit chi-square tests.

**Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason):**  
- Some argue baseline testing is unnecessary or misleading in RCTs because imbalances occur by chance; the primary analysis should not be conditioned on these tests.

**When NOT to use:**  
- When baseline variables are clearly not relevant to outcomes (e.g., irrelevant ancillary measures).  
- When sample sizes are so small or category counts so low that chi-square assumptions are violated (then use Fisher’s exact, or just tabulate descriptively without formal tests).

**Alternative if assumptions violated:**  
- For continuous variables with non-normal distribution or unequal variances: Kruskal–Wallis test or Welch’s ANOVA.  
- For sparse categorical tables: Fisher’s exact test.

### A2. Comparison of pain intensity (VAS) between sealer groups over time

Given repeated VAS measurements, there are two main options:

#### A2a. Mixed-effects linear model (preferred)

**Test:**  
- Linear mixed-effects model (LMM) with:
  - Outcome: VAS score.
  - Fixed effects: group (3 levels), time (9 levels), group × time interaction.
  - Random effect: patient-specific random intercept (and optional random slope for time).
  - Consider appropriate link/transform if needed (e.g., logit transform of scaled VAS).

**Purpose:**  
- Compare pain trajectories over time among groups, accounting for within-patient correlation and repeated measures.

**Why appropriate:**  
1. Repeated VAS measures within subjects violate independence; LMM explicitly models this correlation.  
2. Allows inclusion of all available data under missing-at-random, increasing efficiency and handling occasional missing time points.

**Why it may not be appropriate:**  
- VAS data are bounded (0–100) and potentially skewed; a simple linear model may violate normality and homoscedasticity assumptions if distributions are very skewed or many zeros.

**When NOT to use:**  
- When the VAS outcome is extremely non-normal (e.g., heavy ceiling/floor effects, many zeros) such that residual diagnostics show severe violations.  
- When the statistical expertise or software is not available to fit and interpret LMM correctly.

**Alternative if assumptions violated:**  
- Generalized linear mixed model (GLMM) with:
  - For continuous but bounded: Beta regression (on 0–1 scaled VAS, adjusting for zeros with a zero-inflated model if needed).  
  - For categorized VAS (e.g., no/mild/moderate/severe): ordinal mixed-effects model.

#### A2b. Repeated-measures ANOVA (traditional, less flexible)

**Test:**  
- Repeated-measures ANOVA with within-subject factor “time” and between-subject factor “group”.

**Purpose:**  
- Same as above: compare mean VAS over time between groups.

**Why appropriate:**  
1. Classical approach widely used in clinical dentistry literature; relatively straightforward to implement.  
2. Accounts for within-subject correlation via within-subject factor structure.

**Why it may not be appropriate:**  
- Requires sphericity assumption; violations are likely with 9 time points.  
- Less robust to missing data (listwise deletion usually required).

**When NOT to use:**  
- When there is considerable missing data at some time points.  
- When sphericity is badly violated and corrections (Greenhouse–Geisser) lead to very low effective df.

**Alternative if assumptions violated:**  
- Use LMM as above (A2a).  
- If wanting nonparametric: Friedman test per group, but that does not handle between-group comparisons well; better is mixed-model/GLMM.

### A3. Comparison of incidence of pain (presence vs absence) between groups at specific time points

**Test:**  
- Chi-square test or Fisher’s exact test at each clinically important time point (e.g., 24 h, 48 h, day 3) for proportion of patients with pain (VAS > 0).  
- Alternatively, logistic regression per time point with group as predictor.

**Purpose:**  
- Compare the proportion of patients experiencing any postoperative pain across sealers.

**Why appropriate:**  
1. Primary clinical interpretation often in terms of “pain vs no pain” at key times; simple proportions are readily interpretable.  
2. Pain incidence per time point yields binary data; chi-square/logistic regression is standard for such comparisons.

**Why it may not be appropriate:**  
- Multiple time points ⇒ multiple tests; risk of inflated type I error unless adjusted or pre-specified primary time point is selected.

**When NOT to use:**  
- When cell counts are very small (e.g., very few patients with pain) making chi-square invalid (expected cell counts <5).  
- When focus is on overall pain trajectory rather than isolated time points.

**Alternative if assumptions violated:**  
- Fisher’s exact test for small counts.  
- GLMM with binary outcome (pain yes/no) across all time points, including group, time, and group × time.

---

### A4. Comparison of analgesic intake (ordinal outcome) between groups over time

#### A4a. Ordinal logistic mixed-effects model (preferred)

**Test:**  
- Ordinal mixed-effects model (cumulative logit GLMM) with:
  - Outcome: analgesic category (0, 1, 2).
  - Fixed effects: group, time, group × time.
  - Random intercept for patient.

**Purpose:**  
- Compare distribution and trajectory of analgesic use severity categories between sealer groups.

**Why appropriate:**  
1. Outcome is clearly ordinal; ordinal models respect the ordering and avoid collapsing into binary.  
2. Mixed-effects structure captures repeated measures and correlation within patients, while allowing flexible time effects.

**Why it may not be appropriate:**  
- Requires proportional odds assumption; if violated, interpretation becomes difficult without more complex modeling.  
- Statistical expertise/software may be limited.

**When NOT to use:**  
- When very few patients reach category 2, leading to sparse data in upper category; the model may not converge.  
- When there is strong evidence that proportional odds assumption is violated and sample size is insufficient for a partial proportional odds model.

**Alternative if assumptions violated:**  
- Dichotomize outcome (e.g., 0 vs 1–2) and use binary logistic mixed-effects model.  
- Use nonparametric tests at individual time points (e.g., Kruskal–Wallis) if repeated-measures modeling is not feasible.

#### A4b. Nonparametric comparison at each time point (simple approach)

**Test:**  
- Kruskal–Wallis test for analgesic score at each time point.

**Purpose:**  
- Compare distributions of analgesic categories between groups without assuming normality.

**Why appropriate:**  
1. Ordinal outcome; Kruskal–Wallis respects rank ordering and does not require normality.  
2. Easy to implement and report.

**Why it may not be appropriate:**  
- Ignores the repeated-measures structure; multiple tests across time inflate type I error and fail to model within-subject correlation.

**When NOT to use:**  
- When a full multilevel analysis is planned or mandated.  
- When missing data pattern is nontrivial; Kruskal–Wallis at each time leads to different subsets of patients per time.

**Alternative if assumptions violated:**  
- Move to GLMM as above.

### A5. Primary endpoint specification and multiplicity control

Even though not a “test,” it is mandatory in a statistical sense.

**Plan:**

- Pre-specify:
  - Primary outcome: likely VAS pain intensity at a clinically meaningful time (e.g., 24 h), and/or area under the curve (AUC) over 7 days.
  - Primary comparison: three-group comparison at that time point (e.g., ANOVA or Kruskal–Wallis) and pairwise post-hoc comparisons with multiplicity control.

**Tests:**
- At primary time point (e.g., 24 h VAS):
  - One-way ANOVA (or Kruskal–Wallis) across groups.
  - Post-hoc: Tukey (ANOVA) or Dunn’s test (Kruskal–Wallis) with Bonferroni or Holm adjustment.

**Why appropriate:**  
1. Focuses inference on a pre-specified main time point, controlling overall type I error.  
2. Provides simple summary of “peak” or “most clinically relevant” pain difference.

**Why it may not be appropriate:**  
- If clinical interest is in the whole trajectory rather than one time point; focusing only on one time could miss interesting effects at others.

**When NOT to use:**  
- If the primary analysis is pre-specified as a longitudinal model and no single time point is of primary interest.

**Alternative if assumptions violated:**  
- Use nonparametric tests (Kruskal–Wallis + Dunn) and/or transform VAS.

B. CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED TESTS
---------------------------------

These strengthen rigor and interpretability.

### B1. Area under the curve (AUC) for VAS

**Test:**  
- Compute individual AUC of VAS vs time (e.g., using trapezoidal rule with all recorded time points), then compare AUC across groups via:
  - One-way ANOVA, or
  - Kruskal–Wallis if non-normal.

**Purpose:**  
- Capture overall pain burden over the 7 days in a single metric.

**Why appropriate:**  
1. Integrates intensity and duration of pain into one summary; more clinically meaningful than isolated time points.  
2. Avoids multiplicity from repeated time-point tests.

**Why it may not be appropriate:**  
- Requires reasonably complete longitudinal data; missing data complicates AUC calculation or requires imputation.

**When NOT to use:**  
- When many subjects have missing time points, particularly early ones; AUC may be biased.  
- When VAS is erratic or poorly recorded, making integration unreliable.

**Alternative if assumptions violated:**  
- Use mixed models (A2a) to estimate group-specific mean trajectories and compute model-based AUC or marginal means.

### B2. Time-to-event analysis for onset or resolution of pain

**Test:**  
- Kaplan–Meier survival curves and log-rank test for:
  - Time to first occurrence of pain (VAS > 0), or
  - Time to complete pain resolution (first 0 maintained thereafter).
- Cox proportional hazards model with group as predictor.

**Purpose:**  
- Compare timing of pain emergence or resolution among groups.

**Why appropriate:**  
1. Time-to-event methods handle censoring and varying observation times; some patients may never experience pain.  
2. Provides hazard ratios and survival curves, which are easily interpretable clinically.

**Why it may not be appropriate:**  
- Pain is measured at discrete times, not continuous; exact event times may be interval-censored (e.g., pain appears sometime between 6 and 12 h), complicating standard survival methods.

**When NOT to use:**  
- When sample size is limited and event numbers per group are small.  
- When pain outcomes fluctuate (on/off), making definition of event ambiguous.

**Alternative if assumptions violated:**  
- Use discrete-time survival models (e.g., complementary log-log regression) or logistic GLMM for repeated binary pain.

### B3. Sensitivity analyses with covariate adjustment

**Test:**  
- ANCOVA-type models or mixed models including covariates such as:
  - Age, sex.  
  - Tooth type (first vs second molar).

**Purpose:**  
- Adjust for potential confounders or residual imbalances, improving precision and checking robustness of group comparisons.

**Why appropriate:**  
1. Even in RCTs, covariate adjustment can increase power and handle minor baseline imbalances.  
2. Centre/operator effects may influence pain through subtle differences in technique.

**Why it may not be appropriate:**  
- Risk of overfitting if too many covariates relative to sample size.

**When NOT to use:**

**Alternative if assumptions violated:**

### B4. Handling missing data

**Method (not a test):**  
- For primary mixed models, use maximum likelihood under missing at random (MAR).  
- For AUC or ANOVA analyses, consider:
  - Multiple imputation for missing VAS values, or  
  - Restrict to participants with complete data for main time point.

**Purpose:**  
- Minimize bias from missing follow-up forms or phone contact.

**Why appropriate:**  
1. Realistic to expect some missing pain measurements; explicit plan prevents ad hoc handling.  
2. Mixed models naturally accommodate unbalanced data.

**Why it may not be appropriate:**  
- If missingness is not at random (e.g., patients with more pain drop out differentially by group), MAR may not hold.

**When NOT to use (imputation):**  
- When missingness is minimal (<5%); complex imputation may not be necessary.

**Alternative:**  
- Perform sensitivity analyses under different missing-data assumptions (e.g., best-worst/worst-best scenarios).

TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS
===========================

Tests that should NOT be used for this study, with reasons.

1. **Independent samples t-tests at every time point (pairwise, unadjusted)**  
   - Why inappropriate:
     - Three groups and nine time points: multiple pairwise t-tests would massively inflate type I error.  
     - Ignores correlation across repeated measurements and multiplicity.
   - Better: ANOVA/mixed models with multiplicity adjustments.

2. **Paired t-tests comparing pre- and post-treatment VAS within groups**  
   - There is no pre-treatment VAS reported here; even if baseline pain were recorded, the trial question is between groups, not within.  
   - Paired tests within groups do not address comparative effectiveness and can mislead.

3. **Simple linear regression of “group” as continuous predictor**  
   - Group is categorical with three unordered levels; treating it as continuous imposes an artificial ordering and linearity that is not meaningful.

4. **Correlation coefficients (Pearson/Spearman) between group and pain**  
   - Group is categorical; correlation coefficient is not meaningful. Proper method is ANOVA/GLM/GLMM.

5. **Using parametric tests that ignore repeated measures (e.g., ANOVA on mean VAS per group without accounting for within-patient correlation)**  
   - Collapsing repeated measures without a clear summary (AUC) or ignoring correlation is statistically inefficient and may mis-estimate variability.

6. **Nonparametric tests for repeated measures across groups like multiple Friedman's tests**  
   - Friedman test is for within-subject comparisons of treatments, not for comparing treatment groups between subjects. Mis-application (e.g., per group separately) doesn’t answer between-group questions.

7. **Multiple chi-square tests on every combination of time point and group without adjustment**  
   - Severe multiplicity problem; p-values become uninterpretable.

8. **MANOVA on time points as multiple dependent variables without considering the repeated nature**  
   - Treating repeated measures as independent multivariate outcomes via MANOVA is uncommon and not well suited here; mixed models are more appropriate.

9. **Student’s t-test for proportions (instead of chi-square / logistic regression)**  
   - Proportions (e.g., incidence of pain) are not continuous; using t-tests would violate distribution assumptions.
