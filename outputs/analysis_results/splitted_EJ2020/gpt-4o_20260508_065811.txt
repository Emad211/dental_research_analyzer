### TASK 1: INFORMATION EXTRACTION

#### Study Characteristics
1. **Study Design**:  
   - Randomized controlled trial (RCT), prospective in nature.

2. **Objective**:  
   - To evaluate the effect of two calcium silicate-based root canal sealers (Endoseal MTA and EndoSequence BC Sealer) and one epoxy resin-based sealer (AH Plus) on postoperative pain following single-visit root canal treatment on mandibular molar teeth.

3. **Null Hypotheses**:  
   - The type of sealer used does not affect the incidence and intensity of postoperative pain.
   - The analgesic intake does not differ among the three experimental groups.

4. **Groups**:  
   - Group 1: AH Plus (epoxy resin-based sealer).  
   - Group 2: Endoseal MTA (calcium silicate-based sealer).  
   - Group 3: EndoSequence BC Sealer (calcium silicate-based sealer).  
   - Each group initially planned to include 32 patients, but after dropouts, 30 patients were analyzed per group.

5. **Sample Size**:  
   - Total: 96 patients enrolled, 90 completed the study (30 per group).  
   - Sample size calculation: Based on a previous study, with α = 0.05, β = 0.15 (power = 85%), and a minimum of 24 participants per group. To account for dropouts, 32 participants were recruited per group.

6. **Population**:  
   - Inclusion criteria:  
     - Adults aged 18–60 years with good oral hygiene.  
     - Mandibular first or second molars diagnosed with asymptomatic irreversible pulpitis due to deep caries.  
     - No recent use of analgesics or antibiotics (past 7 days).  
     - Healthy periapical tissues confirmed radiographically.  
   - Exclusion criteria:  
     - Medically compromised patients, symptomatic teeth, advanced periodontal disease, open apex, calcifications, resorptions, systemic/allergic sensitivity to materials, pregnant/lactating women, and teeth with extensive coronal destruction.

7. **Treatment Procedures**:  
   - Single-visit root canal treatment performed by two experienced endodontists.  
   - Standardized protocol for anesthesia, isolation, pulp vitality testing, canal preparation, irrigation, and obturation.  
   - Random allocation to groups using a computer algorithm.  
   - Root canal filling with single cone technique and sealer application.  
   - Post-treatment restoration with composite resin.

8. **Outcome Variables**:  
   - **Primary Outcome**:  
     - Postoperative pain intensity measured using the Visual Analog Scale (VAS) at 6, 12, 24, 48 hours, and on days 3–7.  
   - **Secondary Outcome**:  
     - Frequency of analgesic intake categorized into three levels (0 = no pain, 1 = moderate pain, 2 = severe pain).

9. **Data Collection**:  
   - Pain levels recorded by participants on VAS forms.  
   - Analgesic intake recorded on a separate form.  
   - Daily phone calls for 7 days to ensure compliance and collect data.

### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY
1. **Test Name**: Repeated Measures ANOVA or Mixed-Effects Model  
   - **Purpose**: To compare VAS pain scores over time across the three groups.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. The outcome (VAS score) is continuous and measured repeatedly over time.  
     2. It accounts for within-subject correlations due to repeated measures.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Assumes normality of residuals and sphericity (for ANOVA).  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If data are non-normal or sphericity is violated.  
   - **Alternative**: Non-parametric test (Friedman test) or Generalized Estimating Equations (GEE).

2. **Test Name**: Chi-Square Test or Fisher’s Exact Test  
   - **Purpose**: To compare the frequency of analgesic intake categories across groups.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. The outcome (analgesic intake) is categorical.  
     2. It evaluates differences in proportions among groups.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Small sample sizes in some categories may lead to inaccurate results.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If expected cell counts are <5.  
   - **Alternative**: Fisher’s Exact Test.

3. **Test Name**: One-Way ANOVA  
   - **Purpose**: To compare mean VAS scores at specific time points across groups.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. The outcome (VAS score) is continuous.  
     2. It evaluates group differences at individual time points.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Assumes normality and homogeneity of variance.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If assumptions are violated.  
   - **Alternative**: Kruskal-Wallis test.

---

#### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED
1. **Test Name**: Post-Hoc Pairwise Comparisons (e.g., Tukey’s HSD)  
   - **Purpose**: To identify which groups differ significantly in pain intensity or analgesic intake.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Provides detailed insights into pairwise group differences.  
     2. Controls for Type I error in multiple comparisons.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Increased risk of Type II error if sample size is small.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If overall group differences are not significant.  
   - **Alternative**: Bonferroni correction.

2. **Test Name**: Kaplan-Meier Survival Analysis  
   - **Purpose**: To evaluate time to pain resolution across groups.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Accounts for censored data (e.g., patients dropping out).  
     2. Suitable for time-to-event data.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Assumes proportional hazards, which may not hold.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If time-to-event data are not available.  
   - **Alternative**: Cox Proportional Hazards Model.

---

#### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL
1. **Test Name**: Multivariable Regression Analysis  
   - **Purpose**: To adjust for potential confounders (e.g., age, gender).  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Identifies independent predictors of postoperative pain.  
     2. Adjusts for baseline imbalances.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Requires larger sample size for multiple predictors.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If sample size is insufficient.  
   - **Alternative**: Stratified analysis.

2. **Test Name**: Effect Size Calculations (e.g., Cohen’s d)  
   - **Purpose**: To quantify the magnitude of differences between groups.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Provides clinically meaningful interpretation.  
     2. Complements p-values.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Does not test statistical significance.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If group differences are negligible.  
   - **Alternative**: Confidence intervals for mean differences.
