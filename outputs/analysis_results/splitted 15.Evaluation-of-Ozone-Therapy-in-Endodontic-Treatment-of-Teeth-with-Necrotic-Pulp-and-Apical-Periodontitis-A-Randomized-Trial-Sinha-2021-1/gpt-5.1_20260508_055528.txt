A1. Descriptive Statistics and Baseline Comparisons  

Test name  
- Descriptive statistics + one-way ANOVA (or Kruskal–Wallis) / chi-square tests for baseline comparability.

Purpose  
- To describe baseline characteristics and ensure randomization yielded comparable groups at baseline (especially for age, sex, arch, baseline VAS, PAI score).

Why appropriate (2+ reasons)  
1) Randomization inherently balances groups on average, but empirical confirmation is expected in RCTs; imbalances could affect interpretation of post-treatment differences.  
2) Baseline pain (VAS) is an important predictor of follow-up pain, so any baseline differences should be accounted for (e.g., via ANCOVA).  
3) Age, sex, and arch are pre-specified secondary variables; their distribution across groups matters for subgroup/interaction analyses.

Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)  
- Strictly, testing baseline differences in RCTs is not necessary and can be misleading (since any differences are due to chance, not bias). Overemphasis on “significant” baseline differences may lead to inappropriate post hoc adjustments.

When this test should NOT be used  
- Avoid formal hypothesis testing for baseline comparability if adopting a CONSORT-consistent philosophy that emphasizes description (means, SDs, counts) and uses adjusted models instead of baseline tests.  
- Do not overinterpret baseline p-values as evidence of bias.

Alternative if assumptions are violated  
- For baseline continuous variables with skewness or unequal variances:  
  - Use Kruskal–Wallis test or generalized linear models with suitable link (e.g., log-transformed age).  
- For categorical variables with small cell counts:  
  - Use Fisher’s exact test instead of chi-square.

A2. Primary Analysis – Mixed-Effects Model for Repeated Measures (MMRM)  

Test name  
- Linear mixed-effects model (LMM) for repeated measures, or, minimally, 2-way repeated measures ANOVA (rmANOVA).  

Purpose  
- To assess:  
  1) Overall effect of treatment group on pain over time.  
  2) Overall effect of time on pain.  
  3) Group × time interaction (do pain trajectories differ between groups?).  
- This is the primary efficacy analysis.

Why appropriate (2+ reasons)  
1) The design involves repeated VAS measurements on each subject; LMM/rmANOVA accounts for within-subject correlation and uses all repeated measures jointly.  
2) The intervention effect is primarily about differences in pain trajectories over time, which is naturally captured by the group × time interaction.  
3) LMMs can handle unbalanced data (missing at some timepoints) and unequal group sizes, which occur here due to dropouts; they are more flexible than classical rmANOVA.  
4) LMM allows inclusion of time as categorical (6 levels) and group as categorical (5 levels), with random intercepts (and potentially random slopes) for subjects.

Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)  
- VAS scores may be non-normal and bounded; if distributions are heavily skewed or show ceiling/floor effects, standard LMM assumptions (normality of residuals, homoscedasticity) may be violated.  
- If variance–covariance structure is complex and sample sizes per group are modest, model estimation can be unstable or overparameterized.

When this test should NOT be used  
- When data are extremely non-normal and standard transformations do not help, especially with many zeros or ordinal-like behavior, or when there are severe outliers that cannot reasonably be handled.  
- When there is extensive missing data not missing at random (MNAR), making LMM inferences unreliable.

Alternative if assumptions are violated  
- Use a generalized linear mixed model (GLMM) with appropriate link:  
  - E.g., gamma distribution with log link for positively skewed continuous data.  
  - Alternatively, ordinal mixed models if VAS is categorized into pain levels (none/mild/moderate/severe).  
- If model complexity is too high, use nonparametric approaches (e.g., rank-based repeated measures tests such as the ANOVA-type statistic in the “nparLD” R package).

A3. Post-hoc Pairwise Comparisons (Between Groups at Each Time Point)  

Test name  
- Pairwise comparisons with multiple-comparison adjustment (e.g., Tukey’s HSD for rmANOVA, or estimated marginal means with Bonferroni/Holm adjustment from LMM).

Purpose  
- To identify which specific groups differ in pain at relevant timepoints (e.g., 6, 12, 24, 48 hours, 7 days) if overall group × time interaction is significant.  
- Essential for clinical interpretation: e.g., is sonic/ultrasonic activation superior to no ozone? Is manual activation different from no activation?

Why appropriate (2+ reasons)  
1) Overall F-tests only tell us that differences exist; clinicians need to know where these differences occur (which group vs which, at which time).  
2) Multiple comparison procedures control type I error while allowing meaningful subgroup contrasts (e.g., each ozone activation vs control, or activated ozone vs non-activated ozone).  
3) Estimated marginal means (EMMs) from LMM provide adjusted group means at each time, accounting for missingness and covariates.

Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)  
- If the group × time interaction is not statistically significant, extensive post-hoc pairwise comparisons can be misleading and inflate type I error.  
- With limited sample sizes, multiple testing can substantially reduce power, leading to inconclusive results.

When this test should NOT be used  
- If prespecified primary comparison is a global test without interest in specific pairwise contrasts (which is rare in clinical trials of this type).  
- If the trial is underpowered for many pairwise comparisons; in that case, restrict to a few pre-specified clinically relevant contrasts.

Alternative if assumptions are violated  
- Use nonparametric pairwise tests (e.g., Mann–Whitney U at each time) with Holm–Bonferroni correction; or apply rank-based mixed models.  
- Alternatively, obtain bootstrap confidence intervals for the pairwise differences instead of relying on parametric assumptions.

Why appropriate (2+ reasons)  
1) These variables were pre-specified secondary objectives; including them in the LMM allows estimation of their independent and interaction effects on pain.  
2) Adjusting for these covariates yields more precise estimates of treatment effects and guards against chance imbalances that could bias results.  
3) Testing interactions (e.g., group × sex) can reveal if a therapy is particularly beneficial in specific subgroups (e.g., females, mandible).

Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)

- With many interaction terms relative to sample size (~110), the model can become overfitted.

When this test should NOT be used  
- When some subgroups have very low counts (e.g., <3 per cell), avoid estimating complex interactions (e.g., group × time × sex).

Alternative if assumptions are violated

TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS

Here are tests that should not be used in this study, with reasons.

1. Multiple Independent t-tests at Each Timepoint Without Adjustment  

Why inappropriate  
- Running separate independent-samples t-tests for each pair of groups at each timepoint (e.g., 10 pairwise comparisons × 5 timepoints = 50 tests) would massively inflate type I error.  
- Ignores repeated-measures nature and within-subject correlation; inefficient and statistically unsound.

2. Simple One-Way ANOVA Separately at Each Timepoint as Primary Analysis  

Why inappropriate  
- While one-way ANOVA at each time can be used descriptively, relying on it as primary without considering repeated measures ignores within-subject correlation and reduces power.  
- Does not assess the group × time interaction (pattern over time).

3. Repeated Measures ANOVA Treating VAS as Categorized Outcome (e.g., 0–1, 2–3, etc.)  

Why inappropriate  
- Categorizing VAS into arbitrary categories discards information and violates rmANOVA assumptions (categories are ordinal, not continuous).  
- If VAS is handled as ordinal, nonparametric or ordinal models are more appropriate.

4. Paired t-test Comparing Only Baseline and 24h  

Why inappropriate  
- Ignores rich information at other timepoints (6h, 12h, 48h, 7d).  
- Does not address between-group differences; only within-group change, which is not sufficient in a randomized trial.

5. Simple Logistic Regression Treating Group as Outcome  

Why inappropriate  
- In an RCT, treatment group is randomized and not an outcome; modeling which patients “ended up in which group” given pain is conceptually backwards and meaningless.

6. MANOVA Using All Timepoints as Separate Outcomes Without Repeated Measures Structure  

Why inappropriate  
- MANOVA treats outcomes as independent; here they are repeated measures on the same subject.  
- Many MANOVA methods assume independence of observations and may not be tailored to longitudinal structure; LMM is more appropriate.

7. Bonferroni-Adjusted Single-Timepoint Tests Only  

Why inappropriate  
- Running, say, Kruskal–Wallis with Bonferroni correction at each timepoint as the sole analysis ignores the longitudinal design and has lower power; used alone, this would be an incomplete analysis.

8. Using Parametric Tests Without Checking/Addressing Assumptions  

Why inappropriate  
- If VAS distributions show extreme skewness, zero inflation, or heteroscedasticity, blindly applying rmANOVA or t-tests could lead to biased or invalid inferences.

9. Per-Protocol Only Analysis Without ITT Perspective  

Why inappropriate  
- Excluding all patients lost to follow-up or who took rescue analgesics (as done here) may bias results if those patients differ systematically; an ITT approach (including them with appropriate handling) is preferable.

TASK 4: SUMMARY – JUSTIFICATION OF OVERALL ANALYTICAL APPROACH  
--------------------------------  

- The trial is a 5-arm parallel RCT with repeated VAS pain measurements at 6 timepoints.  
- The primary scientific question is whether different ozone activation techniques (and no ozone) lead to different pain trajectories following single-visit endodontic treatment.  
- Therefore, a mixed-model repeated-measures approach (LMM or repeated-measures ANOVA) with factors group and time, and their interaction, is the most appropriate primary analysis:  
  - It accounts for within-subject correlation across timepoints.  
  - It accommodates unbalanced data from dropouts and missing diaries.  
  - It allows comprehensive assessment of treatment effect over the entire post-op period.  
- Baseline adjustment and inclusion of secondary covariates (sex, age, arch) refine estimates and address the secondary objectives.  
- Post-hoc pairwise comparisons and effect-size estimation facilitate clinically meaningful interpretation (which specific activation techniques provide benefits, at what time).  
- Sensitivity analyses (nonparametric, ANCOVA at key timepoints, subgroup analyses) ensure robustness and explore effect modification.  

Overall, the planned analysis respects the randomized longitudinal design, focuses on the group × time interaction as the core effect, and uses appropriate methods for continuous repeated measures while guarding against assumption violations and multiplicity.
