# CATEGORY A — Mandatory analyses/tests

## A1. Linear mixed-effects model (LMM) for repeated VAS (primary analysis)
- **Test/model:** LMM with fixed effects for **group**, **time**, and **group×time**; random intercept for subject (optionally random slope for time).
- **Purpose:** Estimate and test whether **pain trajectories differ** across the 5 groups over 7 time points (primary objective).
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Handles **correlated repeated measures** properly via random effects/covariance structure.
  2. Uses **all available data** under missing-at-random (MAR), avoiding listwise deletion typical of repeated-measures ANOVA.
  3. Directly targets the clinically relevant **group×time interaction** used in sample size planning.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - If VAS residuals show severe non-normality/heteroskedasticity with strong floor effects (common at later times), LMM with Gaussian errors may misfit.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When outcome is clearly **ordinal/discrete with heavy piling at 0** such that Gaussian assumptions are untenable and transformations fail.
- **Alternatives if assumptions violated:**
  - **Generalized linear mixed model (GLMM)** (e.g., beta regression after rescaling to (0,1), or Tweedie), or
  - **Rank-based mixed models** / **Brunner–Langer nonparametric longitudinal model**, or
  - **GEE with robust (sandwich) SEs** as a marginal model.

**Implementation details**
- Time as **categorical** (baseline, 6h, 12h, 24h, 48h, 7d) to avoid assuming linearity.
- Primary hypothesis: **overall group×time interaction**.
- If significant, proceed to pre-specified contrasts:
  - (i) each ozone group vs control at each post-op time
  - (ii) activated ozone (MDA/PUA/SA) vs NA
  - (iii) PUA vs SA (if clinically motivated)

## A2. Baseline comparability / randomization check (descriptive + appropriate tests)
- **Tests:**  
  - Continuous (e.g., age): **ANOVA** or **Kruskal–Wallis**  
  - Categorical (sex, arch, age group): **Chi-square** or **Fisher’s exact**
- **Purpose:** Describe whether groups appear balanced on key baseline characteristics.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Identifies gross imbalances that may require **covariate adjustment** or stratified reporting.
  2. Supports transparency in CONSORT reporting.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Significance testing for baseline balance can be misleading because any differences are due to chance; emphasis should be on **standardized differences**.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Do not use baseline p-values to decide inclusion/exclusion of covariates mechanistically.
- **Alternatives if assumptions violated:**
  - Use **standardized mean differences (SMDs)** and confidence intervals; for sparse tables use Fisher’s exact.

## A3. Model-based post hoc comparisons with multiplicity control
- **Test:** Estimated marginal means (EMMs) contrasts from LMM with **Holm** or **Tukey** adjustment (depending on contrast set).
- **Purpose:** Identify **which groups differ** at specific time points and estimate mean differences with CIs.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Controls inflated Type I error from multiple pairwise/timepoint comparisons.
  2. Uses the fitted repeated-measures model (consistent SEs accounting for correlation).
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - If the contrast family is very large, adjusted tests may be underpowered and hard to interpret.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Avoid unplanned “fishing” across all pairwise comparisons and timepoints without pre-specification.
- **Alternatives if assumptions violated:**
  - If switching to nonparametric longitudinal methods, use **Dunn-type** or **rank-based** post hoc procedures with Holm/FDR.

# CATEGORY B — Recommended analyses/tests

## B1. Covariate-adjusted mixed model (sex, age group, arch; and baseline pain handling)
- **Model:** Extend LMM to include **sex, age group, arch**, and optionally interactions (e.g., covariate×time). Consider baseline pain either as:
  - included as part of repeated outcome with time categorical (baseline included), or
  - analyze post-baseline with **baseline VAS as covariate** (ANCOVA-style mixed model).
- **Purpose:** Address secondary objective; improve precision; evaluate effect modification.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Directly tests stated secondary objective about demographic/clinical factors.
  2. Adjusts for prognostic factors, potentially reducing residual variance.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Limited sample size per group may make higher-order interactions unstable.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Avoid overfitting: do not include many interaction terms if events/observations per parameter are low.
- **Alternatives if assumptions violated:**
  - Use **GEE** with robust SEs; or simpler models per timepoint with multiplicity control.

## B2. Sensitivity analysis for rescue analgesic use (intercurrent event)
- **Test/model options:**
  - **Time-to-first rescue analgesic**: Kaplan–Meier + **log-rank test**, and/or **Cox model** by group.
  - Or treat rescue use as a binary endpoint: **logistic regression** by group.
- **Purpose:** Avoid bias from excluding participants who take ibuprofen; quantify whether groups differ in need for rescue medication.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Rescue medication is clinically meaningful and likely related to high pain.
  2. Exclusion can induce **informative missingness** and bias treatment effect estimates.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - If very few participants take medication, models may be unstable.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Do not run Cox models if proportional hazards is clearly violated or events are extremely sparse.
- **Alternatives if assumptions violated:**
  - **Fisher’s exact** for any/none rescue use; or **restricted mean survival time (RMST)** comparisons.

## B3. Missing data diagnostics and appropriate handling
- **Tests/approach:**  
  - Describe missingness by group/time; test association of missingness with observed covariates (logistic regression).  
  - Primary LMM assumes **MAR**; add **multiple imputation (MI)** as sensitivity if needed.
- **Purpose:** Ensure inference robustness to dropout/incomplete pain forms.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Loss to follow-up and incomplete diaries occurred.
  2. Missingness may be related to pain (MNAR risk).
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Formal tests for MCAR (e.g., Little’s test) can be underpowered/misleading.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Do not impute without including variables predictive of missingness/outcome.
- **Alternatives if assumptions violated:**
  - **Pattern-mixture** or **selection models**; **tipping-point** analyses.

## B4. Effect size reporting
- **Measures:**  
  - From LMM: **mean differences** at each timepoint; **area under the curve (AUC)** difference (see optional); standardized effects (e.g., **Cohen’s d** at key times).  
  - For interaction: **partial η²** or **marginal R²**.
- **Purpose:** Clinical interpretability beyond p-values.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Pain differences need clinical context.
  2. Aligns with CONSORT emphasis on estimation.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Standardized effects can be sensitive to SD choice in repeated measures.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Avoid Cohen’s d if distributions are highly non-normal with floor effects.
- **Alternatives if assumptions violated:**
  - Report **median differences** or **Hodges–Lehmann** estimates.

## C1. AUC (pain burden) comparison across groups
- **Test:** Compute subject-level **AUC of VAS over time** (e.g., trapezoidal rule from baseline to day 7); compare groups via **ANOVA** or **Kruskal–Wallis**, or regression adjusting covariates.

- **Why appropriate:**

- **Why may not be appropriate:**

- **When NOT to use:**
  - If many missing timepoints prevent reliable AUC calculation.
- **Alternatives if assumptions violated:**

- **Why appropriate:**

  2. Addresses non-normal VAS distributions with floor effects.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Cutpoints are arbitrary; loss of information.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Do not create post hoc thresholds chosen to maximize significance.

- **Test:** RM-ANOVA with Greenhouse–Geisser correction.
- **Purpose:** Provide comparability with traditional methods/literature.

- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Requires **complete cases** and **sphericity**; performs poorly with missing data.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If any notable missingness/dropout or clear sphericity violations.
- **Alternatives if assumptions violated:**
  - Prefer **LMM** or **GEE**.

## TASK 3 — Tests that should NOT be used (and why)

1. **Multiple independent t-tests at each timepoint without multiplicity correction**
   - Inflates Type I error; ignores repeated-measures correlation; encourages selective reporting.

2. **Simple one-way ANOVA on pooled/averaged post-op pain without accounting for time**
   - Loses longitudinal structure; can mask early differences (clinically important here).

3. **Paired t-tests within each group across time as the main analysis**
   - Does not compare groups properly; multiplicity explosion; ignores interaction.

4. **Chi-square tests applied to raw VAS values**
   - VAS is not categorical; would require arbitrary categorization and wastes information.

5. **Correlation (Pearson/Spearman) between group code and VAS**
   - Group is categorical; correlation is not an appropriate inferential framework.

6. **Linear regression treating repeated measures as independent observations**
   - Violates independence; SEs biased downward → false positives.

Covariate-adjusted models address the pre-specified secondary objectives (sex/age group/arch), and sensitivity analyses for **missing data** and **rescue medication** protect against biased conclusions from dropout or protocol-driven exclusions.

### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

#### **CATEGORY A – MANDATORY**
1. **Test Name**: Repeated Measures ANOVA (RM-ANOVA).  
   - **Purpose**: To compare VAS pain scores across time points (baseline, 6, 12, 24, 48 hours, and 7 days) within and between groups.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Longitudinal data with repeated measures for the same subjects.  
     2. Multiple groups (5 groups) with continuous outcome (VAS scores).  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Assumes sphericity, which may not hold.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If sphericity is violated and cannot be corrected.  
   - **Alternative**: Mixed-effects model or Greenhouse-Geisser correction.

2. **Test Name**: One-way ANOVA.  
   - **Purpose**: To compare mean VAS scores between groups at specific time points.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Continuous outcome (VAS scores) with independent groups.  
     2. Assesses differences between treatment protocols.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Assumes normality and homogeneity of variance.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If assumptions are violated.  
   - **Alternative**: Kruskal-Wallis test.

3. **Test Name**: Chi-square test.  
   - **Purpose**: To analyze categorical variables like sex, age group, and arch distribution across groups.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Categorical data (sex, age group, arch).  
     2. Tests for independence between variables.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Requires sufficient sample size in each category.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If expected cell counts are <5.  
   - **Alternative**: Fisher’s exact test.

---

#### **CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED**
1. **Test Name**: Mixed-effects model.  
   - **Purpose**: To analyze VAS scores over time, accounting for repeated measures and random effects.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Handles missing data and dropouts.  
     2. Does not require sphericity assumption.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Computationally intensive.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If simpler models suffice.  
   - **Alternative**: RM-ANOVA with corrections.

2. **Test Name**: Post-hoc tests (e.g., Tukey’s HSD).  
   - **Purpose**: To identify specific group differences after ANOVA.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Controls for Type I error in multiple comparisons.  
     2. Provides pairwise comparisons.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Assumes normality and equal variances.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If ANOVA assumptions are violated.  
   - **Alternative**: Pairwise Wilcoxon tests.

---

#### **CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL**
1. **Test Name**: Mann-Whitney U test.  
   - **Purpose**: To compare VAS scores between two groups at specific time points.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Non-parametric alternative for small sample sizes.  
     2. Does not assume normality.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Less powerful than parametric tests.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If parametric assumptions are met.  
   - **Alternative**: Independent t-test.

2. **Test Name**: Kaplan-Meier survival analysis.  
   - **Purpose**: To analyze time to pain resolution.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Time-to-event data (pain resolution).  
     2. Censoring (e.g., dropouts).  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Not designed for continuous outcomes like VAS scores.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If time-to-event is not a primary focus.  
   - **Alternative**: Mixed-effects model.

A1. Descriptive Statistics and Baseline Comparisons  

Test name  
- Descriptive statistics + one-way ANOVA (or Kruskal–Wallis) / chi-square tests for baseline comparability.

Purpose  
- To describe baseline characteristics and ensure randomization yielded comparable groups at baseline (especially for age, sex, arch, baseline VAS, PAI score).

Why appropriate (2+ reasons)  
1) Randomization inherently balances groups on average, but empirical confirmation is expected in RCTs; imbalances could affect interpretation of post-treatment differences.  
2) Baseline pain (VAS) is an important predictor of follow-up pain, so any baseline differences should be accounted for (e.g., via ANCOVA).  
3) Age, sex, and arch are pre-specified secondary variables; their distribution across groups matters for subgroup/interaction analyses.

Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)  
- Strictly, testing baseline differences in RCTs is not necessary and can be misleading (since any differences are due to chance, not bias). Overemphasis on “significant” baseline differences may lead to inappropriate post hoc adjustments.

When this test should NOT be used  
- Avoid formal hypothesis testing for baseline comparability if adopting a CONSORT-consistent philosophy that emphasizes description (means, SDs, counts) and uses adjusted models instead of baseline tests.  
- Do not overinterpret baseline p-values as evidence of bias.

Alternative if assumptions are violated  
- For baseline continuous variables with skewness or unequal variances:  
  - Use Kruskal–Wallis test or generalized linear models with suitable link (e.g., log-transformed age).  
- For categorical variables with small cell counts:  
  - Use Fisher’s exact test instead of chi-square.

A2. Primary Analysis – Mixed-Effects Model for Repeated Measures (MMRM)  

Test name  
- Linear mixed-effects model (LMM) for repeated measures, or, minimally, 2-way repeated measures ANOVA (rmANOVA).  

Purpose  
- To assess:  
  1) Overall effect of treatment group on pain over time.  
  2) Overall effect of time on pain.  
  3) Group × time interaction (do pain trajectories differ between groups?).  
- This is the primary efficacy analysis.

Why appropriate (2+ reasons)  
1) The design involves repeated VAS measurements on each subject; LMM/rmANOVA accounts for within-subject correlation and uses all repeated measures jointly.  
2) The intervention effect is primarily about differences in pain trajectories over time, which is naturally captured by the group × time interaction.  
3) LMMs can handle unbalanced data (missing at some timepoints) and unequal group sizes, which occur here due to dropouts; they are more flexible than classical rmANOVA.  
4) LMM allows inclusion of time as categorical (6 levels) and group as categorical (5 levels), with random intercepts (and potentially random slopes) for subjects.

Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)  
- VAS scores may be non-normal and bounded; if distributions are heavily skewed or show ceiling/floor effects, standard LMM assumptions (normality of residuals, homoscedasticity) may be violated.  
- If variance–covariance structure is complex and sample sizes per group are modest, model estimation can be unstable or overparameterized.

When this test should NOT be used  
- When data are extremely non-normal and standard transformations do not help, especially with many zeros or ordinal-like behavior, or when there are severe outliers that cannot reasonably be handled.  
- When there is extensive missing data not missing at random (MNAR), making LMM inferences unreliable.

Alternative if assumptions are violated  
- Use a generalized linear mixed model (GLMM) with appropriate link:  
  - E.g., gamma distribution with log link for positively skewed continuous data.  
  - Alternatively, ordinal mixed models if VAS is categorized into pain levels (none/mild/moderate/severe).  
- If model complexity is too high, use nonparametric approaches (e.g., rank-based repeated measures tests such as the ANOVA-type statistic in the “nparLD” R package).

A3. Post-hoc Pairwise Comparisons (Between Groups at Each Time Point)  

Test name  
- Pairwise comparisons with multiple-comparison adjustment (e.g., Tukey’s HSD for rmANOVA, or estimated marginal means with Bonferroni/Holm adjustment from LMM).

Purpose  
- To identify which specific groups differ in pain at relevant timepoints (e.g., 6, 12, 24, 48 hours, 7 days) if overall group × time interaction is significant.  
- Essential for clinical interpretation: e.g., is sonic/ultrasonic activation superior to no ozone? Is manual activation different from no activation?

Why appropriate (2+ reasons)  
1) Overall F-tests only tell us that differences exist; clinicians need to know where these differences occur (which group vs which, at which time).  
2) Multiple comparison procedures control type I error while allowing meaningful subgroup contrasts (e.g., each ozone activation vs control, or activated ozone vs non-activated ozone).  
3) Estimated marginal means (EMMs) from LMM provide adjusted group means at each time, accounting for missingness and covariates.

Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)  
- If the group × time interaction is not statistically significant, extensive post-hoc pairwise comparisons can be misleading and inflate type I error.  
- With limited sample sizes, multiple testing can substantially reduce power, leading to inconclusive results.

When this test should NOT be used  
- If prespecified primary comparison is a global test without interest in specific pairwise contrasts (which is rare in clinical trials of this type).  
- If the trial is underpowered for many pairwise comparisons; in that case, restrict to a few pre-specified clinically relevant contrasts.

Alternative if assumptions are violated  
- Use nonparametric pairwise tests (e.g., Mann–Whitney U at each time) with Holm–Bonferroni correction; or apply rank-based mixed models.  
- Alternatively, obtain bootstrap confidence intervals for the pairwise differences instead of relying on parametric assumptions.

A4. Secondary Factor Analyses – Inclusion of Sex, Age, and Arch  

Test name  
- Extended linear mixed model including sex, age, arch (and possibly their interactions with group or time).  

Purpose  
- To assess whether sex, age group, or arch influence pain trajectories and whether the treatment effect differs across these strata (effect modification).  

Why appropriate (2+ reasons)  
1) These variables were pre-specified secondary objectives; including them in the LMM allows estimation of their independent and interaction effects on pain.  
2) Adjusting for these covariates yields more precise estimates of treatment effects and guards against chance imbalances that could bias results.  
3) Testing interactions (e.g., group × sex) can reveal if a therapy is particularly beneficial in specific subgroups (e.g., females, mandible).

Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)  
- Sample size per subgroup may be small (e.g., few males within a particular group), leading to unstable estimates and inflated type I error for interaction tests.  
- With many interaction terms relative to sample size (~110), the model can become overfitted.

When this test should NOT be used  
- When some subgroups have very low counts (e.g., <3 per cell), avoid estimating complex interactions (e.g., group × time × sex).  
- When the trial was not powered for detection of such interactions; interpret them as exploratory only.

Alternative if assumptions are violated  
- Use stratified analyses:  
  - E.g., perform separate LMMs for maxilla vs mandible or for age categories, with clear caveats about exploratory nature.

TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS

Here are tests that should not be used in this study, with reasons.

1. Multiple Independent t-tests at Each Timepoint Without Adjustment  

Why inappropriate

2. Simple One-Way ANOVA Separately at Each Timepoint as Primary Analysis  

Why inappropriate

3. Repeated Measures ANOVA Treating VAS as Categorized Outcome (e.g., 0–1, 2–3, etc.)  

Why inappropriate  
- Categorizing VAS into arbitrary categories discards information and violates rmANOVA assumptions (categories are ordinal, not continuous).

Why inappropriate  
- Ignores rich information at other timepoints (6h, 12h, 48h, 7d).  
- Does not address between-group differences; only within-group change, which is not sufficient in a randomized trial.

Why inappropriate  
- In an RCT, treatment group is randomized and not an outcome; modeling which patients “ended up in which group” given pain is conceptually backwards and meaningless.

Why inappropriate  
- MANOVA treats outcomes as independent; here they are repeated measures on the same subject.

Why inappropriate

9. Per-Protocol Only Analysis Without ITT Perspective  

Why inappropriate  
- Excluding all patients lost to follow-up or who took rescue analgesics (as done here) may bias results if those patients differ systematically; an ITT approach (including them with appropriate handling) is preferable.  
- While the current study appears per-protocol, future analyses should avoid relying solely on per-protocol.
