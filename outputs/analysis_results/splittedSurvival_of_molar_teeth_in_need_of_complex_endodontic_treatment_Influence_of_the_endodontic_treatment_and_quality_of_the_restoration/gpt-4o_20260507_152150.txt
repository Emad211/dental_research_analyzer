### TASK 1: INFORMATION EXTRACTION

1. **Study Design**: Retrospective cohort study.
2. **Objective**: To evaluate the survival of molar teeth requiring complex endodontic treatment and assess the influence of endodontic treatment quality and restoration quality on outcomes.
3. **Study Groups**:
   - **Endodontic Treatment**: Initial treatment vs. retreatment.
   - **Restoration Type**: Direct vs. indirect restorations.
   - **Cusp Coverage**: Present vs. absent.
   - **Coronal Seal**: Adequate vs. inadequate.
4. **Sample Size**:
   - Initial eligibility: 416 teeth.
   - Final inclusion for tooth survival: 279 teeth.
   - Final inclusion for endodontic healing: 268 teeth.
   - Final inclusion for restorative success: 215 teeth.
5. **Variables**:
   - **Patient Variables**: Age, gender, caries risk (low/medium/high), parafunction (yes/no).
   - **Endodontic Variables**:
     - Pre-endodontic: DETI/ETC score (II/III), endodontic diagnosis (irreversible pulpitis/apical periodontitis), number of root canals treated (3/4), preoperative radiolucency (yes/no), preoperative complications (yes/no).
     - Post-endodontic: Clinical symptoms, healing status (healed/healing/non-healed), postoperative radiolucency (Periapical Index ≥ 3), technical quality of treatment (adequate/inadequate).
   - **Restorative Variables**: Jaw (upper/lower), restoration type (direct/indirect), cusp coverage (yes/no), coronal seal (adequate/inadequate), restoration quality (FDI criteria).
6. **Outcome Measures**:
   - **Tooth Survival**: Presence of the tooth at follow-up.
   - **Endodontic Healing**: Assessed as healed, healing, or non-healed based on clinical and radiographic criteria.
   - **Restorative Success**: Restoration quality assessed using FDI criteria.
7. **Data Collection Methods**:
   - Clinical and radiographic examinations.
   - Periapical Index (PAI) for radiographic assessment.
   - FDI criteria for restoration quality.
   - Telephone anamnesis and general practitioner records for missing follow-up data.

### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS
1. **Chi-Square Test of Independence**:
   - **Purpose**: To assess the association between categorical variables (e.g., restoration type, cusp coverage, coronal seal) and outcomes (tooth survival, endodontic healing, restorative success).
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Both independent and dependent variables are categorical.
     2. Sample size is sufficient for expected frequencies in contingency tables.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Small cell sizes in contingency tables may violate assumptions.
   - **When NOT to Use**: If >20% of cells have expected frequencies <5.
   - **Alternative**: Fisher’s Exact Test.

2. **Logistic Regression**:
   - **Purpose**: To evaluate the effect of multiple predictors (e.g., endodontic treatment type, restoration type, cusp coverage) on binary outcomes (e.g., tooth survival: yes/no).
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Allows adjustment for confounding variables.
     2. Provides odds ratios for interpretation of effect sizes.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Multicollinearity among predictors.
   - **When NOT to Use**: If predictor variables are highly correlated.
   - **Alternative**: Ridge Regression or Principal Component Analysis.

3. **Kaplan-Meier Survival Analysis**:
   - **Purpose**: To estimate tooth survival over time and compare survival curves between groups (e.g., direct vs. indirect restorations).
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Time-to-event data is available.
     2. Censoring is present (e.g., loss to follow-up).
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Assumes non-informative censoring, which may not hold if loss to follow-up is related to outcomes.
   - **When NOT to Use**: If censoring is informative.
   - **Alternative**: Competing risks analysis.

#### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED TESTS
1. **Cox Proportional Hazards Regression**:
   - **Purpose**: To assess the effect of multiple predictors on time-to-event outcomes (e.g., tooth survival).
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Adjusts for covariates in survival analysis.
     2. Handles censored data effectively.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Assumes proportional hazards, which may not hold.
   - **When NOT to Use**: If proportional hazards assumption is violated.
   - **Alternative**: Stratified Cox Regression or Time-Dependent Covariates.

2. **Multinomial Logistic Regression**:
   - **Purpose**: To model outcomes with more than two categories (e.g., endodontic healing: healed, healing, non-healed).
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Handles categorical dependent variables with >2 levels.
     2. Provides relative risk ratios for interpretation.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Requires large sample sizes for stable estimates.
   - **When NOT to Use**: If sample size is insufficient for each outcome category.
   - **Alternative**: Collapsing categories into binary outcomes.

3. **Mixed-Effects Logistic Regression**:
   - **Purpose**: To account for clustering (e.g., multiple teeth from the same patient).
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Accounts for intra-patient correlation.
     2. Provides robust estimates for hierarchical data.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Computationally intensive for large datasets.
   - **When NOT to Use**: If clustering effects are negligible.
   - **Alternative**: Generalized Estimating Equations (GEE).

---

#### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL TESTS
1. **Exploratory Factor Analysis (EFA)**:
   - **Purpose**: To identify latent constructs (e.g., quality of restoration) from observed variables.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Reduces dimensionality of correlated variables.
     2. Provides insights into underlying structures.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Requires large sample sizes for stable factor solutions.
   - **When NOT to Use**: If sample size is insufficient.
   - **Alternative**: Principal Component Analysis (PCA).

2. **Sensitivity Analysis**:
   - **Purpose**: To assess the robustness of results to assumptions (e.g., handling of missing data).
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Evaluates the impact of methodological choices.
     2. Enhances the credibility of findings.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Adds complexity to the analysis.
   - **When NOT to Use**: If primary results are robust.
   - **Alternative**: Multiple Imputation.
