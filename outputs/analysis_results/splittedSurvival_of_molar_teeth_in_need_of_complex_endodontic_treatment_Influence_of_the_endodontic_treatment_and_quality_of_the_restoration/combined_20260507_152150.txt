# CATEGORY A — MANDATORY (analysis incomplete without these)

## A1. Kaplan–Meier survival analysis + log-rank test
- **Purpose:** Estimate and compare **tooth survival over time** between groups (e.g., initial vs retreatment; adequate vs inadequate coronal seal; direct vs indirect).
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1) Outcome is **time-to-event** (extraction/failure) with likely **censoring** (teeth still present at last follow-up).
  2) Provides **nonparametric** survival estimates and visual comparison across clinically meaningful strata.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - If **event times** (dates) are not reliably available or follow-up time is uniform/unknown, KM becomes unreliable.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When only a binary “survived yes/no” at one fixed time point is available without valid time information.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated / not feasible:**
  - If no time-to-event: use **risk comparison at fixed time** via **log-binomial/Poisson** or **logistic regression** (with careful interpretation).

## A2. Cox proportional hazards regression (multivariable)
- **Purpose:** Quantify associations between predictors (endodontic + restorative quality) and **hazard of tooth loss**, adjusted for confounders.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1) Handles **censoring** and varying follow-up.
  2) Produces interpretable **hazard ratios** while adjusting for multiple covariates (caries risk, parafunction, operator, jaw, etc.).
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - **Non-proportional hazards** (PH) for key predictors (e.g., effect of restoration may vary over time).
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If PH assumption is strongly violated and cannot be remedied; or if events are too few for the number of predictors (overfitting).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Time-varying coefficients** in Cox; **stratified Cox**; or **accelerated failure time (AFT)** models (Weibull/log-normal). If clustering by patient: **shared frailty Cox** or **robust sandwich SE**.

## A3. Multivariable model for endodontic healing (3-category outcome): ordinal logistic regression (proportional odds) *with checks*
- **Purpose:** Assess how endodontic/restorative factors relate to ordered healing status: **nonhealed < healing < healed**.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1) Outcome has **natural ordering** and using that order improves efficiency vs multinomial.
  2) Allows **adjustment** for confounding (pre-op radiolucency, retreatment, technical quality, coronal seal, etc.).
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - The **proportional odds assumption** may be violated (effect differs across cutpoints).
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When ordering is clinically questionable (e.g., “healing” not consistently intermediate), or proportional odds clearly fails and partial models aren’t available.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Partial proportional odds model**; or **multinomial logistic regression** (nominal). If simplifying is clinically defensible: dichotomize to **healed/healing vs nonhealed** and use binary logistic (not preferred, but sometimes pragmatic).

## A4. Binary logistic regression for postoperative radiolucency (PAI ≥3)
- **Purpose:** Identify predictors of **persistent/incident radiolucency** at follow-up.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1) Outcome is **binary** (PAI≥3 yes/no).
  2) Supports multivariable adjustment and interaction assessment (e.g., coronal seal × endodontic technical quality).
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - If outcome is common, OR can materially overstate RR; also ignores timing if radiolucency resolution is time-dependent.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When a direct **risk ratio** is required for interpretation and model convergence permits RR modeling; or if repeated radiographs over time are analyzed (longitudinal).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated / interpretation concerns:**
  - **Modified Poisson regression with robust SE** (RR); or **log-binomial** (RR). If clustering: GEE with robust SE.

# CATEGORY B — RECOMMENDED (adds rigor)

## B1. Assumption diagnostics: PH test (Schoenfeld residuals) + visual checks
- **Purpose:** Verify Cox **proportional hazards** assumption.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1) Prevents misleading HRs if effects vary over time.
  2) Guides correct remedial modeling (stratification/time-varying effects).
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - Low event counts reduce power; tests can be unstable.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When not using Cox/time-to-event models.
- **Alternative:**
  - Visual log(-log) plots; time-interaction terms.

## B2. Clustered-data methods (if multiple teeth per patient): robust sandwich SE / GEE / frailty
- **Purpose:** Correct inference for **within-patient correlation**.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1) Tooth-level outcomes may not be independent within the same patient (shared behaviors/caries risk/parafunction).
  2) Prevents underestimated SE and inflated Type I error.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - If truly one tooth per patient (or negligible clustering), these methods add complexity with minimal benefit.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When patient ID is unavailable and clustering cannot be identified.
- **Alternative:**
  - Restrict to one tooth per patient (random selection) as sensitivity analysis.

## B3. Multiple imputation (MI) for missing covariates / “unknown” categories (sensitivity vs main)
- **Purpose:** Reduce bias and improve precision due to missing predictors (e.g., parafunction unknown, caries risk unknown, restoration unknown).
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1) Complete-case analysis can bias estimates if missingness relates to outcomes/exposures.
  2) MI preserves sample size and uncertainty.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - If missingness is very low or mechanism is MNAR and cannot be modeled credibly.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When outcome is missing not at random and no auxiliary variables exist; or when the imputation model is untenable.
- **Alternative:**
  - Missing-indicator approach (less ideal), or pattern-mixture sensitivity analyses.

## B4. Model building & overfitting control: penalized regression (LASSO / ridge) or shrinkage
- **Purpose:** Stabilize multivariable estimates given many predictors and limited events.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1) Many candidate predictors (patient + endodontic + restorative) risk overfitting.
  2) Improves out-of-sample validity; supports parsimonious models.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - Penalized estimates can be harder to interpret clinically; inference (CIs) is less straightforward.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When the primary goal is classical inference on a small set of prespecified predictors and events are ample.
- **Alternative:**
  - Pre-specify limited covariates; use rule-of-thumb events-per-parameter with shrinkage via bootstrap.

## B5. Interaction testing (effect modification)
- **Purpose:** Test whether restoration quality modifies effect of endodontic technical quality (and vice versa).
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1) Clinically plausible synergy: poor coronal seal may negate good endodontics.
  2) Helps tailor clinical recommendations (which factor matters most under which conditions).
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - Underpowered; multiple testing risk.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When sample size/events are insufficient for stable interaction estimates.
- **Alternative:**
  - Stratified analyses with cautious interpretation; Bayesian shrinkage for interactions.

# CATEGORY C — OPTIONAL (supplementary insight)

## C1. Competing risks analysis (Fine–Gray) for tooth loss (if “retreatment/surgery” competes with extraction)
- **Purpose:** If extraction is not the only terminal event and other interventions preclude observation of extraction, estimate subdistribution hazards.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1) “Intervention necessary (re-treatment/surgery)” is part of nonhealed definition and may preclude extraction observation.
  2) Competing risks can change absolute failure estimates.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - Requires clear definition and recording of competing events with dates.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When only extraction is considered event and other procedures are not competing endpoints.
- **Alternative:**
  - Cause-specific Cox models.

## C2. Agreement/reliability: Cohen’s kappa / weighted kappa; ICC (as appropriate)
- **Purpose:** Quantify interobserver reliability for radiographic/FDI scores (even if consensus used clinically).
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1) Validates measurement consistency for subjective ratings (PAI, FDI, technical quality).
  2) Enhances credibility of outcomes/predictors.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - If only consensus scores recorded and no independent duplicate ratings retained.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When there are no paired ratings per observer.
- **Alternative:**
  - Percentage agreement (inferior), or conduct a reliability sub-study.

## C3. Mediation analysis (e.g., restoration → coronal seal → healing/survival)
- **Purpose:** Explore whether coronal seal mediates association between restoration type and outcomes.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1) Mechanistically plausible pathway.
  2) Provides actionable insight (target seal rather than type).
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - Strong assumptions; retrospective design; unmeasured confounding likely.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When temporality between mediator and outcome is unclear or mediator measured only at follow-up.
- **Alternative:**
  - Stratified analysis by seal quality; causal modeling only if timing supports it.

## TASK 3 — Inappropriate tests (should NOT be used)

1. **Paired t-test / repeated-measures ANOVA**  
   - Not a pre/post continuous repeated-measures design; primary outcomes are survival and categorical healing/restoration ratings.

2. **Simple chi-square tests as the sole analysis for survival**  
   - Collapses time and censoring; can misclassify risk when follow-up differs.

3. **Using multiple independent t-tests for FDI or PAI scores**  
   - These are ordinal/categorical; t-tests assume interval scale and normality.

4. **Pearson correlation for associations between categorical predictors and outcomes**  
   - Not appropriate for nominal/ordinal outcomes; ignores confounding and nonlinearity.

5. **One-way ANOVA to compare “healed/healing/nonhealed” across groups**  
   - Outcome is not continuous; violates distributional assumptions and discards ordinal nature.

## TASK 4 — Summary justification of overall approach

- Use **time-to-event methods (KM + Cox)** for tooth survival because extraction risk depends on **follow-up duration** and censoring is expected.  
- Use **multivariable categorical/ordinal regression** for healing and radiographic outcomes to respect outcome scale and adjust for **key confounders** (patient risk, baseline radiolucency, retreatment status, technical quality, and restorative quality).  
- Strengthen validity via **assumption checks**, **handling missing data**, and **accounting for clustering** when multiple teeth belong to the same patient.

### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS
1. **Chi-Square Test of Independence**:
   - **Purpose**: To assess the association between categorical variables (e.g., restoration type, cusp coverage, coronal seal) and outcomes (tooth survival, endodontic healing, restorative success).
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Both independent and dependent variables are categorical.
     2. Sample size is sufficient for expected frequencies in contingency tables.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Small cell sizes in contingency tables may violate assumptions.
   - **When NOT to Use**: If >20% of cells have expected frequencies <5.
   - **Alternative**: Fisher’s Exact Test.

2. **Logistic Regression**:
   - **Purpose**: To evaluate the effect of multiple predictors (e.g., endodontic treatment type, restoration type, cusp coverage) on binary outcomes (e.g., tooth survival: yes/no).
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Allows adjustment for confounding variables.
     2. Provides odds ratios for interpretation of effect sizes.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Multicollinearity among predictors.
   - **When NOT to Use**: If predictor variables are highly correlated.
   - **Alternative**: Ridge Regression or Principal Component Analysis.

3. **Kaplan-Meier Survival Analysis**:
   - **Purpose**: To estimate tooth survival over time and compare survival curves between groups (e.g., direct vs. indirect restorations).
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Time-to-event data is available.
     2. Censoring is present (e.g., loss to follow-up).
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Assumes non-informative censoring, which may not hold if loss to follow-up is related to outcomes.
   - **When NOT to Use**: If censoring is informative.
   - **Alternative**: Competing risks analysis.

#### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED TESTS
1. **Cox Proportional Hazards Regression**:
   - **Purpose**: To assess the effect of multiple predictors on time-to-event outcomes (e.g., tooth survival).
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Adjusts for covariates in survival analysis.
     2. Handles censored data effectively.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Assumes proportional hazards, which may not hold.
   - **When NOT to Use**: If proportional hazards assumption is violated.
   - **Alternative**: Stratified Cox Regression or Time-Dependent Covariates.

2. **Multinomial Logistic Regression**:
   - **Purpose**: To model outcomes with more than two categories (e.g., endodontic healing: healed, healing, non-healed).
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Handles categorical dependent variables with >2 levels.
     2. Provides relative risk ratios for interpretation.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Requires large sample sizes for stable estimates.
   - **When NOT to Use**: If sample size is insufficient for each outcome category.
   - **Alternative**: Collapsing categories into binary outcomes.

3. **Mixed-Effects Logistic Regression**:
   - **Purpose**: To account for clustering (e.g., multiple teeth from the same patient).
   - **Why Appropriate**:

   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Computationally intensive for large datasets.

B. OUTCOME 1 – TOOTH SURVIVAL

Here I assume tooth survival is measured as:  
- Binary status at recall (survived vs extracted) and  
- Ideally, time from completion of endodontic treatment to failure or last follow‑up (time‑to‑event variable).

CATEGORY A – MANDATORY

1) Descriptive analyses  
   - Test: Descriptive summaries (no inferential test)  
   - Purpose:  
     - Characterize the cohort: distributions of patient, endodontic, and restorative variables; rates of survival vs extraction.  
   - Why appropriate:  
     1. Fundamental to understand sample composition and outcome frequencies.  
     2. Helps identify sparse categories, outliers, and informs choice of later models.  
   - Not appropriate when: Not an “inferential test”; but always necessary.  
   - Alternatives: None; this is standard.

2) Kaplan–Meier survival analysis + Log-rank test  
   - Purpose:  
     - Estimate tooth survival over time.  
     - Compare survival curves between key groups (e.g., adequate vs inadequate endodontic quality; direct vs indirect restoration; adequate vs inadequate coronal seal; initial vs retreatment; cusp coverage yes/no).  
   - Why appropriate:  
     1. Time‑to‑event nature of tooth survival with right‑censoring (patients not failing by last follow‑up) is best analysed with survival methods.  
     2. Kaplan–Meier is non‑parametric and does not assume a particular survival distribution; log‑rank compares curves across groups.  
   - Why it may not be appropriate:  
     - If precise dates for endodontic completion and failure/last follow‑up are not available (only status at one time), survival times cannot be computed. Then only cross‑sectional analysis is possible.  
   - When this test should NOT be used:  
     - If only a fixed follow‑up time with no variability and no censoring exists; then it adds no information beyond proportions.  
   - Alternative if assumptions violated or time missing:  
     - If no time data: logistic regression for failure (yes/no) at recall.  
     - For non‑proportional hazards across groups: use stratified Cox models or flexible parametric survival models.

3) Cox proportional hazards regression  
   - Purpose:  
     - Assess the association between multiple predictors and time to tooth failure while adjusting for confounders.  
   - Why appropriate:  
     1. Handles right‑censored survival data and allows estimating hazard ratios for each factor (e.g., cusp coverage, coronal seal, technical quality, caries risk).  
     2. Accommodates both categorical and continuous predictors and is standard for prognostic studies of tooth survival.  
   - Why it may not be appropriate:  
     - Proportional hazards assumption may be violated for some predictors (e.g., effect of coronal seal may differ early vs late).  
   - When this test should NOT be used:  
     - If the event times are not known or are interval‑censored without appropriate extension.  
     - If events are extremely rare (very few failures), leading to model instability.  
   - Alternatives if assumptions violated:  
     - Test PH assumption using Schoenfeld residuals.  
     - If PH violated: use time‑varying covariates, stratified Cox, or parametric survival models (Weibull, log‑logistic) or flexible Royston–Parmar models.  
     - If only binary status at a fixed follow‑up time is available: logistic regression.

4) Multivariable logistic regression for tooth failure (if survival times not available)  
   - Purpose:  
     - Model probability of tooth failure (extraction) at follow‑up as a function of endodontic and restorative variables.  
   - Why appropriate:  
     1. Binary outcome (survival vs failure) with multiple predictors is classic use case for logistic regression.  
     2. Produces odds ratios interpretable as relative odds of failure, controlling for confounders like age, caries risk, operator.  
   - Why it may not be appropriate:  
     - If failure rate is low and sample size limited, many predictors could cause overfitting.  
   - When this test should NOT be used:  
     - If interest is time‑to‑event with varying follow‑up and censoring, survival models are more appropriate.  
   - Alternatives if assumptions violated or events rare:  
     - Penalised logistic regression (e.g., Firth’s method) or exact logistic regression if events <10 per predictor.  
     - For common outcomes and interest in risk ratios rather than odds ratios: Poisson regression with robust variance or log‑binomial models.

CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED

5) Univariable comparisons (Chi‑square / Fisher’s exact / t‑test / Mann–Whitney)  
   - Purpose:  
     - Explore associations between each predictor and tooth survival before multivariable modelling.  
   - Why appropriate:  
     1. Helps to identify potentially important predictors (e.g., caries risk vs survival, cusp coverage vs survival) and interactions.  
     2. Guides variable selection and informs about crude effect sizes.  
   - Why it may not be appropriate:  
     - Multiple testing leads to inflated type I error; univariable results may be misleading without adjustment.  
   - When this test should NOT be used:  
     - As sole basis for final conclusions; must be complemented by adjusted models.  
   - Alternatives:  
     - Use effect sizes and confidence intervals rather than focusing on p‑values.  
     - Use false discovery rate control if many comparisons.

6) Sensitivity analysis excluding “Unknown” categories  
   - Test/approach: repeat key models excluding observations with Unknown covariates.  
   - Purpose:  
     - Assess robustness of results to missing data handling.  
   - Why appropriate:  
     1. Unknown categories might bias parameter estimates if they group heterogeneous patterns of missingness.  
     2. Strengthens confidence in findings if estimates remain similar.  
   - Why it may not be appropriate:  
     - If many observations are lost, power decreases and selection bias may be introduced.  
   - When NOT to use:  
     - When unknown proportion is large and missing not at random; better use multiple imputation.  
   - Alternative:  
     - Multiple imputation using chained equations.

CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL

7) Competing risks analysis  
   - Purpose:  
     - If different types of failure events are recorded (e.g., extraction due to fracture vs due to persistent infection vs prosthetic reason), competing risks models (Fine‑Gray) estimate cause‑specific incidence.  
   - Why appropriate:  
     1. Tooth loss due to different causes may have different determinants.  
     2. Standard Kaplan–Meier may overestimate risk of specific cause‑failure in presence of competing causes.  
   - Why it may not be appropriate:  
     - Requires detailed, reliably coded cause of failure and adequate numbers per cause.  
   - When NOT to use:  
     - If cause of failure is unknown or reliability is poor.  
   - Alternatives:  
     - Standard survival analysis for overall failure.

8) Multilevel survival model (frailty model)  
   - Purpose:  
     - If multiple teeth per patient or clustering by operator is important, incorporate random effects.  
   - Why appropriate:  
     1. Adjusts for within‑cluster correlation, which can otherwise bias standard errors.  
     2. Provides insight into between‑operator or between‑patient variability.  
   - Why it may not be appropriate:  
     - With only two operators and likely one tooth per patient, multilevel modelling might be overkill or unstable.  
   - When NOT to use:  
     - Very small number of clusters or cluster sizes too small.  
   - Alternatives:  
     - Robust standard errors clustered by patient or operator.

     - Less efficient than ordinal model when ordering is valid; more parameters → risk of overfitting.

   - Alternatives:  
     - Exact tests for small samples; combine levels of predictor.

TASK 3 – INAPPROPRIATE TESTS

Below are tests that should not be used and why:

1) Student’s t‑test / ANOVA on FDI or healing categories treated as continuous  
   - Why inappropriate:  
     - FDI and healing outcomes are ordinal categorical, not interval‑scaled; spacing between categories is not equal.  
     - Mean and SD of coded categories are hard to interpret; t‑test assumes normally distributed continuous outcome.  
   - Preferred alternatives:  
     - Ordinal logistic regression; non‑parametric tests (e.g., Mann–Whitney) for simple two‑group comparisons.

2) Linear regression with binary or categorical outcome (survival, healed)  
   - Why inappropriate:  
     - Linear regression assumes continuous, normally distributed residuals; binary outcomes violate these assumptions.  
     - Predicted values can fall outside [0,1].  
   - Preferred alternatives:  
     - Logistic regression; survival models.

3) Parametric tests ignoring censoring (e.g., comparing mean “follow‑up time” only among failures)  
   - Why inappropriate:  
     - Excluding survivors (censored observations) causes serious bias in estimates of time to event.  
   - Alternative:  
     - Kaplan–Meier, Cox regression.

4) Repeated-measures ANOVA  
   - Why inappropriate:  
     - Data appear to have a single post‑treatment examination; there is no repeated measurement series per tooth (longitudinal).  
   - Alternative:  
     - Cross‑sectional models (logistic/ordinal/survival) with single outcome.

5) Multiple independent t‑tests to compare every pair of groups (e.g., comparing mean age in many subgroups)  
   - Why inappropriate:  
     - Inflates type I error; lacks multiplicity control.  
   - Alternative:  
     - ANOVA (when continuous outcome appropriate) or regression with multiple categories; adjust for multiple comparisons if necessary.

6) Correlation coefficients (Pearson/Spearman) as a primary analysis for outcome vs predictors  
   - Why inappropriate:  
     - Outcomes are categorical; correlation coefficients are not appropriate for modelling categorical outcomes, especially with covariate adjustment.  
   - Alternative:  
     - Logistic / ordinal logistic regression, chi‑square tests.

7) McNemar test  
   - Why inappropriate:  
     - Requires paired binary data (before/after on same units). This study uses single post‑treatment evaluation per tooth.  
   - Alternative:  
     - Regular chi‑square or logistic regression for unpaired data.

8) Linear mixed models for continuous outcomes  
   - Why inappropriate:  
     - Outcomes (survival, healing, FDI rating) are not continuous, repeated measures per tooth are not present.  
   - Alternative:  
     - Mixed‑effects logistic/ordinal models only if clustering by patient/operator necessitates it; not linear mixed models.

TASK 4 – SUMMARY OF OVERALL ANALYTICAL APPROACH

The study is a retrospective cohort of endodontically treated molars with three main outcomes: tooth survival (time‑to‑event / binary), endodontic healing (3‑category ordinal), and restorative success (FDI‑based ordinal or binary). The recommended analytical strategy is:

1. Start with descriptive statistics for all variables and outcomes across the three analytic cohorts (survival, healing, restorative).  
2. For tooth survival, use survival analysis (Kaplan–Meier, log‑rank, Cox regression) if dates are available; otherwise use logistic regression for failure status at recall, adjusting for key patient, endodontic, and restorative factors.  
3. For endodontic healing, treat healing as ordered outcome and fit an ordinal logistic model; if assumptions fail, switch to multinomial logistic or binary logistic with clinically justified dichotomisation.  
4. For restorative success, analyse FDI ratings with ordinal logistic models and/or binary logistic models for acceptable vs unacceptable, integrating restorative design variables (direct vs indirect, cusp coverage, coronal seal, material) and patient factors (caries risk, parafunction).  
5. Supplement with univariable comparisons, sensitivity analyses for missing/unknown categories, and, where relevant, clustering adjustments.  

This combination of survival models for tooth retention and ordinal/binary regression for healing and restoration quality is well aligned with the study design, the nature of the outcomes, and the clinical questions about how endodontic and restorative factors influence long‑term prognosis of complex molar endodontic treatment.
