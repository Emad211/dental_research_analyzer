2) Kaplan–Meier survival analysis + Log-rank test  
   - Purpose:  
     - Estimate tooth survival over time.  
     - Compare survival curves between key groups (e.g., adequate vs inadequate endodontic quality; direct vs indirect restoration; adequate vs inadequate coronal seal; initial vs retreatment; cusp coverage yes/no).  
   - Why appropriate:  
     1. Time‑to‑event nature of tooth survival with right‑censoring (patients not failing by last follow‑up) is best analysed with survival methods.  
     2. Kaplan–Meier is non‑parametric and does not assume a particular survival distribution; log‑rank compares curves across groups.  
   - Why it may not be appropriate:  
     - If precise dates for endodontic completion and failure/last follow‑up are not available (only status at one time), survival times cannot be computed. Then only cross‑sectional analysis is possible.  
   - When this test should NOT be used:

3) Cox proportional hazards regression  
   - Purpose:  
     - Assess the association between multiple predictors and time to tooth failure while adjusting for confounders.  
   - Why appropriate:  
     1. Handles right‑censored survival data and allows estimating hazard ratios for each factor (e.g., cusp coverage, coronal seal, technical quality, caries risk).  
     2. Accommodates both categorical and continuous predictors and is standard for prognostic studies of tooth survival.  
   - Why it may not be appropriate:  
     - Proportional hazards assumption may be violated for some predictors (e.g., effect of coronal seal may differ early vs late).  
   - When this test should NOT be used:  
     - If the event times are not known or are interval‑censored without appropriate extension.  
     - If events are extremely rare (very few failures), leading to model instability.  
   - Alternatives if assumptions violated:  
     - Test PH assumption using Schoenfeld residuals.  
     - If PH violated: use time‑varying covariates, stratified Cox, or parametric survival models (Weibull, log‑logistic) or flexible Royston–Parmar models.  
     - If only binary status at a fixed follow‑up time is available: logistic regression.

4) Multivariable logistic regression for tooth failure (if survival times not available)  
   - Purpose:  
     - Model probability of tooth failure (extraction) at follow‑up as a function of endodontic and restorative variables.  
   - Why appropriate:

     2. Produces odds ratios interpretable as relative odds of failure, controlling for confounders like age, caries risk, operator.  
   - Why it may not be appropriate:  
     - If failure rate is low and sample size limited, many predictors could cause overfitting.  
   - When this test should NOT be used:  
     - If interest is time‑to‑event with varying follow‑up and censoring, survival models are more appropriate.  
   - Alternatives if assumptions violated or events rare:  
     - Penalised logistic regression (e.g., Firth’s method) or exact logistic regression if events <10 per predictor.

TASK 3 – INAPPROPRIATE TESTS

Below are tests that should not be used and why:

1) Student’s t‑test / ANOVA on FDI or healing categories treated as continuous  
   - Why inappropriate:  
     - FDI and healing outcomes are ordinal categorical, not interval‑scaled; spacing between categories is not equal.  
     - Mean and SD of coded categories are hard to interpret; t‑test assumes normally distributed continuous outcome.  
   - Preferred alternatives:  
     - Ordinal logistic regression; non‑parametric tests (e.g., Mann–Whitney) for simple two‑group comparisons.

2) Linear regression with binary or categorical outcome (survival, healed)  
   - Why inappropriate:  
     - Linear regression assumes continuous, normally distributed residuals; binary outcomes violate these assumptions.  
     - Predicted values can fall outside [0,1].  
   - Preferred alternatives:  
     - Logistic regression; survival models.

3) Parametric tests ignoring censoring (e.g., comparing mean “follow‑up time” only among failures)  
   - Why inappropriate:  
     - Excluding survivors (censored observations) causes serious bias in estimates of time to event.  
   - Alternative:  
     - Kaplan–Meier, Cox regression.

4) Repeated-measures ANOVA  
   - Why inappropriate:  
     - Data appear to have a single post‑treatment examination; there is no repeated measurement series per tooth (longitudinal).  
   - Alternative:  
     - Cross‑sectional models (logistic/ordinal/survival) with single outcome.

5) Multiple independent t‑tests to compare every pair of groups (e.g., comparing mean age in many subgroups)  
   - Why inappropriate:  
     - Inflates type I error; lacks multiplicity control.  
   - Alternative:  
     - ANOVA (when continuous outcome appropriate) or regression with multiple categories; adjust for multiple comparisons if necessary.

6) Correlation coefficients (Pearson/Spearman) as a primary analysis for outcome vs predictors  
   - Why inappropriate:  
     - Outcomes are categorical; correlation coefficients are not appropriate for modelling categorical outcomes, especially with covariate adjustment.  
   - Alternative:  
     - Logistic / ordinal logistic regression, chi‑square tests.

7) McNemar test  
   - Why inappropriate:  
     - Requires paired binary data (before/after on same units). This study uses single post‑treatment evaluation per tooth.  
   - Alternative:  
     - Regular chi‑square or logistic regression for unpaired data.

8) Linear mixed models for continuous outcomes  
   - Why inappropriate:  
     - Outcomes (survival, healing, FDI rating) are not continuous, repeated measures per tooth are not present.  
   - Alternative:  
     - Mixed‑effects logistic/ordinal models only if clustering by patient/operator necessitates it; not linear mixed models.

4. For restorative success, analyse FDI ratings with ordinal logistic models and/or binary logistic models for acceptable vs unacceptable, integrating restorative design variables (direct vs indirect, cusp coverage, coronal seal, material) and patient factors (caries risk, parafunction).

This combination of survival models for tooth retention and ordinal/binary regression for healing and restoration quality is well aligned with the study design, the nature of the outcomes, and the clinical questions about how endodontic and restorative factors influence long‑term prognosis of complex molar endodontic treatment.
