### General principles
- **Analysis set:** Prefer **intention-to-treat (ITT)** (analyze according to assigned group). If medication adherence/exposure is known, add per-protocol as sensitivity.
- **Significance level:** α = 0.05 (two-sided)

- **Descriptive statistics:**
  - Continuous: mean±SD if approximately normal; otherwise median (IQR)
  - Categorical: n (%)
- **Missing data:** Document extent/reasons. If minimal, complete-case; if non-trivial, consider multiple imputation (recommended below).

# CATEGORY A – MANDATORY tests (core analyses)

## A1) Chi-square test (or Fisher’s exact) for pain incidence at 48 hours (binary)
- **Purpose:** Compare **proportion with pain (VAS 5–10)** between Group 1 vs Group 2 at 48 hours.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Outcome as defined in methods is **binary** (pain vs no pain).
  2. Two **independent** parallel groups.
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - Expected cell counts may be small (n≈30/group), violating chi-square approximation.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When any expected cell count is too low (commonly <5).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Fisher’s exact test** (exact p-value), and report **RR/OR with exact CI** as feasible.

## A2) Effect size estimation for binary outcome (RR/OR, RD with 95% CI)
- **Purpose:** Quantify magnitude of difference in pain incidence between groups.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Clinical interpretability (e.g., “risk of pain reduced by X%”).
  2. P-values alone are insufficient; CIs communicate precision.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - With very low/high event rates, OR can be unstable/misleading; RD may be sample-size sensitive.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Avoid OR as primary effect measure if outcome is common and misinterpretation as RR is likely.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Use **exact methods** for CI (for small samples) or **Newcombe/Wilson** CIs for RD/RR.

## A3) Between-group comparison of baseline characteristics (descriptive + standardized differences)
- **Test name:** Prefer **standardized mean difference (SMD)** / standardized differences (not hypothesis testing), but if journal requires: t-test/Mann–Whitney; chi-square/Fisher.
- **Purpose:** Assess baseline balance and identify covariates for adjusted analyses.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Randomization method is unclear; imbalance is plausible.
  2. Baseline pain (VAS) is measured and could strongly relate to follow-up pain.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Significance testing baseline variables is often discouraged (tests randomization, not balance).
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Do not use baseline p-values to decide whether randomization “worked.”
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Use **SMDs** and clinical judgment; adjust for key prognostic variables regardless of baseline p-values.

# CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED tests (improve rigor)

## B1) Logistic regression (binary pain at 48h) adjusted for baseline VAS (and other covariates if available)
- **Purpose:** Estimate treatment effect on pain incidence while adjusting for **baseline pain** and potential imbalances (age, sex, tooth location if collected).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Primary endpoint is binary; logistic regression matches outcome scale.
  2. Baseline VAS is a likely strong predictor; adjustment improves **precision** and reduces confounding risk given unclear randomization.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - With small sample and few events, model may be overfit and estimates unstable.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When events per parameter are too low (rule-of-thumb concerns), or quasi-separation occurs.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Penalized logistic regression** (Firth correction) or **exact logistic regression**; or simplify to unadjusted RR via log-binomial/Poisson robust if stable.

## B2) Analysis of VAS as continuous/ordinal outcome at 48 hours (secondary)
- **Test name:** **Mann–Whitney U test** (Wilcoxon rank-sum) for VAS at 48h (0–10)
- **Purpose:** Compare distribution of 48h VAS between groups without dichotomizing.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. VAS often non-normal and bounded (0–10); rank-based test is robust.
  2. Avoids information loss from the 0–4/5–10 dichotomy.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - If there are many ties (common with discrete VAS reporting) interpretation becomes about stochastic dominance, not medians strictly.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If primary outcome is strictly defined as binary and protocol forbids additional analyses (not the case here; recommended as secondary).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Permutation test** for difference in means/medians; or **ordinal logistic regression** if treating VAS as ordinal categories.

## B3) ANCOVA / linear regression for 48h VAS adjusting for baseline VAS (if VAS treated approximately continuous)
- **Purpose:** Compare mean 48h VAS between groups controlling for baseline VAS.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Baseline VAS adjustment can increase power/precision.
  2. Straightforward clinical interpretation (adjusted mean difference).
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - If VAS is highly skewed, bounded, or has ceiling/floor effects, linear model assumptions may be poor.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When residuals are markedly non-normal/heteroscedastic and transformation doesn’t help, or when VAS is essentially ordinal with few distinct values.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Quantile regression** (median regression), **robust regression**, or **ordinal logistic regression**; or analyze binary pain with logistic regression as primary.

## B4) Within-group change in VAS from baseline to 48h (paired analysis; supportive)
- **Test name:** **Wilcoxon signed-rank** (or paired t-test if normal)
- **Purpose:** Describe whether pain changed over time within each group.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Baseline and 48h VAS are repeated on same patient.
  2. Provides context (both groups may improve similarly).
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Within-group significance does not answer the comparative treatment question and can mislead.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Do not use within-group p-values as evidence of superiority/non-inferiority between groups.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Sign test** (if many ties/outliers).

## B5) Adverse/analgesic consumption reporting (if collected): comparison of acetaminophen use
- **Test name:** Chi-square/Fisher for “took any medication” in SOS group; or count models for tablets consumed.
- **Purpose:** Contextualize exposure and interpret efficacy.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. In SOS arm, actual intake varies; exposure is clinically meaningful.
  2. Helps interpret null findings (e.g., high SOS use could mimic scheduled dosing).
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Medication intake data were not reported as collected; may be unavailable.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If adherence/intake data are unreliable or not collected systematically.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Descriptive summaries only; sensitivity analysis excluding non-adherent participants (per-protocol).

## B6) Missing data handling: Multiple imputation (if >~5% missing outcomes)
- **Purpose:** Reduce bias and improve efficiency if follow-up VAS missing.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Follow-up via phone is prone to nonresponse.
  2. MI can be valid under missing-at-random assumptions with baseline predictors.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - With very small sample, MI models can be unstable; if missing not at random, MI may still be biased.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Extremely small sample with complex imputation model; or when missingness mechanism strongly suggests MNAR and cannot be modeled.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Sensitivity analyses** (best/worst case), pattern-mixture models, or transparent complete-case with bias discussion.

## TASK 3: Inappropriate tests (should NOT be used)

1. **One-way ANOVA (unadjusted) as primary test**
   - **Why inappropriate:** Only two groups (t-test equivalent) and outcome is primarily **binary**; ANOVA doesn’t match defined endpoint.

2. **Multiple independent t-tests across arbitrary VAS cutpoints**
   - **Why inappropriate:** Inflates Type I error; data-driven thresholds.

3. **Paired t-test comparing Group 1 vs Group 2**
   - **Why inappropriate:** Groups are **independent**; pairing does not exist.

4. **Correlation (Pearson/Spearman) between group code and pain as the main analysis**
   - **Why inappropriate:** Group is categorical; correlation is not the correct inferential framework for treatment comparison.

5. **Relying only on within-group pre–post significance**
   - **Why inappropriate:** Does not answer the randomized comparative question; can be significant in both groups even if no difference.

6. **Complex multivariable models with many predictors (stepwise regression)**
   - **Why inappropriate:** n=60 → high overfitting risk; biased coefficients and p-values.

## TASK 4: Summary justification of the overall approach

The protocol defines a **two-arm clinical trial** with a **binary primary endpoint (pain vs no pain at 48h)** derived from VAS, so the core analysis must compare **pain incidence between independent groups** (chi-square/Fisher) with **effect sizes and 95% CIs**. Because baseline VAS is recorded and randomization details are unclear, **adjusted modeling (logistic regression/ANCOVA)** is recommended to improve precision and mitigate imbalance. Secondary analyses should avoid information loss by also analyzing **VAS on its original scale** (nonparametric or robust/ordinal methods), with transparent handling of missing follow-up data.
