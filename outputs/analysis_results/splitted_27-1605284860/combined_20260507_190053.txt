# CATEGORY A – MANDATORY tests (core analyses)

## A1) Chi-square test (or Fisher’s exact) for pain incidence at 48 hours (binary)
- **Purpose:** Compare **proportion with pain (VAS 5–10)** between Group 1 vs Group 2 at 48 hours.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Outcome as defined in methods is **binary** (pain vs no pain).
  2. Two **independent** parallel groups.
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - Expected cell counts may be small (n≈30/group), violating chi-square approximation.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When any expected cell count is too low (commonly <5).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Fisher’s exact test** (exact p-value), and report **RR/OR with exact CI** as feasible.

## A2) Effect size estimation for binary outcome (RR/OR, RD with 95% CI)
- **Purpose:** Quantify magnitude of difference in pain incidence between groups.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Clinical interpretability (e.g., “risk of pain reduced by X%”).
  2. P-values alone are insufficient; CIs communicate precision.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - With very low/high event rates, OR can be unstable/misleading; RD may be sample-size sensitive.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Avoid OR as primary effect measure if outcome is common and misinterpretation as RR is likely.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Use **exact methods** for CI (for small samples) or **Newcombe/Wilson** CIs for RD/RR.

## A3) Between-group comparison of baseline characteristics (descriptive + standardized differences)
- **Test name:** Prefer **standardized mean difference (SMD)** / standardized differences (not hypothesis testing), but if journal requires: t-test/Mann–Whitney; chi-square/Fisher.
- **Purpose:** Assess baseline balance and identify covariates for adjusted analyses.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Randomization method is unclear; imbalance is plausible.
  2. Baseline pain (VAS) is measured and could strongly relate to follow-up pain.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Significance testing baseline variables is often discouraged (tests randomization, not balance).
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Do not use baseline p-values to decide whether randomization “worked.”
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Use **SMDs** and clinical judgment; adjust for key prognostic variables regardless of baseline p-values.

# CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED tests (improve rigor)

## B1) Logistic regression (binary pain at 48h) adjusted for baseline VAS (and other covariates if available)
- **Purpose:** Estimate treatment effect on pain incidence while adjusting for **baseline pain** and potential imbalances (age, sex, tooth location if collected).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Primary endpoint is binary; logistic regression matches outcome scale.
  2. Baseline VAS is a likely strong predictor; adjustment improves **precision** and reduces confounding risk given unclear randomization.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - With small sample and few events, model may be overfit and estimates unstable.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When events per parameter are too low (rule-of-thumb concerns), or quasi-separation occurs.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Penalized logistic regression** (Firth correction) or **exact logistic regression**; or simplify to unadjusted RR via log-binomial/Poisson robust if stable.

## B2) Analysis of VAS as continuous/ordinal outcome at 48 hours (secondary)
- **Test name:** **Mann–Whitney U test** (Wilcoxon rank-sum) for VAS at 48h (0–10)
- **Purpose:** Compare distribution of 48h VAS between groups without dichotomizing.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. VAS often non-normal and bounded (0–10); rank-based test is robust.
  2. Avoids information loss from the 0–4/5–10 dichotomy.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - If there are many ties (common with discrete VAS reporting) interpretation becomes about stochastic dominance, not medians strictly.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If primary outcome is strictly defined as binary and protocol forbids additional analyses (not the case here; recommended as secondary).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Permutation test** for difference in means/medians; or **ordinal logistic regression** if treating VAS as ordinal categories.

## B3) ANCOVA / linear regression for 48h VAS adjusting for baseline VAS (if VAS treated approximately continuous)
- **Purpose:** Compare mean 48h VAS between groups controlling for baseline VAS.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Baseline VAS adjustment can increase power/precision.
  2. Straightforward clinical interpretation (adjusted mean difference).
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - If VAS is highly skewed, bounded, or has ceiling/floor effects, linear model assumptions may be poor.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When residuals are markedly non-normal/heteroscedastic and transformation doesn’t help, or when VAS is essentially ordinal with few distinct values.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Quantile regression** (median regression), **robust regression**, or **ordinal logistic regression**; or analyze binary pain with logistic regression as primary.

## B4) Within-group change in VAS from baseline to 48h (paired analysis; supportive)
- **Test name:** **Wilcoxon signed-rank** (or paired t-test if normal)
- **Purpose:** Describe whether pain changed over time within each group.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Baseline and 48h VAS are repeated on same patient.
  2. Provides context (both groups may improve similarly).
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Within-group significance does not answer the comparative treatment question and can mislead.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Do not use within-group p-values as evidence of superiority/non-inferiority between groups.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Sign test** (if many ties/outliers).

## B5) Adverse/analgesic consumption reporting (if collected): comparison of acetaminophen use
- **Test name:** Chi-square/Fisher for “took any medication” in SOS group; or count models for tablets consumed.
- **Purpose:** Contextualize exposure and interpret efficacy.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. In SOS arm, actual intake varies; exposure is clinically meaningful.
  2. Helps interpret null findings (e.g., high SOS use could mimic scheduled dosing).
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Medication intake data were not reported as collected; may be unavailable.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If adherence/intake data are unreliable or not collected systematically.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Descriptive summaries only; sensitivity analysis excluding non-adherent participants (per-protocol).

## B6) Missing data handling: Multiple imputation (if >~5% missing outcomes)
- **Purpose:** Reduce bias and improve efficiency if follow-up VAS missing.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Follow-up via phone is prone to nonresponse.
  2. MI can be valid under missing-at-random assumptions with baseline predictors.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - With very small sample, MI models can be unstable; if missing not at random, MI may still be biased.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Extremely small sample with complex imputation model; or when missingness mechanism strongly suggests MNAR and cannot be modeled.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Sensitivity analyses** (best/worst case), pattern-mixture models, or transparent complete-case with bias discussion.

## TASK 3: Inappropriate tests (should NOT be used)

1. **One-way ANOVA (unadjusted) as primary test**
   - **Why inappropriate:** Only two groups (t-test equivalent) and outcome is primarily **binary**; ANOVA doesn’t match defined endpoint.

2. **Multiple independent t-tests across arbitrary VAS cutpoints**
   - **Why inappropriate:** Inflates Type I error; data-driven thresholds.

3. **Paired t-test comparing Group 1 vs Group 2**
   - **Why inappropriate:** Groups are **independent**; pairing does not exist.

4. **Correlation (Pearson/Spearman) between group code and pain as the main analysis**
   - **Why inappropriate:** Group is categorical; correlation is not the correct inferential framework for treatment comparison.

5. **Relying only on within-group pre–post significance**
   - **Why inappropriate:** Does not answer the randomized comparative question; can be significant in both groups even if no difference.

6. **Complex multivariable models with many predictors (stepwise regression)**
   - **Why inappropriate:** n=60 → high overfitting risk; biased coefficients and p-values.

2) Checks plausibility of data (outliers, ranges) and guides choice of later tests (normality, skewness).  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Not a hypothesis test; cannot be used to claim differences without formal testing.  

When NOT to use:  
- N/A; descriptive statistics are always used, but not as stand-alone evidence of differences.  

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- For non-normal data, report median and IQR instead of mean and SD; use robust summaries or transformations if needed.

1.2 Baseline VAS comparison between groups  

- Primary aim: Verify that randomization achieved similar baseline pain distribution.  
- Test name:  
  - If approximately normal: Independent samples t-test.  
  - If non-normal: Mann–Whitney U test.  

Purpose:  
- Compare baseline pain between groups to ensure no substantial imbalance.

Why appropriate (for independent t-test):  
1) Outcome (baseline VAS) is continuous and measured on similar scale in both groups.  
2) Groups are independent (different patients), with equal or comparable sample sizes.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- If VAS is markedly skewed, ordinal, or distributions differ significantly in shape/variance, t-test assumptions are violated.  

When NOT to use (t-test):  
- When Shapiro–Wilk or visual inspection (histogram/QQ-plot) shows clear non-normality and sample size is too small for CLT to compensate, or variances are very unequal.  

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- Mann–Whitney U test (nonparametric comparison of medians/distributions).  

2. Primary Outcome: Incidence of Pain at 48 Hours  

2.1 Chi-square test (or Fisher’s exact test) for 2×2 table  

- Test name: Pearson Chi-square test of independence; Fisher’s exact test if expected counts are small.  
- Purpose: Compare the proportion of patients with pain (VAS 5–10) at 48 hours between Group 1 and Group 2.

Why appropriate:  
1) Outcome is categorical (pain vs no pain), and groups are independent.  
2) Sample size around 30 per group is adequate for Chi-square if expected cell counts ≥5; if not, Fisher’s exact provides exact p-values.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- If data are sparse (very few events), Chi-square may not be valid due to low expected counts.  

When NOT to use:  
- When any expected cell count <5 (rule of thumb) for Chi-square.  
- When groups are not independent (e.g., paired/matched design), which is not the case here.  

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- Fisher’s exact test for small samples or sparse data.  
- Exact unconditional tests (e.g., Barnard’s) if software available.  

2.2 Effect size estimation  

- Not a “test” but essential:  
  - Risk difference (RD), risk ratio (RR), and 95% confidence intervals.  
- Purpose: Quantify magnitude of between-group difference in pain incidence.

Why appropriate:  
1) Provides clinically interpretable measure beyond p-value (e.g., absolute difference in pain proportion).  
2) Supports assessment of clinical relevance and comparison with prior studies.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- If misinterpreted without considering baseline risk or wide confidence intervals.  

When NOT to use:  
- N/A – effect size reporting is standard; only caution is in interpretation if sample size is small.  

Alternative:  
- Odds ratio (OR) via logistic regression if adjusting for covariates.

3. Secondary Outcome: Continuous VAS at 48 Hours  

3.1 Independent samples t-test (or Mann–Whitney U) for 48-hour VAS  

- Test name:  
  - Independent t-test (if VAS ~ normal).  
  - Mann–Whitney U (if non-normal).  
- Purpose: Compare mean/median VAS at 48 hours between the two groups (continuous measure rather than dichotomized).

Why appropriate:  
1) VAS is inherently continuous and often treated as interval scale; t-test is standard for comparing means between two independent groups.  
2) Provides additional detail compared to binary pain/no pain classification (captures changes in moderate pain distributions).  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- VAS distributions can be skewed and bounded (0–10), violating t-test assumptions, especially with small n.  

When NOT to use (t-test):  
- When VAS distributions are highly skewed or show strong ceiling/floor effects, and sample size is small.  

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- Mann–Whitney U test, or transformation (e.g., log or square-root) followed by t-test if clinically meaningful.  

3.2 Paired analysis of change in VAS (within-group)  

- Test name:  
  - Paired t-test (baseline vs 48h within each group).  
  - Wilcoxon signed-rank test (if non-normal).  
- Purpose: Assess whether pain significantly decreased from baseline to 48h within each treatment group.

Why appropriate:  
1) Repeated measure (baseline vs 48h) on same individual, so paired tests are correct.  
2) Helps demonstrate overall efficacy of treatment (endodontic procedure plus analgesia) irrespective of regimen.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- If there is substantial measurement error or non-comparable conditions between baseline and follow-up (e.g., different measurement methods).  

When NOT to use (paired t-test):  
- When change scores are highly non-normal or sample size is very small, or if there is significant missing data at follow-up.  

Alternative:  
- Wilcoxon signed-rank test (nonparametric), or generalized linear mixed models if repeated measurements beyond two timepoints were present (not the case here).

4. Logistic Regression for Pain at 48 Hours  

- Test name: Binary logistic regression.  
- Outcome: Pain at 48 hours (yes/no).  
- Predictors: Group (regular vs on-demand) as main predictor; adjust for baseline pain (continuous baseline VAS) and possibly age, gender, tooth type/location (if available).  

Purpose:  
- Estimate adjusted odds ratio for pain between groups, controlling for potential confounders or baseline imbalance.

Why appropriate:  
1) Binary outcome with multiple predictors; logistic regression is standard.  
2) Adjustment for baseline VAS increases precision and controls for any random imbalance, improving internal validity.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- With n=60 and multiple predictors, model could be overfit if events (number with pain) are few; rule-of-thumb is ≥10 events per predictor variable.  

When NOT to use:

Alternative if assumptions violated or sparse data:  
- Exact logistic regression (if event counts are low).  
- Stratified analysis (e.g., Mantel–Haenszel risk ratio stratified by baseline pain category).  

5. ANCOVA for 48-Hour VAS Adjusting for Baseline VAS  

- Test name: Analysis of covariance (ANCOVA).  
- Outcome: 48-hour VAS (continuous).  
- Predictor: Group (regular vs SOS).  
- Covariate: Baseline VAS.  

Purpose:  
- Compare groups on 48-hour VAS, adjusting for baseline VAS, providing an adjusted mean difference.

Why appropriate:  
1) Controls for baseline differences in pain; more efficient than using change scores.  
2) Increases statistical power by reducing residual variance.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Requires approximate linear relationship between baseline VAS and 48-hour VAS, homogeneity of regression slopes, and normality of residuals.  

When NOT to use:  
- If assumption of equal slopes (interaction between group and baseline) is violated, or if residuals are highly non-normal and heteroscedastic.  

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- Nonparametric ANCOVA methods (e.g., rank ANCOVA) or analyze change scores with nonparametric tests.

Why it may not be appropriate:

When NOT to use:

TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS  

These tests should not be used for this specific study, with reasons.

1. Paired t-test or McNemar’s test for between-group comparison at 48 hours  

- Why inappropriate:  
  - The two groups are independent; paired tests assume dependent/paired observations (e.g., same patient before/after or matched pairs).  
  - Using paired tests would underestimate variance and inflate Type I error.  

2. Repeated Measures ANOVA or Mixed Models for multiple timepoints  

- Why inappropriate:  
  - Only two timepoints are used (baseline and 48h).  
  - For a simple baseline vs follow-up analysis, paired tests or ANCOVA are sufficient.  
  - Repeated measures ANOVA is overkill and requires more timepoints to capture trends.  

3. One-way ANOVA with more than two groups  

- Why inappropriate:  
  - There are only two groups (regular vs on-demand).  
  - ANOVA for multiple groups is unnecessary; independent t-test (or equivalent) suffices.  

4. Chi-square test for independence applied to ordinal VAS categories beyond binary split without justification  

- Why inappropriate:  
  - VAS is continuous; arbitrary categorization into many bins (e.g., 0–3, 4–6, 7–10) loses information and may create sparse cells.  
  - Ordinal regression or analysis of continuous VAS is preferable.  

5. Parametric tests assuming normality without checking (e.g., simple t-test on heavily skewed VAS with small sample)  

- Why inappropriate:  
  - VAS can be skewed and bounded; blindly assuming normality can mislead.  
  - Need to inspect distributions or use robust/nonparametric methods.  

6. Multiple unadjusted post-hoc pairwise tests for subgroups without correction  

- Why inappropriate:  
  - Study is not powered for extensive subgroup analyses.  
  - Multiple uncorrected tests inflate Type I error and may generate spurious findings.

TASK 4: SUMMARY AND JUSTIFICATION OF OVERALL ANALYTICAL APPROACH  

- Core question: Does prescribing acetaminophen regularly (every 6 hours) after single-visit endodontic treatment reduce post-operative pain at 48 hours compared with on-demand (SOS) use?

- Variables:  
  - Main outcome: 48-hour pain status (VAS categorized 0–4 vs 5–10).  
  - Secondary: Continuous VAS at 48 hours, baseline VAS, and change in VAS.

- The primary analytic approach uses:  
  - Descriptive statistics to characterize the sample and baseline comparability.  
  - A 2×2 comparison of pain incidence between groups using Chi-square or Fisher’s exact test, with risk ratios and confidence intervals.  
  - Continuous VAS comparisons (t-test/Mann–Whitney) to exploit the full information of VAS data.  
  - Adjusted models (logistic regression for binary outcome; ANCOVA for VAS) to control for baseline pain and other covariates, improving precision and validity.

- This approach:  
  - Matches the study design (two independent groups, pre–post measurements).  
  - Respects the nature of the data (binary and continuous outcomes, bounded VAS).  
  - Is transparent, interpretable for clinicians, and consistent with standard practice in clinical trials of analgesic regimens.  

Overall, the analysis plan centers on robust between-group comparisons of 48-hour pain (both binary and continuous), appropriate adjustment for baseline pain, and judicious use of nonparametric alternatives where assumptions of parametric tests might be violated.
