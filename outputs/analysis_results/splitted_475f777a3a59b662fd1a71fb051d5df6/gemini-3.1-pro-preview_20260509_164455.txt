### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS
*Tests without which the analysis would be incomplete.*

**1. Shapiro-Wilk Test**
*   **Purpose:** To assess the normality of the distribution for continuous variables (NCF and fragment length) within each group.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) It is highly robust and provides the highest power for testing normality in smaller sample sizes ($n=20$ per group). 
    2) It dictates whether parametric or non-parametric tests must be used for the primary analysis.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** In some cases, with exactly $n=20$, it might lack the power to detect slight deviations from normality, though it remains the gold standard.
*   **When this test should NOT be used:** When analyzing categorical data (e.g., fracture mode).
*   **Alternative if assumptions are violated:** Visual inspection of Q-Q plots and histograms, or the Kolmogorov-Smirnov test (though less powerful).

**2. Levene’s Test**
*   **Purpose:** To assess the homogeneity of variances across the 7 independent groups.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) Equal variance is a strict assumption for standard One-Way ANOVA. 
    2) It is less sensitive to departures from normality than Bartlett’s test.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** If the data is heavily skewed, the mean-based Levene's test can be inaccurate (though the median-based version can be used).
*   **When this test should NOT be used:** When comparing dependent/paired samples.
*   **Alternative if assumptions are violated:** Brown-Forsythe test.

**3. One-Way Analysis of Variance (ANOVA)**
*   **Purpose:** To determine if there are statistically significant differences in the mean NCF and fragment lengths across the 7 instrument groups.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) The study compares a single continuous dependent variable across more than two independent categorical groups. 
    2) It controls the overall Type I error rate, which would be inflated if multiple t-tests were used.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It assumes normal distribution and equal variances; fatigue data (like NCF) is often skewed (e.g., Weibull or log-normal distributions).
*   **When this test should NOT be used:** If the data is ordinal, highly skewed, or if variances are vastly unequal.
*   **Alternative if assumptions are violated:** Kruskal-Wallis H test (if normality is violated) or Welch’s ANOVA (if normality is met but variances are unequal).

**4. Tukey’s Honestly Significant Difference (HSD) Post-Hoc Test**
*   **Purpose:** To perform pairwise comparisons between specific instrument groups to see exactly *which* ones differ, after a significant ANOVA result.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) It strictly controls the Family-Wise Error Rate (FWER) during multiple comparisons. 
    2) It is highly optimized for study designs with equal sample sizes ($n=20$ per group).
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It is overly conservative if the researcher is only interested in comparing each group to a single control, rather than all possible pairs.
*   **When this test should NOT be used:** If Levene's test shows unequal variances, or if the omnibus ANOVA is not statistically significant.
*   **Alternative if assumptions are violated:** Games-Howell post-hoc test (if variances are unequal) or Dunn’s test with Bonferroni correction (if Kruskal-Wallis was used).

#### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED TESTS
*Tests that enhance the quality and rigor of the analysis.*

**1. Kaplan-Meier Survival Analysis & Log-Rank Test**
*   **Purpose:** To analyze the "time to fracture" (or cycles to fracture) accounting for instruments that did not break.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) The methodology states tests were limited to 600 seconds. Any instrument surviving past this point represents "right-censored" data. Standard ANOVA cannot handle censored data. 
    2) It provides a highly visual and clinically relevant "survival curve" for the instruments.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** If *all* instruments fractured well before the 600-second mark, there is no censored data, making standard ANOVA sufficient.
*   **When this test should NOT be used:** When the outcome is not time-to-event or if the exact time of failure is unknown (interval censoring).
*   **Alternative if assumptions are violated:** Cox Proportional-Hazards model (if adjusting for covariates).

**2. Weibull Reliability Analysis**
*   **Purpose:** To calculate the probability of instrument survival at specific cycle counts and determine the predictability of failure.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) It is the engineering and biomechanical gold standard for material fatigue and failure testing. 
    2) The Weibull modulus (shape parameter) provides insight into the reliability and predictability of the instrument's failure, which means more clinically than just "average cycles to failure."
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It requires a specific data distribution; if the failure mechanism is mixed (e.g., both torsional and cyclic), a single Weibull distribution may not fit well.
*   **When this test should NOT be used:** For analyzing the fragment length data, as this is not a time-to-failure metric.
*   **Alternative if assumptions are violated:** Lognormal distribution survival analysis.

#### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL TESTS
*Tests that provide supplementary insights.*

**1. Pearson Correlation Coefficient**
*   **Purpose:** To evaluate the linear relationship between Fragment Length and NCF.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) It can reveal if instruments that break further up the shaft (longer fragments) survive longer or shorter. 
    2) It provides insight into the biomechanics of the 60° curvature stress point.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It assumes a linear relationship; the relationship between fracture location and fatigue life might be non-linear.
*   **When this test should NOT be used:** If the data for either variable is heavily skewed or contains extreme outliers.
*   **Alternative if assumptions are violated:** Spearman’s Rank Correlation.

### TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS

*   **Independent Samples t-test:** Should NOT be used because there are 7 groups. Running multiple t-tests (e.g., Group 1 vs 2, Group 1 vs 3) will drastically inflate the Type I error rate (false positives).
*   **Paired t-test / Repeated Measures ANOVA:** Should NOT be used because the instruments are independent. An instrument is tested until it breaks; it cannot be tested again. There are no repeated measures on the same subject.
*   **Chi-Square Test of Independence (for NCF):** Should NOT be used because NCF is a continuous numerical variable, not categorical. (It *could* be used for fracture mode, but since the authors are just verifying cyclic fatigue to exclude torsional fracture, the variance will likely be zero).
*   **Multivariate Analysis of Variance (MANOVA):** While there are two dependent variables (NCF and fragment length), they measure entirely different physical phenomena. Combining them into a single synthetic variable via MANOVA would complicate clinical interpretation without adding value.

### TASK 4: SUMMARY

The overall analytical approach must treat this as an independent, multi-group comparative study. The primary analytical pathway relies on checking assumptions (Shapiro-Wilk, Levene's) followed by an omnibus test (One-Way ANOVA or Welch's/Kruskal-Wallis) to determine if core mass and alloy significantly affect fatigue life (NCF) and fragment length. Post-hoc testing (Tukey or Games-Howell) is strictly required to identify which specific files outperform the others. 

Crucially, because the experimental design includes a 600-second cutoff, the biostatistical approach *must* account for potential right-censoring. Therefore, incorporating Survival Analysis (Kaplan-Meier) and engineering-standard Weibull Reliability Analysis will elevate the paper from a basic comparative study to a highly rigorous, clinically applicable biomechanical evaluation. Finally, the discrepancy in the stated sample size (120 total vs. 7 groups of 20 = 140) must be resolved prior to running the analysis.
