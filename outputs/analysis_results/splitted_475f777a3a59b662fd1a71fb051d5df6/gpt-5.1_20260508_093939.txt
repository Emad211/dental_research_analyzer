### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY ANALYSES

#### A1. Descriptive statistics and distribution assessment

**Test / Procedure name:**  
- Descriptive statistics + distribution diagnostics (e.g., Shapiro–Wilk test, Q–Q plots, histograms, skewness/kurtosis)

**Purpose:**  
- To summarize central tendency and dispersion of NCF and fragment length by group, and assess normality and potential outliers to inform choice of inferential tests.

**Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):**  
1. NCF and fragment length are continuous outcomes; descriptive summaries (mean, SD, median, IQR) are essential for interpretation and comparison.  
2. Parametric tests (ANOVA, t-tests) require approximate normality and homoscedasticity; distribution diagnostics guide whether these assumptions are reasonable.

**Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason):**  
- For small sample sizes (e.g., in subgroups, if some groups have fewer failures), normality tests like Shapiro–Wilk have low power and can be misleading.

**When this should NOT be used:**  
- None; descriptive statistics and visual diagnostics should always be done. But overreliance on formal normality tests in very small samples is discouraged.

**Alternative if assumptions are violated:**  
- If clearly non-normal and sample size is small, use robust summaries (median, IQR) and nonparametric inference (see A2/A3).

#### A2. One-way ANOVA for NCF (primary group comparison)

**Test name:**  
- One-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) on NCF across instrument systems.

**Purpose:**  
- To compare mean NCF between the 6 instrument groups, determining if any group differs in cyclic fatigue resistance.

**Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):**  
1. Design: independent groups with a continuous outcome and roughly equal sample size (n = 20/group) suits ANOVA.  
2. Sample size is moderate and balanced, making ANOVA robust to modest deviations from normality and variance equality.

**Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason):**  
- If NCF distributions are severely skewed or contain many censored (“run-out”) values at the upper limit (600 s), the normality assumption may fail, and ANOVA on raw NCF becomes questionable.

**When this test should NOT be used:**  
- When more than ~10–20% of instruments in one or more groups do not fracture within 600 s and are censored; then NCF is not fully observed and standard ANOVA is inappropriate.  
- When homogeneity of variance is grossly violated (e.g., very large differences in SD across groups) and transformations do not help.

**Alternative if assumptions are violated:**  
- If data are non-normal but uncensored and variance heterogeneity modest: use **Welch ANOVA**.  
- If strong non-normality: use **Kruskal–Wallis test** on NCF.  
- If censoring is substantial: use **survival analysis (log-rank test, Cox regression)** on time to fracture (see A4).

---

#### A3. Multiple comparisons / post hoc tests for NCF

**Test name:**  
- Post hoc multiple comparison procedures (e.g., Tukey’s HSD; or Games–Howell if variances unequal).

**Purpose:**  
- To identify which specific instrument pairs differ in mean NCF if ANOVA is significant.

**Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):**  
1. With >2 groups, ANOVA only tells us that at least one group differs; post hoc tests locate these differences.  
2. Tukey’s HSD controls family-wise error rate with balanced group sizes and is straightforward to interpret.

**Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason):**  
- If variances are unequal and/or sample sizes unbalanced, Tukey’s standard HSD may give misleading results.

**When this test should NOT be used:**  
- When the overall ANOVA F-test is not significant; pairwise post hoc tests then inflate type I error without clear benefit.  
- When data are heavily tied/censored and ANOVA itself is inappropriate.

**Alternative if assumptions are violated:**  
- For unequal variances: **Games–Howell** post hoc test.  
- For nonparametric data using Kruskal–Wallis: **Dunn’s test** (or Conover–Iman) with appropriate multiplicity correction (e.g., Bonferroni, Holm).

---

#### A4. Survival analysis for time to fracture (accounting for censoring)

**Test name:**  
- Kaplan–Meier survival curves + log-rank test comparing instrument groups.

**Purpose:**  
- To compare the distribution of **time to fracture** across groups while correctly handling tests terminated without fracture at 600 s (right-censoring).

**Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):**  
1. Time to event (fracture) with administrative censoring at 600 s is a classic survival data structure; Kaplan–Meier/log-rank is standard.  
2. It avoids arbitrary handling of run-outs (e.g., assigning them fixed maximum NCF), thus giving more valid comparison.

**Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason):**  
- If almost all instruments fracture well before 600 s (minimal censoring), survival methods add complexity with little gain.

**When this test should NOT be used:**  
- If times are recorded with substantial rounding to only a few discrete values and there is no censoring, survival methods are unnecessary; standard ANOVA on time or NCF is adequate.

**Alternative if assumptions are violated:**  
- If proportional hazards assumption is violated for log-rank / Cox models, use:
  - **Restricted mean survival time (RMST) comparison** or  
  - **Nonparametric tests for interval-censored data** (if timing is coarse) or 
  - Stratified or time-dependent Cox models.

#### A5. Two-way ANOVA (or equivalent) by taper/alloy group (if explicitly analyzed)

**Test name:**  
- Two-way ANOVA on NCF with factors:
  - Taper/core-mass group (e.g., 0.04 vs 0.06/variable)  
  - Alloy category (e.g., conventional vs heat-treated vs twisted), if definable.

**Purpose:**  
- To formally test main effects of **core mass (taper group)** and **alloy type**, and their interaction, on cyclic fatigue resistance.

**Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):**  
1. The stated research question is the impact of **core mass** and **alloy**, not just “brand”; factorial ANOVA aligns with the hypothesis about these two parameters.  
2. Balanced design with ~20 observations per cell (or per combination category) supports factorial analysis.

**Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason):**  
- Alloy type and taper/core mass may not be orthogonal across instruments; classification may be ambiguous or some combinations missing (e.g., no conventional alloy in certain taper categories), making a full factorial ANOVA impossible.

**When this test should NOT be used:**  
- When some factor combinations are absent or n is too small in some cells (e.g., 0 or very few instruments), leading to unstable estimates or non-estimable interaction terms.

**Alternative if assumptions are violated / structure unsuitable:**  
- Use **general linear model** with instrument as fixed effect and include relevant continuous covariates (e.g., rpm, nominal core area if measured).

#### A6. Comparison of fragment lengths across groups

**Test name:**  
- One-way ANOVA (or Kruskal–Wallis) for fragment length.

**Purpose:**  
- To verify that the length of the fractured segment is similar across groups (confirming fractures occur in equivalent regions of curvature).

**Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):**  
1. Fragment length is continuous and measured in all fractured instruments; differences might confound interpretation of cyclic fatigue (different stress regions).  
2. Demonstrating non-significant differences (or small effect sizes) supports comparability of fracture location and test standardization.

**Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason):**  
- If some instruments are censored (no fracture), fragment length is undefined for those; groups with few fractures may have small n for fragment length, making parametric analysis underpowered.

**When this test should NOT be used:**  
- When a group has too few fractures (e.g., <5) to support meaningful inferential statistics. In that case, descriptive analysis only.

**Alternative if assumptions are violated:**  
- Use **Kruskal–Wallis** test if distribution non-normal or sample size per group small.

#### B3. Homogeneity of variance tests

**Test name:**  
- Levene’s test or Brown–Forsythe test.

**Purpose:**  
- To assess equality of variances across groups before/with ANOVA on NCF and fragment length.

**Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):**

**Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason):**  
- In small samples, Levene’s test has low power and a non-significant result doesn’t guarantee equal variances.

**When this test should NOT be used:**  
- If sample sizes are very small (e.g., n < 10 per group) or if ANOVA result is clearly robust; but even then it can still be run with caution.

**Alternative if assumptions are violated:**  
- Use **Welch ANOVA** and appropriate post hoc tests (Games–Howell).  
- Consider variance-stabilizing transformations.

## TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS AND WHY

Below are statistical tests/methods that should NOT be used for this study, with rationale.

1. **Independent-samples t-test for all pairwise comparisons without correction**
   - **Why inappropriate:**  
     - There are 6 groups; pairwise t-tests (15 comparisons) without multiplicity correction inflate type I error substantially.  
   - **Better alternative:** ANOVA + appropriate post hoc procedure.

2. **Paired t-test or repeated-measures ANOVA**
   - **Why inappropriate:**  
     - Each instrument is measured once and belongs to a single group; there are no repeated measurements on the same unit.  
   - **Better alternative:** One-way ANOVA (independent groups).

3. **Chi-square test for comparing NCF or time to fracture**
   - **Why inappropriate:**  
     - NCF and time to fracture are continuous; dichotomizing them (e.g., high vs low fatigue resistance) wastes information and reduces power.  
   - **Better alternative:** ANOVA, Kruskal–Wallis, or survival methods on continuous data.

4. **Simple linear regression of NCF on rpm alone to infer instrument differences**
   - **Why inappropriate:**  
     - RPM is fixed by design for each instrument; regression of NCF on rpm confounds instrumental and design differences and does not answer the question about instrument/alloy/core mass.  
   - **Better alternative:** Group-based comparisons with rpm as covariate in a multi-variable model if needed.

5. **Correlation between instrument group (categorical) and NCF using Pearson correlation**
   - **Why inappropriate:**  
     - Pearson correlation requires numeric variables; coding categories as arbitrary numbers (1–6) misrepresents relationships.  
   - **Better alternative:** ANOVA or regression with dummy variables.

6. **Student’s t-test to compare more than two groups simultaneously**
   - **Why inappropriate:**  
     - t-test is limited to two groups; multiple t-tests across several groups leads to inflated type I error and ignores overall group structure.  
   - **Better alternative:** One-way ANOVA or Kruskal–Wallis.

7. **Parametric methods ignoring censoring (e.g., ANOVA on time to fracture when many tests end at 600 s without proper handling)**
   - **Why inappropriate:**  
     - Censored observations are not true times; treating them as equal to 600 s biases estimates and violates assumptions.  
   - **Better alternative:** Kaplan–Meier + log-rank, Cox regression, or parametric survival models.

8. **Logistic regression modelling “fracture vs no fracture by 600 s” as the primary outcome**
   - **Why inappropriate:**  
     - There is rich information on the time to fracture; collapsing to a binary indicator loses resolution and power.  
   - **Better alternative:** Survival analysis; NCF/time to fracture as continuous outcome.

9. **MANOVA on NCF and time to fracture simultaneously**
   - **Why inappropriate:**  
     - Time to fracture and NCF are linearly related given rpm; they are not independent multivariate outcomes, so MANOVA adds no value and can violate assumptions.  
   - **Better alternative:** Analyze one primary outcome (NCF or time) and, if desired, the other as supportive.

## TASK 4: SUMMARY – JUSTIFICATION OF OVERALL ANALYTICAL APPROACH

The study is a controlled in vitro experiment with six independent instrument groups, balanced sample sizes, and continuous primary outcomes (NCF, time to fracture) with potential right-censoring. The core scientific question concerns the effect of instrument design factors (core mass, alloy) on cyclic fatigue resistance.

A suitable analysis framework therefore:

1. Starts with robust **descriptive statistics and distribution checks** for NCF and fragment length.
2. Uses **group-comparison methods** aligned with the design:  
   - **One-way ANOVA** (or nonparametric equivalent) to test for overall differences among instruments.  
   - **Post hoc tests** to localize differences while controlling for multiple comparisons.
3. Incorporates **survival analysis** (Kaplan–Meier, log-rank, possibly Cox/AFT models) to properly address censoring (tests terminated at 600 s).
4. Optionally employs **factorial or regression models** to more directly answer the mechanistic question about alloy and core mass, adjusting for rpm and other design parameters.
5. Examines **fragment length** to confirm comparable fracture location and thus standardization of stress conditions.

This approach respects the structure of the data (continuous outcomes, possible censoring, independent groups, balanced design), aligns with the stated hypothesis (core mass and alloy effects), and avoids loss of information or inflated error rates that would arise with inappropriate binary or pairwise methods.
