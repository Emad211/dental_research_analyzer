# CATEGORY A — MANDATORY tests/models

### A1) Descriptive statistics with uncertainty (proportions, rates)
- **Test/model:** Estimation of proportions with **95% CIs** (Wilson/binomial), and/or **rate** estimates for counts.
- **Purpose:** Quantify service mix by payment method (and by dentist group).
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Outcomes are categorical/count-based; proportions are the natural summary for “service mix.”
  2. CIs communicate precision and are needed for interpretation beyond p-values.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):** If each record can contain multiple services and categories overlap, simple proportions per record may be ill-defined without a clear counting rule.
- **When NOT to use:** When denominators differ in meaning across dentists (e.g., “services” vs “patients” vs “visits”) and have not been standardized.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:** Use dentist-level **compositional** summaries (e.g., multinomial proportions per dentist-period) with bootstrap CIs.

### A2) Mixed-effects multinomial (or ordinal) regression for service category ~ payment method
- **Test/model:**  
  - **Multinomial mixed-effects logistic regression** (4-category nominal outcome), *or*  
  - **Ordinal mixed-effects model** (cumulative logit) if categories can be justified as ordered by aggressiveness.
  - Include **random intercept for dentist**; fixed effect for **payment method**; consider period indicator.
- **Purpose:** Primary inferential test of association between payment method and service mix while accounting for clustering within dentist.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Outcome is multi-category; multinomial/ordinal models match the data-generating structure.
  2. Random effects address non-independence of records within dentist and allow unequal records per dentist/period.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - If the dataset is actually analyzed at dentist-period aggregated level only (36–72 rows), record-level mixed models may be unnecessary or unstable.
- **When NOT to use:** When there are sparse cells (e.g., surgery extremely rare) causing separation/non-convergence.
- **Alternatives if assumptions violated:**
  - Collapse categories (e.g., combine aggressive + highly aggressive).
  - Use **GEE multinomial** with robust SEs (population-average).
  - Use **penalized multinomial logistic regression** (Firth-type / ridge) if separation.

### A3) Within-dentist paired comparison for the “both periods” dentists
- **Test/model (choose based on aggregation):**
  - If using dentist-period proportions: **paired t-test** on proportion difference (with transformation if needed) *or* **Wilcoxon signed-rank**.
  - If using counts by category: **McNemar test** is not directly applicable for >2 categories; instead use **paired multinomial modeling** (mixed model already covers this) or analyze a binary contrast (e.g., “aggressive-or-worse” vs not) with McNemar.
- **Purpose:** Leverage the natural experiment (same dentist under two payment methods) to reduce confounding by dentist characteristics.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Controls for time-invariant dentist factors (skill, preferences).
  2. Aligns with the study’s key design feature: payment method change.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):** Only a subset of dentists are paired; paired analysis alone would discard salary-only/FFS-only dentists.
- **When NOT to use:** When the “both-period” group is too small to provide stable estimates or when the measure is not comparable across periods.
- **Alternatives if assumptions violated:** Use the mixed-effects multinomial/ordinal model with a random dentist effect and a payment indicator (preferred unified approach).

# CATEGORY B — RECOMMENDED tests/models

### B1) Two-part approach: dentist-period aggregation + regression on compositional outcomes
- **Test/model:**  
  - Compute per dentist-period the vector of proportions across 4 categories (summing to 1).  
  - Fit **Dirichlet regression** (or multinomial logistic regression on aggregated counts with dentist as cluster).
- **Purpose:** Model service mix as a composition and provide dentist-level inference.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. “Service mix” is inherently compositional; categories compete for the same total.
  2. Dentist-period is a clinically meaningful unit; reduces within-dentist record correlation concerns.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):** Dirichlet regression can struggle with zeros (e.g., no surgeries for many dentists).
- **When NOT to use:** When many dentists have structural zeros in categories.
- **Alternatives if assumptions violated:** Zero-inflated Dirichlet variants; or multinomial logistic regression on counts with small-sample robust SEs / bootstrap.

### B2) Negative binomial (or Poisson) mixed model for counts of specific services
- **Test/model:** **GLMM Poisson** or **negative binomial** for counts (e.g., number of extractions per dentist-period), with offset = total records/visits if needed; random intercept for dentist.
- **Purpose:** Assess payment method effect on specific service volumes (e.g., surgery, extractions, scaling).
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Outcomes are counts; NB handles overdispersion common in clinical service counts.
  2. Allows differing exposure/denominator via offsets and accounts for dentist clustering.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):** If counts are bounded by sampling design (exactly 85 records) and represent proportions better than “rates.”
- **When NOT to use:** When the count is extremely sparse with many zeros and model fit is unstable.
- **Alternatives if assumptions violated:** Zero-inflated NB; hurdle model; or analyze as binary (any/none) with mixed logistic regression.

### B3) Model diagnostics / assumption checks
- **Test:**  
  - For ordinal model: **proportional odds assumption** test (Brant-type) / graphical checks.  
  - For GLMMs: residual/overdispersion checks; influence diagnostics at dentist level.
- **Purpose:** Validate that the chosen model is credible.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Payment-method conclusions depend on correct link/variance assumptions.
  2. Sparse categories and clustering can distort standard errors if unchecked.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):** Some formal tests are unreliable for mixed models; may rely on simulation-based diagnostics.
- **When NOT to use:** When software/test is known invalid for the fitted mixed model.
- **Alternatives:** Parametric bootstrap checks; posterior predictive checks (Bayesian).

### B4) Sensitivity analysis for time-period confounding (seasonality/case-mix drift)
- **Test/model:** Add **period indicator** (Oct vs Mar) and, if possible, patient-level covariates (age, sex) if available; otherwise acknowledge limitation.
- **Purpose:** Distinguish payment-method effects from temporal changes.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Payment method is perfectly confounded with calendar time in this clinic.
  2. Dental demand/service mix may vary seasonally or with management changes.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):** If no covariates beyond payment period exist, adjustment is limited.
- **When NOT to use:** When period and payment are identical and no independent variation exists (cannot statistically separate).
- **Alternatives:** Restrict inference to “combined change package” (payment+outsourcing+time), or analyze only dentists present in both periods (difference-in-differences not possible without a control clinic).

# CATEGORY C — OPTIONAL analyses

### C1) Inter-rater reliability (if multiple abstractors)
- **Test:** **Cohen’s kappa / weighted kappa** for category assignment; ICC for counts.
- **Purpose:** Quantify extraction consistency.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Chart review is error-prone; reliability supports validity.
  2. Service categorization contains subjective grouping.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):** Not feasible if only one extractor or no duplicate abstraction.
- **When NOT to use:** When no repeated ratings exist.
- **Alternatives:** Adjudication + audit sample with percent agreement.

### C2) Nonparametric comparison across the 3 dentist groups (salary-only / FFS-only / both)
- **Test:** **Kruskal–Wallis** (on dentist-level proportion of aggressive services), with Dunn post-hoc.
- **Purpose:** Simple group comparison when modeling is not desired.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Dentist-level outcomes may be non-normal.
  2. Provides an assumption-light check consistent with descriptive findings.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):** Ignores within-dentist pairing for the “both” group unless handled carefully.
- **When NOT to use:** When pairing is central and you can fit mixed models.
- **Alternatives:** Mixed models; permutation tests respecting clustering.

### C3) Effect size reporting
- **Test/metric:** **Odds ratios / relative risk ratios** (multinomial), marginal predicted probabilities, and absolute risk differences.
- **Purpose:** Quantify magnitude, not only significance.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Policy-relevant interpretation for payment method effects.
  2. Facilitates comparison across categories and studies.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):** ORs can exaggerate perceived effects when outcomes are common.
- **When NOT to use:** When model fit is poor or separation exists.
- **Alternatives:** Predicted probabilities; risk differences via marginal standardization.

## TASK 3 — Inappropriate tests (should NOT be used)

1. **Independent-samples t-test on record-level outcomes**  
   - Why inappropriate: records are clustered within dentists (violates independence); outcome is categorical, not continuous.

2. **Simple chi-square test of independence on all records (salary vs FFS)**  
   - Why inappropriate: ignores dentist-level clustering and paired dentists; p-values will be anti-conservative.

3. **One-way ANOVA on raw counts without offsets/denominators**  
   - Why inappropriate: counts depend on number of sampled charts and dispersion; violates normality/constant variance; ignores clustering.

4. **Pearson correlation between payment method (binary) and service category code (1–4)**  
   - Why inappropriate: treats categorical/ordinal outcome as interval-scaled; correlation is not meaningful for multinomial structure.

5. **McNemar test for the 4-category outcome**  
   - Why inappropriate: McNemar is for paired **binary** outcomes only (unless you first create a justified binary contrast).

## TASK 4 — Summary justification of analytical approach

Primary inference should treat **service mix as a multi-category outcome** and **account for clustering by dentist** and the presence of **within-dentist repeated observations** for dentists working under both payment methods. A **mixed-effects multinomial (or ordinal) regression** provides a unified analysis that uses all available records, yields interpretable effect estimates, and respects the hierarchical/paired elements of the design. Dentist-period aggregated and count-model sensitivity analyses strengthen robustness, while diagnostics and multiplicity control prevent overinterpretation of multiple service endpoints.

A3a. Paired t‑test (per outcome)  

Purpose  
- To test whether the mean proportion (across dentists) of a given service category differs between salary and FFS among dentists who worked in both periods.  

Why appropriate  
1) Data are naturally paired: each dentist is observed under both payment systems. Paired t‑test is designed for comparing mean differences in paired continuous outcomes.  
2) The outcome (proportion) is approximately continuous in [0,1] and sample size of dentists (n≈12) may allow approximate normality of differences if not extremely skewed.  

Why it may not be appropriate  
- Proportions bounded between 0 and 1 often show non‑normal distributions, especially with small n, and equal variance assumption may be violated.  

When NOT to use  
- When the distribution of within‑dentist differences is highly non‑normal (e.g., many 0s and 1s) or when n is very small (n<10) or data are ordinal/non‑interval.  

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Wilcoxon signed‑rank test on paired differences (see A3b).  

A3b. Wilcoxon signed‑rank test (paired non‑parametric)  

Purpose  
- To compare the median difference in proportions of a given service category between salary and FFS across the same dentists.  

Why appropriate  
1) Suitable for paired data without assuming normality of the difference distribution.  
2) Robust to outliers and skewed distributions in small samples of dentists.  

Why it may not be appropriate  
- Less power than paired t‑test under true normality; yields inference about median of paired differences, which some may find less intuitive than mean differences.  

When NOT to use  
- When there are many tied pairs or when outcome is nominal without ordering.  

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Permutation test for paired data; or mixed‑effects logistic/binomial model at record level.  

---

A4. Between‑dentist comparison of service mix (salary‑only vs FFS‑only)  

At dentist level, for salary‑only dentists vs FFS‑only dentists: compare mean proportion (or rate) of each service category.

A4a. Independent samples t‑test  

Purpose  
- To compare mean proportion of each service category between salary‑only and FFS‑only dentists.  

Why appropriate  
1) Groups are independent (distinct sets of dentists).  
2) Outcome (proportion per dentist) is continuous; with n≈12 per group, central limit theorem may support approximate normality if no extreme skewness.  

Why it may not be appropriate  
- Variances of proportions between groups may be unequal; normality may not hold well with small n and proportions near 0 or 1.  

When NOT to use  
- When distributions are clearly non‑normal, or sample size is very small, or when there is extreme heteroscedasticity.  

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Welch’s t‑test (if variances unequal but approximate normality holds) or Mann–Whitney U test (see A4b).  

A4b. Mann–Whitney U test (Wilcoxon rank‑sum)  

Purpose  
- To compare the distribution (median) of per‑dentist service proportions between salary‑only and FFS‑only groups without assuming normality.  

Why appropriate  
1) Non‑parametric test suitable for independent groups with ordinal or continuous outcomes.  
2) Robust to skew and outliers; does not require homogeneity of variances.  

Why it may not be appropriate  
- Tests for differences in distribution/median rank, not directly for mean differences; interpretation may be less intuitive, and power lower than parametric tests when assumptions are met.  

When NOT to use  
- When outcome is nominal with no ordering or when there are many ties (e.g., many zero proportions) that impede test assumptions.  

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Permutation test; or transform data (e.g., logit transformation of proportion) followed by t‑test if appropriate.  

---

A5. Comparison of overall service‑mix distribution by payment method  

Here the outcome is multinomial: four categories of service per visit. Primary analysis can be at record level with dentist as cluster, or at dentist-aggregated level.

A5a. Chi‑square test of independence (at dentist‑aggregated level)  

Procedure  
- For each dentist, aggregate counts across categories.  
- Construct a contingency table of aggregated counts by payment method (salary vs FFS) and service category.  
- Use dentist as the unit (e.g., summing across dentists per group).  

Purpose  
- To test whether the distribution of service categories differs between salary and FFS overall.  

Why appropriate  
1) Service category is nominal with ≥2 levels; chi‑square is standard for association in contingency tables.  
2) Allows simultaneous assessment across all four categories rather than multiple univariate tests.  

Why it may not be appropriate  
- Ignores clustering within dentists if performed at visit level without adjustment; expected counts may be small in some cells, violating chi‑square assumptions.  

When NOT to use  
- When any expected cell counts <5 in a 2×4 table OR when observations are not independent but clustering is ignored.  

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Fisher’s exact test (if feasible) or multinomial logistic regression with dentist as random effect or robust standard errors.  

A5b. Multinomial logistic regression with robust or clustered standard errors  

Purpose  
- To model the probability of each service category as a function of payment method, adjusting for clustering by dentist.  

Why appropriate  
1) Service category is multinomial; multinomial logistic regression fits this structure naturally.  
2) Can incorporate clustering (via robust sandwich standard errors by dentist or random intercepts) and allows estimation of category‑specific odds ratios comparing FFS vs salary.  

Why it may not be appropriate  
- More complex; requires large sample size at record level and enough variation within dentists and categories. Some categories may be rare, leading to unstable estimates.  

When NOT to use  
- When some service categories are extremely rare or not observed under one payment method; when sample size is small and model does not converge.  

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Collapse categories to binary outcomes and use mixed‑effects logistic regression; or analyze proportions at dentist‑level using paired/independent tests as above.

A6. Control for clustering at dentist level in record‑level analyses  

Recommended core approach if you keep patient‑record level data.

A6a. Mixed‑effects binomial/logistic regression for specific categories  

Example: For each visit, define a binary outcome: “preventive vs other” or “aggressive vs other”.  

Purpose  
- To assess association between payment method and probability of a given service type, accounting for multiple records per dentist (random intercept for dentist).  

Why appropriate  
1) Handles hierarchical data (records nested within dentists) and non‑independence.  
2) Can estimate adjusted odds ratios for FFS vs salary for each service category; flexible to include covariates if available.  

Why it may not be appropriate  
- Requires sufficient numbers of events per dentist; convergence and estimation may be problematic if some dentists provide almost all of one type or if data are sparse.  

When NOT to use  
- When number of dentists is very small, when variation is almost entirely at dentist level, or when sample size per dentist is extremely unbalanced or low.  

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Cluster‑robust standard errors in simple logistic regression; or dentist‑level aggregated analysis as in A3–A4.

B1. Sensitivity analysis using dentist‑level rates (negative binomial / Poisson models)  

Outcome: count of services in a category per dentist per period, with offset = log(total number of visits sampled for that dentist/period).  

Test  
- Poisson or negative binomial regression with payment method (salary vs FFS) as predictor; unit = dentist‑period.  

Purpose  
- To model service rates (e.g., preventive services per 85 visits) and compare them between payment methods.  

Why appropriate  
1) Count data with exposure (total visits) is classic use for Poisson/negative binomial models.  
2) Allows direct modeling of rate ratios (FFS vs salary) and provides robust inference even when distribution is skewed.  

Why it may not be appropriate  
- Poisson assumes equidispersion; if variance > mean, negative binomial is preferred. Small number of dentist‑period units may make model unstable.  

When NOT to use  
- When number of dentist‑period observations is very small, or when many zeros or separation cause model non‑convergence.  

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Non‑parametric tests on per‑dentist rates (Mann–Whitney or signed‑rank) or generalized estimating equations (GEE) at record level.

B2. Stratified within‑dentist analysis by time/order effect  

If information on exact dates or patient flows is available, one could check whether any seasonal/time trend might confound the payment method contrast (October vs March).

Test  
- Mixed‑effects models including “calendar month/season” or “period” as covariate in addition to payment method, or treat payment method and time as aligned but discuss potential confounding qualitatively.  

Purpose  
- To ensure that observed differences in service mix are not simply due to seasonal or temporal variation.  

Why appropriate  
1) Payment methods are perfectly confounded with time windows; adjusting or at least acknowledging this is methodologically important.  
2) Even basic sensitivity (e.g., comparing with prior or subsequent months if available) can test robustness.  

Why it may not be appropriate  
- In the existing design, payment method and time are perfectly collinear; statistically, you cannot separate them unless additional time points or external data exist.  

When NOT to use  
- When no additional temporal data are available; in that case, restrict to descriptive sensitivity checks and explicit limitations.  

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Use external data or literature to contextualize potential seasonal differences; emphasize this as a limitation rather than solve statistically.

B3. Adjustment for multiple comparisons  

You will likely test multiple outcomes (four service categories, multiple group comparisons).  

Test  
- Bonferroni or Holm–Bonferroni correction; or false discovery rate (FDR) control such as Benjamini–Hochberg.  

Purpose  
- To control family‑wise error or false discovery rate when many hypotheses are tested.  

Why appropriate  
1) Multiple tests on related outcomes (e.g., each service category) inflate type I error; correction ensures more conservative inference.  
2) Particularly important in exploratory categories beyond the primary endpoint (e.g., preventive vs aggressive).  

Why it may not be appropriate  
- Can be overly conservative with small sample and correlated outcomes, leading to failure to detect real effects.  

When NOT to use  
- When a single primary outcome is pre‑specified and only a few secondary outcomes are explored; in such cases, clearly labeling exploratory analyses may suffice.  

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Report unadjusted p‑values but clearly distinguish confirmatory vs exploratory analyses; or use FDR rather than Bonferroni to retain power.

B4. Effect size estimation and confidence intervals  

Test / Procedure  
- For each comparison: compute effect sizes (mean/median differences, risk differences, odds ratios or rate ratios) with 95% confidence intervals.  

Purpose  
- To quantify magnitude and precision of associations, not just statistical significance.  

Why appropriate  
1) Clinically meaningful interpretation of how payment methods change service mix requires magnitude, not only p‑values.  
2) Confidence intervals convey both direction and uncertainty, critical for policy implications.  

Why it may not be appropriate  
- For very small samples, CIs may be very wide, but that in itself is informative; not a reason to avoid them.  

When NOT to use  
- Not applicable; effect sizes and CIs should essentially always be reported.  

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Use bootstrap methods for CIs if distribution assumptions are doubtful.

B5. Subgroup analyses by dentist group  

Test  
- Interaction terms in regression models or stratified analyses:  
  - Compare response to payment method in dentists who worked in both periods vs dentists only in one period.  

Purpose  
- To explore whether change in service mix with payment method differs between experienced (both‑period) dentists vs those hired only in one system.  

Why appropriate  
1) Dentists who chose to work in only one payment system may differ systematically from those who worked in both; subgroup analysis helps understand selection effects.  
2) Interaction terms allow formal testing for effect modification.  

Why it may not be appropriate  
- Smaller sample sizes in subgroups reduce power and may generate unstable estimates; risk of data dredging if not pre‑specified.  

When NOT to use  
- When sample size in subgroups is too small or when no a priori hypothesis about effect modification exists.  

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Descriptive comparison without formal hypothesis testing; or pool groups with caution and interpret qualitatively.

C1. Cluster‑bootstrapped confidence intervals  

Purpose  
- To obtain robust confidence intervals for key outcomes (e.g., difference in preventive proportion between payment methods) using resampling at dentist level.  

Why appropriate  
1) Accounts for clustering and small sample uncertainty without strong parametric assumptions.  
2) Useful when normality assumptions are questionable and analytic variance estimators may be unreliable.  

Why it may not be appropriate  
- Computationally heavier and requires expertise; results may be sensitive to the number of clusters (dentists).  

When NOT to use  
- When the number of dentists is too small (<20) making bootstrap unreliable; or when computation resources are minimal.  

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Analytical variance estimates with non‑parametric tests or mixed models.

C2. Ordinal regression for ordered aggressiveness  

If you treat service categories as ordered (no treatment < preventive < aggressive < highly aggressive).

Test  
- Cumulative logit (proportional odds) ordinal regression with payment method as predictor, clustered by dentist.  

Purpose  
- To model the probability of more aggressive service as a function of payment method, using all categories in a single ordered model.  

Why appropriate  
1) Categories are conceptually ordinal by aggressiveness; ordinal regression uses this information efficiently.  
2) Produces a single summary effect (odds of more aggressive treatment) rather than multiple binary models.  

Why it may not be appropriate  
- Proportional odds assumption may not hold; categories may not be strictly ordered (e.g., “no treatment” vs “preventive”).  

When NOT to use  
- When the ordering is controversial or when tests show clear violation of proportional odds assumption.  

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Multinomial logistic regression (un-ordered) or separate binary logistic models per contrast.

C3. Sensitivity analyses excluding rare categories  

Purpose  
- To test robustness of results by collapsing or excluding very rare service categories (e.g., highly aggressive surgery) and re‑running primary analyses.  

Why appropriate  
1) Rare categories can create unstable estimates; collapsing them may clarify main contrasts (e.g., preventive vs all others).  
2) Helps ensure that main conclusions are not driven by few extreme events.  

Why it may not be appropriate  
- Loss of informational detail about highly aggressive services; could mask clinically important but rare events.  

When NOT to use  
- When rare categories are central to primary hypothesis (e.g., overtreatment measured by complex/highly aggressive services).  

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Use exact tests or Bayesian methods for rare events; or report descriptive statistics only for rare categories.

TASK 3 – INAPPROPRIATE TESTS AND WHY  

1. Simple record‑level chi‑square tests or t‑tests that ignore clustering by dentist  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Violates independence assumption; each dentist contributes multiple records.  
  - Underestimates standard errors, inflates type I error, and overstates significance.  

2. One‑way ANOVA treating each record as independent observation across dentists  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Same clustering issue; patient records within a dentist are correlated.  
  - Also mismatches the question if the primary comparison is payment method, not dentist identity.  

3. Paired t‑test applied to record‑level data instead of dentist‑level aggregates  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Payment method period is not paired at visit level (patients in salary vs FFS periods are different individuals).  
  - Pairing exists at dentist level, not at visit level.  

4. Linear regression of binary outcomes without robust or clustered SEs  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - For binary outcome (e.g., preventive yes/no), linear regression violates distributional assumptions (non-normal errors, heteroscedasticity) and may yield predictions outside [0,1].  
  - Without clustering, standard errors are biased.  

5. Repeated‑measures ANOVA at dentist level for proportions if sphericity and normality are not checked  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Only two time points per dentist; paired tests/mixed models are simpler and more appropriate.  
  - RM‑ANOVA assumptions (sphericity) are not relevant for 2 levels and may mislead if misapplied.  

6. Correlation coefficients (Pearson/Spearman) between payment method (binary) and service count without group comparison  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Payment method is categorical; correlation is not the correct metric for comparing two groups; risk of misinterpretation.  

7. Logistic regression without including dentist as random effect or cluster  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Again ignores clustering; leads to overly narrow CIs and possibly spurious significance.

TASK 4 – SUMMARY OF OVERALL ANALYTICAL APPROACH  

The study aims to evaluate how dentists’ service mix changes between salary and FFS payment systems using retrospective patient records from a single clinic. The data have a clear hierarchical structure: visits nested within dentists, with a subset of dentists observed under both payment systems.

The proposed analytical strategy is:

1) Summarize service mix descriptively at both record and dentist levels.  
2) Aggregate to dentist‑period level to respect clustering and align with the conceptual unit of analysis (dentist behavior).  
3) Use paired analyses (paired t‑test or Wilcoxon signed‑rank) for dentists working in both payment systems to estimate within‑dentist changes in the proportions of preventive/aggressive services.  
4) Use independent group comparisons (t‑tests or Mann–Whitney) to compare salary‑only vs FFS‑only dentists, recognizing this is more susceptible to selection bias.  
5) Where record‑level modeling is desirable, apply mixed‑effects (multilevel) logistic or multinomial models with dentist as random effect or cluster‑robust SEs to account for non‑independence.  
6) Report effect sizes and confidence intervals and consider adjustments for multiple testing.  
7) Conduct sensitivity analyses (alternate model families, transformations, bootstrap CIs, possible clustering/time issues) to evaluate robustness and explicitly acknowledge residual confounding by calendar time.

This combination of dentist‑level paired and independent comparisons, supported by multilevel modeling, addresses the main methodological challenges (clustering, paired vs independent structures, multiple categories) and provides a rigorous framework for assessing the impact of payment method on the dental service mix.
