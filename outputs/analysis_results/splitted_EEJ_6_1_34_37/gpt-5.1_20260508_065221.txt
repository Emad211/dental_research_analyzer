CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS  
-----------------------------  

A1. Descriptive statistics and baseline comparability  

Test / Approach:  
- Descriptive statistics (means, SDs, medians, IQRs) for continuous variables; counts and percentages for categorical variables.  
- Baseline group comparisons:  
  - Independent samples t-test or Mann–Whitney U test for continuous baseline variables (e.g., age, baseline VAS).  
  - Chi-square test or Fisher’s exact test for categorical variables (e.g., sex, tooth location, anesthesia type).  

Purpose:  
- Ensure groups are comparable at baseline after randomization and to describe the sample.  
- Identify potential imbalances that may warrant covariate adjustment.  

Why appropriate (at least 2 reasons):  
1) Randomized parallel-group design with independent groups: standard to check baseline balance.  
2) Age, baseline VAS, and similar variables are continuous; t-test / Mann–Whitney U are appropriate for comparing means/medians between two independent groups.  
3) Categorical baseline characteristics (e.g., sex, jaw, tooth) are nominal; chi-square/Fisher’s exact is appropriate for group proportions.  

Why it may not be appropriate (1 reason):  
- If sample sizes are very small in particular categories (e.g., very few mandibular teeth) chi-square assumptions (expected counts ≥5) may be violated.  

When this test should NOT be used:  
- t-test: not when data are heavily skewed with very unequal variances and small sample size, or when outcome is ordinal with very few levels.  
- Chi-square: not when expected cell counts are too low (<5) consistently.  

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- For continuous baseline variables: use nonparametric Mann–Whitney U; report medians.  
- For categorical baseline variables with sparse cells: use Fisher’s exact test.  

A2. Primary analysis: Comparison of postoperative pain between groups at each time point  

Two possible primary approaches; one should be pre-specified. Given the design, I recommend a mixed model (A3) as the primary; however, since many dental papers use separate tests at each time point, this substep describes that approach. If mixed model is used as primary, this becomes secondary.  

Test / Approach:  
- For each time point (24 h, 48 h, 72 h, 1 week), compare VAS between NaOCl gel vs solution:  
  - Independent samples t-test (if approximate normality and equal variances).  
  - Mann–Whitney U test (if non-normal or ordinal treatment of VAS).  

Purpose:  
- Quantify and test differences in postoperative pain between groups at clinically relevant time points, especially day 1 (primary time point per conclusion).  

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):  
1) Two independent groups at each time point; t-test / Mann–Whitney directly address between-group differences.  
2) VAS is often treated as a continuous outcome; with n≈30 per group, t-test is robust to moderate non-normality.  
3) Clinically, specific time points (particularly 24 h) are of primary interest; multiple time-point comparisons align with clinical questions.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- It ignores within-subject correlation over time and the longitudinal nature of data, and inflates type I error due to multiple testing if unadjusted.  

When this test should NOT be used:  
- t-test: when VAS distributions are severely skewed with outliers and small group size, or when variances differ greatly (especially if n is unequal).  
- Mann–Whitney U: when many ties and highly discrete data (e.g., patients use only a few categories).  

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- Use Mann–Whitney U instead of t-test if normality/equal variance are suspect.  
- Use robust t-tests (e.g., Welch t-test) if variances differ.  
- Preferably use a repeated measures approach (A3) which models all observations jointly.  

A3. Primary analysis: Longitudinal comparison of pain trajectories (Mixed-effects model or Repeated Measures ANOVA)  

Preferred primary analysis:  
- Linear mixed-effects model (LMM) with:  
  - Outcome: VAS at each time point.  
  - Fixed effects: group (gel vs solution), time (categorical: 24 h, 48 h, 72 h, 1 week), and group × time interaction.  
  - Random effect: subject-level random intercept.  
  - Optional: adjust for baseline VAS, age, or other significant baseline imbalances (ANCOVA mixed model).  

Alternative (less robust but common):  
- Repeated-measures ANOVA with within-subject factor time (4 levels) and between-subject factor group (2 levels), including group × time interaction.  

Purpose:  
- Assess whether postoperative pain over time differs between NaOCl gel and solution, accounting for within-subject correlation and multiple time points.  
- Provide estimates and tests of overall group effect, time effect, and group × time interaction (i.e., does pain reduction over time differ by group?).  

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):  
1) Longitudinal outcome structure: same individuals measured at multiple time points; mixed model / repeated-measures ANOVA explicitly model within-subject correlation.  
2) Increases efficiency by using all repeated measurements, improves power relative to separate tests.  
3) Accounts for missing data under reasonable assumptions (LMM is more robust to missing at random than ANOVA).  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Repeated-measures ANOVA requires complete cases (or ad hoc imputation) and sphericity; these assumptions may not hold.  
- LMM assumes linearity and normally distributed residuals; severe deviation may limit interpretability.  

When this test should NOT be used:  
- Repeated measures ANOVA: not appropriate when missing data patterns are non-trivial (dropouts or missing time points) or sphericity is clearly violated and corrections (Greenhouse-Geisser, Huynh-Feldt) are not used.  
- Mixed model: not if there are too few time points and too small sample size for model convergence, or if data are heavily non-normal and transformations are not feasible.  

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- For non-normal continuous outcomes:  
  - Transform VAS (e.g., log, square-root) and re-fit LMM.  
  - Use generalized linear mixed model (GLMM) with appropriate distribution (e.g., beta regression if VAS scaled 0–1 and truncated, or robust methods).  
- For ordinal treatment of VAS:  
  - Use cumulative link mixed models (ordinal mixed models).  
- Nonparametric longitudinal methods (e.g., rank-based repeated measures tests) if necessary, though less widely used.  

A4. Secondary outcome: Analgesic use between groups  

Test / Approach:  
- Binary analgesic use (yes/no) at specific time windows (e.g., any analgesic within first 24 h; or over 1 week):  
  - Chi-square test or Fisher’s exact test for between-group comparison.  
- If count outcome (number of tablets over 1 week):  
  - Independent samples t-test (if roughly normal) or Mann–Whitney U (if skewed).  
  - Alternatively, Poisson or negative binomial regression for counts.  

Purpose:  
- Assess whether NaOCl gel reduces the need for analgesics compared with solution, supporting clinical relevance.  

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):  
1) Analgesic use is categorical (yes/no); chi-square / Fisher’s exact are standard to compare proportions between independent groups.  
2) For counts, t-test/Mann–Whitney compare central tendency; regression models can account for dispersion.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Very low event rates may lead to small expected counts, invalidating chi-square approximations.  
- Self-reported analgesic use may be misclassified and variable.  

When this test should NOT be used:  
- Chi-square: not when >20% of expected cells <5; use Fisher’s instead.  
- t-test: not when counts are extremely skewed with many zeros.  

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- Use Fisher’s exact test for binary analgesic use.  
- Use nonparametric Mann–Whitney U for counts.  
- Use exact logistic regression if sample or event numbers are very small.  

A5. Secondary outcome: Unscheduled appointment rates  

Test / Approach:  
- Compare proportion of patients with at least one unscheduled appointment between groups using chi-square or Fisher’s exact test.  

Purpose:  
- Evaluate whether NaOCl gel reduces clinically significant episodes of pain or complications requiring extra visits.  

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):  
1) Binary outcome; chi-square/Fisher’s are standard for independent group comparisons.  
2) Clinically important safety/outcome measure, aligns with study aims on postoperative pain.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Very low event rates could lead to unstable estimates or unusable chi-square.  

When this test should NOT be used:

Alternative if assumptions violated:

Alternative if assumptions violated:

Test / Approach:

Purpose:  
- Control the family-wise error rate or clearly distinguish primary vs exploratory analyses.  

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):  
1) Repeated testing across four time points inflates type I error; adjustment preserves overall error rate.  
2) Protects against spurious significant findings at later time points.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Bonferroni-type corrections can be conservative, reducing power, especially in small samples.  

When this should NOT be used:  
- If a mixed-model with a global interaction test is the primary analytic tool; individual time points can be interpreted as part of model estimation.  

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- Use false discovery rate (FDR) procedures (e.g., Benjamini–Hochberg) as a less conservative alternative.

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- In small samples, formal tests of normality have low power; overreliance may lead to unnecessary nonparametric analyses.  

When this should NOT be used:  
- Strictly speaking, it can always be used, but results should be interpreted cautiously in small samples.  

Alternative if assumptions violated:

TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS  
============================  

List of tests that should NOT be used for this study, with reasons:  

1. Paired t-test to compare groups  
- Reason: Paired t-test assumes paired observations (e.g., same subjects under two conditions or matched pairs). Here, groups are independent (different patients), so paired t-test is invalid and inflates type I error or misestimates variance.  

2. One-way ANOVA for more than two independent groups  
- Reason: Only two treatment groups (NaOCl gel vs solution). Using one-way ANOVA would be redundant and complicate interpretation; t-test (or equivalent) is sufficient and simpler.  

3. Repeated-measures ANOVA treating group as within-subject factor  
- Reason: Group (gel vs solution) is a between-subject factor; treating it as within-subject would wrongly assume each subject receives both treatments (crossover design).  

4. Pearson correlation to assess treatment effect  
- Reason: Treatment is a categorical variable; correlation coefficient is inappropriate for comparing treatment effects and does not correctly capture group differences.  

5. Simple pre-post within-group tests only (e.g., paired t-test within each group without between-group comparison)  
- Reason: This would show pain reduction within groups but not directly address the primary question: whether groups differ from each other. Between-group comparisons are essential in randomized trials.  

6. Chi-square test for trend on VAS treated as ordinal categories without justification  
- Reason: VAS is a continuous measure; arbitrarily categorizing into multiple levels for trend tests wastes information and reduces power.  

7. Non-parametric tests that ignore repeated measures (e.g., Kruskal–Wallis on pooled data across time)  
- Reason: Would ignore within-subject correlation and time structure, violating data hierarchy and misrepresenting variance.  

8. Multiple unadjusted post hoc tests without clear primary endpoint  
- Reason: Performing many unplanned comparisons (e.g., multiple subgroup and time-point tests) without correction inflates type I error and undermines credibility.
