- **Design:** Prospective, parallel-group, randomized controlled clinical trial; **patient-blinded** (single-blind).  
- **Objective:** Compare **postoperative pain** after root canal treatment when using **5.25% NaOCl gel** vs **5.25% NaOCl solution** during instrumentation.
- **Null hypothesis:** No difference between NaOCl gel and solution groups in postoperative pain.

### Setting/ethics
- **Ethics approval:** Faculty of Dentistry, University of A; decision no. **2017-91**.
- **Consent:** Written informed consent obtained.

### Participants
- **Target population:** Healthy adults (ASA I) with **molar teeth** diagnosed with:
  - **Periapical diagnosis:** symptomatic apical periodontitis
  - **Pulpal diagnosis:** symptomatic irreversible pulpitis
  - **Radiographs:** **no radiolucency**; only healthy/widened PDL included
  - **Baseline pain:** **>50 mm** on **10 cm VAS**
- **Age:** included 18–65 years (excluded <18 or >65).
- **Exclusions (tooth/patient):** previous RCT, root fracture, swelling, pathological mobility, periodontal pocket depth >3 mm; antibiotics or analgesics within 1 month prior; radiographic lesions.

### Diagnostic/clinical measurements at baseline
- **Sensibility tests:** Electric pulp tester (Digitest) and cold test (Endo Frost).
- **Percussion test:** finger and instrument.

### Randomization/blinding/sample size

- **Planned/enrolled:** **30 per group** (60 total) to increase power/offset attrition.
- **Randomization method:** web program (randomizer.org).
- **Blinding:** patients blinded to allocation; operator blinding not stated (likely not).

### Interventions (groups)
All teeth received standardized RCT steps; **only irrigation form differs during preparation**.

# CATEGORY A – MANDATORY (core analyses)

## A1) Linear Mixed-Effects Model (LMM) for repeated VAS
- **Purpose:** Compare postoperative pain trajectories between groups across time (24h, 48h, 72h, 1 week).
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. **Repeated measures** on the same patient require modeling within-subject correlation.
  2. Handles **unequal variances/correlation** over time and can include **group × time interaction** to test whether differences occur at specific times (e.g., day 1).
  3. Uses all available data under MAR, reducing bias vs complete-case repeated-measures ANOVA.
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - If VAS distribution is extremely non-normal with heavy floor effects (many zeros), LMM residual assumptions may be poor.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If outcome is not reasonably continuous (e.g., pain recorded only as categories), or there is severe zero-inflation not addressable by transformation/modeling.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Generalized linear mixed model (GLMM)** (e.g., Gamma with log link, or Tweedie) or **rank-based mixed model**; alternatively **GEE with robust SE**.

**Recommended LMM specification**
- Fixed effects: group (gel/solution), time (categorical), group×time.
- Random effects: subject-specific intercept (± random slope for time if supported).
- Primary estimand: adjusted mean difference (gel–solution) at **24h** (day 1), plus differences at other time points.
- Multiplicity: adjust for multiple time-point contrasts (see B-category).

---

## A2) Model-based post-hoc contrasts (estimated marginal means) for between-group differences at each time point
- **Purpose:** Quantify and test group differences at 24h/48h/72h/1 week.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Directly addresses the clinical question (“less pain on day 1”) with **time-specific estimates**.
  2. Uses the fitted repeated-measures model, maintaining proper covariance handling.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - If interaction is not included or model is misspecified, time-specific contrasts can be misleading.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When there is no pre-specified plan and many unstructured comparisons are made post hoc without multiplicity control.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Same contrasts derived from **GLMM/GEE** or **nonparametric longitudinal methods**.

---

## A3) Effect size estimation with confidence intervals
- **Purpose:** Provide clinical magnitude (not just p-values): e.g., mean difference in VAS (mm) with 95% CI at each time point; optionally standardized effect size.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Pain differences need **clinical interpretation**; CIs show precision.
  2. Recommended by CONSORT; supports decision-making beyond significance.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Standardized effect sizes can be unstable in small samples and depend on SD choice.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Do not report only standardized effects without raw VAS differences.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Report **median differences** with bootstrap CIs, or model-based CIs from robust methods.

# CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED (strengthen rigor)

## B1) Multiplicity adjustment for multiple time-point comparisons
- **Test/procedure:** Holm (family-wise) or Benjamini–Hochberg (FDR) applied to the set of time-point contrasts.
- **Purpose:** Control false positives across several hypothesis tests (24h, 48h, 72h, 1 week).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Multiple contrasts inflate type I error.
  2. Maintains interpretability when highlighting “day 1” while still testing others.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - If only one primary time point was truly pre-specified (e.g., 24h) and others are secondary/exploratory.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Don’t apply overly conservative corrections to a single pre-specified primary test.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Pre-specify a **single primary endpoint** (e.g., 24h VAS) and treat other times as secondary with hierarchical testing.

---

## B2) Sensitivity analysis: GEE (robust sandwich SE) for repeated VAS
- **Purpose:** Confirm inference robustness to correlation structure/residual distribution assumptions.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Provides **population-averaged** estimates with **robust SE** even if covariance is misspecified.
  2. Useful when LMM residual normality is doubtful.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Less efficient in small samples; requires enough subjects for robust SE reliability.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Very small samples with sparse data and high missingness can yield unstable sandwich SE.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Bootstrap SE for LMM/GLMM; or Bayesian mixed models with appropriate likelihood.

---

## B3) Analgesic intake comparison (binary/ordinal/count depending on data recorded)
- **Test/model (choose based on actual recording):**
  - If **any analgesic use yes/no** over follow-up: **logistic regression** (or mixed logistic if measured per time point).
  - If **count of tablets/doses**: **negative binomial regression** (or Poisson if equidispersion).
- **Purpose:** Assess whether NaOCl form affects rescue medication use.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Analgesic use is clinically meaningful and related to pain.
  2. Appropriate regression aligns with distribution (binary/count) and can adjust for covariates.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - If analgesic data were poorly standardized (different drugs/doses) and not comparable.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Don’t analyze “dose” as continuous mg without harmonizing potency (e.g., ibuprofen vs paracetamol).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Use **nonparametric tests** (Mann–Whitney) for ordinal categories; or convert to **standardized equivalents** before modeling.

---

## B4) Unscheduled appointment (yes/no) comparison
- **Test/model:** Fisher’s exact test (or logistic regression if events adequate).
- **Purpose:** Compare complication/flare-up proxies between groups.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Outcome is binary; likely **rare events**, favoring Fisher’s exact.
  2. Clinically important safety/quality indicator.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Very low event counts yield wide CIs; may be underpowered.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Don’t use chi-square if expected cell counts are small.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Exact logistic regression, or report risk difference with exact CI.

---

## B5) Baseline covariate-adjusted primary model (ANCOVA-style within LMM)
- **Purpose:** Improve precision and address any chance imbalance (e.g., baseline VAS, age, tooth location maxillary/mandibular).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Baseline pain varies and is prognostic; adjustment increases power/precision.
  2. Randomization does not guarantee balance in small samples.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Overfitting risk with many covariates (n=60).
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Do not add many post-randomization variables (mediators) that could bias estimates.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Penalized regression (rarely needed here) or parsimonious pre-specified covariate set.

## TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS (should NOT be used)

1. **Independent t-tests at each time point without accounting for repeated measures**
   - Ignores within-subject correlation and inflates type I error; also multiplies tests.

2. **Repeated-measures ANOVA (classic) as the primary analysis**
   - Requires stronger assumptions (sphericity, complete data) and handles missingness poorly; less flexible than mixed models.

3. **Chi-square test for rare binary outcomes (unscheduled appointments)**
   - Expected cell counts may be <5; Fisher’s exact is preferable.

4. **Simple “change score” analysis using baseline-to-24h only (if baseline is pre-op) as the sole endpoint**
   - Discards later time points and does not reflect the planned longitudinal outcome structure.

5. **Pearson correlation between group (coded 0/1) and VAS**
   - Not appropriate framing; does not model repeated measures and provides limited inferential structure.

Secondary binary/count outcomes (analgesic intake, unscheduled visits) should be analyzed with **appropriate generalized models** (logistic/negative binomial or exact methods), and robustness should be checked via **GEE/sensitivity analyses** and assumption diagnostics.

TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS  
============================  

List of tests that should NOT be used for this study, with reasons:  

1. Paired t-test to compare groups  
- Reason: Paired t-test assumes paired observations (e.g., same subjects under two conditions or matched pairs). Here, groups are independent (different patients), so paired t-test is invalid and inflates type I error or misestimates variance.  

2. One-way ANOVA for more than two independent groups  
- Reason: Only two treatment groups (NaOCl gel vs solution). Using one-way ANOVA would be redundant and complicate interpretation; t-test (or equivalent) is sufficient and simpler.  

3. Repeated-measures ANOVA treating group as within-subject factor  
- Reason: Group (gel vs solution) is a between-subject factor; treating it as within-subject would wrongly assume each subject receives both treatments (crossover design).  

4. Pearson correlation to assess treatment effect  
- Reason: Treatment is a categorical variable; correlation coefficient is inappropriate for comparing treatment effects and does not correctly capture group differences.  

5. Simple pre-post within-group tests only (e.g., paired t-test within each group without between-group comparison)  
- Reason: This would show pain reduction within groups but not directly address the primary question: whether groups differ from each other. Between-group comparisons are essential in randomized trials.  

6. Chi-square test for trend on VAS treated as ordinal categories without justification  
- Reason: VAS is a continuous measure; arbitrarily categorizing into multiple levels for trend tests wastes information and reduces power.  

7. Non-parametric tests that ignore repeated measures (e.g., Kruskal–Wallis on pooled data across time)  
- Reason: Would ignore within-subject correlation and time structure, violating data hierarchy and misrepresenting variance.  

8. Multiple unadjusted post hoc tests without clear primary endpoint  
- Reason: Performing many unplanned comparisons (e.g., multiple subgroup and time-point tests) without correction inflates type I error and undermines credibility.
