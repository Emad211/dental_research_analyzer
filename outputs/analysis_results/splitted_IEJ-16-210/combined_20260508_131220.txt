## TASK 1 — INFORMATION EXTRACTION (study characteristics)

### Study design & setting
- **Design:** Double-blind, randomized, parallel-group clinical trial.
- **Setting:** Isfahan Dental School (Iran).
- **Registration/ethics:** IR.MUI.REC.1396.3.272; IRCT20201031049199N1; CONSORT followed.
- **Randomization method:** Endodontist rolled a **die** immediately before treatment (odd → Cartridge 1; even → Cartridge 2). Investigators/patients blinded to contents. (Note: allocation concealment is weak; authors acknowledge selection-bias risk.)

### Objective
- **Primary objective:** Determine whether adding **clonidine** to **4% articaine + 1:100,000 epinephrine** for **inferior alveolar nerve block (IANB)** reduces **post-treatment pain** after single-visit root canal therapy in **symptomatic irreversible pulpitis**.

### Participants
- **Population:** Patients age **15–60**, **ASA I** (healthy), with **symptomatic irreversible pulpitis** in a **mandibular posterior tooth**.
- **Key exclusions:** diabetes, hypertension, cardiovascular disease; contraindication to clonidine/articaine/epinephrine; pregnancy/lactation; drug abuse; β-blocker use; periodontal problems/active infection/inflammation; no response to cold test; radiographic pathology other than widened PDL; pulpal necrosis/partial necrosis at access; multiple dental treatments needed; opioid use week prior or 72h after; noncooperation in pain assessment; block failure requiring additional injections; change in treatment plan.
- **Diagnosis confirmation:** Cold test with Endo Ice applied up to 15s; **exaggerated reaction and lingering >10s** recorded; radiographic evaluation.

### Groups (interventions)
- **Intervention (Case; Cartridge No.1):**
  - IANB with **1.8 mL 4% articaine + epi 1:100,000** + **0.2 mL clonidine** (Catapressan 150 µg/mL)  
  - Total volume **2.0 mL**; clonidine dose = **30 µg**.
- **Control (Cartridge No.2):**
  - Same articaine/epi + **0.2 mL distilled water** (total 2.0 mL).

### Procedures / standardization
- **Topical:** 20% benzocaine gel for 1 minute.
- **IANB technique:** standard; 27-gauge 1.5-inch needle; aspirate; inject over 2 min.
- **Block success criterion:** **profound lip numbness within 15 min**; otherwise considered failed and **excluded**.
- **Endodontic treatment:** single-visit, RaCe rotary system; temporary eugenol dressing; occlusion checked.

### Outcomes & measurements

#### Primary outcome (as described)
- **Post-operative pain intensity** measured by **170-mm Heft-Parker VAS (HP-VAS)**.
- **Time points:** baseline (pre-treatment questionnaire includes pain intensity) and follow-ups at **6, 12, 24, 48, 72 hours** via phone/text.
- **Pain categories (ordinal cutoffs):**
  - 0 = no pain
  - >0 to ≤54 = mild
  - >54 to <114 = moderate
  - ≥114 = severe

#### Secondary/safety outcomes
- **Hemodynamics:** **blood pressure and heart rate** measured **pre-injection** and **5 min post-injection**.
- **Adverse symptoms** at follow-ups: nausea, vomiting, drowsiness, dizziness, headaches.
- **Rescue analgesic use:** instructed ibuprofen up to **400 mg** for emergency pain.
  - Protocol deviation: those who consumed analgesic post-op were **excluded from analysis** (explicitly: 1 person in clonidine group).

- **Follow-up issues:** mentions **lost to follow-up (n=1)** and **discontinued (n=1) for not profound lip numbness** in each group (wording suggests per group; totals unclear from provided excerpt).

## TASK 2 — STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

### General SAP principles (apply to all analyses)
- **Analysis population (recommended):**
  - **Intention-to-treat (ITT):** all randomized participants analyzed in assigned groups.
  - **Per-protocol (PP):** exclude major protocol deviations (e.g., anesthesia failure requiring extra injection), but *do not* exclude rescue analgesic users—handle analytically.
- **Primary estimand:** difference in post-treatment pain trajectory (HP-VAS) over 72h between groups.
- **Multiplicity:** multiple time points → control family-wise error or use a single global test from a repeated-measures model plus pre-specified contrasts.
- **Missing data:** phone follow-ups may be missing; use mixed models (valid under MAR) and/or multiple imputation; avoid complete-case-only.
- **Rescue analgesics:** treat as an outcome and/or incorporate in sensitivity analyses (e.g., censoring/penalized pain scores); excluding users biases toward benefit.

---

### CATEGORY A — MANDATORY tests/models

#### A1) Linear Mixed-Effects Model (LMM) for repeated HP-VAS (continuous)
- **Purpose:** Compare **pain over time** between groups while accounting for within-subject correlation and baseline pain.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Outcome measured repeatedly (6–72h); LMM directly models correlated observations.
  2. Can include **group, time, and group×time interaction** to test differential pain trajectories.
  3. Handles **incomplete follow-up** under MAR without discarding subjects.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - HP-VAS may be **non-normal** and bounded (0–170), with skew/zero inflation.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If residual diagnostics show severe non-normality/heteroscedasticity that cannot be remedied, or if pain is primarily ordinal with heavy heaping.
- **Alternatives if assumptions violated:**
  - **Generalized linear mixed model (GLMM)** with appropriate distribution (e.g., **Gamma** with log link; **Tweedie**), or
  - **Rank-based mixed model / nonparametric longitudinal methods**, or
  - Analyze **ordinal pain categories** with an ordinal mixed model (see B2).

*Model specification (minimum):*  
HP-VAS ~ group + time (categorical) + group×time + baseline pain + (random intercept for participant). Consider random slope for time if supported.

#### A2) Baseline comparability/descriptive inference for key covariates
- **Test(s):**  
  - **Two-sample t-test** (or **Wilcoxon rank-sum**) for continuous baseline variables (e.g., age, baseline pain).  
  - **Chi-square test** (or **Fisher’s exact**) for categorical baseline variables (e.g., sex).
- **Purpose:** Describe balance and identify variables for adjusted analyses.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. RCT expects balance; reporting is standard and informs adjustment decisions.
  2. Identifies chance imbalances that may affect precision.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Significance testing of baseline can be misleading; emphasis should be on **standardized differences**.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Do not use p-values to decide “randomization failed” or to gatekeep covariates mechanically.
- **Alternatives if assumptions violated:**
  - For continuous: **Wilcoxon rank-sum**; for categorical with small counts: **Fisher’s exact**; use **standardized mean differences (SMDs)** regardless.

#### A3) Analysis of BP and HR change (safety/physiologic effect)
- **Test/model:** **ANCOVA or LMM** on post value adjusting for baseline (or analyze change score with two-sample t-test).
- **Purpose:** Evaluate whether clonidine affects hemodynamics shortly after injection.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Continuous outcomes measured twice; baseline-adjusted post values improve precision.
  2. Directly addresses safety concern (hypotension/bradycardia).
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - If measurements are highly non-normal or contain outliers due to device error.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If there are multiple post measurements requiring a different repeated-measures structure (not the case here).
- **Alternatives if assumptions violated:**
  - **Robust regression** or **Wilcoxon** on change scores; or **quantile regression**.

### CATEGORY B — RECOMMENDED tests/models

#### B1) Multiple-comparisons controlled contrasts at each time point (from LMM)
- **Test:** Pre-specified **estimated marginal means** (EMMs) comparing groups at 6/12/24/48/72h with **Holm** (or Benjamini–Hochberg if controlling FDR).
- **Purpose:** Identify *when* differences occur while controlling multiplicity.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Clinical interpretability at each follow-up time.
  2. Uses model-based estimates with correct SEs under correlation structure.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Overemphasis on multiple timepoint p-values can distract from overall effect.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If timepoints were not pre-specified or study not powered for time-specific comparisons.
- **Alternatives if assumptions violated:**
  - Do contrasts from **GLMM** or **nonparametric longitudinal model**.

#### B2) Ordinal mixed model for pain categories (no/mild/moderate/severe)
- **Test/model:** **Cumulative logit mixed model** (proportional odds) with random intercept; group, time, group×time, baseline category.
- **Purpose:** Analyze clinically meaningful pain severity levels.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Pain categories are **ordered** and may be more robust to VAS skew.
  2. Provides **odds ratios** for being in a lower pain category.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Proportional odds assumption may be violated; categories may be sparse at later times.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If category counts are too small or assumption grossly violated without remedy.
- **Alternatives if assumptions violated:**
  - **Partial proportional odds model**, **adjacent-category model**, or collapse to binary (e.g., moderate/severe vs none/mild) with **logistic mixed model**.

#### B3) Rescue analgesic use analysis
- **Test/model options (depending on data):**
  - **Logistic regression** (use vs no use within 72h), or
  - **Time-to-event (Cox proportional hazards)** for time to first ibuprofen.
- **Purpose:** Quantify clinically important failure of pain control.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Avoids biased practice of excluding analgesic users.
  2. Captures a patient-centered endpoint; complements VAS.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Very low event count (appears rare) → unstable estimates.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If analgesic data are incomplete/unreliable or near-zero events.
- **Alternatives if assumptions violated:**
  - **Firth penalized logistic regression** (small events), or **exact logistic regression**; for Cox violations, use **restricted mean survival time**.

#### B4) Adverse-event comparison
- **Test:** **Fisher’s exact** (preferred if low counts) for each AE; optionally a composite “any AE”.
- **Purpose:** Compare safety symptom rates between groups.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Binary outcomes with potentially small cell counts.
  2. Directly addresses stated adverse effects list.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Multiple AE endpoints → multiplicity; may be underpowered.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If events are extremely rare and inferential testing is uninterpretable; then report descriptively.
- **Alternatives if assumptions violated:**
  - Exact methods; or descriptive with confidence intervals only.

### CATEGORY C — OPTIONAL (supplementary insight)

#### C1) Area Under the Curve (AUC) for pain over 0–72h
- **Test:** Compare group AUC using **t-test/ANCOVA** (or **Wilcoxon**) and/or model-based AUC from LMM.
- **Purpose:** Provide a single summary of total pain burden.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Clinically intuitive “overall pain experience”.
  2. Reduces multiplicity vs many timepoint comparisons.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Requires handling missing timepoints carefully; assumes time grid comparable.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If follow-up times are highly inconsistent or missingness is heavy/non-ignorable.
- **Alternatives if assumptions violated:**
  - Model-based AUC from mixed model; multiple imputation before AUC.

#### C2) Effect size reporting
- **Method:** **Mean difference** with 95% CI at each time; **standardized mean difference (Hedges g)**; for ordinal model, **common OR**.
- **Purpose:** Quantify magnitude beyond p-values.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Enables clinical interpretation and meta-analysis.
  2. Supports CONSORT-style reporting.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - SMD can be less interpretable clinically than raw mm difference.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Don’t rely solely on SMD without raw scale estimates.
- **Alternatives if assumptions violated:**
  - Use **median difference** or **Hodges–Lehmann** estimate if nonparametric.

#### C3) Sensitivity analyses for missing data and rescue medication
- **Tests/models:** Pattern-mixture sensitivity; “worst-case” imputation; tipping-point.
- **Purpose:** Assess robustness to departures from MAR and to analgesic effects.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Phone follow-ups are prone to missingness.
  2. Rescue analgesics can mask pain scores.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Requires additional assumptions; may be complex for small samples.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If data are nearly complete and analgesic use is negligible; then keep simple.
- **Alternatives if assumptions violated:**
  - Report both ITT primary mixed model and PP as pragmatic sensitivity.

## TASK 3 — INAPPROPRIATE TESTS (should NOT be used)

1. **Multiple independent t-tests at each follow-up time without adjustment**
   - Ignores within-subject correlation and inflates Type I error (5 timepoints).
2. **Paired t-test within each group (pre vs each post) as main inference**
   - Answers a different question (change over time within group), not treatment effect; also multiplicity.
3. **One-way ANOVA treating repeated measures as independent**
   - Violates independence assumption; incorrect SEs/p-values.
4. **Complete-case analysis only (dropping missing follow-ups) as primary**
   - Biased if missingness related to pain; wastes randomized information.
5. **Excluding participants who used rescue analgesics (as done in article)**
   - Post-randomization exclusion induces bias; analgesic use is likely related to outcome.
6. **Simple chi-square test on pain categories pooled across all times**
   - Discards timing and correlation; may be misleading.
7. **Pearson correlation between treatment group and pain**
   - Treatment is binary; correlation is not the right framework and is less interpretable than regression.

## TASK 4 — SUMMARY (overall analytical approach)

A two-arm randomized, double-blind trial with pain measured repeatedly over 72 hours requires a **longitudinal treatment-effect model**. The core analysis should use a **mixed-effects framework** (continuous HP-VAS and/or ordinal categories) with **group×time** to estimate differential pain trajectories, while appropriately handling **within-subject correlation** and **incomplete follow-up**. Safety outcomes (BP/HR and adverse symptoms) should be analyzed with baseline-adjusted models and exact methods where counts are small. Rescue analgesic use should be **analyzed, not excluded**, with sensitivity analyses to ensure robustness.

### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS
1. **Test Name**: Independent Samples t-test (or Mann-Whitney U test if non-normal distribution).
   - **Purpose**: Compare mean post-operative pain scores between the case and control groups at each time point.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     - The study involves two independent groups (case and control).
     - Pain scores are continuous variables suitable for parametric or non-parametric analysis.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:
     - Assumes normality and homogeneity of variance; may be violated.
   - **When NOT to Use**: If pain scores are heavily skewed or sample sizes are unequal.
   - **Alternative**: Mann-Whitney U test for non-parametric data.

2. **Test Name**: Repeated Measures ANOVA (or Friedman test if non-normal distribution).
   - **Purpose**: Assess changes in pain scores over time within each group.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     - Pain scores are measured at multiple time points (6, 12, 24, 48, and 72 hours).
     - Suitable for within-group comparisons of continuous data.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:
     - Assumes sphericity; may be violated.
   - **When NOT to Use**: If data are non-normal or sphericity assumption fails.
   - **Alternative**: Friedman test for non-parametric data.

3. **Test Name**: Chi-Square Test (or Fisher’s Exact Test for small sample sizes).
   - **Purpose**: Compare categorical variables (e.g., adverse effects) between groups.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     - Adverse effects are categorical (presence/absence).
     - Suitable for comparing proportions between independent groups.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:
     - Small sample sizes may lead to inaccurate results.
   - **When NOT to Use**: If expected cell counts are <5.
   - **Alternative**: Fisher’s Exact Test.

1. **Test Name**: Mixed-Effects Model.
   - **Purpose**: Analyze pain scores over time while accounting for repeated measures and group effects.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     - Accounts for both within-subject (time) and between-subject (group) variability.
     - Handles missing data effectively.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:
     - Requires advanced statistical software and expertise.
   - **When NOT to Use**: If data collection is incomplete or assumptions of linearity fail.

   - **Purpose**: Assess predictors of adverse effects (binary outcome).

   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:
     - Requires large sample size for reliable estimates.
   - **When NOT to Use**: If adverse effects are rare (<10% prevalence).
   - **Alternative**: Exact Logistic Regression.

   - **Why Appropriate**:
     - Provides deeper insights into potential effect modifiers.
     - Useful for hypothesis generation.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:
     - Reduces statistical power due to smaller sample sizes.
   - **When NOT to Use**: If subgroup sizes are too small (<10 per group).
   - **Alternative**: Pooling subgroups or meta-regression.

TASK 2 – STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN  
==================================  

General conventions  
- Two‑sided α = 0.05.  
- Report effect sizes with 95% confidence intervals.  
- Check distributional assumptions using plots (histograms/QQ plots) and tests (e.g., Shapiro‑Wilk) for key continuous variables.  
- Handle missing data explicitly: describe extent/pattern and use appropriate methods (e.g., mixed models or sensitivity analyses).  

Define analysis populations  
- Intention‑to‑treat (ITT): all randomized patients who received any portion of assigned IANB, analyzed by randomized group, regardless of block success, analgesic use, or protocol deviations.  
- Per‑protocol (PP): patients with successful IANB (profound lip numbness), completed single‑visit treatment, provided pain data, and did not take rescue analgesics.  
- Primary efficacy analysis for pain should ideally be ITT using a model that can handle missing repeated measures (e.g., mixed model); PP as sensitivity.  

Primary endpoint  
- Main endpoint: severity of post‑operative pain over time (HP VAS) from 6 to 72 hours post‑treatment, comparing clonidine vs control.  

Secondary endpoints  
- Pain at each time point separately.  
- Proportion of patients with moderate/severe pain at each time point.  
- Time course of pain reduction from baseline.  
- Analgesic consumption (yes/no, and if available number of doses).  
- Adverse events (each type separately, and combined occurrence).  
- Hemodynamic changes from baseline to 5 min post anesthesia.  

CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS  
-----------------------------  

1. Descriptive statistics and baseline comparability  

Test / approach  
- Descriptive statistics; comparison of baseline characteristics.  

Purpose  
- Summarize the sample and assess whether randomization achieved comparable groups at baseline.  

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1. RCTs should document baseline comparability to interpret treatment effects credibly.  
2. Helps identify potential confounders or imbalances that may require adjustment (e.g., baseline pain).  

Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)  
- Formal hypothesis testing of baseline variables can be misleading in small–moderate samples (statistically non‑significant differences can still be clinically relevant; conversely, significant differences may be due to chance in large samples).  

When NOT to use  
- Avoid over‑interpreting multiple hypothesis tests on baseline variables as evidence for or against successful randomization.  

Alternatives if assumptions violated  
- Replace significance testing with standardized differences (e.g., absolute standardized mean difference, SMD) and descriptive comparison.  

Implementation  
- Continuous variables (age, baseline VAS, SBP/DBP/HR): mean ± SD (or median, IQR).  
- Categorical variables (sex, tooth type, etc.): counts and percentages.  
- For formal comparison (optional):  
  - t‑test vs Mann–Whitney U for continuous.  
  - Chi‑square vs Fisher’s exact for categorical.  

2. Primary analysis of pain over time (continuous VAS)  

Preferred Test  
- Linear mixed‑effects model (LMM) for repeated measures.  

Purpose  
- To compare the trajectory of pain scores over time between clonidine and control, accounting for within‑patient correlation.  

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1. Pain measurements are repeated in the same individuals; LMM correctly models within‑subject correlation.  
2. LMM can handle missing pain measurements under a missing at random assumption without excluding entire subjects (better than complete‑case repeated‑measures ANOVA).  
3. Allows inclusion of baseline VAS and other covariates, increasing precision.  

Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)  
- If VAS scores are highly skewed, show floor effects (many zeros), or the relationship to time is non‑linear, standard LMM assumptions (normal residuals, homoscedasticity) may be violated.  

When NOT to use  
- When sample size is very small (e.g., <10 per group) or convergence fails, making the model unstable.  
- When the data are better represented as ordinal categories (e.g., most values clustered at discrete levels or floor/ceiling).  

Alternatives if assumptions violated  
- Transform VAS (e.g., log(x+1)) or use generalized linear mixed models (GLMM) with appropriate link (e.g., gamma, negative binomial, or cumulative logit for ordinal pain categories).  
- Non‑parametric repeated measures methods (e.g., Brunner–Langer rank‑based ANOVA for longitudinal data).  

Model specification (example)  
- Outcome: HP VAS score.  
- Fixed effects: group (clonidine vs control), time (categorical: baseline, 6, 12, 24, 48, 72 h), group × time interaction, baseline VAS (if baseline not included in repeated outcome).  
- Random effects: random intercept for patient; consider random slope for time if data support.  
- Key parameter of interest: group × time interaction and/or overall group difference across 6–72 h.  
- Report estimated mean pain at each time by group, with differences and 95% CIs, and p‑values for main and interaction effects.  

3. Secondary analysis of pain at each post‑operative time point  

Test  
- Two‑sample t‑test for continuous VAS at each time point (e.g., 6, 12, 24, 48, 72 h).  

Purpose  
- To compare mean pain intensity between groups at individual clinically relevant times.  

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1. Simple and transparent; complements longitudinal mixed‑model results by giving cross‑sectional snapshots.  
2. Appropriate for continuous outcomes when distributions are approximately symmetric and sample sizes per group are similar.  

Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)  
- Multiple testing at 5 time points inflates type I error if not adjusted.  
- VAS distributions can be skewed or show heavy tails, violating the normality assumption.  

When NOT to use  
- When sample size at a time point becomes very small or highly unequal between groups.  
- When distributions are markedly non‑normal and variances differ.  

Alternatives if assumptions violated  
- Mann–Whitney U test for VAS (robust to non‑normality).  
- Use the estimates from LMM as primary and treat per‑time comparisons as exploratory, possibly with adjustment for multiple comparisons (e.g., Holm correction).  

4. Comparison of categorical pain levels  

Test  
- Chi‑square test or Fisher’s exact test for pain categories (no/mild vs moderate/severe; or 4‑level classification) at each time point.  

Purpose  
- To compare the proportion of patients experiencing clinically significant pain between groups.  

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1. Pain categories are clinically interpretable and align with the VAS thresholds defined in the study.  
2. Chi‑square test is standard for comparing proportions between two independent groups.  

Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)  
- If expected cell counts are <5 (especially for severe pain), chi‑square assumptions are violated.  

When NOT to use  
- When the contingency table is sparse (many zeros or low counts) so that asymptotic chi‑square approximation is poor.  

Alternatives if assumptions violated  
- Fisher’s exact test (for 2×2 tables) or exact multinomial tests for larger tables.  
- Collapsing categories (e.g., moderate/severe vs none/mild) to ensure adequate counts.  

5. Hemodynamic changes (SBP/DBP/HR)  

Test  
- Within‑group: paired t‑test (pre vs 5 min) for SBP, DBP, HR.  
- Between‑group:  
  - Two‑sample t‑test on change scores (Δ = 5 min – baseline) for SBP, DBP, HR.  

Purpose  
- To assess whether clonidine produces different acute cardiovascular responses compared with control.  

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1. Measurements are continuous and likely approximately normal in this sample.  
2. Paired t‑test appropriately accounts for within‑subject pairing (pre vs post).  
3. Change score t‑test efficiently compares groups.  

Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)  
- Distributions of BP or HR changes may be skewed or have outliers, violating normality assumptions.  

When NOT to use  
- When sample size is very small or repeated measures strongly non‑normal.  

Alternatives if assumptions violated  
- Wilcoxon signed‑rank test (within group) and Mann–Whitney U test (between group) on change scores.  
- ANCOVA: post‑measure as outcome, group as factor, baseline as covariate (often preferred to change scores).  

6. Adverse events  

Test  
- For each adverse event (nausea, vomiting, drowsiness, dizziness, headache):  
  - Chi‑square test or Fisher’s exact test comparing proportions between groups over the entire 72‑h period.  

Purpose  
- To test whether clonidine increases, decreases, or has no effect on incidence of adverse effects.  

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1. Outcomes are binary (presence/absence of each symptom); chi‑square/Fisher’s are standard for binary comparisons.  
2. Groups are randomized and independent; there is a clear effect of interest (risk difference or risk ratio).  

Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)  
- Expected counts may be very low if events are rare, violating chi‑square assumptions.  

When NOT to use  
- For very rare or zero events leading to unstable estimates or non‑informative p‑values.  

Alternatives if assumptions violated  
- Fisher’s exact test for 2×2 tables.  
- Combine events into “any adverse event” vs none to increase counts.  
- Estimate risk difference and exact confidence intervals without hypothesis tests.  

7. Analgesic consumption  

Test  
- Comparison of proportion using rescue analgesic (yes/no) between groups using Fisher’s exact or chi‑square.  

Purpose  
- To evaluate whether clonidine reduces the need for rescue pain medication.  

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1. Binary outcome with two independent groups.  
2. Clinically meaningful endpoint complementing VAS pain intensity.  

Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)  
- Extremely low usage rates may yield very small cell counts, undermining asymptotic tests.  

When NOT to use  
- When there are zero users in both groups (no information).  

Alternatives if assumptions violated  
- Descriptive comparison with risk difference and exact 95% CI; no formal test.  
- If counts allow, logistic regression adjusting for baseline pain.  

CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED TESTS  
-------------------------------  

8. Model including baseline pain as covariate (ANCOVA / mixed model)  

Test  
- Linear mixed model or repeated‑measures ANCOVA including baseline VAS as a covariate.  

Purpose  
- To improve precision and control for any baseline imbalance in pain.  

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1. Baseline pain is strong predictor of post‑operative pain; accounting for it reduces residual variance.  
2. Randomization does not guarantee perfect balance; adjusting for baseline increases robustness.  

Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)  
- If the relationship between baseline and post‑operative pain is highly non‑linear, a simple linear covariate may be mis‑specified.  

When NOT to use  
- When baseline data are missing for a substantial portion and are not missing at random.  

Alternatives if assumptions violated  
- Include baseline as categorical (e.g., quartiles or categories of pain) in the model.  
- Use change from baseline as outcome (less efficient but simpler).  

9. Sensitivity analysis using non‑parametric methods  

Test  
- Brunner–Langer non‑parametric rank‑based ANOVA for longitudinal data (e.g., nparLD package in R).  

Purpose  
- To verify robustness of conclusions when normality of VAS is questionable.  

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1. Does not require normality; robust to outliers and skewed distributions.  
2. Handles longitudinal design with group and time factors.  

Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)  
- Interpretation in terms of rank effects is less intuitive for clinicians than mean VAS differences.  

When NOT to use  
- When sample size is too small or data structure complex (e.g., different time schedules) for available software.  

Alternatives if assumptions violated  
- Ordinal mixed models with pain category as outcome.  

10. Logistic regression for “moderate/severe pain”  

Test  
- Binary logistic regression at a specified time (e.g., 24 h) with outcome: moderate/severe pain (VAS >54 mm) vs none/mild.  

Purpose  
- To estimate odds ratio (or risk ratio via log‑binomial/Poisson model) for clinically important pain, adjusting for covariates.  

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1. Aligns with clinically meaningful threshold for pain.  
2. Allows adjustment for baseline VAS, age, sex, tooth type, etc.  

Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)  
- If outcome is rare, logistic regression may produce unstable estimates and wide CIs.  

When NOT to use  
- With extremely low event counts (e.g., <5 events in a group).  

Alternatives if assumptions violated  
- Exact logistic regression; or report unadjusted risk difference with exact CIs.  

11. Multiple comparisons adjustment  

Test  
- Holm–Bonferroni or Benjamini–Hochberg procedure applied to the set of secondary/time‑specific comparisons.  

Purpose  
- To control family‑wise error or false discovery rate given multiple pain time points and secondary endpoints.  

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1. Limits inflation of type I error across multiple tests.  
2. Holm procedure is more powerful than simple Bonferroni while maintaining strong control of family‑wise error.  

Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)  
- Can be overly conservative, potentially masking real differences when the number of tests is large relative to sample size.  

When NOT to use  
- For clearly pre‑specified primary endpoint(s), which should have a single main test; adjustments applied mainly to exploratory or secondary outcomes.  

Alternatives if assumptions violated  
- Control FDR (Benjamini–Hochberg) rather than FWER if emphasis is on discovery.  
- Present unadjusted p‑values and interpret cautiously, emphasizing effect sizes and CIs.  

12. Exploratory subgroup analyses  

Test  
- Interaction tests within mixed models or logistic regressions: group × age group, group × sex, group × baseline pain category.  

Purpose  
- To explore whether clonidine’s effect differs in specific subgroups (e.g., younger vs older patients).  

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1. Helps generate hypotheses for future studies and assess consistency of effect.  
2. Uses existing data efficiently without inflating sample size.  

Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)  
- Trial is not powered for subgroup analyses; risk of spurious findings is high.  

When NOT to use  
- When subgroups are small (e.g., <10 per cell) or imbalance is extreme.  

Alternatives if assumptions violated  
- Restrict subgroup analyses to a small number of clinically compelling hypotheses, and treat results as exploratory.  

CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL TESTS  
---------------------------  

13. Area under the curve (AUC) for pain over 72 h  

Test  
- Compute individual AUC for VAS–time curves (e.g., trapezoidal rule) and compare between groups using t‑test or Mann‑Whitney.  

Purpose  
- Summarize overall pain burden over 72 h into a single metric per patient.  

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1. Captures both intensity and duration of pain.  
2. Simplifies repeated measures into a single outcome for straightforward comparison.  

Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)  
- Requires complete or near‑complete data; handling uneven missingness is not trivial.  

When NOT to use  
- When many patients have missing time points, especially early or late, or when pain trajectory is not well approximated by linear interpolation.  

Alternatives if assumptions violated  
- Use mixed models as the main analysis rather than AUC.  

14. Time‑to‑event analysis: time to pain resolution  

Test  
- Kaplan–Meier analysis and log‑rank test for time to first occurrence of “no pain” (VAS = 0) or “no/mild pain” (VAS ≤54 mm).  

Purpose  
- To compare how quickly patients in each group achieve minimal pain.  

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1. Uses all available repeated pain assessments and handles right‑censoring naturally.  
2. Clinically intuitive metric: time until relief.  

Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)  
- Pain is measured at discrete times, not continuously; true event time is interval‑censored, while standard methods assume exact event times.  

When NOT to use  
- When time intervals between measurements are wide and pain fluctuates frequently between categories.  

Alternatives if assumptions violated  
- Discrete‑time survival models or multi‑state models, though may be overkill for this sample size.  

15. Graphical diagnostics and visualization  

Test / approach  
- Residual diagnostics for mixed models; plots of mean VAS over time by group with error bars; individual spaghetti plots.  

Purpose  
- To visually assess model fit and to communicate results clearly.  

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1. Helps identify deviations from assumptions (non‑linearity, heteroscedasticity, outliers).  
2. Facilitates clinical interpretation and dissemination.  

Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)  
- With small sample sizes, graphs can be noisy; over‑interpretation of patterns is possible.  

When NOT to use  
- Never categorically inappropriate; but interpret cautiously.  

Alternatives if assumptions violated  
- Adjust model choices (e.g., transform data, change covariance structure) guided by diagnostics.  

TASK 3 – INAPPROPRIATE TESTS  
=============================  

Tests that should NOT be used in this study (with reasons):  

1. Independent samples t‑test on change scores only, ignoring repeated measures structure  
- Reason: Loses information about intermediate time points and correlation among them; less efficient and potentially biased if missingness varies across time.  
- Better: Mixed‑effects models or repeated‑measures ANOVA.  

2. Simple pre‑post paired t‑test within each group, then comparing significance levels  
- Reason: Comparing “significant vs non‑significant” within groups is an invalid way to compare groups; it does not directly test group differences.  
- Better: Directly test group × time interaction or compare change scores between groups.  

3. Multiple unadjusted t‑tests or Mann‑Whitney tests at each time point as the sole primary analysis  
- Reason: Inflated type I error; does not account for the longitudinal nature of data; inefficient use of information.  
- Better: Pre‑specified primary longitudinal model, with per‑time comparisons as secondary.  

4. One‑way ANOVA treating each time point across all patients as independent observations  
- Reason: Violates independence assumption; repeated measures on the same patients are correlated.  
- Better: Repeated‑measures ANOVA or mixed‑effects models.  

5. Pearson correlation between clonidine dose and pain  
- Reason: Dose is fixed and identical in all clonidine patients (30 μg), with 0 in controls; correlation is meaningless; exposure is categorical, not continuous.  
- Better: Group comparison analyses as outlined.  

6. Linear regression treating VAS pain categories (no/mild/moderate/severe coded 0–3) as interval data without justification  
- Reason: Ordinal categories do not necessarily have equal spacing; linear regression may misrepresent relationships.  
- Better: Ordinal logistic regression or analyze original continuous VAS scores.  

7. Parametric tests (t‑test/ANOVA) for proportions or counts without transformation or appropriate modeling  
- Reason: Proportions and counts are bounded and often non‑normal, especially with low counts; assumptions of normality and homoscedasticity are violated.  
- Better: Chi‑square/Fisher’s, logistic or Poisson/negative binomial regression.  

8. Stepwise variable selection procedures (automatic stepwise regression) to choose covariates  
- Reason: Data‑driven selection is unstable, inflates type I error, and yields biased estimates in small to moderate samples.  
- Better: Pre‑specify a small set of clinically relevant covariates.  

9. Per‑protocol analysis only, ignoring ITT  
- Reason: Excluding participants after randomization may introduce bias and compromise randomization benefits.  
- Better: Primary ITT analysis with PP as sensitivity.  

TASK 4 – SUMMARY OF ANALYTICAL APPROACH  
=======================================  

- The study is a parallel‑group, double‑blind RCT with repeated pain measurements over 72 hours. The central question is whether adding a fixed low dose of clonidine to articaine/epinephrine reduces post‑operative pain after endodontic treatment.  
- The proposed analysis plan:  
  - Use descriptive statistics to document the sample and baseline comparability.  
  - Conduct a primary longitudinal analysis of continuous HP VAS pain scores using a linear mixed‑effects model, including group, time, and their interaction, optionally adjusting for baseline pain and key covariates.  
  - Complement this with time‑specific comparisons of mean VAS and proportions with clinically significant pain, while managing multiplicity and emphasizing effect sizes and CIs.  
  - Analyze hemodynamic changes, adverse events, and analgesic use as secondary endpoints with appropriate tests for continuous and categorical data.  
  - Perform sensitivity analyses using non‑parametric or ordinal methods if model assumptions are questionable, and explore AUC or time‑to‑relief as optional summaries.  
- This approach respects the randomized design, properly handles the repeated measures nature of the data, limits bias through ITT analysis, and provides clinically interpretable results with quantified uncertainty.
