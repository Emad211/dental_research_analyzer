### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

*Note: Standard endodontic surgical outcomes (e.g., Rud and Andreasen criteria) are typically categorical (Success vs. Failure). The following plan assumes a categorical primary outcome.*

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY (Core Analysis)

**1. Independent Samples t-test**
*   **Purpose:** To compare baseline continuous variables (e.g., patient age) between Group A and Group B.
*   **Why appropriate:**
    1. Ensures that the two independent groups are comparable at baseline, ruling out age as an immediate confounding factor.
    2. It is the standard parametric test for comparing the means of two independent groups.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** If the continuous data (e.g., age or baseline lesion size) is heavily skewed or contains significant outliers, the mean is not a reliable measure of central tendency.
*   **When NOT to use:** Do not use if the data violates the assumption of normality or if variances are highly unequal (homoscedasticity violation).
*   **Alternative:** Mann-Whitney U test (if data is non-parametric/skewed).

**2. Pearson Chi-Square Test (or Fisher’s Exact Test)**
*   **Purpose:** To compare categorical baseline characteristics (e.g., sex, tooth type) and the primary categorical outcome (Success vs. Failure) between the two groups at the 2-year mark.
*   **Why appropriate:**
    1. The study compares two independent groups using categorical variables.
    2. It provides a straightforward, easily interpretable p-value to determine if the proportion of successful surgeries differs significantly by operator specialty.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It does not account for time-to-event data or patients who may have dropped out before the 2-year follow-up.
*   **When NOT to use:** Do not use if more than 20% of the expected cell frequencies are less than 5, or if any expected cell frequency is less than 1.
*   **Alternative:** Fisher’s Exact Test (if expected cell counts are too low).

#### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED (Enhancing Rigor)

**1. Multivariable Logistic Regression**
*   **Purpose:** To evaluate the effect of the operator (Endodontist vs. Oral Surgeon) on surgical success while adjusting for potential confounders.
*   **Why appropriate:**
    1. The introduction explicitly states that "sex, age, tooth type, preoperative signs statistically affect surgical healing." This test controls for these variables.
    2. It provides an Odds Ratio (OR), quantifying *how much more likely* a surgery is to succeed under one specialty compared to the other.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** If the sample size is too small relative to the number of predictors (rule of thumb is 10-15 events per predictor), the model may overfit.
*   **When NOT to use:** Do not use if the outcome is continuous (e.g., exact reduction of lesion size in millimeters) rather than binary (Success/Fail).
*   **Alternative:** Multiple Linear Regression (if the outcome is measured as a continuous reduction in lesion size).

**2. Kaplan-Meier Survival Analysis with Log-Rank Test**
*   **Purpose:** To compare the "time-to-failure" between the two groups over the 2-year follow-up period.
*   **Why appropriate:**
    1. Patients are followed biannually for 2 years; failures may happen at 6, 12, 18, or 24 months. This test utilizes all time-to-event data.
    2. It accounts for "censored" data (patients who drop out of the study or complete the 2 years without failing).
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It assumes that the risk of failure is independent of the reasons for censoring (e.g., if patients drop out *because* their surgery failed, the data is biased).
*   **When NOT to use:** Do not use if exact follow-up times are not recorded or if outcomes are only assessed at a single cross-sectional point in time.
*   **Alternative:** Cox Proportional Hazards Regression (if you also need to adjust for covariates like age and tooth type over time).

#### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL (Supplementary Insights)

**1. Cohen’s Kappa Statistic**
*   **Purpose:** To measure inter-rater reliability for the radiographic assessment of outcomes.
*   **Why appropriate:**
    1. Radiographic assessment of periapical healing is subjective. Establishing agreement between two independent blinded examiners enhances the validity of the outcome data.
    2. It accounts for agreement occurring by chance, unlike simple percentage agreement.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It can be overly sensitive to the prevalence of the condition (the "Kappa paradox"), yielding low values even when absolute agreement is high.
*   **When NOT to use:** Do not use if the radiographic outcome is measured on a continuous scale (e.g., exact area in $mm^2$).
*   **Alternative:** Intraclass Correlation Coefficient (ICC) (if the radiographic assessment is continuous).

### TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS

*   **Paired Samples t-test:** This test is used for comparing two measurements from the *same* subjects (e.g., pre-op vs. post-op continuous lesion size). Because this study compares two *different* groups of patients (Group A vs. Group B), an independent test is required.
*   **One-Way ANOVA:** ANOVA is used to compare means across *three or more* independent groups. Since this study only has two groups (Endodontist vs. Oral Surgeon), an independent t-test is sufficient and mathematically equivalent.
*   **Pearson Correlation Coefficient:** This is used to measure the linear relationship between two continuous variables. The primary independent variable here is categorical (operator type), making correlation analysis inappropriate for the main hypothesis.

### TASK 4: SUMMARY

The overall analytical approach should follow a logical progression from descriptive to inferential, and finally to adjusted longitudinal analysis. 

First, baseline characteristics must be compared using **Independent t-tests** and **Chi-square tests** to ensure the non-probability sampling did not result in skewed groups (e.g., oral surgeons getting all the difficult molar cases). Second, the primary outcome (success vs. failure) should be evaluated using a **Chi-square test**. Because the study features a 2-year biannual follow-up, incorporating **Kaplan-Meier survival analysis** is highly recommended to capture the *timing* of surgical failures, accounting for potential patient dropouts. Finally, because the literature cited in the introduction acknowledges that age, sex, and tooth type influence healing, a **Multivariable Logistic Regression** is vital to isolate the true effect of the operator's specialty by controlling for these confounding variables.
