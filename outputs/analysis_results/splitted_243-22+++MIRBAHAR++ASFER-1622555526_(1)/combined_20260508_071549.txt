- **When NOT to use:**
  - Not applicable (descriptives always needed); only avoid misleading summaries (e.g., mean for extreme skew).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Use medians/IQR, robust summaries, or transformations.

#### A2) Primary between-group comparison of outcome at 2 years (binary success/failure)
*(Most dental periapical surgery studies define a binary “success/healed vs failure/not healed”; since the definition is not shown, plan assumes a binary primary endpoint. If outcome is ordinal/continuous, see A4.)*
- **Test:** **Chi-square test** (or **Fisher’s exact** if sparse counts)
- **Purpose:** Compare proportion of successful outcomes at 2 years between groups.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Two independent groups (endodontist vs oral surgeon).
  2. Outcome is categorical (success/failure), typical for clinical healing endpoints.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - If outcome is not binary (ordinal radiographic categories or continuous scores).
- **When this test should NOT be used:**
  - If expected cell counts are small (e.g., <5 in any cell) → chi-square unreliable.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Fisher’s exact test** (small samples) or **exact unconditional** methods.

#### A3) Effect size estimation for primary endpoint
- **Test/Method:** **Risk difference**, **Risk ratio**, and/or **Odds ratio** with **95% CI**
- **Purpose:** Quantify magnitude and precision of group difference (not only p-values).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Clinically interpretable (absolute/relative differences in success).
  2. Supports reporting standards and meta-analysis compatibility.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - OR can overstate effects when outcome is common; RR/RD may be preferable.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Avoid OR as “risk” when outcome common and audience may misinterpret.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Use **log-binomial** or **Poisson with robust SE** to estimate RR if logistic regression is used but RR desired.

#### A4) If outcome is ordinal radiographic healing category at 2 years (instead of binary)
- **Test:** **Wilcoxon rank-sum (Mann–Whitney U)** *or* **Ordinal logistic regression**
- **Purpose:** Compare distribution of ordered healing categories between groups.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Handles ordinal outcomes without assuming normality.
  2. Appropriate for independent groups.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - If categories are nominal (unordered) or if proportional odds assumption fails (for ordinal regression).
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Do not use Mann–Whitney if many ties with few categories and you need model-based adjustment.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Partial proportional odds model** or **multinomial logistic regression**.

- **Test/Model:** **Kaplan–Meier curves + log-rank test**, plus **Cox proportional hazards model**

- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - If failure time is not captured (only status at 2 years).
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When follow-up times are essentially identical and no censoring and only final status known.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - If proportional hazards fails → **restricted mean survival time (RMST)** or **time-varying Cox**.

- **Why may not be appropriate:**

   - **Why inappropriate:** Only two groups; and outcome likely binary/ordinal, not continuous normal.

3. **Multiple independent t-tests at each follow-up timepoint without correlation adjustment**
   - **Why inappropriate:** Inflates Type I error and ignores within-subject correlation over time.

4. **Pearson correlation (group vs outcome)**
   - **Why inappropriate:** Group is categorical; correlation is not the right framework for group comparisons.

5. **Simple linear regression for binary outcome**
   - **Why inappropriate:** Violates assumptions (non-normal errors, heteroscedasticity), can predict outside [0,1].

  - Very small or sparse cell counts (e.g., expected counts <5 for multiple cells; especially if one group has very few failures), or if the design is paired or clustered in a way that violates independence (e.g., multiple teeth per patient without accounting for clustering).  
- Alternative if assumptions are violated:  
  - Fisher’s exact test for 2×2 tables with small expected counts.  
  - For >2 outcome categories with small cells, use exact multinomial tests or collapse categories (e.g., combine uncertain with failure or success as justified clinically), or apply exact Chi‑square methods.

A3. Estimation of Effect Size for Primary Outcome  

Method: Risk difference, risk ratio, and odds ratio with 95% confidence intervals  
- Purpose:  
  - Quantify the magnitude of difference in success rates between groups with uncertainty intervals.  
- Why appropriate:  
  1) Complements p‑values by providing clinically interpretable effect sizes (e.g., absolute difference in success probability).  
  2) Enables comparison with other studies and meta‑analysis, and supports clinical decision-making (how much better/worse one group is).  
- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - If outcome is extremely rare or extremely common, OR may be misleading (overstates relative effect vs RR).  
- When not to use:  
  - Never fully omitted; some effect measure is always desirable, but specific measure choice (RR vs OR) may be constrained by modeling approach (logistic regression inherently yields OR).  
- Alternatives if assumptions violated:  
  - Use adjusted estimates from logistic regression (adjusted OR) or from log-binomial / Poisson regression with robust variance to obtain adjusted RRs; use exact methods if sample sizes or counts are very small.

A4. Comparison of Baseline Characteristics Between Groups  

Tests:  
- For continuous variables (e.g., age): Independent samples t‑test or Mann‑Whitney U test  
- For categorical variables (sex, tooth type, presence of pre‑operative radiolucency, etc.): Chi‑square / Fisher’s exact tests  

- Purpose:  
  - Assess comparability of groups at baseline and identify potential confounders for multivariable modeling.  
- Why appropriate:  
  1) Groups are non‑randomly formed; baseline imbalance is possible and must be documented.  
  2) Use of appropriate tests by variable type ensures valid inference about group differences.  
- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - Multiple testing inflation: if many baseline variables are tested, p‑values can be misleading.  
- When not to use:  
  - Some argue against formal hypothesis testing for baseline variables in observational studies; however, describing and noting material imbalances is still crucial.  
- Alternatives if assumptions are violated:  
  - For age: if normality not met, Mann‑Whitney U instead of t‑test.  
  - For small categorical cell counts: Fisher’s exact instead of Chi‑square.  
  - Complement hypothesis tests with standardized differences (absolute difference in means/SD or % difference).

A5. Multivariable Modeling of Primary Outcome  

Test: Multivariable logistic regression  
- Purpose:  
  - Model the probability of success (binary outcome) as a function of operator group (main predictor) adjusting for important covariates (age, sex, tooth type, preoperative radiolucency, preoperative symptoms, etc.).  
- Why appropriate:  
  1) Outcome is binary; logistic regression is the standard for multivariable modeling in this context.  
  2) Non‑randomized design; regression adjustment is important to control for confounding and estimate adjusted effect of operator type.  
- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - Sample size may be limited relative to the number of covariates (risk of overfitting; need ~10 events per parameter as a rough rule).  
  - If the outcome is extremely common (e.g., success ~95–98%), separation or quasi‑complete separation may occur (group predicts outcome almost perfectly), causing estimation problems.  
- When this test should NOT be used:  
  - Very small number of failures (events) leading to unstable estimates, large standard errors, or non‑convergence.  
  - If multiple teeth per patient and clustering is substantial, simple logistic regression ignoring clustering violates independence assumptions.  
- Alternatives if assumptions are violated:  
  - Penalized logistic regression (Firth’s correction) or exact logistic regression if small sample or separation.  
  - If repeated events per patient: use multilevel (mixed‑effects) logistic regression with random intercepts for patient, or use generalized estimating equations (GEE) with robust standard errors.  
  - If interest in relative risk rather than odds: use log-binomial or Poisson regression with robust variance.

A6. Handling of Follow‑up / Missing Data  

Method:  
- Descriptive comparison of loss to follow‑up between groups; sensitivity analysis under different missing‑data assumptions  
- Purpose:  
  - Evaluate whether missing follow‑up data differ by group (which could bias outcomes).  
- Why appropriate:  
  1) Two‑year follow-up in a real-world setting often involves some attrition; it is critical to account for it.  
  2) Non‑random missingness by group or covariate may bias group comparisons.  
- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - If loss to follow‑up is truly negligible (e.g., 0–1 patients), complex methods are unnecessary.  
- When not to use:  
  - Not applicable; some consideration of missing data is always required, but specific techniques may vary.  
- Alternatives if assumptions are violated:  
  - If missingness is non‑ignorable: perform sensitivity analyses (best‑case/worst‑case scenarios), or use multiple imputation where feasible; but be transparent about limitations.

CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED ANALYSES  

These enhance rigor but are not absolutely required for a minimal analysis.

B1. Time‑to‑Event Analysis (If Data Permit)  

Test: Kaplan–Meier survival analysis + log‑rank test  
- Purpose:  
  - Compare time to failure (e.g., time until clinical/radiographic failure, or extraction) between groups.  
- Why appropriate:  
  1) Follow‑up is up to 2 years with scheduled biannual visits; time to failure provides more information than binary success/failure at 2 years.  
  2) Accommodates censoring for patients who do not fail by the last follow‑up or are lost to follow-up.  
- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - If only final outcome at 2 years was recorded and intermediate timing of failures is unknown, time‑to‑event analysis cannot be properly performed.  
- When this test should NOT be used:  
  - When timing of events is not recorded or is very imprecise (only “failed within 2 years” without dates).  
- Alternatives if assumptions are violated:  
  - If differential censoring or violation of proportional hazards is suspected, either:  
    - Use stratified log‑rank tests or restricted mean survival time (RMST) comparisons.  
    - Or revert to binary outcomes at fixed time points (as in Category A) and interpret cautiously.

B2. Cox Proportional Hazards Regression  

- Purpose:  
  - Model hazard (instantaneous risk) of failure over time as a function of operator group and covariates.  
- Why appropriate:  
  1) Provides adjusted hazard ratios, which can be more efficient than simple binary logistic regression if time‑to‑event data is detailed.  
  2) Can handle censored data and adjust for multiple covariates.  
- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - Requires proportional hazards assumption; if not met (e.g., hazards cross), HR interpretation becomes problematic.  
  - Requires adequate number of events to support regression.  
- When not to use:  
  - Few events (failures) or lack of reliable event time data.  
  - Strong non‑proportional hazards that cannot be reasonably addressed.  
- Alternatives if assumptions are violated:  
  - Time‑dependent Cox models or stratified Cox models, or analysis of restricted mean survival time.  
  - If time data is poor, use logistic regression on fixed time point outcome as primary.

B3. Analysis of Radiographic Healing Grades  

Test:  
- If ordinal categories (e.g., complete, incomplete, uncertain, failure):  
  - Cochran–Armitage trend test or Mantel–Haenszel test for trend  
  - Ordinal logistic regression (proportional odds model)  

- Purpose:  
  - Compare distribution of ordered healing categories between groups and assess whether operator group is associated with poorer or better graded healing.  
- Why appropriate:  
  1) Radiographic healing status is typically ordinal; tests designed for ordinal outcomes exploit this ordering for greater power.  
  2) Provides richer information than collapsing to success/failure only.  
- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - If categories are not clearly ordered, or if the proportional odds assumption in ordinal logistic regression is violated.  
- When not to use:  
  - Very small numbers in some categories leading to unstable estimates.  
- Alternatives if assumptions are violated:  
  - Collapse categories into binary outcome (e.g., “acceptable” vs “unacceptable” healing) and apply binary methods.  
  - Use non‑parametric tests for categorical data (e.g., generalized Fisher’s exact).

B4. Adjusted Analysis for Tooth-Level Covariates with Clustering  

Test: Multilevel (hierarchical) logistic regression or GEE  
- Purpose:  
  - Account for potential clustering of multiple teeth within the same patient, and/or patients nested within units, if the dataset includes multiple teeth per person.  
- Why appropriate:  
  1) Independence assumption is violated if multiple teeth from the same patient are included; mixed models or GEE adjust standard errors accordingly.  
  2) Provides more accurate inference and allows for modeling patient- and tooth-level predictors simultaneously.  
- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - If each patient contributed only one tooth (no clustering), hierarchical modeling adds unnecessary complexity.  
  - Sample size may be insufficient to estimate random effects reliably.  
- When not to use:  
  - Confirmed single‑tooth-per-patient design with minimal clustering structure.  
- Alternatives if assumptions are violated:  
  - If clustering is small and random effects models are unstable, use robust standard errors (sandwich estimators) to mitigate mild intra‑patient correlation.

B5. Sensitivity Analyses for Outcome Definitions  

Method: Reanalysis with alternative success criteria  
- Purpose:  
  - Assess robustness of conclusions to variations in how “success” is defined (e.g., stricter radiographic criteria, inclusion/exclusion of “uncertain” cases).  
- Why appropriate:  
  1) Outcome definitions in periapical surgery can be somewhat subjective; demonstrating robustness increases credibility.  
  2) Helps interpret borderline differences or controversial definitions.  
- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - Requires sufficient data and clear alternative definitions; may not be feasible if data were recorded only in a coarse binary fashion.  
- When not to use:  
  - When outcome was only recorded as a binary variable without underlying gradation.  
- Alternatives if assumptions are violated:  
  - Explicitly acknowledge limitations in outcome measurement and refrain from over‑interpretation.

B6. Subgroup Analyses  

Test: Stratified Chi‑square / logistic regression interaction tests  
- Purpose:  
  - Explore whether the effect of operator group differs across important subgroups (e.g., tooth type: anterior vs posterior; maxilla vs mandible; presence vs absence of pre‑operative radiolucency).  
- Why appropriate:  
  1) Biological plausibility that operator differences may be more pronounced in technically complex teeth (posterior, multi‑rooted).  
  2) Helps identify specific scenarios where one operator type may have an advantage.  
- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - Inflates type I error if many subgroups are explored without prespecification; small subgroup sizes reduce power.  
- When not to use:  
  - Very small or sparse subgroups; unplanned, post‑hoc subgroup analysis should be clearly labeled exploratory.  
- Alternatives if assumptions are violated:  
  - Report stratified descriptive results without formal p‑values, or include interaction terms in a single logistic model while emphasizing exploratory nature.

- Why it may not be appropriate:

- When not to use:

- Alternatives if assumptions are violated:

C4. Graphical Presentations

  2) Useful for presentations and teaching.

- Alternatives if assumptions are violated:  
  - Not applicable.

TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS  

Below are tests that should NOT be used in this study, with reasons.

1. Paired t‑test / McNemar’s Test for Group Comparisons  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Groups are independent (different sets of patients operated by different units). There is no natural pairing of observations between groups.  
- Consequence:  
  - Using paired tests would underestimate variance and inflate type I error.  
- Appropriate alternative:  
  - Independent samples t‑test for continuous variables; Chi‑square/Fisher’s exact for binary outcomes.

2. One‑Way ANOVA for Repeated-Measures on the Same Patients  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - If analyzing repeated outcomes over multiple follow‑up visits, the appropriate methods are repeated‑measures ANOVA or mixed models; simple one‑way ANOVA ignoring within‑patient correlation is invalid.  
- Appropriate alternative:  
  - Mixed‑effects models or generalized estimating equations; or focus on final 2‑year outcome if that is the specified endpoint.

3. Parametric Tests for Non‑Normal or Ordinal Data Without Justification  
Examples:  
- Using mean ± SD and t‑tests for clearly skewed age distributions or for ordinal scores (e.g., radiographic healing grade).  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Violates assumptions of normality and equal variances; can produce misleading p‑values and confidence intervals.  
- Appropriate alternative:  
  - Non‑parametric tests like Mann‑Whitney U; ordinal regression for ordered categories.

4. Use of Simple Linear Regression for Binary Outcome  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Linear regression assumes continuous outcome and can yield predicted probabilities outside [0,1]; residuals not normally distributed; heteroscedasticity.  
- Appropriate alternative:  
  - Logistic regression (binary), or other generalized linear models as applicable.

5. Repeated Use of Multiple Unadjusted Chi‑square Tests as the Only Analysis  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Multiple tests across many covariates inflate type I error and do not account for confounding; a multivariable model is required to isolate effect of operator group.  
- Appropriate alternative:  
  - Logistic regression with operator group and key covariates, supplemented by selective univariable analyses.

6. Complex Machine Learning Methods (e.g., Random Forests, Neural Networks)  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Sample size (N=120) is too small for high‑dimensional predictive models; risk of severe overfitting; interpretability is poor relative to needs of a comparative clinical study.  
- Appropriate alternative:  
  - Conventional logistic regression with carefully selected covariates.

7. Multiple Imputation Without Clear Justification / Diagnostics  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - If missingness is minimal, multiple imputation adds unnecessary complexity and potential mis‑specification; with small N and few variables, imputation can be unstable.  
- Appropriate alternative:  
  - If missingness is low, complete‑case analysis with sensitivity checks; otherwise, simple and transparent methods with clear assumptions.

TASK 4: SUMMARY / JUSTIFICATION OF THE OVERALL APPROACH  

The study is a non‑randomized, two‑group comparative observational study of periapical surgery outcomes with a binary primary endpoint (success vs failure at 2 years), moderate sample size (n=60 per group), and potential confounding from clinical and demographic variables.

The core analytical strategy therefore centers on:  
- Descriptive characterization of the two groups,  
- Primary comparison of success proportions using Chi‑square/Fisher’s exact tests,  
- Estimation of effect sizes (risk differences/ratios, ORs), and  
- Multivariable logistic regression to adjust for confounders and provide adjusted estimates of the association between operator group and outcome.

Where feasible, time‑to‑event methods and ordinal outcome modeling can provide additional detail and robustness. Attention to assumptions (independence, sample size, normality, proportional hazards) is critical; where assumptions are violated, suitable non‑parametric or generalized models must be used.

This approach is appropriate because it:  
- Aligns with the binary nature of the primary outcome and the independent two‑group design,  
- Accounts for non‑random allocation and potential confounding,  
- Provides clinically interpretable effect sizes with confidence intervals, and  
- Is commensurate with the sample size and typical data quality in retrospective dental surgery studies.
