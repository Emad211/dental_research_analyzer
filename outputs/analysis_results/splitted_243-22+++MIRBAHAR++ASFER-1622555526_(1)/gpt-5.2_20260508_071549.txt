## TASK 2 — Statistical analysis plan

### A. MANDATORY analyses (needed for a complete analysis)

#### A1) Descriptive statistics (baseline and follow-up)
- **Test/Method:** Descriptive summaries (means/SD or medians/IQR; counts/%), plus group comparisons for baseline balance (see A2).
- **Purpose:** Describe sample, procedures, and outcomes by group and overall.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Required to interpret outcome differences and generalizability.
  2. Identifies imbalance and data quality issues (missingness/outliers).
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - If key baseline variables were not collected, balance assessment is limited.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Not applicable (descriptives always needed); only avoid misleading summaries (e.g., mean for extreme skew).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Use medians/IQR, robust summaries, or transformations.

#### A2) Primary between-group comparison of outcome at 2 years (binary success/failure)
*(Most dental periapical surgery studies define a binary “success/healed vs failure/not healed”; since the definition is not shown, plan assumes a binary primary endpoint. If outcome is ordinal/continuous, see A4.)*
- **Test:** **Chi-square test** (or **Fisher’s exact** if sparse counts)
- **Purpose:** Compare proportion of successful outcomes at 2 years between groups.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Two independent groups (endodontist vs oral surgeon).
  2. Outcome is categorical (success/failure), typical for clinical healing endpoints.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - If outcome is not binary (ordinal radiographic categories or continuous scores).
- **When this test should NOT be used:**
  - If expected cell counts are small (e.g., <5 in any cell) → chi-square unreliable.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Fisher’s exact test** (small samples) or **exact unconditional** methods.

#### A3) Effect size estimation for primary endpoint
- **Test/Method:** **Risk difference**, **Risk ratio**, and/or **Odds ratio** with **95% CI**
- **Purpose:** Quantify magnitude and precision of group difference (not only p-values).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Clinically interpretable (absolute/relative differences in success).
  2. Supports reporting standards and meta-analysis compatibility.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - OR can overstate effects when outcome is common; RR/RD may be preferable.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Avoid OR as “risk” when outcome common and audience may misinterpret.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Use **log-binomial** or **Poisson with robust SE** to estimate RR if logistic regression is used but RR desired.

#### A4) If outcome is ordinal radiographic healing category at 2 years (instead of binary)
- **Test:** **Wilcoxon rank-sum (Mann–Whitney U)** *or* **Ordinal logistic regression**
- **Purpose:** Compare distribution of ordered healing categories between groups.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Handles ordinal outcomes without assuming normality.
  2. Appropriate for independent groups.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - If categories are nominal (unordered) or if proportional odds assumption fails (for ordinal regression).
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Do not use Mann–Whitney if many ties with few categories and you need model-based adjustment.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Partial proportional odds model** or **multinomial logistic regression**.

### B. RECOMMENDED analyses (increase rigor/validity)

#### B1) Multivariable regression to adjust for confounding (nonrandomized groups)
- **Test/Model:**  
  - **Logistic regression** (binary outcome)  
  - **Ordinal logistic regression** (ordinal outcome)  
  - **Linear regression** (continuous outcome, if applicable)
- **Purpose:** Estimate adjusted effect of operator/unit on outcome controlling for baseline covariates (e.g., age, sex, tooth type, baseline radiolucency, pre-op symptoms).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Group assignment is not randomized → confounding likely.
  2. Provides adjusted effect sizes and improves internal validity.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - If important confounders were not measured (residual confounding remains).
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When events are too few for the number of predictors (overfitting); avoid naïve stepwise selection.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Penalized regression** (Firth logistic, ridge/lasso) or reduced covariate set based on a priori confounders.

#### B2) Repeated-measures / longitudinal analysis across follow-up visits (if outcomes recorded biannually)
- **Test/Model:**  
  - **GEE** (population-averaged) for binary/ordinal outcomes  
  - **Mixed-effects models** (subject-specific)  
- **Purpose:** Use all follow-up timepoints, account for within-patient correlation, evaluate healing trajectory.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Follow-up is longitudinal (“biannually”) → repeated measures correlated.
  2. Increases power and uses partially observed follow-up data.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - If only final (2-year) outcome was recorded, there is no longitudinal structure to model.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When timing is inconsistent and not recorded; or when outcome only measured once.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Robust (sandwich) SE**; or simplify to final-timepoint analysis.

#### B3) Time-to-event analysis (if “failure” timing is available)
- **Test/Model:** **Kaplan–Meier curves + log-rank test**, plus **Cox proportional hazards model**
- **Purpose:** Compare time until failure/recurrence between groups over 2 years.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Handles censoring (loss to follow-up) appropriately.
  2. Uses event timing rather than collapsing to a single endpoint.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - If failure time is not captured (only status at 2 years).
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When follow-up times are essentially identical and no censoring and only final status known.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - If proportional hazards fails → **restricted mean survival time (RMST)** or **time-varying Cox**.

#### B4) Handling missing data
- **Test/Method:** Missingness assessment + **Multiple imputation (MI)** (if appropriate)
- **Purpose:** Reduce bias and improve precision if loss to follow-up or missing covariates occur.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Two-year follow-up commonly has attrition; complete-case may bias results.
  2. MI can incorporate predictors of missingness and outcomes.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - If missingness is minimal or clearly MNAR without plausible modeling.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When outcome is missing not at random and no sensitivity analysis is planned.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Inverse probability weighting**; **best/worst-case sensitivity**; MNAR sensitivity models.

### C. OPTIONAL analyses (supplementary insights)

#### C1) Subgroup / interaction analysis (effect modification)
- **Test/Model:** Regression with **interaction terms** (group × tooth type, group × arch, group × baseline lesion size/radiolucency, etc.)
- **Purpose:** Determine whether operator effect differs by clinically relevant strata.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Operator technique differences may matter more in certain tooth types/lesion severities.
  2. Generates hypotheses and clinical tailoring insights.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Underpowered; high multiplicity and false positives.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When subgroup sizes are small or not pre-specified.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Report stratified descriptives with CIs; avoid formal interaction testing.

#### C2) Sensitivity analyses for “success” definition
- **Test/Method:** Re-analyze using alternative dichotomizations (e.g., “complete healing only” vs “complete+incomplete”)
- **Purpose:** Check robustness to outcome definition ambiguity.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Success criteria not specified in excerpt and may vary.
  2. Ensures conclusions not driven by a single classification rule.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - If success definition is standardized and fixed a priori.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When it becomes data-driven post hoc without disclosure.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Use ordinal modeling instead of dichotomizing.

#### C3) Inter-rater reliability (if radiographs read by >1 assessor)
- **Test:** **Cohen’s kappa / weighted kappa** (categorical/ordinal) or **ICC** (continuous score)
- **Purpose:** Quantify agreement in radiographic/clinical outcome assessment.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Radiographic interpretation is subjective; reliability affects validity.
  2. Supports measurement quality.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - If only one assessor and no duplicate readings.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When assessments are not independent (assessors not blinded; consensus reads only).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Gwet’s AC1/AC2 for kappa paradox; bootstrap CIs.

## TASK 3 — Inappropriate tests (should NOT be used)

1. **Paired t-test**
   - **Why inappropriate:** Groups are independent (different patients/operators), not paired/repeated on same subject for the primary comparison.

2. **One-way ANOVA (as the main analysis)**
   - **Why inappropriate:** Only two groups; and outcome likely binary/ordinal, not continuous normal.

3. **Multiple independent t-tests at each follow-up timepoint without correlation adjustment**
   - **Why inappropriate:** Inflates Type I error and ignores within-subject correlation over time.

4. **Pearson correlation (group vs outcome)**
   - **Why inappropriate:** Group is categorical; correlation is not the right framework for group comparisons.

5. **Simple linear regression for binary outcome**
   - **Why inappropriate:** Violates assumptions (non-normal errors, heteroscedasticity), can predict outside [0,1].
