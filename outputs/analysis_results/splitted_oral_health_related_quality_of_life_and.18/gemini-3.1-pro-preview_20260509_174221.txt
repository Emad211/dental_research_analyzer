### TASK 1: INFORMATION EXTRACTION

**Study Characteristics:**
*   **Study Design:** Longitudinal prospective study.
*   **Objectives:** To investigate factors affecting the oral health-related quality of life (QOL) and satisfaction of patients before and after root canal treatment (RCT) performed by operators with varying levels of expertise.
*   **Independent Variables / Groups:**
    *   *Operator Expertise (3 levels):* Undergraduate students, Postgraduate students, Specialists.
    *   *Time (2 levels):* Before treatment, 2 weeks post-treatment.
*   **Dependent Variables / Measurements:**
    *   *Oral Health-Related QOL:* Measured using the validated Arabic version of the OHIP-17 questionnaire (assessed before and 2 weeks after treatment).
    *   *Patient Satisfaction:* Measured using a semantic differential scale (assessed 2 weeks after treatment).
    *   *Clinical/Radiographic Outcome:* Assessed via a radiographic assessment sheet (implied, though specific metrics are not detailed in the text).
*   **Sample Size:** 250 patients (calculated based on a previous study by Liu et al.).
*   **Inclusion Criteria:** 18–60 years of age, medically and mentally fit, capable of providing informed consent, referred for primary RCT.
*   **Exclusion Criteria:** Uncontrolled diabetes, hypertension, blood disorders, pregnancy, undergoing orthodontic treatment, failed RCT indicated for retreatment.
*   **Data Collection Method:** Independent examiner conducted interviews using the OHIP-17 and satisfaction scales to prevent operator-induced bias.

### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

Given that questionnaire data (OHIP-17 and semantic differential scales) yield discrete ordinal data that is conventionally summed to create continuous scores, the analysis must account for both parametric and non-parametric possibilities.

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS
*(Tests without which the analysis would be incomplete)*

**1. Two-Way Mixed ANOVA (Repeated Measures with Between-Subjects Factor)**
*   **Purpose:** To evaluate the effect of Time (Before vs. After) and Operator Expertise (Student vs. Postgrad vs. Specialist) on OHIP-17 scores, and their interaction.
*   **Why appropriate:**
    1. It simultaneously analyzes the within-subjects factor (longitudinal change in QOL) and the between-subjects factor (operator expertise).
    2. It tests for an *interaction effect* (e.g., does the magnitude of QOL improvement depend on who performed the root canal?), which is the core objective of the study.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** OHIP-17 scores are often highly skewed (not normally distributed), which violates the assumptions of ANOVA.
*   **When NOT to use:** If the data is severely non-normal or if the assumption of sphericity/homogeneity of variance is grossly violated.
*   **Alternative:** Friedman test (for within-group changes) combined with Kruskal-Wallis H test (for between-group differences on change scores).

**2. One-Way ANOVA**
*   **Purpose:** To compare patient satisfaction scores (measured only at 2 weeks post-treatment) across the three operator expertise groups.
*   **Why appropriate:**
    1. It compares the means of a continuous/interval variable across three independent groups.
    2. It controls the overall Type I error rate, unlike running multiple t-tests.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** Semantic differential scales can sometimes yield strictly ordinal data rather than interval data, making mean comparisons mathematically flawed.
*   **When NOT to use:** If the satisfaction data is heavily skewed or variances are unequal (failed Levene’s test).
*   **Alternative:** Kruskal-Wallis H test.

**3. Descriptive Statistics (Means, SDs, Medians, IQRs, Frequencies)**
*   **Purpose:** To summarize sociodemographic data, baseline characteristics, and raw outcome scores.
*   **Why appropriate:**
    1. Mandatory for Table 1 (Sociodemographics) to describe the study population.
    2. Provides context for the magnitude of clinical changes before inferential testing.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** Means/SDs are inappropriate for categorical variables (e.g., gender).
*   **When NOT to use:** Do not use Means/SDs for nominal data or highly skewed continuous data.
*   **Alternative:** Use Medians/Interquartile Ranges (IQR) for skewed continuous data; Frequencies/Percentages for categorical data.

#### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED TESTS
*(Tests that enhance the quality and rigor of the analysis)*

**1. Chi-Square Test of Independence (or Fisher's Exact Test)**
*   **Purpose:** To compare baseline sociodemographic variables (e.g., gender, education level) across the three operator groups.
*   **Why appropriate:**
    1. Ensures that the three operator groups are comparable at baseline (checking for selection bias).
    2. If groups differ significantly at baseline, those variables must be controlled for in later analyses.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** If expected cell counts in the contingency table are too small (less than 5).
*   **When NOT to use:** When more than 20% of expected cell frequencies are < 5.
*   **Alternative:** Fisher’s Exact Test.

**2. Post-Hoc Tests (e.g., Tukey's HSD or Bonferroni Correction)**
*   **Purpose:** To determine exactly *which* operator groups differ from one another if the ANOVA yields a significant result.
*   **Why appropriate:**
    1. ANOVA only tells you that *at least one* group is different; post-hoc tests identify the specific differences (e.g., Specialist vs. Student).
    2. Adjusts the p-value to prevent false positives (Type I error) from multiple comparisons.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** If the initial ANOVA is not statistically significant, post-hoc testing is generally unwarranted.
*   **When NOT to use:** When the omnibus test (ANOVA) p-value is > 0.05, or if variances are unequal.
*   **Alternative:** Games-Howell post-hoc test (if the assumption of equal variances is violated).

#### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL TESTS
*(Tests that provide supplementary insights)*

**1. Multivariable Linear Regression (or Ordinal Logistic Regression)**
*   **Purpose:** To predict post-treatment satisfaction or QOL while adjusting for potential confounders (e.g., age, gender, baseline QOL, tooth type).
*   **Why appropriate:**
    1. Allows the researchers to isolate the independent effect of "operator expertise" by holding demographic variables constant.
    2. Can identify other significant predictors of patient satisfaction.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** Requires a larger sample size to avoid overfitting (though N=250 is likely sufficient for a few variables).
*   **When NOT to use:** If there is high multicollinearity between predictor variables.
*   **Alternative:** Non-parametric regression models or Ordinal Logistic Regression (if the satisfaction scale is treated strictly as ordinal categories).

**2. Pearson or Spearman Correlation**
*   **Purpose:** To assess the relationship between the *change* in OHIP-17 scores and the final Patient Satisfaction scores.
*   **Why appropriate:**
    1. Provides insight into construct validity (do patients with the greatest QOL improvement also report the highest satisfaction?).
    2. Easy to interpret for clinical audiences.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** Correlation does not imply causation and does not account for group differences.
*   **When NOT to use:** If the relationship between the variables is non-linear.
*   **Alternative:** Spearman's Rank Correlation (if data is non-normal or ordinal).

### TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS

The following tests should **NOT** be used in this study:

1.  **Independent Samples t-test for Operator Expertise:** Inappropriate because there are *three* groups of operators (students, postgraduates, specialists). Using multiple t-tests inflates the Type I error rate (false positives). One-way ANOVA is required.
2.  **Independent Samples t-test for Before vs. After OHIP scores:** Inappropriate because the data is longitudinal (the same patients are measured twice). This violates the assumption of independence. A paired test (Paired t-test or Mixed ANOVA) must be used.
3.  **Pearson Chi-Square for OHIP-17 Total Scores:** Inappropriate because OHIP-17 total scores are continuous/discrete quantitative variables. Chi-square is strictly for categorical/nominal data.

### TASK 4: SUMMARY

**Justification of the Overall Analytical Approach:**
The analytical strategy is designed to address the longitudinal and multi-group nature of the study design. Because the primary objective is to evaluate how QOL changes over time depending on who performs the root canal, a **Two-Way Mixed ANOVA** is the most robust and elegant approach, as it captures both the within-subject changes (Time) and between-subject differences (Operator Expertise) simultaneously. 

Furthermore, because questionnaire data (OHIP-17 and semantic differential scales) frequently violate parametric assumptions of normality, the plan heavily emphasizes checking these assumptions and provides direct non-parametric alternatives (e.g., Kruskal-Wallis, Friedman). Finally, ensuring baseline comparability across the three operator groups via Chi-square tests is vital; if patients assigned to students were systematically different from those assigned to specialists, the results would be confounded. This comprehensive approach ensures statistical rigor, minimizes Type I and Type II errors, and aligns perfectly with modern evidence-based endodontic research standards.
