# CATEGORY A – MANDATORY tests (without these analysis is incomplete)

## A1) Linear mixed-effects model (LMM) for OHIP-17 over time
- **Test/model:** Linear Mixed-Effects Model (restricted maximum likelihood) with random intercept for participant.
- **Purpose:** Estimate **pre–post change** in OHIP-17 and whether change differs by **operator expertise**.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Handles **repeated measures** (baseline and 2-week) while accounting for **within-person correlation**.
  2. Tests the key scientific question via **Time × Operator** interaction (differential improvement by expertise).
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - OHIP-17 totals may be **non-normal**, bounded, or heavily skewed; LMM assumes approximately normal residuals.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If OHIP-17 is treated strictly as **ordinal** and distributional assumptions are untenable even after transformation, or if only item-level ordinal data are to be modeled.
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
  - **Generalized linear mixed model (GLMM)** (e.g., gamma or negative binomial if score is count-like), or
  - **Ordinal mixed model** (cumulative link mixed model) at item-level, or
  - **Rank-based mixed model / nonparametric longitudinal methods**.

**Minimum fixed effects to specify:**
- Time (baseline vs 2-week), Operator group (3 levels), and **Time×Operator**.
**Recommended covariates (if available):**
- baseline pain/complaint severity (not provided), age, sex, education, tooth type/diagnosis (not provided), baseline OHIP (implicitly included via repeated measures).

**Primary estimands to report:**
- Mean change within each operator group with 95% CI.
- Difference-in-differences (interaction contrasts): (Δ specialist − Δ student), (Δ specialist − Δ postgraduate), etc.

## A2) Descriptive statistics + baseline comparability across operator groups
- **Tests:**  
  - Continuous: **ANOVA** (or **Kruskal–Wallis** if non-normal)  
  - Categorical: **Chi-square** test (or **Fisher’s exact** for small expected counts)
- **Purpose:** Describe sample and assess whether operator groups differ at baseline (important due to likely non-random allocation).
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Operator expertise groups may differ in demographics/clinical characteristics → potential confounding.
  2. Provides transparency for interpreting adjusted vs unadjusted effects.
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - “Significance testing” baseline differences can be misleading; imbalance is better handled by **standardized differences** and modeling.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Do not use baseline p-values as a basis to include/exclude covariates (“p<0.05 rule”).
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
  - For continuous: **Kruskal–Wallis** or permutation ANOVA.
  - For categorical with small cells: **Fisher’s exact**.

# CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED tests (increase rigor/quality)

## B1) ANCOVA / regression of 2-week OHIP adjusting for baseline OHIP and covariates
- **Test/model:** Linear regression (or GLM) with **2-week OHIP** as outcome, predictors: operator group + baseline OHIP + covariates.
- **Purpose:** Provide an **adjusted** estimate of group differences at follow-up controlling for baseline status.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Generally **more efficient and less biased** than analyzing change scores when baseline differs.
  2. Allows incorporation of confounders (age/sex/education, etc., if collected).
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - If follow-up missingness is substantial and not MCAR, complete-case ANCOVA may bias results.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If outcome distribution makes linear model unsuitable and no adequate transformation/GLM is used.
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
  - Robust regression; GLM (gamma/negative binomial); quantile regression.

---

## B2) Ordinal logistic regression for satisfaction (if ordinal)
- **Test/model:** **Proportional odds model** (ordinal logistic regression), predictor: operator group (+ covariates).
- **Purpose:** Model satisfaction while respecting its **ordinal** nature and adjust for confounders.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Semantic differential scales are commonly ordinal; ordinal regression avoids forcing interval assumptions.
  2. Produces interpretable **odds ratios** for higher satisfaction across expertise levels.
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - **Proportional odds assumption** may be violated.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When satisfaction is truly continuous/interval with many values, linear regression may be preferable.
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
  - **Partial proportional odds model** or multinomial logistic regression.

---

## B3) Multiple-comparison adjustment for pairwise group contrasts
- **Test/procedure:** Tukey HSD (ANOVA context), Holm correction, or FDR depending on number of contrasts.
- **Purpose:** Control Type I error when comparing student vs postgraduate vs specialist.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Three groups implies multiple pairwise comparisons.
  2. Prevents overstating evidence across many outcomes/items.
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - Overly conservative if only a small number of pre-specified contrasts exist.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If only a **single pre-specified primary contrast** is tested (e.g., specialist vs non-specialist).
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
  - Permutation-based adjusted p-values.

---

## B4) Reliability/internal consistency of OHIP-17 in the study sample
- **Test:** **Cronbach’s alpha** (or preferably **McDonald’s omega**).
- **Purpose:** Assess internal consistency of OHIP-17 total score in this population.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Supports interpretability of a **summed score** as a scale.
  2. Kappa reported in paper is not internal consistency; alpha/omega addresses a different measurement property.
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - Alpha can be inflated by many items and assumes tau-equivalence; OHIP domains may be multidimensional.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If analyzing OHIP at item/domain level without using a total score.
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
  - Omega, confirmatory factor analysis-based reliability.

---

## B5) Missing data assessment and sensitivity analyses
- **Tests/approach:**  
  - Compare baseline characteristics of completers vs non-completers (chi-square/t-test)  
  - If missingness meaningful: **multiple imputation** (MI) or mixed models (which can handle MAR under likelihood).
- **Purpose:** Evaluate and mitigate bias from loss to follow-up at 2 weeks.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Longitudinal studies commonly have missing follow-up outcomes.
  2. MI/sensitivity improves credibility of conclusions.
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - MI requires correct imputation model; can be unstable with small sample or many variables missing.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If missing data are extremely sparse and clearly MCAR, MI adds complexity without benefit.
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
  - Pattern-mixture sensitivity analyses; worst/best-case bounds.

## TASK 3: Tests that should NOT be used (and why)

1. **Independent-samples t-test** for comparing OHIP pre vs post  
   - Wrong because measurements are **paired within the same patient**; ignores within-subject correlation and inflates variance.

2. **Multiple separate paired t-tests within each operator group without an interaction model** (as the primary approach)  
   - Does not directly test whether **changes differ between groups** and increases multiplicity.

3. **Chi-square tests on summed OHIP-17 total score categorized ad hoc (e.g., “good/bad QOL”) as primary analysis**  
   - Unnecessary dichotomization reduces power and interpretability and introduces threshold arbitrariness.

4. **Pearson correlation between operator expertise (coded 1/2/3) and outcomes as main analysis**  
   - Treats a nominal exposure as continuous/linear and fails to properly compare groups or adjust for confounding.

5. **Simple one-way ANOVA on change scores as the only longitudinal method** (without checking baseline imbalance or using repeated-measures modeling)  
   - Change-score ANOVA can be less efficient and may be biased if baseline differs systematically across operator groups.

---

## TASK 4: Summary justification of the overall approach
A valid analysis must (1) respect the **paired pre–post** OHIP-17 structure, (2) compare **three independent operator expertise groups**, and (3) directly test whether improvement differs by expertise via a **Time × Group** effect while reporting interpretable effect sizes and uncertainty. Mixed-effects (or ANCOVA-style) modeling provides the most defensible framework, with appropriate nonparametric/ordinal alternatives if the scale properties or assumptions do not hold.
