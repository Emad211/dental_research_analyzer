TASK 1: INFORMATION EXTRACTION  
===============================

1. Study design  
- Type: Single-blind randomized controlled clinical trial.  
- Parallel group, 1:1 allocation.  
- Setting: Department of Endodontics, Faculty of Dentistry, Erciyes University, Turkey.  
- Registration: www.clinicaltrials.in.th (ID: 20201221002).  
- Reporting guideline: PRIRATE (Preferred Reporting Items for Randomized Trials in Endodontics).  
- Ethics: Approved by ethics committee (no: 2019/628).

2. Objective / Primary question  
- To evaluate the effect of low-level laser therapy (LLLT) on the success of inferior alveolar nerve block (IANB) in mandibular molar teeth with symptomatic irreversible pulpitis (SIP).  
- Primary comparison: IANB + LLLT vs IANB + sham LLLT (mock laser).

3. Study groups  
- Group 1: IANB + LLLT  
  - Standard IANB with 3.6 mL 4% articaine HCl with 1:100,000 epinephrine.  
  - After confirmation of lip numbness and two consecutive negative responses to electric pulp test.  
  - LLLT applied (active) around apices of mesial and distal roots with diode laser (940 nm, Epic 10) at 0.5 W, “Pain therapy” mode, 30 s on mesial, 30 s circumferential on distal, tip at ~10 mm from tissue.  
  - Then access cavity preparation and canal shaping under rubber dam.

- Group 2: IANB (control)  
  - Same standard IANB protocol.  
  - Same laser tip positioning, but laser not activated (mock / placebo).  
  - Then access cavity preparation and shaping under rubber dam.

4. Sample size  
- Initial screening: 352 assessed for eligibility.  
- Excluded: 264 (228 not meeting inclusion criteria, 24 declined, 12 other reasons).  
- Randomized: 88 patients (44 per group).  
- Analyzed: 44 per group; no loss to follow-up; no exclusions post-randomization.  
- Sample size determination: Pilot study; α = 0.05, power (1−β) = 0.80, effect size = 0.782; required ≥40 per group; planned/included = 44 per group.

5. Inclusion criteria  
- Age 18–65 years.  
- Healthy individuals (ASA I–II: without systemic disease or with mild systemic disease).  
- Mandibular molar teeth diagnosed with SIP:  
  - Diagnosis via cold test (1,1,1,2 tetrafluoroethane, Green Endo-Ice) and digital electric pulp tester (Parkell Inc.).  
  - Positive response to electric pulp test.  
  - Prolonged response to cold test.  
- Periapical status: periapical radiographs without radiolucency (except widened PDL with intact lamina dura).  
- Preoperative pain: only patients with moderate or severe preoperative pain on VAS (45–100 mm).

6. Exclusion criteria  
- Analgesic or anti-inflammatory drug use within 12 h before treatment.  
- Allergy to NSAIDs.  
- Pregnant or breastfeeding women.  
- Patients taking opioids.  
- History of gastrointestinal disease.  
- Teeth with periodontal disease.  
- Systemic disorders and/or sensitivity to articaine 1:100,000 epinephrine.  
- Presence of periapical radiolucency.  
- Patients with more than one painful tooth.  
- Teeth without pulpal bleeding after access cavity preparation (excluded intra-operatively).  
- Patients without lip numbness and/or without two consecutive negative electric pulp tests at 15 minutes after IANB (excluded before treatment).

7. Randomization and blinding  
- Randomization: Computer-generated random table (www.random.org), 1:1 ratio, performed by a dental assistant.  
- Blinding:  
  - First operator (injecting anaesthetic) blinded to intervention.  
  - Second operator performed LLLT (knows allocation).  
  - Third operator (endodontic treatment) not informed of allocation (blinded).  
  - Patients blinded via sham laser in control (placebo LLLT).  
- Overall: Single-blind (operators partially blinded, patient-blinded; outcome assessor is the patient’s own VAS marking).

8. Interventions and clinical procedures  
- Standard IANB: 3.6 mL 4% articaine HCl + 1:100,000 epinephrine injected over 1 minute.  
- Onset confirmation (15 min): lip numbness + two consecutive negative electric pulp tests; otherwise exclusion.  
- LLLT:  
  - Diode laser, 940 nm wavelength.  
  - 0.5 W power, “Pain therapy” mode.  
  - Tip ~10 mm from tissue around root apices.  
  - 30 s mesial root area, 30 s distal root circumferentially.  
- Control: same positioning but laser off.  
- Endodontic treatment: Access cavity and canal shaping under rubber dam.  
- Supportive anaesthesia (both groups):  
  - Buccal, intraligamentary, or intrapulpal anaesthesia for patients with anaesthesia “failure”, to allow treatment continuation (supportive injections themselves are not part of primary outcome classification; rather they are a consequence of anaesthesia failure).

9. Outcomes, variables, and measurements  

9.1. Pain measurement  
- Tool: 100-mm horizontal Visual Analog Scale (VAS).  
  - 0 mm = no pain; 100 mm = worst pain.  
  - Markings every 10 mm; only 0 and 10 (at ends) numeric labels; patient marks position which is later measured in mm (0–100).  
- Time points:  
  - Preoperative VAS (baseline pain before anaesthesia).  
  - Intraoperative VAS during treatment:  
    - “Pain value at time of operation” measured again with VAS.  
    - Additional marking by patient if pain is felt during:  
      - Access cavity preparation  
      - Canal shaping  
- Pain categorical classification (per Yaylali et al., 2017):  
  - 0–4 mm: no pain  
  - 5–44 mm: mild pain  
  - 45–74 mm: moderate pain  
  - 75–100 mm: severe pain  

9.2. Primary outcome (anaesthesia success)  
- Operational definition:  
  - Anaesthesia categorized as unsuccessful if pain values at any intraoperative stage (any of: access cavity, canal shaping, etc.) are >44 mm VAS (i.e., moderate or severe pain).  
  - Therefore, successful anaesthesia = maximum intraoperative VAS ≤44 mm (no/mild pain).  
- Derived binary variable:  
  - Success (yes/no) per patient.  
  - Reported success rates:  
    - IANB group: 34%  
    - IANB + LLLT group: 57%  
  - They report a significant difference (p = 0.032) between groups.

9.3. Secondary variables (explicit & implicit)  
- Pain category post-anaesthesia (after onset, during treatment), as per Table 1:

  | Group | No pain | Mild | Moderate | Severe |

  | IANB | 7 | 8 | 23 | 6 |
  | IANB + LLLT | 12 | 13 | 16 | 3 |

  - From this, we can derive:  
    - Categorical VAS pain category (4 levels) post-anaesthesia per group.  
    - Collapsed success vs failure (success = no+mild; failure = moderate+severe).  

- Demographics and baseline characteristics (not fully provided but likely collected):  
  - Age (continuous or categorical).  
  - Sex.  
  - Baseline (preoperative) VAS pain (continuous, also categorized).  
  - Tooth position (e.g., first vs second molar), potentially.  
  - ASA status (I vs II).  

- Process variables:  
  - Need for supportive anaesthesia (yes/no, type).  
  - Exclusions due to negative electric pulp testing or lack of lip numbness (pre-treatment).  

10. Data collection methods  
- VAS: Patients mark pain intensity on a 100-mm horizontal line; measured in mm by investigators.  
- Electric pulp test: Digital pulp tester; outcomes recorded (positive/negative).  
- Cold test: Green Endo-Ice; response recorded.  
- Radiographs: Periapical radiographs to confirm periapical status.

TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN  
=================================

Because the article already reports a p-value for anaesthesia success, the plan below is written as if designing or auditing the analysis from scratch.

General principles  
- Analysis population:  
  - Primary: Intention-to-treat (ITT) – all randomized patients analyzed in their assigned group.  
  - Given that no loss to follow-up and no post-randomization exclusions are reported, ITT ≈ per-protocol. Explicitly specify handling of any potential intraoperative exclusions (none reported).  
- Significance levels:  
  - Two-sided α = 0.05 for primary outcome.  
  - For secondary outcomes, consider adjustment for multiple comparisons if many tests are run, or treat them as exploratory.  
- Software: Any robust statistical package (R, Stata, SAS, SPSS).  
- Reporting: Provide effect sizes with 95% confidence intervals (CIs), not only p-values.

A. MANDATORY TESTS  
-------------------

1) Comparison of primary binary outcome: anaesthesia success (yes/no)  
- Primary Test: Pearson’s Chi-square test (with continuity correction as needed or exact test)  

  - Purpose:  
    Compare the proportion of anaesthesia success between IANB + LLLT and IANB alone.

  - Why appropriate (≥ 2 reasons):  
    1. Outcome is binary (success vs failure); groups are independent and randomized.  
    2. Sample sizes are moderate (44 per group), satisfying typical chi-square requirements (expected counts generally ≥5).  
    3. Main question is difference in proportions; Chi-square is standard for two-group randomized trials with binary outcomes.

  - Why it may not be appropriate (≥ 1 reason):  
    - If any cell in the 2×2 table has very low expected counts (<5), the chi-square approximation may be inaccurate.

  - When this test should NOT be used:  
    - Very small samples with sparse data (e.g., expected cell counts <5).  
    - When the primary interest includes adjustment for covariates (then logistic regression is more appropriate).  
    - For paired or matched data (not applicable here).

  - Alternative if assumptions violated:  
    - Fisher’s exact test (2×2) for small or sparse samples, with exact p-value and exact or mid-P CIs for the risk difference or odds ratio.  

  - Reporting:  
    - Report success proportion per group, absolute risk difference, relative risk (RR), odds ratio (OR), with 95% CIs.  
    - Example (structure):  
      - IANB+LLLT: 57% (25/44) vs IANB: 34% (15/44).  
      - Risk difference: 23 percentage points (95% CI...).  
      - RR and OR with CIs.  
    - Provide p-value from Chi-square or Fisher’s exact test.

2) Comparison of pain category distribution post-anaesthesia (4-level ordinal VAS categories)  
- Primary Test: Chi-square test for independence or Cochran–Armitage trend test (if ordered categories)  

  - Purpose:  
    Assess whether the distribution of post-anaesthesia pain categories (none, mild, moderate, severe) differs between groups.

  - Why appropriate (≥ 2 reasons):  
    1. Pain categories are nominal with an inherent order; grouped by treatment arm; data are independent.  
    2. Chi-square test assesses overall association; Cochran–Armitage trend test can test for a trend across ordered pain levels.  

  - Why it may not be appropriate (≥ 1 reason):  
    - Some category counts (e.g., severe pain) may be small; expected cell frequencies may be <5, invalidating the chi-square approximation.  

  - When this test should NOT be used:  
    - Very sparse tables (many small expected counts).  
    - When you wish to explicitly model the ordinal nature (then an ordinal logistic model is preferable).  

  - Alternative if assumptions violated:  
    - Collapsing categories (e.g., success vs failure) and using Fisher’s exact test.  
    - Ordinal logistic regression (proportional odds model) which uses the ordinal information efficiently.

3) Baseline comparability of groups  
Although not strictly “power-critical”, establishing baseline comparability is mandatory in RCT reporting.

a) Continuous baseline variables (e.g., age, baseline VAS)  
- Test: Independent samples t-test (if approximate normality) or Mann–Whitney U test  

  - Purpose:  
    Compare mean (or distribution) of baseline continuous variables between groups to document balance.

  - Why appropriate (≥ 2 reasons):  
    1. Randomized groups; expectation of balance, but descriptive checks are standard.  
    2. Independent samples t-test is appropriate for continuous data with approximate normal distributions; Mann–Whitney works under non-normality.  

  - Why it may not be appropriate (≥ 1 reason):  
    - For small sample sizes with substantial skewness or outliers, t-test assumptions may be violated.  

  - When this test should NOT be used:  
    - For heavily skewed variables or ordinal data with few categories (use nonparametric tests).  
    - If the intent is not to test but to simply describe (arguably, baseline tests can be avoided and descriptive summaries used).  

  - Alternative if assumptions violated:  
    - Mann–Whitney U test for non-normal distributions.  
    - Transformations (e.g., log-transform) if scientifically interpretable.

b) Categorical baseline variables (e.g., sex, ASA class, tooth type, baseline pain category)  
- Test: Chi-square test or Fisher’s exact test  

  - Purpose:  
    Compare distributions of categorical baseline variables between groups.

  - Why appropriate (≥ 2 reasons):  
    1. Data are categorical; groups are independent.  
    2. Randomization ensures approximate balance; these tests help identify large imbalances that could warrant adjusted analyses.  

  - Why it may not be appropriate (≥ 1 reason):  
    - For very small categories, expected cell counts can be too low; Fisher’s exact test preferable.  

  - When this test should NOT be used:  
    - Some argue baseline significance testing is unnecessary in RCTs; alternatives are descriptive presentation only.  

  - Alternative if assumptions violated:  
    - Fisher’s exact test for small cells.

4) Descriptive statistics and confidence intervals for VAS pain  
- Descriptive only but effectively mandatory: Means, medians, SDs or IQRs for preoperative and intraoperative VAS by group.

  - Purpose:  
    Provide central tendency and variability to support interpretation of pain differences, even if the primary analysis uses binary/categorical outcomes.

  - If inferential:  
    - For continuous VAS (0–100 mm), t-test or Mann–Whitney U test between groups at specific time points, with 95% CIs for differences.

  - Alternative if non-normal:  
    - Mann–Whitney U and report medians and IQRs.

B. RECOMMENDED TESTS  
---------------------

5) Logistic regression for anaesthesia success (primary outcome)  
- Test: Binary logistic regression  

  - Purpose:  
    1. Estimate adjusted effect of LLLT on anaesthesia success controlling for potential baseline covariates (age, sex, baseline pain, tooth type, ASA status).  
    2. Provide odds ratio for LLLT vs control, with 95% CI, and explore predictors of anaesthesia success.

  - Why appropriate (≥ 2 reasons):  
    1. Outcome is binary and logistic regression is the standard for modeling binary outcomes.  
    2. Allows adjustment for any accidental imbalance in covariates despite randomization.  
    3. Provides more information than simple chi-square (effect size, adjusted OR, exploration of confounding or effect modification).  

  - Why it may not be appropriate (≥ 1 reason):  
    - Sample size is modest (88 total); including too many covariates risks overfitting. Typical rule of thumb is ≥10 events per predictor (events ≈ number of failures or successes, whichever is smaller).  

  - When this test should NOT be used:  
    - If the number of events (e.g., failures) is too small relative to the number of predictors to be included.  
    - If baseline covariates are not reliably measured.  

  - Alternative if assumptions violated / small-sample concerns:  
    - Exact logistic regression for small samples.  
    - Reduce model to minimal covariates or only unadjusted analysis (Chi-square/Fisher’s exact).  

  - Optional additional features:  
    - Interaction terms (e.g., LLLT × baseline pain category) to test effect modification.  
    - Report pseudo-R² and calibration diagnostics if model complexity warrants.

6) Ordinal logistic regression for pain category (post-anaesthesia)  
- Test: Proportional odds (ordinal logistic) model  

  - Purpose:  
    Model the ordered pain categories (none/mild/moderate/severe) as an ordinal outcome, assessing effect of LLLT on overall pain distribution.

  - Why appropriate (≥ 2 reasons):  
    1. Pain categories are ordinal; ordinal logistic regression uses ordering to increase power compared with dichotomization.  
    2. Provides an odds ratio for being in a lower (better) pain category vs higher categories, accounting for covariates if needed.  

  - Why it may not be appropriate (≥ 1 reason):  
    - Proportional odds assumption may be violated (effect of LLLT not consistent across thresholds).  
    - Sample size and number of categories may limit robustness.  

  - When this test should NOT be used:  
    - When proportional odds assumption fails markedly and cannot be remedied.  
    - When there are too few observations in some categories.  

  - Alternative if assumptions violated:  
    - Collapsing categories (e.g., success vs failure) and using logistic regression.  
    - Generalized ordered logit models (partial proportional odds).

7) Analysis of continuous VAS pain scores  
- Tests: Independent samples t-test or Mann–Whitney U at specific time points; optionally linear regression  

  - Purpose:  
    1. Compare mean or median preoperative VAS pain between groups to confirm baseline comparability.  
    2. Compare intraoperative VAS scores between groups at key stages (e.g., access cavity preparation, canal shaping).

  - Why appropriate (≥ 2 reasons):  
    1. VAS is recorded as continuous 0–100 mm; parametric tests can be applied if distribution is not extremely skewed.  
    2. Provides more nuanced information than dichotomized outcomes, e.g., magnitude of pain reduction.  

  - Why it may not be appropriate (≥ 1 reason):  
    - VAS data may be skewed, especially with many patients at low pain (floor effect) or high pain (ceiling effect).  

  - When this test should NOT be used:  
    - When distributions are highly skewed or with strong outliers and sample size is small.  

  - Alternative if assumptions violated:  
    - Mann–Whitney U test.  
    - Transformations (e.g., square-root) if consistent with interpretation.

8) Sensitivity analysis for different success thresholds  
- Test: Recompute success definition (e.g., VAS ≤20 vs >20) and repeat primary analysis with Fisher’s exact or logistic regression  

  - Purpose:  
    Test robustness of conclusions to the chosen VAS cut-off (45 mm) by using alternative clinically relevant thresholds.

  - Why appropriate (≥ 2 reasons):  
    1. Cut-offs for pain categories are somewhat arbitrary; exploring alternate thresholds assesses robustness.  
    2. Enhances credibility of primary findings.  

  - Why it may not be appropriate (≥ 1 reason):  
    - Multiple thresholds can lead to multiplicity problems and fishing for significant results.  

  - When this test should NOT be used:  
    - If pre-specified in the protocol only one threshold is allowed and post hoc analyses would contradict pre-specification.  

  - Alternative:  
    - Predefine one or two alternative thresholds and clearly label these as sensitivity analyses.

9) Analysis of supportive anaesthesia usage  
- Test: Chi-square or Fisher’s exact test for proportion requiring supplemental anaesthesia  

  - Purpose:  
    Assess whether LLLT reduces the need for supplementary injections (buccal, intraligamentary, intrapulpal).

  - Why appropriate (≥ 2 reasons):  
    1. Supplemental anaesthesia is clinically relevant; fewer supplemental injections imply better primary block success.  
    2. Binary outcome (yes/no supplemental anaesthesia); groups independent.  

  - Why it may not be appropriate (≥ 1 reason):  
    - Might be redundant if defined directly by same VAS-based success variable; measurement overlap.  

  - When this test should NOT be used:  
    - If support use is completely determined by success/failure definition and adds no independent information.  

  - Alternative:  
    - Treat supplemental anaesthesia as an outcome in a logistic regression jointly with other data or as a secondary descriptive outcome.

TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS AND WHY  
====================================

Below are tests that should NOT be used for this study, with reasons.

1) Paired t-test / Wilcoxon signed-rank test for between-group comparisons  
- Inappropriate because:  
  - Groups are independent; paired tests assume same subjects measured twice or matched pairs.  
  - Using paired tests would artificially inflate significance by ignoring independence.

2) Repeated measures ANOVA / mixed models for primary outcome  
- Inappropriate because:  
  - Primary outcome is a single binary endpoint (overall success vs failure), not repeated continuous measures over time.  
  - No repeated continuous outcomes across multiple time points are explicitly defined; only pre- and intraoperative VAS extremes are used to define success.

3) One-way ANOVA for categorical (binary) outcomes  
- Inappropriate because:  
  - ANOVA assumes continuous outcomes; success is binary.  
  - This violates assumptions and misinterprets the nature of the outcome.

4) McNemar’s test  
- Inappropriate because:  
  - Designed for paired binary data (e.g., before/after on same subject).  
  - Here groups are independent.

5) Correlation tests (Pearson/Spearman) between treatment arm and outcome  
- Inappropriate because:  
  - Treatment is a binary randomization factor; correlation coefficients are not suitable primary tests of treatment effect.  
  - More appropriate methods are Chi-square or logistic regression.

6) Chi-square goodness-of-fit test to “expected success rate” from literature  
- Inappropriate because:  
  - The goal is comparison between randomized groups, not between observed proportion and a fixed external proportion.  
  - External comparisons do not account for current sample structure.

7) Multiple unplanned post hoc tests for different cutoffs without adjustment  
- Inappropriate because:  
  - Increases Type I error; may lead to cherry-picked significant results.  
  - Should be pre-specified or handled as clearly labeled exploratory analyses with caution.

8) Unadjusted multiple t-tests across many time points without accounting for multiplicity  
- Inappropriate because:  
  - Inflates Type I error; multiplicity needs to be controlled if many tests are run.  

9) Structural equation modeling / complex multivariate models  
- Inappropriate because:  
  - Sample size (n=88) is too small for complex multivariate frameworks; overfitting and non-identifiability would be a major concern.  
  - The research question is straightforward and does not warrant such complexity.
