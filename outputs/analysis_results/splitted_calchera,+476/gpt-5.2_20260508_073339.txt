## CATEGORY A — Mandatory tests (analysis would be incomplete)

### A1. Overall between-group comparison of apical leakage (5 groups)
**Test name:**  
- If leakage is **continuous**: **One-way ANOVA** (with Welch variant if variances unequal)  
- If leakage is **ordinal/non-normal**: **Kruskal–Wallis test**

**Purpose:** Test whether apical leakage differs among the 5 obturation/sealer combinations.

**Why appropriate (≥2):**
1. Outcome is compared across **>2 independent groups** (parallel design).
2. Provides the essential global inference before pairwise comparisons (controls type I error structure).

**Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
- ANOVA: sensitive to **non-normality** and **heteroscedasticity** with small/unequal n.
- Kruskal–Wallis: tests distributional differences; not specifically differences in means; less informative for effect sizes on original scale.

**When NOT to use:**
- If multiple slices per tooth are analyzed as separate observations without accounting for clustering (violates independence).
- If outcome is binary (e.g., leakage yes/no) → use logistic methods.

**Alternative if assumptions violated:**
- For continuous: **Welch ANOVA** (unequal variances), or **permutation ANOVA**.
- For ordinal: **ordinal logistic regression** (see Recommended).

---

### A2. Multiple comparisons / planned contrasts aligned to hypotheses
**Test name:**  
- After ANOVA: **Tukey HSD** (all-pairs) or **planned contrasts** with multiplicity control  
- After Kruskal–Wallis: **Dunn test** with Holm adjustment

**Purpose:** Identify which groups differ; specifically test the study’s two null hypotheses.

**Why appropriate (≥2):**
1. Directly addresses clinically relevant contrasts (BioRoot vs ZOE; warm vs cold within BioRoot).
2. Controls family-wise error or FDR when making multiple pairwise inferences.

**Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
- Tukey assumes roughly normal residuals and homogeneity (less critical with equal n, but still).
- Dunn on ordinal data may have reduced power and does not incorporate covariates.

**When NOT to use:**
- If only **pre-specified** comparisons are intended, do not use all-pairs Tukey (unnecessary multiplicity penalty).
- If analysis is model-based (e.g., regression), use model-based contrasts instead.

**Alternative if assumptions violated:**
- **Permutation-based pairwise tests** with multiplicity correction.
- **Bootstrap CIs** for pairwise differences.

*Minimum planned contrasts to match hypotheses (examples):*
- **Sealer effect within warm carrier-based:** Group 2 vs Group 1 (BioRoot vs ZOE with Thermafil)
- **Sealer effect within warm vertical:** Group 5 vs Group 3 (BioRoot vs ZOE with warm vertical)
- **Warm vs cold within BioRoot:** Group 4 (single cone BioRoot) vs Group 5 (warm vertical BioRoot) and/or vs Group 2 (Thermafil BioRoot)

---

### A3. Inter-rater agreement (two independent observers)
**Test name:**  
- If outcome is **ordinal**: **Weighted Cohen’s kappa**  
- If outcome is **continuous**: **Intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC, two-way random, absolute agreement)**

**Purpose:** Quantify reliability of leakage scoring between observers and justify combining/averaging ratings.

**Why appropriate (≥2):**
1. Two observers independently scored the same specimens → reliability is essential to validate measurement.
2. Agreement affects inference (high disagreement inflates noise and may bias group comparisons).

**Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
- Kappa is sensitive to **prevalence** and marginal distributions.
- ICC assumes approximately continuous scale and can be distorted by restricted range.

**When NOT to use:**
- If a consensus score is produced *before* recording independent ratings (no longer independent).
- If each observer scored different teeth (not the case here).

**Alternative if assumptions violated:**
- **Gwet’s AC1/AC2** (more stable than kappa under prevalence imbalance) for categorical/ordinal.
- **Bland–Altman analysis** (Recommended) for continuous.

## CATEGORY B — Recommended tests (enhance rigor)

### B1. Regression modeling to separate “sealer” and “technique” effects (partially crossed design)
**Test name:**  
- If continuous: **Linear model (ANCOVA/linear regression)** with factors for group or for technique+sealer (with careful parameterization)  
- If ordinal: **Ordinal logistic regression (proportional odds model)**  
- If clustering (slice-level or rater-level retained): **Mixed-effects model** (linear mixed model or cumulative link mixed model)

**Purpose:** Estimate effect sizes for sealer/technique while accommodating the study’s design nuances and potential clustering.

**Why appropriate (≥2):**
1. The design is not a clean 2×3 factorial (missing ZOE+single cone), so modeling via **group indicators** or constrained contrasts is clearer than “factorial ANOVA”.
2. Mixed models can correctly handle **multiple slices per tooth** and/or **two ratings per tooth** without violating independence.

**Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
- Requires adequate sample size per parameter; with small n, models may be unstable.
- Ordinal proportional odds assumption may be violated.

**When NOT to use:**
- If only one tooth-level summary is used and simple group comparisons fully address hypotheses (then regression is optional but still helpful).
- If the ordinal scale has very few levels and sparse cells (model convergence issues).

**Alternative if assumptions violated:**
- For non-normal continuous residuals: **robust regression** or **quantile regression**.
- For violated proportional odds: **partial proportional odds model** or **multinomial logistic regression**.

---

### B2. Assumption checks (for parametric analyses)
**Test name:**  
- **Shapiro–Wilk** (normality of residuals)  
- **Levene’s test** or **Brown–Forsythe** (homogeneity of variances)

**Purpose:** Validate whether ANOVA/linear model assumptions are reasonable.

**Why appropriate (≥2):**
1. Supports transparent choice of parametric vs nonparametric methods.
2. Detects heteroscedasticity that can inflate type I error.

**Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
- With moderate n, these tests can be over/under-powered; graphical diagnostics are often more informative.

**When NOT to use:**
- As the sole justification for modeling choice; do not “test your way” into analysis without considering effect sizes/robustness.

**Alternative if assumptions violated:**
- **Welch ANOVA**, **robust standard errors (HC3)**, transformations, or nonparametric/permutation methods.

---

### B3. Effect sizes and confidence intervals
**Test name:**  
- For ANOVA: **eta-squared / partial eta-squared**, **omega-squared**, plus **pairwise mean differences with 95% CI**  
- For Kruskal–Wallis/Dunn: **Cliff’s delta** or **rank-biserial correlation** with CI

**Purpose:** Quantify magnitude and precision beyond p-values.

**Why appropriate (≥2):**
1. Microleakage differences need clinical interpretation (not just significance).
2. Facilitates comparison across techniques and future meta-analysis.

**Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
- Some effect sizes are sensitive to distribution shape and outliers; interpretation may be less intuitive.

**When NOT to use:**
- Do not report only standardized effect sizes without raw-scale differences (clinical meaning lost).

**Alternative if assumptions violated:**
- **Bootstrap CIs** for mean/median differences and effect sizes.

---

### B4. Bland–Altman analysis (if continuous outcome)
**Test name:** **Bland–Altman plot with limits of agreement**

**Purpose:** Visualize observer agreement and any systematic bias across leakage magnitude.

**Why appropriate (≥2):**
1. Complements ICC by showing **bias** and **heteroscedastic disagreement**.
2. Helps decide whether averaging observers is defensible.

**Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
- Not suitable for purely ordinal scores.

**When NOT to use:**
- If leakage is scored categorically/ordinally.

**Alternative if assumptions violated:**
- For ordinal: agreement tables + weighted kappa / Gwet AC.

## CATEGORY C — Optional (supplementary insights)

### C1. Trend/ordered alternatives across “warmness” or technique hierarchy (if prespecified)
**Test name:** **Jonckheere–Terpstra test** (ordinal trend)

**Purpose:** Test for a monotonic trend in leakage across an ordered set of techniques (if a credible order is prespecified).

**Why appropriate (≥2):**
1. More powerful than Kruskal–Wallis when an **a priori ordering** exists.
2. Works well for ordinal outcomes.

**Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
- Requires defensible ordering; may be arbitrary (Thermafil vs warm vertical not clearly “more/less”).

**When NOT to use:**
- If no pre-specified, scientifically justified order exists.

**Alternative if assumptions violated:**
- Pairwise contrasts or ordinal regression with appropriate coding.

---

### C2. Sensitivity analyses for how slice-level data are summarized
**Test name:** Sensitivity comparisons using:
- tooth-level **max**, **mean**, or **apical-most slice** leakage

**Purpose:** Show robustness of conclusions to the outcome aggregation rule.

**Why appropriate (≥2):**
1. Sectioning yields potentially multiple measurements per tooth.
2. Leakage is often clinically driven by worst-case (max) vs typical-case (mean).

**Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
- Post hoc multiple analyses can inflate false positives if not clearly labeled as sensitivity.

**When NOT to use:**
- If only one standardized apical-third measure exists per tooth.

**Alternative if assumptions violated:**
- Mixed-effects model retaining all slices with tooth random effect.

---

### C3. Outlier and influence diagnostics (continuous models)
**Test name:** **Cook’s distance / leverage / DFBetas**

**Purpose:** Evaluate whether a small number of specimens drive results.

**Why appropriate (≥2):**
1. In vitro leakage data can be skewed with occasional extreme leakage.
2. Enhances credibility of group difference findings.

**Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
- With very small sample sizes, influence measures can be unstable.

**When NOT to use:**
- For purely ordinal models (different diagnostics needed).

**Alternative if assumptions violated:**
- Robust regression / trimming / winsorizing with transparent reporting.

## TASK 3 — Inappropriate tests (should NOT be used)

1. **Paired t-test / Wilcoxon signed-rank test** for group comparisons  
   - Groups are **independent** (each tooth in only one group).

2. **Chi-square test of independence** (as primary analysis)  
   - Outcome is leakage score/amount (ordinal/continuous), not simple categorical counts unless it’s dichotomized (dichotomization would waste information).

3. **Simple multiple t-tests without multiplicity correction**  
   - Inflates type I error across 5-group comparisons.

4. **Factorial two-way ANOVA (Sealer × Technique) as if fully crossed**  
   - The design appears **incomplete** (no ZOE + single cone group), so standard interaction terms are not estimable/interpretable without constraints.

5. **Treating slices or rater scores as independent replicates in ANOVA/KW**  
   - Violates independence (pseudo-replication); requires clustering handling or tooth-level summary.

6. **Pearson correlation** between groups/techniques  
   - Not relevant: predictors are categorical experimental assignments.

Use a **tooth-level primary leakage outcome** (or a mixed-effects model if slice-level is retained), quantify **inter-observer reliability**, then perform a **global 5-group comparison** followed by **multiplicity-controlled planned contrasts** that directly test the two null hypotheses (BioRoot vs ZOE within warm techniques; BioRoot warm vs BioRoot cold). Prefer **regression/mixed models** to handle the partially crossed design and any clustering, and report **effect sizes with confidence intervals** for interpretability.
