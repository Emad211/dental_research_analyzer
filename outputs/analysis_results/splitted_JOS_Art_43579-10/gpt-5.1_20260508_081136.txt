CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS  
-----------------------------  

A1. Descriptive statistics and visualization  
- Purpose:  
  - Summarize central tendency and variability of anxiety scores before and after microsurgery; help assess distributional assumptions.  
- What to do:  
  - For VAS_pre and VAS_post:  
    - Mean, standard deviation, median, interquartile range, minimum, maximum.  
  - For ΔVAS = VAS_post – VAS_pre:  
    - Mean, SD, median, IQR.  
  - For binary anxiety (≥3):  
    - n (%) anxious pre and post.  
  - Graphs:  
    - Boxplots or violin plots for VAS_pre vs VAS_post.  
    - Histogram or kernel density of ΔVAS.  
    - Paired line plot (spaghetti plot) to show individual changes.  
- Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):  
  1. Necessary to understand central tendency and variability before choosing/in­terpreting inferential tests.  
  2. Helps assess normality and potential outliers, guiding choice between parametric and non-parametric tests.  
- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - Some descriptive indices (e.g., mean/SD) may be misleading if distribution is highly skewed; in such case, median/IQR more informative.  
- When not to use:  
  - Never “not use” descriptives; they are always appropriate, but certain summaries (like mean) shouldn’t be emphasized when assumptions are clearly violated.  
- Alternatives if assumptions are violated:  
  - Emphasize medians and non-parametric visualizations (e.g., median with IQR, empirical cumulative distribution function).  

A2. Paired t-test (for continuous VAS: pre vs post)  
- Purpose:  
  - Test whether the mean dental anxiety score differs before and after microsurgery.  
- Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):  
  1. Data are paired measurements (same subjects measured twice), exactly matching the paired t-test structure.  
  2. VAS scores are often treated as approximately continuous, and with n ≈ 35, the t-test is reasonably robust to mild non-normality of the difference scores.  
- Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason):  
  - The distribution of ΔVAS (post – pre) might be markedly non-normal (e.g., heavy skew, outliers, ceiling/floor effects), making the t-test less valid.  
- When this test should NOT be used:  
  - If ΔVAS is highly skewed or has extreme outliers and transformations do not yield approximate normality.  
  - If the scale is deemed strictly ordinal and analysis must respect that.  
- Alternative if assumptions are violated:  
  - Wilcoxon signed-rank test (see B1) for comparing matched pairs using only rank information.  

A3. Effect size estimation for pre–post difference  
- Purpose:  
  - Quantify the magnitude of change in anxiety, not just statistical significance.  
- What to do:  
  - Compute mean difference: MD = mean(VAS_post – VAS_pre) with 95% confidence interval.  
  - Standardized effect size for paired data (Cohen’s d for paired samples) = MD / SD(ΔVAS).  
- Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):  
  1. Effect sizes are necessary to interpret the clinical relevance of any difference, especially in small samples where p-values alone may be misleading.  
  2. Paired Cohen’s d is standard for pre–post continuous outcomes and can be compared with other studies.  
- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - With strongly skewed data or prominent floor/ceiling effects, Cohen’s d may not reflect clinically meaningful change.  
- When not to use:  
  - When the distribution is extremely non-normal and only non-parametric summaries are considered acceptable; then rank-based effect sizes might be preferred.  
- Alternative if assumptions are violated:  
  - Report non-parametric effect size measures such as:  
    - Rank-biserial correlation for Wilcoxon signed-rank test.  
    - Median difference with Hodges–Lehmann estimate and 95% CI.  

CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED TESTS  
-------------------------------  

B1. Wilcoxon Signed-Rank Test (pre vs post VAS)  
- Purpose:  
  - Test whether the distribution of VAS_post differs from VAS_pre for paired data without assuming normality.  
- Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):  
  1. Appropriate for paired or matched samples when the difference scores are not normally distributed.  
  2. VAS is ordinal/continuous; Wilcoxon uses rank information and is robust to outliers and non-normality with small–moderate n.  
- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - Less powerful than the paired t-test when normality holds.  
  - It tests differences in distributions (medians), not strictly mean differences.  
- When this test should NOT be used:  
  - When differences have many exact ties (e.g., many patients mark exactly same pre and post scores), which reduces the effective sample size and can distort p-values.  
  - When the variable is binary (then McNemar’s test is appropriate).  
- Alternative if assumptions are violated:  
  - If discrete outcomes with many ties or small sample: sign test (very conservative).  
  - If more time points are available (not in this study), Friedman test.  

B2. McNemar’s Test (for change in binary anxiety status)  
- Purpose:  
  - Evaluate whether the proportion of patients classified as “anxious” (VAS ≥ 3) changes from pre to post microsurgery, accounting for pairing.  
- Implementation:  
  - Construct 2×2 table:  
    - Rows: Anxiety_pre (yes/no).  
    - Columns: Anxiety_post (yes/no).  
  - Apply McNemar’s test to discordant pairs.  
- Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):  
  1. Designed for paired nominal data with two categories, which is the case for anxiety status (yes/no) measured twice.  
  2. Incorporates within-subject correlation properly, unlike an unpaired chi-square.  
- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - If nearly all patients are either anxious at both times or not anxious at both times, there may be too few discordant pairs to support the test.  
- When this test should NOT be used:  
  - When sample size is extremely small or when no discordant pairs exist (then no inference about change is possible).  
  - When the outcome has more than two categories (then use Bowker’s test of symmetry).  
- Alternative if assumptions are violated:  
  - Exact McNemar’s test (binomial-based) for small samples or few discordant pairs.  
  - For multi-category ordinal outcomes (e.g., categorized VAS in >2 levels), use Stuart–Maxwell or Bowker’s test.  

B3. Normality and distribution checks (Shapiro–Wilk test + Q–Q plots)  
- Purpose:  
  - Objectively and visually assess whether the differences ΔVAS approximate normality, supporting or discouraging use of the paired t-test.  
- Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):  
  1. Shapiro–Wilk is standard for small–moderate sample sizes and has good power to detect non-normality.  
  2. Q–Q plots provide visual assessment that complements formal tests (which can be overly sensitive or insensitive depending on n).  
- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - With n = 35, Shapiro–Wilk might flag minor deviations from normality that do not materially affect the t-test; decision should not be based on p-value alone.  
- When this test should NOT be used:  
  - For extremely small n (e.g., ≤10) where such tests have very low power.  
- Alternative if assumptions are violated:  
  - Use non-parametric tests (Wilcoxon signed-rank) and focus on medians.  

B4. Sensitivity analyses (parametric vs non-parametric comparison)  
- Purpose:  
  - Assess robustness of conclusions by comparing results of paired t-test and Wilcoxon signed-rank test.  
- Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):  
  1. Ensures that conclusions (e.g., “no significant difference”) are not heavily dependent on modeling assumptions.  
  2. With borderline normality, consistent results across tests strengthen confidence.  
- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - Multiplicity of tests could confuse interpretation if results diverge; must pre-specify main analysis and label others as sensitivity checks.  
- When this should NOT be used:  
  - When sample size is extremely small and one of the tests is clearly invalid; choose most appropriate approach and stick with it.  
- Alternative if assumptions are violated:  
  - If non-parametric and parametric results conflict, prioritize the test whose assumptions are better supported (often Wilcoxon) and discuss reasons.  

B5. Confidence intervals for proportions (pre and post anxiety prevalence)  
- Purpose:  
  - Quantify uncertainty around proportion of anxious patients (VAS ≥3) before and after microsurgery.  
- Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):  
  1. Complements McNemar’s test by providing interpretable ranges for pre- and post-anxiety prevalence.  
  2. Useful for comparing with other studies or planning future trials.  
- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - With very small n in a group (e.g., very few anxious subjects), standard Wald CIs may be inaccurate.  
- When this should NOT be used:  
  - Never omit CIs entirely; but avoid naive Wald intervals in small samples.  
- Alternative if assumptions are violated:  
  - Use exact (Clopper–Pearson) or Wilson score confidence intervals for proportions.  

CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL TESTS  
---------------------------  

C1. Sign Test (for pre–post difference)  
- Purpose:  
  - Simple non-parametric test for paired data that uses only the direction of change (ignores magnitude).  
- Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):  
  1. Requires minimal assumptions; robust when data have many ties or are ordinal with questionable spacing.  
  2. Simple to compute and interpret as number of increases vs decreases.  
- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - Less powerful than Wilcoxon signed-rank test because it discards magnitude information.  
- When this test should NOT be used:  
  - When sample size is limited and more efficient non-parametric tests (Wilcoxon) are available and valid.  
- Alternative if assumptions are violated:  
  - Wilcoxon signed-rank test is generally preferable unless assumptions of symmetry are grossly violated.  

C2. Exploratory regression of post anxiety on pre anxiety  
- Purpose:  
  - Describe the relationship between pre- and post-microsurgery anxiety quantitatively (e.g., is post anxiety strongly predicted by pre anxiety?).  
- Model:  
  - Simple linear regression: VAS_post = β0 + β1 × VAS_pre + ε.  
- Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):  
  1. Provides insight into how much variation in post anxiety is explained by baseline anxiety (R²).  
  2. Can help inform whether baseline anxiety alone is a sufficient predictor or whether other covariates (if available) should be considered in future studies.  
- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - With only 35 patients and no additional predictors, clinical utility may be limited; regression assumptions (linear relationship, homoscedasticity) may not hold.  
- When this should NOT be used:  
  - To infer causal effects (pre → post) beyond what the design allows; regression here is purely descriptive.  
- Alternative if assumptions are violated:  
  - Use non-parametric correlation (Spearman’s rho) between VAS_pre and VAS_post.  

C3. Non-parametric effect size for Wilcoxon (rank-biserial correlation)  
- Purpose:  
  - Quantify magnitude of change when using Wilcoxon signed-rank test.  
- Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):  
  1. Provides an interpretable effect size that aligns with the Wilcoxon test’s rank-based approach.  
  2. Useful for meta-analyses or comparisons across studies that also used non-parametric methods.  
- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - Interpretation can be less intuitive for clinicians compared to mean differences or Cohen’s d.  
- When this should NOT be used:  
  - If the primary analysis is parametric and the audience is unfamiliar with rank-based measures; may be unnecessary.  
- Alternative if assumptions are violated:  
  - Use Hodges–Lehmann estimator (median of pairwise differences) with CI as a more intuitive summary.  

C4. Graphical exploratory analyses  
- Purpose:  
  - Provide qualitative understanding of data patterns.  
- What to do:  
  - Paired spaghetti plot to show individual trajectories.  
  - Bland–Altman plot for pre vs post VAS to inspect agreement and systematic bias.  
- Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):  
  1. Visual inspection may reveal subgroups (patients whose anxiety increases vs decreases).  
  2. Supports transparency and understanding for readers beyond p-values.  
- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - With only 35 points, some plots may be less informative or look noisy.  
- When this should NOT be used:  
  - Graphs should not be used to replace formal inferential tests for main claims.  
- Alternative if assumptions are violated:  
  - None needed; graphical methods are flexible; they just need careful interpretation.

TASK 3 – INAPPROPRIATE TESTS  
=============================  

Tests that should NOT be used for this study, with rationale:  

1. Independent (unpaired) t-test for comparing pre vs post VAS  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Pre and post measurements are on the same individuals; observations are not independent.  
  - Using an independent t-test ignores within-subject correlation, underestimates standard error, and can yield misleading p-values and CIs.  

2. Pearson’s chi-square test for independent proportions (for pre vs post anxiety prevalence)  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Pre and post anxiety states are paired within the same subjects; using a standard chi-square test for independence assumes independent groups.  
  - Proper method is McNemar’s test (paired binary outcome).  

3. One-way ANOVA (between-group)  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - No multiple independent groups; only one group measured twice.  
  - Repeated-measures ANOVA would be theoretically possible for >2 time points, but with only 2 time points, paired t-test suffices and is equivalent to a 2-level repeated-measures ANOVA.  

4. Repeated-measures ANOVA with more than two levels or factors  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Study has only two time points; a 1-factor (time) repeated-measures ANOVA would be identical to a paired t-test but unnecessarily complex.  
  - No additional within- or between-subject factors are specified.  

5. Logistic regression for change in binary anxiety (pre vs post)  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - With only 35 subjects and no covariates, logistic regression modeling change is overkill and may mis-handle pairing unless sophisticated longitudinal modeling is used.  
  - The correct simple test is McNemar’s, specifically designed for paired binary outcomes.  

6. Survival analysis methods (Kaplan–Meier, Cox regression)  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - No time-to-event outcome; only two fixed time-point measures of anxiety.  

7. Correlation test between “pre vs post” scores as a means to evaluate change  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Correlation between VAS_pre and VAS_post reflects association, not change. High correlation can exist even if the mean changes substantially, and vice versa; it does not answer the primary research question.  

8. Parametric tests assuming more complex distributional structures (e.g., mixed-effects models)  
- Why inappropriate for this dataset:  
  - Only two time points, no random clustering beyond subject, no additional covariates described; mixed models add complexity without gain.
