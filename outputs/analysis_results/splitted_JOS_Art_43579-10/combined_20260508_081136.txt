## CATEGORY A – MANDATORY tests

### A1. Paired t-test (primary continuous comparison)
- **Purpose:** Test whether mean anxiety differs **pre vs post** within the same individuals.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Design is **paired/repeated measures** (each participant measured twice).
  2. Outcome (VAS distance) is treated as **approximately continuous**, commonly analyzed with parametric methods.
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - ΔVAS may be **non-normal** with n=35, or VAS may show ceiling/floor effects.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Clear strong non-normality of ΔVAS (e.g., heavy skew/outliers) that cannot be reasonably addressed, or VAS treated strictly as ordinal.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Wilcoxon signed-rank test** (A2) and/or **bootstrap CI** for mean/median paired difference.

### A2. Wilcoxon signed-rank test (nonparametric paired comparison)
- **Purpose:** Test whether the **median** within-person change differs from 0.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Handles **non-normal** paired differences.
  2. Suitable when VAS is considered **ordinal/non-Gaussian** or has outliers.
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - Assumes the distribution of paired differences is **symmetrical** (often overlooked).
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Many ties/zero differences or markedly asymmetric differences; or if interest is specifically in the **mean** difference.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Sign test** (less powerful but fewer assumptions) or **permutation test** for paired differences; report **bootstrap CI**.

### A3. McNemar test (paired binary outcome: “anxious” yes/no)
- **Purpose:** Compare proportion anxious **pre vs post** using the cut-off (e.g., VAS ≥3).
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Same individuals classified twice → **paired nominal** data.
  2. Specifically tests change in marginal proportions using **discordant pairs**.
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - Dichotomizing VAS **loses information** and power; results depend on a possibly arbitrary cut-off.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If any cell counts are extremely small without using an exact approach; or if the cut-off is not validated for this population/procedure.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Exact McNemar test** (binomial exact) when discordant counts are small.

## CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED tests/analyses

### B1. Normality/outlier diagnostics on paired differences
- **Test name:** Shapiro–Wilk test on `ΔVAS` (plus graphical checks: Q–Q plot).
- **Purpose:** Assess whether paired differences are approximately normal to support paired t-test.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. The parametric primary analysis relies on **normality of differences**, not of raw scores.
  2. Helps justify choice between t-test vs nonparametric/robust methods.
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - With n=35, normality tests can be **underpowered** or overly sensitive; decisions should not rely on p-values alone.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - As the sole criterion to choose the method; avoid “test then choose” as a rigid rule.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Use **robust methods** (bootstrap CI for mean difference; Yuen’s trimmed-mean test for paired data) and present both parametric and nonparametric results as sensitivity.

### B2. Effect size estimation for paired designs
- **Test/metric name:** Paired standardized mean difference (**Cohen’s dz**) with 95% CI.
- **Purpose:** Quantify magnitude of change beyond statistical significance.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. With small samples, p-values alone are misleading; effect sizes convey **clinical relevance**.
  2. Paired effect sizes account for within-subject correlation.
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - If VAS is heavily non-normal or treated as ordinal, dz may be less interpretable.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When reporting only categorical outcomes; or when distribution makes mean/SD unrepresentative.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Rank-biserial correlation** for Wilcoxon signed-rank; or **Cliff’s delta** on paired differences.

### B3. Confidence intervals via bootstrap for paired change
- **Test name:** Nonparametric bootstrap CI for mean or median ΔVAS.
- **Purpose:** Provide robust uncertainty estimates without strong distributional assumptions.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Useful for small-to-moderate n and non-normal ΔVAS.
  2. Produces interpretable **CI for change**, aligning with estimation-focused reporting.
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - If data have many ties/limited unique values, bootstrap distributions can be unstable.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Extremely small sample sizes or complex dependence structures (not the case here).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Exact methods** (for medians) or **permutation CI** for paired differences.

### B4. Agreement/association between pre and post VAS
- **Test name:** Intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC) or Pearson/Spearman correlation (descriptive).
- **Purpose:** Describe consistency/relationship of anxiety levels before and after.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Characterizes whether individuals retain rank/order (high pre tends to high post).
  2. Helps interpret whether “no mean change” hides **individual variability**.
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - Correlation/ICC does **not** test change and can be misinterpreted as treatment effect.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - As evidence of “no difference” or “effect”; it addresses association, not mean change.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Use **Spearman** instead of Pearson; report paired plots.

## TASK 3: Inappropriate tests (should NOT be used)

1. **Independent-samples t-test / Mann–Whitney U**
   - Wrong because observations are **paired** (same participants pre and post), violating independence.

2. **One-way ANOVA for pre vs post**
   - Overcomplicated and typically assumes independent groups; paired structure is not handled unless using **repeated-measures ANOVA** (but with only 2 time points, paired t-test is equivalent and clearer).

3. **Chi-square test of independence (unpaired) for anxious yes/no pre vs post**
   - Ignores pairing; correct approach is **McNemar** (or exact McNemar).

4. **Pearson correlation between pre and post as a test of “difference”**
   - Correlation measures association, not change; high correlation can coexist with large mean change and vice versa.

5. **Linear regression treating pre and post rows as independent without subject ID**
   - Violates independence; would underestimate SE and inflate type I error.

6. **Multiple-comparisons post hoc tests (e.g., Tukey)**
   - Not relevant: only two time points and no multiple groups described.
