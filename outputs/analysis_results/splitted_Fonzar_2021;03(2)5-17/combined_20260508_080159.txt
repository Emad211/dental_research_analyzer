# CATEGORY A – MANDATORY

## A1. Kaplan–Meier (KM) survival estimation (with 95% CI)
- **Purpose:** Estimate tooth **survival function** over 20 years; report survival at 20 years.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Handles **right-censoring** (common in long follow-up).
  2. Directly matches the reported endpoint “20-year clinical survival rate”.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - Treats other event types (e.g., extraction for non-endodontic causes) as the same failure unless defined otherwise; also ignores clustering unless adjusted.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When the primary question is **cause-specific failure** with substantial **competing risks** (KM overestimates incidence for specific causes).
- **Alternative if assumptions/structure violated:**
  - **Cumulative incidence function (CIF)** under competing risks; or use **marginal Cox with robust SE** if clustering is main concern for inference.

## A2. Log-rank test (univariable survival comparisons)
- **Purpose:** Compare survival curves across key groups (e.g., one- vs two-session; indication groups; radiolucency yes/no; primary vs retreatment).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Standard nonparametric test for **time-to-event** differences with censoring.
  2. Useful screening prior to multivariable modeling.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Sensitive to **non-proportional hazards** and ignores covariate adjustment and clustering.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Strong **non-proportional hazards** or when patient-level clustering is severe and unaddressed.
- **Alternative:**
  - **Weighted log-rank** (e.g., Fleming–Harrington) for non-PH patterns; or proceed directly to **Cox model with robust SE/frailty**.

## A3. Multivariable Cox proportional hazards regression (with clustering adjustment)
- **Purpose:** Estimate adjusted associations between predictors (age, sessions, indication, radiolucency, retreatment) and **hazard of failure/extraction**.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Models **time-to-event** with censoring and yields interpretable **hazard ratios**.
  2. Allows **multivariable adjustment** for confounding; can account for clustering using **robust sandwich SE** (patient as cluster) or a **shared frailty** term.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - If **proportional hazards (PH)** assumption is violated, or if exact event times are poorly known.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If event times are only known in coarse intervals (then discrete-time models may be better), or if competing risks are central and cause-specific hazards are not the desired estimand.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - For PH violation: **time-varying coefficients**, stratified Cox, or **accelerated failure time (AFT)** models.
  - For competing risks: **Fine–Gray** (subdistribution) or **cause-specific Cox** depending on estimand.

#### A2. Estimation of survival (time-to-event) – Kaplan–Meier analysis

**Test/Method name**  
- Kaplan–Meier survival analysis (with log-rank test for comparisons).

**Purpose**  
- To estimate survival probabilities of endodontically treated teeth over up to 20 years.  
- To compare survival curves between subgroups (e.g., one vs two sessions; preoperative radiolucency yes/no).

**Why appropriate (2 reasons)**  
1. Tooth survival clearly has a time dimension; failure (extraction, loss) may occur at different times; K–M can handle variable follow-up and censoring.  
2. Log-rank test provides a non-parametric comparison of survival distributions across groups, common in dental prognosis studies.

**Why it may not be appropriate (1 reason)**  
- If the exact time of failure is not known (only status at 20 years), then survival analysis is not feasible; only cross-sectional outcome at 20 years can be analyzed.

**When this test should NOT be used**  
- When there is **no time-to-event information** (only “failed/not failed at a fixed time point”), or when censoring information is unreliable.

- If only fixed 20-year status is known: use **logistic regression** (binary outcome).

## TASK 3 – INAPPROPRIATE TESTS

Based on the type of research question and outcome, the following are **not appropriate or clearly inferior**:

1. **Independent-samples t-test / ANOVA as main inferential tools for survival outcomes**  
   - Why not:
     - They cannot handle censoring or time-to-event nature of data.  
     - They ignore varying follow-up durations.  
   - They might describe differences in mean follow-up time but not prognosis.

2. **Simple chi-square tests as the only method for comparing prognosis between groups**  
   - Why not:
     - Only compares proportions at 20 years ignoring time; inaccurate when follow-up varies.  
     - Do not adjust for confounders; oversimplify complex multivariable relationships.

3. **Linear regression of “years survived” without censoring adjustment**  
   - Why not:
     - Censored observations bias results; linear regression assumes all times are fully observed.  
     - Outcome distribution is non-normal and bounded by follow-up time.

4. **Paired t-tests or Wilcoxon signed-rank tests**  
   - Inappropriate unless there are genuine paired measurements (e.g., pre- and post-treatment continuous scores for the same tooth), which are not indicated here. They do not address survival/failure as an outcome.

5. **Correlation coefficients (Pearson/Spearman) as primary analyses of prognosis**  
   - Why not:
     - Correlation is symmetric and does not reflect causal or prognostic relationships well.  
     - Cannot appropriately handle binary or time-to-event outcomes with censoring.

6. **Multiple testing without adjustment across many predictors**  
   - Why not:
     - Inflates type I error. Regression-based, multivariable modeling is more appropriate.

These tests can still be used in **limited descriptive or exploratory roles** (e.g., chi-square for simple associations), but they must not replace appropriate survival or regression modeling when studying long-term prognosis.

Given the topic—20-year prognosis of root canal treatment in teeth from patients with advanced periodontitis—the appropriate analytical strategy is:
