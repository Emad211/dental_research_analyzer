# CATEGORY A – MANDATORY

## A1. Kaplan–Meier (KM) survival estimation (with 95% CI)
- **Purpose:** Estimate tooth **survival function** over 20 years; report survival at 20 years.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Handles **right-censoring** (common in long follow-up).
  2. Directly matches the reported endpoint “20-year clinical survival rate”.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - Treats other event types (e.g., extraction for non-endodontic causes) as the same failure unless defined otherwise; also ignores clustering unless adjusted.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When the primary question is **cause-specific failure** with substantial **competing risks** (KM overestimates incidence for specific causes).
- **Alternative if assumptions/structure violated:**
  - **Cumulative incidence function (CIF)** under competing risks; or use **marginal Cox with robust SE** if clustering is main concern for inference.

## A2. Log-rank test (univariable survival comparisons)
- **Purpose:** Compare survival curves across key groups (e.g., one- vs two-session; indication groups; radiolucency yes/no; primary vs retreatment).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Standard nonparametric test for **time-to-event** differences with censoring.
  2. Useful screening prior to multivariable modeling.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Sensitive to **non-proportional hazards** and ignores covariate adjustment and clustering.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Strong **non-proportional hazards** or when patient-level clustering is severe and unaddressed.
- **Alternative:**
  - **Weighted log-rank** (e.g., Fleming–Harrington) for non-PH patterns; or proceed directly to **Cox model with robust SE/frailty**.

## A3. Multivariable Cox proportional hazards regression (with clustering adjustment)
- **Purpose:** Estimate adjusted associations between predictors (age, sessions, indication, radiolucency, retreatment) and **hazard of failure/extraction**.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Models **time-to-event** with censoring and yields interpretable **hazard ratios**.
  2. Allows **multivariable adjustment** for confounding; can account for clustering using **robust sandwich SE** (patient as cluster) or a **shared frailty** term.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - If **proportional hazards (PH)** assumption is violated, or if exact event times are poorly known.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If event times are only known in coarse intervals (then discrete-time models may be better), or if competing risks are central and cause-specific hazards are not the desired estimand.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - For PH violation: **time-varying coefficients**, stratified Cox, or **accelerated failure time (AFT)** models.
  - For competing risks: **Fine–Gray** (subdistribution) or **cause-specific Cox** depending on estimand.

# CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED

## B1. Competing risks analysis (CIF + Fine–Gray and/or cause-specific Cox)
- **Purpose:** Separate risk of **extraction for endodontic reasons** vs **other reasons** (periodontal/prosthetic/fracture), if coded.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Prevents biased interpretation when multiple mutually exclusive failure causes exist.
  2. Aligns with conclusion reporting “only 0.9% for endodontic reasons”.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - If cause of extraction is unreliable/misclassified or not recorded.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If only “extraction yes/no” is known with no valid cause attribution.
- **Alternative:**
  - Treat all extractions as a single composite endpoint (standard Cox/KM), and present cause breakdown descriptively.

## B2. Model diagnostics and assumption checks
### (i) PH assumption checks (Schoenfeld residuals, log–log plots)
- **Purpose:** Validate Cox model assumptions.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Ensures hazard ratios are meaningful over time.
  2. Detects time-varying effects (likely over 20 years).
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - With few events, tests have low power and may be unstable.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Not “never”, but interpret cautiously with sparse events.
- **Alternative:**
  - Pre-specify clinically plausible time interactions; use AFT models.

### (ii) Functional form for age (restricted cubic splines)
- **Purpose:** Avoid incorrect linearity assumption for age.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Age effects are often **nonlinear**.
  2. Improves fit and reduces residual confounding.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Requires adequate event counts to support flexible terms.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Very small number of failures.
- **Alternative:**
  - Categorize age (with loss of information) or use fractional polynomials.

## B3. Cluster handling sensitivity analysis (robust SE vs frailty)
- **Purpose:** Assess robustness to within-patient correlation (multiple teeth per patient).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Tooth outcomes within a patient are not independent.
  2. Frailty can quantify unobserved patient heterogeneity.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Frailty models can be unstable with small clusters or sparse events.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If most patients contribute only one tooth (minimal clustering).
- **Alternative:**
  - Marginal Cox with **sandwich estimator** (often more stable).

## B4. Multiple imputation for missing covariates (if applicable)
- **Purpose:** Reduce bias and preserve power when baseline predictors are missing.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Long-term datasets often have missing covariates.
  2. MI is generally preferable to complete-case analysis under MAR.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - If missingness is MNAR and not addressable with available data; or if outcome times/censoring not properly incorporated.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When missingness is trivial (<~5%) and complete-case is defensible.
- **Alternative:**
  - Inverse probability weighting; sensitivity analyses (best/worst case).

# CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL

## C1. 20-year survival probability via complementary log–log discrete-time model
- **Purpose:** If follow-up is recorded in **intervals** (annual visits), model discrete hazards.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Better when event times are interval-censored/visit-based.
  2. Can incorporate time-varying covariates (e.g., restoration status).
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Less familiar to dental audiences; needs proper time structure.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When exact event dates are known and Cox is sufficient.
- **Alternative:**
  - Standard Cox / AFT.

## C2. Net survival / multi-state model (tooth: complication → treated → extraction)
- **Purpose:** Model “complications successfully treated” as intermediate states.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Separates pathways to extraction from reversible complications.
  2. Quantifies transition intensities.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Requires detailed timing of complications and treatments.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If complication onset/treatment dates are not recorded.
- **Alternative:**
  - Composite endpoint; or recurrent event methods if multiple complications.

## C3. Interaction testing (effect modification)
- **Purpose:** Evaluate whether predictors differ by, e.g., retreatment status, radiolucency, tooth type.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Clinically plausible heterogeneity (e.g., age effect differs by indication).
  2. Improves clinical interpretability.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Inflates type I error; low power with limited events.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Sparse events or many interactions.
- **Alternative:**
  - Pre-specified limited interactions; shrinkage/penalization.

## C4. Penalized regression (ridge/LASSO/Firth correction) for sparse events
- **Purpose:** Stabilize estimates if endodontic-cause extractions are rare (0.9% suggests sparsity).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Reduces overfitting with many covariates vs events.
  2. Handles separation/small-sample bias.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Coefficients less straightforward; requires tuning choices.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Adequate events per parameter and stable standard Cox is fine.
- **Alternative:**
  - Pre-specify limited covariates; combine categories.

## TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS (and why)

- **Independent-samples t-test / one-way ANOVA** for “survival rate at 20 years”  
  - Survival is time-to-event with censoring; these ignore censoring and timing.
- **Simple chi-square/Fisher’s exact** comparing extracted vs not at 20 years as the primary analysis  
  - Discards time information and mishandles differential follow-up/censoring.
- **Paired t-test / repeated-measures ANOVA**  
  - Not applicable unless continuous repeated outcomes are measured; survival outcome is not continuous repeated measurement.
- **Linear regression on time-to-extraction** among only extracted teeth  
  - Selection bias (conditions on failure) and ignores censoring.
- **Pearson correlation** between predictors and “survival”  
  - Wrong outcome type; confounding and censoring not addressed.
