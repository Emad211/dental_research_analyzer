#### A2. Estimation of survival (time-to-event) – Kaplan–Meier analysis

**Test/Method name**  
- Kaplan–Meier survival analysis (with log-rank test for comparisons).

**Purpose**  
- To estimate survival probabilities of endodontically treated teeth over up to 20 years.  
- To compare survival curves between subgroups (e.g., one vs two sessions; preoperative radiolucency yes/no).

**Why appropriate (2 reasons)**  
1. Tooth survival clearly has a time dimension; failure (extraction, loss) may occur at different times; K–M can handle variable follow-up and censoring.  
2. Log-rank test provides a non-parametric comparison of survival distributions across groups, common in dental prognosis studies.

**Why it may not be appropriate (1 reason)**  
- If the exact time of failure is not known (only status at 20 years), then survival analysis is not feasible; only cross-sectional outcome at 20 years can be analyzed.

**When this test should NOT be used**  
- When there is **no time-to-event information** (only “failed/not failed at a fixed time point”), or when censoring information is unreliable.  
- When hazards are extremely non-proportional and the only interest is cause-specific cumulative incidence in the presence of competing risks (in which case K–M for a specific cause can be misleading).

**Alternative if assumptions are violated**  
- If only fixed 20-year status is known: use **logistic regression** (binary outcome).  
- If competing risks (e.g., extraction for non-endodontic reasons vs endodontic reasons) are central: use **cumulative incidence functions** and **Gray’s test** or competing risk regression (Fine–Gray model).

---

#### A3. Multivariable regression to identify predictors of failure

Given the study conclusions explicitly identify predictors, a regression model is mandatory. Choice depends on whether time-to-event data exist and how outcomes are defined:

##### A3a. Cox proportional hazards regression (preferred if times are available)

**Purpose**  
- To evaluate the association between predictors (age, sessions, indication, preoperative radiolucency, retreatment) and the **hazard of tooth failure** over time.

**Why appropriate (2 reasons)**  
1. Accommodates censored time-to-event data and varying follow-up times.  
2. Estimates adjusted hazard ratios, allowing simultaneous control for multiple confounders.

**Why it may not be appropriate (1 reason)**  
- If proportional hazards assumption is strongly violated for key predictors and cannot be remedied by time-varying effects or stratification.

**When this test should NOT be used**  
- When only a binary status at 20 years is known with no time stamps.  
- When event count is too low relative to number of predictors (risk of overfitting).

**Alternative if assumptions are violated**  
- Use **parametric survival models** (e.g., Weibull, log-logistic) or **flexible parametric survival models**, or if only binary outcome at 20 years is available, use **multivariable logistic regression**.  
- If competing risks are critical (endodontic vs non-endodontic failure), use **cause-specific Cox** or **Fine–Gray competing risks regression**.

##### A3b. Logistic regression (if only 20-year status is known)

**Purpose**  
- To model the odds of failure (or survival) at 20 years as a function of predictors.

**Why appropriate (2 reasons)**  
1. Suitable for a binary outcome (extracted vs survived) at a fixed time point.  
2. Allows multivariable adjustment, yielding odds ratios and confidence intervals.

**Why it may not be appropriate (1 reason)**  
- Odds ratios may overstate risk if outcome is common (e.g., if failure >10–20%); misinterpretation as risk ratio is common.

**When NOT to use**  
- When there is substantial variation in follow-up time and censoring; survival methods are preferable.  
- When sample size or events per variable are too low for reliable multivariable fitting.

**Alternative if assumptions are violated**  
- Use **penalized logistic regression** (Firth’s correction, ridge, or lasso) if sparse data or separation occur.  
- Use **Poisson or log-binomial regression** to estimate risk ratios if interest is in risk rather than odds.

### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED ANALYSES

These add rigor and insight but are not absolutely essential.

#### B1. Multilevel (clustered) modeling to account for teeth within patients

**Test/Method name**  
- Frailty model for Cox (shared frailty) or mixed-effects logistic regression / GEE.

**Purpose**  
- To account for **intra-patient correlation** when multiple teeth per patient are included.

**Why appropriate (2 reasons)**  
1. Teeth from the same patient are not statistically independent; ignoring clustering underestimates SEs and inflates type I error.  
2. Reflects biological and behavioral factors shared within patients (e.g., hygiene, systemic conditions).

**Why it may not be appropriate (1 reason)**  
- If only one tooth per patient is analyzed, or if the number of patients is too small to support random effects estimation.

**When NOT to use**  
- When design intentionally includes only one tooth per patient, or when clusters are extremely unbalanced and small.

**Alternative if assumptions are violated**  
- Use **robust sandwich variance estimators** (cluster-robust SEs) for Cox or logistic models instead of random effects.  
- Use **GEE** for marginal estimates with robust SEs.

---

#### B2. Competing risks analysis

**Test/Method name**  
- Competing risks regression (Fine–Gray) and cumulative incidence functions.

**Purpose**  
- To separate and model **endodontic vs non-endodontic reasons** for tooth loss as distinct failure types.

**Why appropriate (2 reasons)**  
1. The same tooth cannot fail for both endodontic and non-endodontic reasons; these are competing risks.  
2. Standard K–M for “endodontic failure” treating other causes as censored overestimates incidence if competing risks are substantial.

**Why it may not be appropriate (1 reason)**  
- If the number of events for specific cause (e.g., endodontic failure = 0.9% of teeth) is extremely low, estimates may be unstable.

**When NOT to use**  
- When cause-of-failure information is unreliable or missing for many extracted teeth.  
- When overall survival is the only clinically relevant endpoint.

**Alternative if assumptions are violated**  
- Use **cause-specific Cox regression** (model hazard for each cause separately) if Fine–Gray is unstable.  
- If even cause-specific models are too unstable, analyze only overall failure vs survival.

---

**Purpose**  
- To explore whether the effect of predictors differs across clinically relevant subgroups, e.g.:
  - Anterior vs posterior teeth.

**Why appropriate (2 reasons)**  
1. Clinicians may want to know if certain subgroups (e.g., older patients) have distinctly different prognosis.  
2. Helps detect effect modification, which can influence treatment planning.

**Why it may not be appropriate (1 reason)**  
- Increased risk of false positives with multiple subgroup analyses; may be underpowered.

**When NOT to use**

- When not pre-specified in the analysis plan.

**Alternative if assumptions are violated**  
- Limit to a few **pre-specified** interactions and report them as exploratory, with appropriate caution and adjustment (e.g., false discovery rate) if many tests are run.

#### B4. Sensitivity analyses

**Purpose**  
- To assess robustness of findings to key assumptions and analytic choices, e.g.:
  - Different definitions of “failure” (e.g., counting “successfully treated complications” as success vs failure).  
  - Alternative categorizations of age or radiolucency.  
  - Excluding teeth with incomplete data.

**Why appropriate (2 reasons)**  
1. Helps ensure conclusions are not driven by arbitrary definitions or modeling choices.  
2. Builds credibility, especially in a long-term retrospective study where missing data and variable follow-up are common.

**Why it may not be appropriate (1 reason)**  
- Excessive or post-hoc sensitivity analyses can look like data dredging and produce contradictory results.

**When NOT to use**

**Alternative if assumptions are violated**

## TASK 3 – INAPPROPRIATE TESTS

Based on the type of research question and outcome, the following are **not appropriate or clearly inferior**:

1. **Independent-samples t-test / ANOVA as main inferential tools for survival outcomes**  
   - Why not:
     - They cannot handle censoring or time-to-event nature of data.  
     - They ignore varying follow-up durations.  
   - They might describe differences in mean follow-up time but not prognosis.

2. **Simple chi-square tests as the only method for comparing prognosis between groups**  
   - Why not:
     - Only compares proportions at 20 years ignoring time; inaccurate when follow-up varies.  
     - Do not adjust for confounders; oversimplify complex multivariable relationships.

3. **Linear regression of “years survived” without censoring adjustment**  
   - Why not:
     - Censored observations bias results; linear regression assumes all times are fully observed.  
     - Outcome distribution is non-normal and bounded by follow-up time.

4. **Paired t-tests or Wilcoxon signed-rank tests**  
   - Inappropriate unless there are genuine paired measurements (e.g., pre- and post-treatment continuous scores for the same tooth), which are not indicated here. They do not address survival/failure as an outcome.

5. **Correlation coefficients (Pearson/Spearman) as primary analyses of prognosis**  
   - Why not:
     - Correlation is symmetric and does not reflect causal or prognostic relationships well.  
     - Cannot appropriately handle binary or time-to-event outcomes with censoring.

6. **Multiple testing without adjustment across many predictors**  
   - Why not:
     - Inflates type I error. Regression-based, multivariable modeling is more appropriate.

These tests can still be used in **limited descriptive or exploratory roles** (e.g., chi-square for simple associations), but they must not replace appropriate survival or regression modeling when studying long-term prognosis.
