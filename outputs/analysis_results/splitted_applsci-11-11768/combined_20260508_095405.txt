# CATEGORY A — Mandatory (analysis incomplete without these)

## A1) Descriptive statistics (baseline and follow-up)
- **Test/Method:** Descriptive summaries (mean±SD or median[IQR], counts/percent; plots).
- **Purpose:** Describe cohort characteristics and outcome distributions at each time point.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Needed to characterize sample and assess comparability across prognostic groups (e.g., smokers vs non-smokers).
  2. Informs choice of modeling approach (distributional shape; sparse outcomes).
- **Why may not be appropriate:** Not a hypothesis test; cannot infer associations.
- **When NOT to use:** Never “instead of” inferential modeling for prognostic conclusions.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:** Use robust summaries (median/IQR) and distribution plots.

## A2) Primary prognostic modeling for success at 12 months — multivariable logistic regression
- **Test:** **Binary logistic regression** (multivariable).
- **Purpose:** Estimate association between preoperative factors and **probability of success** at 12 months (odds ratios).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Primary question is *which factors affect outcome*; logistic regression directly models binary success/failure.
  2. Allows **confounding control** (age, smoking, lesion size, baseline symptoms, etc.).
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - If success definition is not binary or if events are too few causing separation/overfitting.
- **When this test should NOT be used:**
  - When outcome is ordinal/continuous and dichotomization would discard information (unless binary success is explicitly primary).
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
  - **Penalized logistic regression (Firth)** for small-sample/rare events or separation.
  - **Poisson regression with robust SE** to estimate risk ratios if outcome is common and RR preferred.

## A3) Longitudinal analysis of radiographic healing (PAI over time) — ordinal mixed model (or GEE)
- **Test:** **Cumulative link mixed model (ordinal mixed-effects model)** for PAI at baseline/6/12 months (random intercept for patient).
- **Purpose:** Model change in **PAI (ordinal)** over time and association with predictors.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. PAI is **ordinal**; ordinal models respect ranking without assuming equal intervals.
  2. Accounts for **within-patient correlation** across repeated follow-ups.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - If proportional odds assumption fails, or if PAI has sparse categories.
- **When this test should NOT be used:**
  - When PAI is treated as continuous with normality assumptions *without justification*; or when only one time point exists.
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
  - **Partial proportional odds model**.
  - Collapse PAI into fewer clinically meaningful categories and use **mixed logistic** (e.g., healed vs not).
  - **GEE ordinal model** (population-averaged) if random effects not desired.

## A4) Longitudinal analysis of clinical signs (binary) — GEE logistic or mixed logistic
- **Test:** **Mixed-effects logistic regression** or **GEE with logit link** for repeated binary outcomes (each sign or composite clinical healing).
- **Purpose:** Assess how clinical symptom prevalence changes from 6 to 12 months and whether predictors affect trajectories.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Clinical signs are **binary** and repeated.
  2. Accounts for **correlation** within individuals over time.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Too few events per sign → unstable models.
- **When this test should NOT be used:**
  - When analyzing repeated outcomes with ordinary chi-square tests ignoring within-person correlation.
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
  - Use **exact methods** for sparse data (conditional exact logistic) at a single time point.
  - Combine signs into a **composite** clinical failure indicator to increase event count.

# CATEGORY B — Recommended (adds rigor)

## B1) Model diagnostics and assumption checks
- **Method:** 
  - Logistic model: linearity in logit (restricted cubic splines), multicollinearity (VIF), influential points, calibration.
  - Ordinal model: proportional odds tests; residual checks where applicable.
- **Purpose:** Validate modeling assumptions and robustness.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Prognostic conclusions depend on model validity.
  2. Identifies need for transformations or non-linear terms.
- **Why may not be appropriate:** Some checks have low power in small n=40.
- **When NOT to use:** Don’t over-interpret formal tests; prefer graphical diagnostics.
- **Alternatives:** Penalization/bootstrapping for stability.

## B2) Non-linear effects for continuous predictors (age, lesion size)
- **Test/Method:** **Restricted cubic splines** (within regression).
- **Purpose:** Capture non-linear associations without arbitrary categorization.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Lesion size effects may not be linear.
  2. Avoids information loss from cut-points.
- **Why may not be appropriate:** Overfitting risk with small sample.
- **When NOT to use:** If events are too few; then keep linear term or clinically pre-specified categories.
- **Alternative:** Fractional polynomials; or pre-specified categories with caution.

## B3) Agreement/reliability for PAI scoring (if >1 rater or repeated scoring)
- **Test:** **Weighted Cohen’s kappa** (2 raters) or **ICC** (if treating PAI quasi-continuously), intra-rater kappa.
- **Purpose:** Quantify reproducibility of radiographic scoring.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. PAI is observer-dependent; reliability affects outcome validity.
  2. Weighted kappa accounts for ordinal nature.
- **Why may not be appropriate:** If only one rater and no repeat scoring performed.
- **When NOT to use:** When the scale is not ordinal or categories extremely imbalanced (kappa paradox).
- **Alternative:** Gwet’s AC1/AC2 for imbalanced categories.

## B4) Multiple testing control for numerous signs/symptoms
- **Test/Method:** **Holm** or **Benjamini–Hochberg (FDR)** adjustment.
- **Purpose:** Control false positives when testing many clinical endpoints.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Several signs are assessed; unadjusted testing inflates type I error.
  2. Improves interpretability of secondary analyses.
- **Why may not be appropriate:** If analyses are clearly labeled exploratory and limited.
- **When NOT to use:** Don’t adjust the single pre-specified primary endpoint.
- **Alternative:** Emphasize effect sizes/CIs; pre-specify a composite endpoint.

## B5) Missing data handling and sensitivity
- **Method:** Describe missingness; **multiple imputation** for covariates/outcomes (where defensible); sensitivity analyses (best/worst-case for success).
- **Purpose:** Reduce bias from incomplete follow-up.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Lost follow-up can bias estimates in longitudinal studies.
  2. MI better than complete-case if MAR plausible.
- **Why may not be appropriate:** MI unstable with very small n and complex models.
- **When NOT to use:** If missing not at random and no auxiliary info; then do sensitivity analyses.
- **Alternative:** Inverse probability weighting; pattern-mixture sensitivity.

# CATEGORY C — Optional (supplementary insights)

## C1) McNemar test (paired binary change from 6→12 months) for a single symptom
- **Test:** **McNemar’s test**.
- **Purpose:** Test within-patient change in a binary sign between two follow-ups.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Paired binary data at two time points.
  2. Simple summary of improvement.
- **Why may not be appropriate:** Does not adjust for covariates; limited to two time points.
- **When NOT to use:** When there are >2 time points or need adjustment for predictors.
- **Alternative:** Mixed/GEE logistic model.

## C2) Wilcoxon signed-rank test for change in PAI (baseline→12 months)
- **Test:** **Wilcoxon signed-rank** (paired ordinal).
- **Purpose:** Unadjusted within-subject change in PAI.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. PAI is ordinal; test does not assume normality.
  2. Useful as a simple corroborative analysis.
- **Why may not be appropriate:** Cannot include covariates; ignores intermediate time point.
- **When NOT to use:** As the main analysis of predictors.
- **Alternative:** Ordinal mixed model.

## C3) Kaplan–Meier / time-to-healing (only if healing time is well-defined)
- **Test:** **Kaplan–Meier** curves + **Cox regression**.
- **Purpose:** Model time until “healed” if healing date is known (more than just 6/12 month visits).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Handles censoring.
  2. Provides dynamic view of healing.
- **Why may not be appropriate:** With only fixed visit assessments (6 and 12 months), time-to-event is interval-censored.
- **When NOT to use:** If exact healing time is unknown.
- **Alternative:** **Interval-censored survival models**; or stick to longitudinal mixed models.

## C4) Interaction testing (effect modification)
- **Test:** Interaction terms (e.g., smoking×lesion size, age×smoking) in regression.
- **Purpose:** Identify subgroups with different prognostic effects.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Clinically plausible effect modification.
  2. Can guide personalized prognosis.
- **Why may not be appropriate:** Low power / high overfitting risk with n=40.
- **When NOT to use:** If not pre-specified and events are few.
- **Alternative:** Stratified descriptive analyses; penalized models.

## TASK 3 — Inappropriate tests (should NOT be used)

1. **Independent-samples t-test / ANOVA** to compare “PAI means” between groups  
   - **Why not:** PAI is **ordinal**, often non-normal, and repeated over time; mean comparisons ignore correlation and scale properties.

2. **Pearson chi-square tests** comparing 6- and 12-month outcomes as if independent samples  
   - **Why not:** Same patients measured twice; violates independence → wrong p-values.

3. **Multiple separate univariable tests only (no multivariable model)** to claim “factors affecting outcome”  
   - **Why not:** Confounding likely (e.g., smoking correlated with age); unadjusted associations are not sufficient for prognostic inference.

4. **Linear regression on binary success**  
   - **Why not:** Predictions outside [0,1], heteroskedastic errors; logistic/Poisson robust is appropriate.

5. **Repeated-measures ANOVA** for PAI  
   - **Why not:** Assumes continuous normally distributed outcome and sphericity; PAI is ordinal.

### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY ANALYSES

These are essential to answer the stated research question.

#### A1. Descriptive statistics

- **Purpose:** Summarize patient, tooth, and lesion characteristics, and overall healing rates.

#### A2. Comparison of baseline characteristics between outcome groups

**Primary outcome grouping:**
- Group 1: Successful healing at 12 months.
- Group 2: Unsuccessful/uncertain healing at 12 months.

**A2.1. Chi-square test (or Fisher’s exact test) for categorical predictors**

- **Variables:**
  - Smoking status (smoker vs non-smoker).
  - Sex (male vs female).
  - Preoperative symptoms (present vs absent).
  - Age categorized (e.g., <30 vs ≥30, or other clinically meaningful cut-offs) – if used as categorical.
- **Test name:** Pearson’s Chi-square test; Fisher’s exact test when needed.

**Purpose:**
- Examine association between categorical preoperative factors and binary healing outcome.

**Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):**
1. Outcome and predictors are categorical; the test is designed to evaluate independence/association in contingency tables.
2. Sample size per cell is moderate (40 total); with proper grouping, expected frequencies can be adequate for chi-square; otherwise Fisher’s exact test is suitable.

**Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason):**
- With small cell counts (e.g., very few smokers), chi-square assumptions on expected frequencies (<5) may be violated, inflating type I error.

**When not to use:**
- When >20% of expected cell counts are <5 or any expected cell <1.
- When the predictor has many sparse categories (e.g., multiple symptom patterns with small n).

**Alternative if assumptions violated:**
- **Fisher’s exact test** for 2×2 tables.
- **Exact chi-square** or **Monte Carlo** methods for larger but sparse tables.
- Consider collapsing categories (e.g., grouping several symptom types) to increase cell counts.

---

**A2.2. Independent samples t-test or Mann–Whitney U test for continuous predictors**

- **Variables:**
  - Age (years).
  - Preoperative lesion size (mm or mm²).

**Test name:**
- Independent samples t-test (if parametric assumptions met).
- Mann–Whitney U test (nonparametric alternative).

**Purpose:**
- Compare mean (or median) age and lesion size between success vs failure groups.

**Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):**
1. Outcome is binary, predictor is continuous – standard 2-group comparison framework.
2. With reasonably symmetric distributions and adequate sample size per group, t-test provides powerful comparison of means; if distribution is skewed or outlier-prone, Mann–Whitney is robust.

**Why it may not be appropriate:**
- t-test: sensitive to non-normality and unequal variances in small samples.
- Mann–Whitney: tests distribution shift, not necessarily means; interpretation requires care.

**When not to use:**
- t-test should not be used if:
  - Normality assumption clearly violated and n per group very small (e.g., <15).
  - Variance heterogeneity is extreme.
- Mann–Whitney should be avoided if:
  - Data are heavily tied or ordinal with few categories; consider ordinal methods instead.

**Alternatives if assumptions violated:**
- **Welch’s t-test** if variances unequal but approximate normality holds.
- **Mann–Whitney U test** if severe non-normality or outliers.

#### A3. Multivariable logistic regression for predictors of healing

- **Test name:** Binary logistic regression.

**Outcome:**
- Success (1) vs failure/uncertain (0) at 12 months.

**Candidate predictors:**
- Age (continuous).
- Sex (male/female).
- Smoking status (smoker vs non-smoker).
- Preoperative lesion size (continuous or categorized into clinically meaningful groups).
- Preoperative symptoms (present vs absent).
- Possibly baseline PAI score (if available) and other relevant covariates (e.g., cause of lesion – failed RCT vs open apex due to trauma).

**Purpose:**
- Estimate adjusted associations (odds ratios and 95% CI) between preoperative factors and healing, controlling for confounders.

**Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):**
1. Outcome is binary; logistic regression is the standard method for modeling binary outcomes with multiple predictors.
2. Allows simultaneous adjustment for multiple factors, providing more accurate estimates of each factor’s independent association with healing.

**Why it may not be appropriate:**
- With only 40 patients and limited number of events (failures), a full multivariable model with many predictors risks overfitting and unstable estimates.

**When not to use:**
- When events-per-variable (EPV) ratio is too low. A rough guideline: at least 10 events per predictor; with e.g., 10 failures, we should include at most 1 predictor (or 2 in penalized models).

**Alternative if assumptions violated:**
- **Penalized logistic regression** (e.g., Firth’s correction) to reduce small-sample bias.
- **Exact logistic regression** if there are rare exposures (e.g., very few smokers).
- **Univariate logistic regression** plus careful interpretation when sample size is too limited for multivariable models; restrict adjustment to key confounders.

#### A4. Analysis of longitudinal radiographic outcome (pre vs 6 vs 12 months)

Assuming PAI is recorded on the same teeth at different times.

**A4.1. Repeated measures analysis for PAI (ordinal/continuous approach)**

- **If PAI treated as continuous-like (scores 1–5):**
  - **Test name:** Friedman test (nonparametric repeated-measures ANOVA) or repeated-measures ANOVA if assumptions hold.

**Purpose:**
- Evaluate whether there is a statistically significant change in PAI scores over time (pre-op, 6 months, 12 months).

**Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):**
1. Same subjects measured at multiple time points → within-subject design.
2. Friedman test does not require normality and is suitable for ordinal data like PAI.

**Why it may not be appropriate:**
- PAI is ordinal, not strictly continuous; repeated-measures ANOVA may not be suitable unless assumptions (approximate normality, equal intervals) are acceptable.
- Small sample size may limit power.

**When not to use:**
- Repeated measures ANOVA should not be used if:
  - Data are not approximately normal and PAI is clearly ordinal.
  - Sphericity assumption is violated (and cannot be corrected reasonably given small n).
- Friedman test should not be used if:
  - There are substantial missing values at one or more time points (it requires complete cases).

**Alternative if assumptions violated:**
- **Wilcoxon signed-rank test** comparing two selected time points (e.g., baseline vs 12 months).
- **Ordinal mixed-effects models** (cumulative logit mixed models) if more advanced modeling is possible and sample size permits (likely borderline with n=40).

**A4.2. Evolution of lesion size (if measured continuously)**

- **Test name:** Paired t-test (baseline vs 12 months) or Wilcoxon signed-rank test.

**Purpose:**
- Quantify and test reduction in lesion size over time.

**Why appropriate:**
1. Paired design (same tooth at two time points).
2. Continuous measurement of lesion size suits paired t-test if normality of differences holds; otherwise Wilcoxon signed-rank is robust.

**Reasons against:**
- If lesion size is poorly measured (large measurement error) or heavily skewed, standard parametric tests may mislead.

**Alternative:**
- Wilcoxon signed-rank test if normality violated.
- Bootstrap confidence intervals for mean change.

## TASK 3 – INAPPROPRIATE TESTS

Tests that should not be used for this study (with reasons):

1. **Student’s t-test or ANOVA for PAI (as if it were continuous) without justification**
   - PAI is ordinal; intervals between categories are not guaranteed equal.
   - Treating PAI as continuous ignores its ordinal nature and may violate assumptions of normality and homoscedasticity.

2. **Linear regression with binary outcome (e.g., “healing” as 0/1)**
   - Violates assumptions:
     - Predicted values can fall outside [0, 1].
     - Residuals are not normally distributed nor homoscedastic.
   - Logistic regression is the appropriate model.

3. **Parametric repeated-measures ANOVA on clearly non-normal ordinal data without transformation or checks**
   - Repeated-measures ANOVA assumes continuous normally distributed outcomes and sphericity; PAI is ordinal.
   - Friedman or ordinal mixed models are more appropriate.

4. **Multiple uncorrected pairwise tests across time points and predictors without overall plan**
   - Increases type I error inflation.
   - Prefer structured repeated-measures tests or models with appropriate control of family-wise error or false discovery rate.

5. **Chi-square tests on sparse multi-category tables without considering exact methods**
   - If any cell has expected count < 1 or >20% of cells have expected counts < 5, chi-square p-values become unreliable.
   - Fisher’s exact or exact/Monte-Carlo methods should be used instead.

6. **Paired t-test for non-normal, highly skewed lesion size differences without checking assumptions**
   - With small sample size and skewed differences, type I error control is compromised.
   - Wilcoxon signed-rank test is safer.

7. **Complex high-parameter models (e.g., Cox with many covariates, multivariable ordinal logistic regression with 5–6 predictors) in n=40**
   - Leads to overfitting, unstable estimates, failure to converge, and misleading inferences.

## TASK 4 – SUMMARY AND JUSTIFICATION OF ANALYTICAL APPROACH

The study is a prospective cohort of 40 patients undergoing standardized periapical surgery, designed to identify preoperative predictors (age, smoking, lesion size, preoperative symptoms) of postoperative healing assessed clinically and radiographically.

The analytical approach should therefore:

1. **Describe the cohort** clearly (descriptive statistics) to contextualize results.
2. **Compare preoperative characteristics** between healed vs non-healed groups using chi-square / Fisher’s exact and t-test / Mann–Whitney, as appropriate, to identify potential prognostic factors.
3. **Model the outcome multivariately** with logistic regression, to adjust for confounding and estimate independent effects of key predictors (age, smoking, lesion size, symptoms), while respecting limitations of the small sample (e.g., limiting number of predictors, using penalized methods if needed).
4. **Account for the longitudinal nature of healing** by examining changes in PAI and lesion size over time using appropriate repeated-measures nonparametric methods (Friedman, Wilcoxon) and considering ordinal methods if outcome categories are used.
5. **Evaluate robustness and reliability** through model diagnostics, sensitivity analyses for outcome definitions, and interrater reliability for PAI if applicable.

This plan balances methodological rigor with the constraints of sample size and measurement scales, uses standard and widely accepted methods for binary and longitudinal outcomes in endodontic surgery, and allows clinically meaningful interpretation of the influence of age, smoking, and lesion size on periapical healing after surgical endodontic treatment.
