## TASK 1 — Information extraction (study characteristics)

### Study design and setting
- **Design:** CONSORT-labeled **parallel-group randomized clinical trial** (1:1 allocation).
- **Setting:** Single academic center (Dept. of Conservative Dentistry & Endodontics, PGIDS, Rohtak, India).
- **Recruitment period:** **Dec 2018–Jun 2019**
- **Registration/Ethics:** ClinicalTrials.gov **NCT04358887**; IEC approval **PGIDS/IEC/2018/26**.
- **Operator:** **Single surgeon/operator** performed all procedures (operator not blinded).
- **Blinding:**
  - **Patients blinded** to group.
  - **Intraoperative bleeding evaluators blinded** (sham piezo device present in both groups).
  - Hemorrhage scored by **2 blinded observers + surgeon**, but **only blinded observers’ assessment used**; discrepancies resolved by **consensus discussion**.

### Objectives
- **Primary aim:** Evaluate whether **piezoelectric surgery** affects **patients’ quality of life (QoL) during the first week postsurgery**.
- **Secondary aim:** Compare **intraoperative hemorrhage control** between **piezosurgery vs carbide bur**.

### Participants (eligibility)
- **Inclusion:**
  - Medically fit for oral surgery (**ASA I–II**), **16–55** years.
  - Previously treated teeth with symptomatic/asymptomatic apical periodontitis.
  - **Large periapical lesion ≥5 mm**, **<20 mm** (maxillary/mandibular anterior teeth).
  - **Intact buccal cortical plate on CBCT**.
  - Adequate root canal treatment on PA radiograph and CBCT; appropriate final restoration; no coronal leakage.
- **Exclusion:**
  - Systemic disease (e.g., diabetes, uncontrolled HTN grade III, hepatic/renal disease, bleeding disorders), pregnancy/lactation, anticoagulant/antiplatelet drugs.
  - Teeth unrestorable/fractured/perforated; apicomarginal defects; periodontal probing depth **>4 mm**.

### Randomization and unit of analysis concern
- **Randomization unit stated:** **40 patients** randomized (20/arm) using **opaque envelopes** in blocks (block size not specified).
- **Teeth:** **74 teeth** across 40 patients ⇒ **some patients contributed >1 tooth**, creating **within-patient clustering** (non-independence) for outcomes measured per tooth or per surgical site.

### Interventions (groups)
- **Experimental:** Piezoelectric device for **bone cutting and lesion/tissue separation**, plus piezo use for resection/refinement in that group.
- **Control:** **Surgical carbide bur** for osteotomy and **curettes** for debridement; carbide bur for root-end resection.
- **Shared in both groups:**
  - Microsurgical approach with operating microscope.
  - Root-end preparation: piezoelectric ultrasonic unit with retro tips in **both** groups.
  - Root-end filling: **MTA**.
  - Post-op: CHX 0.2% rinse x 1 week; **ibuprofen 400 mg PRN**; no antibiotics.

### Outcomes / variables and measurement
1. **Intraoperative hemorrhage control (secondary outcome)**
   - **Scale:** ordinal **0/1/2** (Vy et al):
     - 0 = no control; 1 = intermittent slight bleeding; 2 = complete control.
   - Also dichotomized:
     - **Adequate = 2**
     - **Inadequate = 0 or 1**
   - **Raters:** 2 blinded observers (primary); consensus if disagreement.

2. **Postoperative quality of life (primary outcome domain) — assessed daily for 7 days**
   - **Instrument:** Modified “patient’s perception questionnaire” (Del Fabbro et al).
   - **Domains (Likert 1–5):**
     - Functional limitations: mouth opening, chewing, speaking, sleeping, daily routine, work.
     - Symptoms: swelling, nausea, bad taste/breath.
   - **Pain:** **VAS 0–100** daily.
   - **Analgesic use:** number of ibuprofen tablets **per day** for 7 days.
   - **Data capture:** patient self-completed **daily**; returned after 7 days; reminders via phone; training and validated local-language questionnaire provided.

### Sample size
- Based on **postoperative pain** difference:
  - Power **80%**, alpha **0.05**
  - Clinically significant difference **0.8** (units not clearly stated in text)
  - Pooled SD **0.824**
  - Required **16/group**; inflated for **20% attrition** ⇒ **40 patients total** (**20/group**).

## TASK 2 — Statistical analysis plan (SAP)

### Core analysis principles (apply to all outcomes)
- **Analysis populations**
  - **Primary:** Intention-to-treat (**ITT**) at the **patient level** (all randomized patients analyzed as allocated).
  - **Sensitivity:** Per-protocol (exclude major protocol deviations) if definable.
- **Unit of analysis**
  - Primary endpoint is **patient-reported** (QoL/pain/analgesics), naturally **patient-level repeated measures**.
  - If any outcomes are recorded per **tooth/surgical site**, use **clustered methods** (mixed models with patient random intercept or GEE) to address **multiple teeth per patient**.
- **Descriptives**
  - Baseline: age, sex, ASA class, tooth type/jaw (if available), lesion size, symptom status, etc.
  - Continuous: mean(SD) or median(IQR) depending on distribution; categorical: n(%).
- **Multiplicity**
  - Many QoL items across 7 days: pre-specify a **primary QoL endpoint** (e.g., pain AUC or mean pain days 1–7) and treat others as secondary/exploratory.
  - If multiple items tested, control **false discovery rate (FDR)** or use a hierarchical testing order.
- **Missing data**
  - Daily diaries commonly missing: use **mixed-effects models** (valid under MAR) as primary.
  - Sensitivity: **multiple imputation** (multilevel) and/or pattern-mixture sensitivity for MNAR if missingness is substantial.
- **Reporting**
  - Effect sizes with **95% CI**:
    - Continuous: mean difference or standardized mean difference; for repeated measures: model-based marginal mean difference.
    - Ordinal: proportional odds ratio.
    - Binary: risk ratio or odds ratio (with caution for interpretability).

# CATEGORY A — Mandatory tests/models

## A1) Linear mixed-effects model (LMM) for repeated VAS pain (0–100) over 7 days
- **Purpose:** Compare **pain trajectories** between groups across postoperative days (time effect, group effect, group×time interaction).
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Handles **repeated measures** within patient with correct correlation structure.
  2. Uses all available observations under **MAR**, reducing bias vs complete-case.
  3. Provides interpretable estimates (e.g., difference in mean pain on each day or averaged over days).
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - VAS may be **non-normal**, bounded (0–100), and potentially skewed with floor effects.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When distribution is extremely non-Gaussian with severe floor/ceiling effects and residual diagnostics show major violations that cannot be remedied.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **GLMM** with appropriate family (e.g., **beta regression** after scaling 0–1 and handling 0/1 inflation) or **robust LMM**; or **rank-based mixed model** / **GEE with robust SE**.

**Model specification (recommended):**  
Outcome = pain; fixed effects = group, day (categorical), group×day; random intercept for patient (and consider random slope for day). If multiple teeth per patient influence pain, still patient is the reporting unit.

---

## A2) Cumulative link (proportional odds) ordinal logistic regression for hemorrhage score (0/1/2)
- **Purpose:** Compare intraoperative hemorrhage control **on the original ordinal scale**.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Outcome is **ordered categorical**; proportional odds model respects ordering and uses all levels.
  2. Produces a single interpretable effect (odds of better hemorrhage control) and can incorporate clustering if needed.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - **Proportional odds assumption** may be violated (effect differs across thresholds).
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If proportional odds is clearly violated and partial proportional odds is not used; or if sample size is too small causing sparse cells.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Partial proportional odds model**; **multinomial logistic regression** (loses ordering); or **exact**/permutation approach for small samples; or **ordinal GEE** if clustering by patient/tooth.

---

## A3) Mixed-effects ordinal model (or GEE) for Likert QoL items (1–5) over 7 days
- **Purpose:** Compare group differences in **functional limitation and symptom ratings** across time.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Likert responses are **ordinal**; ordinal mixed models avoid treating them as interval without justification.
  2. Handles **within-patient repeated measures** and missing diary entries under MAR.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - Complex: many items and limited sample size may lead to **model instability**.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If sparse responses in some categories/day cause convergence failure.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Collapse categories (e.g., 1–2 vs 3 vs 4–5) and use **binary/ordinal GEE**; or analyze **summary scores** (see Recommended) with LMM/robust methods.

---

## A4) Model for analgesic intake counts over 7 days (Poisson/Negative Binomial mixed model or GEE)
- **Purpose:** Compare **analgesic consumption** between groups over time (daily counts) or total week count.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Analgesic tablets/day are **count data** (often skewed, many zeros).
  2. NB model addresses **overdispersion** common in medication use; mixed/GEE handles repeated measures.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - If nearly everyone has 0 or 1 tablet/day, standard count models may fit poorly.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If counts are not true counts (e.g., capped/ordinal reporting) or extreme zero-inflation not handled.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Zero-inflated negative binomial**; **hurdle model**; or analyze **binary use (any vs none)** with logistic mixed model.

---

# CATEGORY B — Recommended tests/models

## B1) Baseline comparability: t-test/Welch test or Mann–Whitney; chi-square/Fisher exact (descriptive, not for “testing randomization”)
- **Purpose:** Summarize baseline characteristics and identify potential imbalance to consider in adjusted models.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Provides transparent reporting of baseline distribution by arm.
  2. Helps justify covariate-adjusted sensitivity analyses when important imbalance exists.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - Hypothesis testing at baseline can be misleading (randomization already addresses confounding in expectation).
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Do not use baseline p-values to decide eligibility of covariates or to claim “groups are identical.”
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Use nonparametric equivalents (Welch/Mann–Whitney; Fisher exact).

---

## B2) Covariate-adjusted primary models (ANCOVA-type adjustment within mixed models)
- **Purpose:** Improve precision by adjusting for prognostic variables (e.g., baseline symptom status, lesion size, jaw/tooth location, sex/age) if measured.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Increases **statistical efficiency** and may reduce residual variance.
  2. Addresses chance imbalances and supports generalizability.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - Risk of **overfitting** given n=40, especially with many predictors.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When covariates are numerous relative to events/subjects or are post-randomization variables.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Use **penalized regression** (ridge) or pre-specify only 1–2 key covariates.

---

## B3) Area Under the Curve (AUC) / time-averaged summaries for pain and key QoL domains + between-group comparison
- **Purpose:** Provide a single clinically interpretable summary over 7 days (e.g., pain burden).
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Reduces multiplicity and simplifies interpretation vs testing each day.
  2. Captures overall postoperative experience (duration + intensity).
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - AUC can obscure **timing differences** (e.g., early vs late pain).
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If daily measurement times are inconsistent or missingness is extreme and not appropriately handled.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Use mixed model estimated marginal means averaged over days; or **nonparametric** comparison of AUC (e.g., permutation test).

---

## B4) Inter-rater reliability for hemorrhage scoring (between the 2 blinded observers)
- **Test:** **Weighted Cohen’s kappa** (ordinal) or **ICC (agreement)** depending on formulation.
- **Purpose:** Quantify consistency of bleeding scores prior to consensus.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Outcome is ordinal; weighted kappa accounts for partial agreement.
  2. Supports validity of the hemorrhage endpoint measurement.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - If only consensus scores are recorded and raw independent ratings are not retained.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When ratings are not independent (e.g., observers influenced each other during scoring).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Gwet’s AC2** (more stable than kappa under prevalence issues).

---

## B5) Sensitivity analysis accounting for multiple teeth per patient (if hemorrhage recorded per tooth/surgery)
- **Purpose:** Ensure inference is valid under clustering.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. 74 teeth in 40 patients implies non-independence risk.
  2. Mixed/GEE yields correct SEs and CIs.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - If hemorrhage and surgery are strictly **one per patient** in the analyzed dataset.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When clustering does not exist in the analysis unit.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Cluster-robust SEs at patient level.

# CATEGORY C — Optional (supplementary) analyses

## C1) Time-to-event analysis for “pain resolution” (e.g., first day VAS ≤10 and stays ≤10)
- **Test:** Kaplan–Meier + log-rank; Cox model.
- **Purpose:** Compare speed of recovery.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Captures clinically meaningful recovery timing.
  2. Handles censoring if diaries incomplete.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - Requires a clearly defined and validated “resolution” threshold; small sample limits precision.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If daily measurement is too coarse or missingness precludes identifying event time reliably.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Discrete-time survival model; or compare proportion resolved by day 7.

## C2) Mediation-style exploratory analysis: does hemorrhage control mediate QoL improvement?
- **Purpose:** Explore mechanism (piezo → better hemostasis → better QoL).
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Aligns with biological rationale stated in introduction.
  2. May guide future trials.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - Underpowered; strong assumptions for causal mediation unlikely met.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - As confirmatory inference or with substantial unmeasured confounding.
- **Alternative:**
  - Report stratified descriptive comparisons by hemorrhage category.

## C3) Subgroup analyses (pre-specified only if clinically justified): symptomatic vs asymptomatic, lesion size categories
- **Purpose:** Assess heterogeneity of treatment effect.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Could identify populations benefiting most.
  2. Generates hypotheses.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - Very small n → misleading interaction tests.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Post hoc, data-driven subgrouping.
- **Alternative:**
  - Present effect estimates with wide CIs without formal inference.

## TASK 3 — Tests that should NOT be used (and why)

1. **Multiple independent t-tests per day per outcome (without correction)**
   - Inflates **Type I error** dramatically and ignores within-patient correlation.

2. **Paired t-test between groups**
   - Groups are **independent** (parallel RCT), not matched pairs.

3. **Simple chi-square test treating hemorrhage score 0/1/2 as nominal without ordinality**
   - Discards ordering information; may be underpowered and less interpretable.

4. **Standard (non-clustered) regression treating each tooth as independent** (if >1 tooth per patient)
   - Violates independence; SEs too small → false positives.

5. **One-way ANOVA on Likert 1–5 items as if continuous** (as primary)
   - Likert is ordinal; ANOVA assumptions (interval scale, normal residuals) often unjustified—okay only as sensitivity with robust methods, not primary.

6. **Repeated-measures ANOVA requiring complete cases**
   - Diary data likely incomplete; RM-ANOVA is less flexible (sphericity, listwise deletion).

---

## TASK 4 — Summary justification of overall approach

A valid analysis must (i) respect the **randomized parallel-group** design, (ii) properly handle **repeated daily patient-reported outcomes** over 7 days, and (iii) analyze intraoperative bleeding on its **ordinal scale** while accounting for potential **clustering** (multiple teeth per patient) and missing diary entries. Mixed-effects (or GEE) models provide the most defensible primary framework because they address within-subject correlation, accommodate incomplete follow-up under reasonable assumptions, and yield clinically interpretable group differences with confidence intervals.
