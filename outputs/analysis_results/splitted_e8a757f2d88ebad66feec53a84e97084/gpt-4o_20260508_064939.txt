### TASK 1: INFORMATION EXTRACTION

#### Study Characteristics:
1. **Study Design**:  
   - Randomized Clinical Trial (RCT) following CONSORT guidelines.

2. **Objectives**:  
   - **Primary Objective**: To evaluate the impact of piezoelectric surgery on the quality of life (QoL) during the first week post-surgery.  
   - **Secondary Objective**: To compare intraoperative hemorrhage control between piezosurgery and conventional carbide burs.

3. **Study Groups**:  
   - **Piezo Group**: Patients undergoing surgery with a piezoelectric device.  
   - **Control Group**: Patients undergoing surgery with a conventional carbide bur.

4. **Sample Size**:  
   - Total: 40 patients (74 teeth).  
   - 20 patients in each group.  
   - Sample size calculation based on postoperative pain (clinically significant difference = 0.8, pooled SD = 0.824, power = 80%, significance level = 5%, and 20% attrition rate).

5. **Randomization**:  
   - Block randomization with opaque envelopes.  
   - Patients and independent observers were blinded, but operator blinding was not possible.

6. **Eligibility Criteria**:  
   - **Inclusion**:  
     - Medically fit patients (ASA I or II).  
     - Age: 16–55 years.  
     - Symptomatic/asymptomatic apical periodontitis in previously treated teeth with large periapical lesions (≥5 mm).  
     - Intact buccal cortical plate on CBCT imaging.  
     - Maxillary and mandibular anterior teeth with periradicular lesions <20 mm.  
     - Adequate root canal treatment and final restoration.  
   - **Exclusion**:  
     - Systemic diseases, pregnancy, lactation, anticoagulant/antiplatelet therapy.  
     - Teeth with deep pockets (>4 mm), fractures, perforations, or apicomarginal defects.

7. **Variables**:  
   - **Primary Outcome**:  
     - Quality of life (QoL) assessed using a modified patient perception questionnaire (5-point Likert scale).  
   - **Secondary Outcome**:  
     - Hemorrhage control during surgery (scored as 0, 1, or 2).  
   - **Other Variables**:  
     - Pain (VAS scale, 0–100).  
     - Analgesic intake (number of tablets/day).  

8. **Data Collection Methods**:  
   - QoL: Modified patient perception questionnaire (validated and translated into local language).  
   - Pain: Visual Analog Scale (VAS).  
   - Hemorrhage Control: Scored by blinded evaluators using a 3-point scale (0 = no control, 1 = intermittent control, 2 = complete control).  
   - Analgesic Intake: Self-reported by patients.  
   - Data collected daily for 7 days post-surgery.

9. **Surgical Procedure**:  
   - Performed under an operating microscope by a single operator.  
   - Piezo Group: Bone cutting with piezoelectric device (specific tips and settings detailed).  
   - Control Group: Bone cutting with surgical carbide bur.  
   - Root-end resection and filling performed in both groups.  

10. **Blinding**:  
    - Patients and independent observers were blinded.  
    - Operator blinding was not feasible.

### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS
1. **Test Name**: Independent Samples t-Test (or Mann-Whitney U Test if assumptions are violated).  
   - **Purpose**: Compare mean QoL scores and VAS pain scores between the piezo and control groups.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. The study involves comparing continuous outcomes (QoL and pain) between two independent groups.  
     2. The sample size (n = 20 per group) is adequate for parametric testing if normality is met.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Assumptions of normality and homogeneity of variances may not hold.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If data is heavily skewed or contains outliers.  
   - **Alternative**: Mann-Whitney U Test (non-parametric).

2. **Test Name**: Chi-Square Test (or Fisher’s Exact Test if expected cell counts are <5).  
   - **Purpose**: Compare the proportion of patients achieving adequate hemorrhage control (score = 2) between groups.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. The outcome is categorical (adequate vs. inadequate hemorrhage control).  
     2. The groups are independent.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Small sample size in some categories may violate assumptions.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If expected frequencies are too low.  
   - **Alternative**: Fisher’s Exact Test.

3. **Test Name**: Repeated Measures ANOVA (or Friedman Test if assumptions are violated).  
   - **Purpose**: Analyze changes in QoL and pain scores over time (days 1–7) within and between groups.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. The study involves repeated measurements of continuous outcomes over time.  
     2. It accounts for within-subject correlations.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Assumptions of sphericity and normality may not hold.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If data is non-normal or variances are unequal across time points.  
   - **Alternative**: Friedman Test (non-parametric).

#### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED TESTS
1. **Test Name**: Mixed-Effects Model.  
   - **Purpose**: Assess group differences in QoL and pain over time while accounting for repeated measures.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Handles missing data more effectively than ANOVA.  
     2. Accounts for random effects (e.g., inter-patient variability).  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Requires advanced statistical software and expertise.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If the dataset is too small for model convergence.  
   - **Alternative**: Repeated Measures ANOVA.

2. **Test Name**: Logistic Regression.  
   - **Purpose**: Model the likelihood of achieving adequate hemorrhage control (score = 2) based on group and other covariates.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Allows adjustment for potential confounders.  
     2. Provides odds ratios for interpretation.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Requires a larger sample size for reliable estimates.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If the sample size is insufficient or the outcome is rare.  
   - **Alternative**: Chi-Square Test.

#### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL TESTS
1. **Test Name**: Effect Size Calculation (e.g., Cohen’s d for continuous outcomes).  
   - **Purpose**: Quantify the magnitude of differences between groups.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Provides clinically meaningful interpretation of results.  
     2. Complements p-values.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Not inferential, only descriptive.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If the primary focus is hypothesis testing.  
   - **Alternative**: Confidence intervals for mean differences.

2. **Test Name**: Subgroup Analysis.  
   - **Purpose**: Explore potential differences in outcomes by age, gender, or lesion size.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Identifies effect modifiers.  
     2. Provides additional insights into patient subgroups.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Risk of overfitting and multiple testing errors.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If the sample size is too small for subgroup comparisons.  
   - **Alternative**: Descriptive statistics.
