TASK 1 – INFORMATION EXTRACTION  

1. Study design  
- Type: Retrospective observational cohort study.  
- Setting: Single private endodontic practice (specialist L.A.), Argentina.  
- Timeframe: Cases treated between 1999 and 2019; radiographic follow‑up 1–17 years.  
- Population source: 144 consecutive patients referred for endodontic evaluation due to presence of an intraoral sinus tract.  
- Included sample: 137 teeth from 128 patients that met the inclusion criteria and underwent root canal treatment or retreatment by a single operator.  
- Unit of analysis: Primarily tooth; some variables are at patient level (age, sex, systemic condition).  
- Blinding: Outcome radiographic evaluations performed by 2 blinded endodontists; operator not involved in outcome scoring.

2. Objectives  
- Primary:  
  - Describe clinical and radiographic outcome (healed/healing/diseased) of root canal treatment/retreatment in teeth with chronic apical abscess and intraoral sinus tract treated by a single experienced operator.  
- Secondary:  
  - Describe demographic and baseline characteristics of these consecutive cases.  
  - Evaluate the influence of preoperative, intraoperative and postoperative variables on treatment/retreatment outcome of teeth with sinus tracts.  
  - Evaluate short‑term healing/closure of the sinus tract (up to 30 days) and its relationship with long‑term periradicular healing.

3. Eligibility criteria  

3.1 Inclusion (tooth level)  
- Tooth with an intraoral sinus tract and radiographic evidence of periradicular bone destruction.  
- Pulpal status: necrotic pulp confirmed by thermal pulp tests or previously root‑canal treated tooth.  
- Tooth restorable and with indication for root canal treatment or retreatment.  
- Tooth mobility score <3.  
- Periodontal status: no periodontal pockets >4 mm.  
- Patient without significant systemic disease (assessed at patient level).  
- Patient gave informed consent.

3.2 Exclusion (implied)  
- Teeth not restorable.  
- Teeth without radiographic periradicular bone destruction.  
- Teeth with vital pulp.  
- Teeth with mobility ≥3 or periodontal pockets >4 mm.  
- Patients with significant systemic disease.  
- Referred patients with sinus tracts who did not meet the above inclusion criteria (7 teeth excluded: 144 referred → 137 treated).

4. Groups / categories  

4.1 Outcome categories (tooth level) – final periradicular outcome  
Based on clinical findings and periapical radiographs (PAI). For each tooth, the root with highest PAI used in multi‑rooted teeth.  

- Healed:  
  - Asymptomatic and functional.  
  - Sinus tract disappeared (closed).  
  - No radiographic apical periodontitis (PAI 1–2).  

- Healing:  
  - Asymptomatic and functional.  
  - Sinus tract disappeared.  
  - Radiographic lesion decreased in size (PAI score decreased but still >2).  
  - Follow‑up shorter than 4 years.  

- Diseased:  
  - Symptomatic and nonfunctional and/or  
  - Sinus tract persisted or recurred and/or  
  - Radiographic lesion unchanged or enlarged (PAI 3–5).

For many analyses, this can be dichotomized:  
- Favorable = Healed + Healing.  
- Unfavorable = Diseased.  

4.2 Short‑term sinus tract status (tooth level)  
- Time points: 7, 14, 21, 30 days after treatment.  
- Status at each visit: healed (closed) vs not healed (active).  
- Persistence: not healed after 30 days.  
- Recurrence: re‑emergence after prior closure at any time.

4.3 Lesion size classification (preoperative, tooth level)  
Measured on periapical radiograph (largest diameter):  
- Small: ≤5 mm.  
- Large: >5 to <10 mm.  
- Very large: ≥10 mm.

4.4 Tooth / sinus tract / treatment categories  
Preoperative:  
- Sinus tract location: buccal / distal / palatal–lingual.  
- Tooth type:  
  - Likely categories: anterior / premolar / molar, or more specific (incisor, canine, etc.), not explicitly detailed but recorded.  
- Arch: maxillary vs mandibular.  
- Root canal status:  
  - Necrotic pulp (no prior treatment).  
  - Previously root‑canal treated (retreatment).  
- Symptoms: presence/absence of symptoms pre‑treatment (not further classified in Methods).  
- Periodontal probing depth (continuous, later categorized as ≤4 mm by inclusion criteria; could still have variation below 4 mm).  
- Tooth mobility score: ordinal; all <3, but still potentially varying (e.g., 0–2).  
- Patient age (continuous); sex (male/female).

Intraoperative:  
- Number of treatment sessions: single vs multiple visits.  
- Working length (WL): continuous measurement, but in practice most likely analyzed as categorized if mis‑length occurs; Methods only say it was recorded.  
- Obturation technique:  
  - Lateral compaction.  
  - Schilder’s vertical compaction.  
- Quality of root canal filling:  
  - Adequate (all canals obturated; no voids; apical terminus 0–1 mm short of radiographic apex).  
  - Inadequate (any deviation).  
- Filling of lateral canals: present vs absent.

Postoperative:  
- Presence of coronal restoration at follow‑up: yes/no.  
- Type of coronal restoration: type recorded (not specified in text, but stated “presence, type and quality” were evaluated).  
- Quality of coronal restoration:  
  - Adequate (intact, no detectable defects or recurrent caries).  
  - Inadequate.

5. Sample size and follow‑up  

- Patients: 144 initially referred; 128 included (treated and meeting criteria).  
- Teeth: 137 teeth included.  
- Follow‑up duration: 1–17 years (continuous; can also be categorized, e.g., <4 vs ≥4 years, because of “healing” definition).  
- Follow‑up process:  
  - Patients contacted annually by phone for recall; at visit, clinical exam plus periapical radiograph.  
  - Radiographic assessment using Periapical Index (PAI) scored from 1–5 by two experienced, calibrated, blinded endodontists.  

6. Measurements and data collection methods  

6.1 Clinical measures  
- Pulp status: thermal pulp tests (type of test not specified, but likely cold/heat).  
- Sinus tract identification: visual; tracing with gutta‑percha point to determine involved tooth; radiographed.  
- Periodontal probing: probing depths measured in mm; inclusion ≤4 mm.  
- Mobility: ordinal mobility score (e.g., Miller index).  
- Symptoms at baseline and follow‑up:  
  - Spontaneous pain (yes/no).  
  - Tenderness to percussion/palpation (yes/no).  
  - Sinus tract presence (yes/no).  
  - Root fracture, loss of function, mobility, and probing depth abnormalities.  

6.2 Radiographic measures  
- Preoperative radiographic lesion size: mm, categorized as small / large / very large.  
- PAI scoring:  
  - PAI 1–5 per tooth (max score per tooth).  
  - Preoperative, postoperative, and follow‑up radiographs all stored in Keynote and reviewed on computer in standardized conditions.  
  - Two blinded evaluators; disagreement resolved by consensus; prior calibration on 100 reference radiographs.

6.3 Treatment protocol details (for descriptive purposes)  
- Anaesthesia and rubber dam isolation for all cases.  
- Chemomechanical preparation:  
  - Irrigant: 2.5% sodium hypochlorite.  
  - Instruments: diverse manual or rotary NiTi systems (varied over years).  
- Working length: determined via electronic apex locator; confirmed radiographically.  
- Single vs multiple visits:  
  - Single visit if canal dry after preparation.  
  - Multiple visits if canal wet or anatomy complex; interappointment medication with calcium hydroxide; repeated once if needed.  
- Retreatment:  
  - Removal of previous filling with Gates‑Glidden burs, Hedström files, and xylol solvent; then same protocol as primary treatment.  
- Obturation: lateral condensation or Schilder’s vertical compaction.

7. Outcomes to be analyzed  
Primary outcome (tooth level):  
- Long‑term periradicular treatment outcome: three‑category ordinal variable (Healed / Healing / Diseased).  

Key derived outcomes:  
- Binary: Favorable vs Unfavorable outcome.  
- Time‑to‑sinus‑tract closure (in days, with censoring at 30 days, and indicator of persistent >30 days or recurrence).  
- Association between early sinus tract closure (≤30 days) and long‑term periradicular outcome.  

Potential covariates / predictors:  
- Patient‑level: age, sex.  
- Tooth‑level: tooth type, arch, sinus tract location, lesion size category, pre‑treatment symptoms, periodontal probing depth, mobility score, root canal status (initial vs retreatment).  
- Treatment‑level: number of visits, obturation technique, WL, quality of root filling, presence of lateral canals filled.  
- Postoperative: type and quality of coronal restoration, follow‑up time.

TASK 2 – STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN  

Below, tests are grouped into Mandatory (A), Recommended (B), Optional (C). Because the outcome is primarily categorical (3‑level), with multiple categorical predictors and some continuous (age, follow‑up time, lesion size in mm), the main approaches will be descriptive statistics, contingency‑table analysis, and regression models (logistic/multinomial and possibly survival).  

CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS  

A1. Descriptive statistics (no hypothesis test, but essential)  
- Purpose:  
  - Summarize baseline characteristics, treatment details, and outcome distributions.  
- Why appropriate:  
  1) Provides essential context about the cohort, including demographics, lesion characteristics, and treatment modalities.  
  2) Needed to assess data distributions and inform choice of subsequent statistical tests.  
- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - Not a formal inferential test; cannot establish associations or causality.  
- When NOT to use:  
  - Never “not used”; always needed at least minimally.  
- Alternatives if assumptions violated:  
  - If normality is poor for continuous variables, prefer median (IQR) instead of mean (SD).  

Implementation:  
- Continuous variables (e.g., age, follow‑up time, lesion diameter): mean ± SD if approximately normal; median (IQR) if skewed; min–max.  
- Categorical variables (sex, tooth type, lesion size category, outcome): counts and percentages.  

A2. Chi‑square test of independence (or Fisher’s exact test)  
- Purpose:  
  - Examine association between categorical predictors and categorical outcomes.  
    - Example: lesion size category vs outcome (favorable vs unfavorable).  
    - Root canal status (initial vs retreatment) vs outcome.  
    - Quality of root filling (adequate/inadequate) vs outcome.  
    - Early sinus tract closure (≤30 days vs >30 days/persistent) vs long‑term outcome.  
- Why appropriate:  
  1) Outcome and most predictors are categorical or ordinal, making contingency‑table analysis natural.  
  2) Allows testing association between two categorical variables in a retrospective cohort without requiring distributional assumptions beyond expected cell counts.  
- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - If some cells have very low expected counts (<5), chi‑square approximation may be invalid.  
- When NOT to use:  
  - Sparse contingency tables with small cell counts, especially >20% of cells with expected count <5, or any cell with expected count <1.  
- Alternative if assumptions are violated:  
  - Fisher’s exact test for 2×2 tables (or Monte Carlo‑based exact tests for larger tables).  
  - Collapse categories to increase cell counts, where clinically justifiable.  

Implementation:  
- Start with 3‑level outcome and 3‑level lesion size → 3×3 chi‑square; if sparse, collapse to favorable vs unfavorable and/or lesion size small vs medium/large, etc., plus Fisher’s exact.  

A3. Binary logistic regression  
- Purpose:  
  - Model probability of favorable outcome (Healed+Healing vs Diseased) as a function of multiple predictors simultaneously.  
  - Estimate adjusted odds ratios (ORs) for factors such as:  
    - Root canal status (initial vs retreatment).  
    - Lesion size category.  
    - Tooth type, arch.  
    - Sinus tract closure within 30 days.  
    - Quality of root filling.  
    - Quality of coronal restoration.  
    - Number of visits (single vs multiple).  
- Why appropriate:  
  1) Primary clinically relevant dichotomy is favorable vs unfavorable; logistic regression is standard for binary outcomes.  
  2) Allows adjustment for confounding by including several covariates; improves causal inference compared with univariable comparisons.  
- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - Limited number of events (diseased teeth) may lead to overfitting and unstable estimates if too many predictors are included.  
- When NOT to use:  
  - When events per predictor are too low (rule‑of‑thumb <10 events per variable; in small diseased group).  
  - When the outcome is very rare or very common (complete separation issues).  
- Alternatives if assumptions are violated:  
  - Penalized logistic regression (e.g., Firth correction) for small or separated data.  
  - Exact logistic regression (if software and sample size allow).  
  - Simpler stratified analyses or univariable tests if multivariable modeling is not feasible.  

Assumptions to check:  
- Linearity of log‑odds for continuous predictors (e.g., age, lesion size in mm; use restricted cubic splines or categorize if violated).  
- Absence of severe multicollinearity.  

A4. Inter‑rater agreement: Cohen’s kappa (pre‑consensus)  
- Purpose:  
  - Quantify reliability of PAI scoring between the two blinded radiographic evaluators before consensus.  
- Why appropriate:  
  1) PAI is an ordinal categorical scale; kappa is standard for agreement beyond chance.  
  2) Ensures that radiographic outcome classification is reproducible, supporting validity of the main outcome.  
- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - If ratings are only available after consensus (no individual ratings retained), kappa cannot be calculated.  
- When NOT to use:  
  - When only final consensus scores are recorded (no independent ratings).  
- Alternatives if assumptions are violated:  
  - If data are ordinal and one wants to weight disagreements by magnitude, use weighted kappa (quadratic or linear).  
  - If >2 raters were involved (not here), use Fleiss’ kappa or intraclass correlation for ordinal scales.  

CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED TESTS  

B1. Ordinal/multinomial logistic regression  
- Purpose:  
  - Model the 3‑category outcome (Healed / Healing / Diseased) without collapsing into binary, thereby using more information.  
- Why appropriate:  
  1) The outcome is inherently ordered (Healed better than Healing better than Diseased); ordinal logistic regression exploits this ordering and is more efficient than treating it as nominal or binary.  
  2) Allows assessment of whether predictors shift the odds toward better vs worse outcome categories.  
- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - Proportional odds assumption may be violated (effects of predictors not constant across cumulative splits).  
  - Sample size in some categories (e.g., Healing vs Healed vs Diseased) may be too small for stable estimates.  
- When NOT to use:  
  - Clear violation of proportional odds assumption, especially with small sample size.  
  - Very small counts in one outcome category.  
- Alternatives if assumptions are violated:  
  - Multinomial (polytomous) logistic regression treating outcome as nominal (no ordering assumption).  
  - Collapsing categories (e.g., Healed vs Healing+Diseased) if clinically justified.  

B2. Survival analysis (Kaplan–Meier and log‑rank test) for time to sinus tract closure  
- Purpose:  
  - Examine time‑to‑event data for sinus tract closure over the 30‑day period, accommodating cases that never close (censored at 30 days) and recurrences.  
  - Compare closure time across subgroups (e.g., lesion size, root canal status).  
- Why appropriate:  
  1) Sinus tract closure is a time‑dependent outcome; survival methods are designed to handle censoring (persistent tracts at 30 days).  
  2) Kaplan–Meier curves and log‑rank tests allow visual and statistical comparison of closure patterns between groups.  
- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - Exact closure time is only observed at discrete follow‑up visits (7,14,21,30 days) – interval censoring rather than exact times; standard Kaplan–Meier assumes known event time.  
- When NOT to use:  
  - If only the status at 30 days (closed vs not) is available, without intermediate time information; in that case a simple binary analysis is more appropriate.  
- Alternatives if assumptions are violated:  
  - Methods for interval‑censored survival data (Turnbull estimator; parametric models).  
  - Logistic regression modeling probability of closure by 30 days if time detail is insufficient.  

B3. Cox proportional hazards regression (for sinus tract closure)  
- Purpose:  
  - Model hazard of sinus tract closure over time, accounting for multiple predictors and censored cases (non‑closure by 30 days).  
- Why appropriate:  
  1) Allows multivariable adjustment (e.g., lesion size, root canal status, tooth type, etc.) for time‑to‑closure outcome.  
  2) Provides clinically interpretable hazard ratios for earlier vs delayed closure across subgroups.  
- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - Short follow‑up window (30 days) and discrete follow‑up times may yield limited information for full Cox modeling.  
  - Proportional hazards assumption may be difficult to test with few events/time points.  
- When NOT to use:  
  - Very few non‑closure events or minimal variation in closure time.  
  - Violated proportional hazards or heavy clustering that’s not modeled.  
- Alternatives if assumptions are violated:  
  - Parametric time‑to‑event models (e.g., Weibull) if hazard seems non‑proportional but structured.  
  - Logistic regression on closure by 30 days as a binary outcome.  

B4. Test for trend across ordered categories (e.g., Cochran–Armitage trend test)  
- Purpose:  
  - Assess whether increasing lesion size category (small → large → very large) is associated with a monotonic trend in proportion of favorable outcomes.  
- Why appropriate:  
  1) Lesion size is ordinal; trend test has greater power than a general chi‑square when a monotonic trend is hypothesized.  
  2) Clinically the effect is expected to be directional (larger lesions → worse prognosis), which aligns with trend testing.  
- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - If relationship is non‑monotonic or categories are not truly ordered.  
- When NOT to use:  
  - When data show a clearly non‑monotone relationship, or when category boundaries are arbitrary and uninterpretable as ordered.  
- Alternatives if assumptions are violated:  
  - Standard chi‑square test (no trend assumption).  
  - Logistic regression with lesion size as continuous (mm) or as dummy variables.  

B5. Non‑parametric tests for continuous predictors vs outcome (e.g., Mann–Whitney U / Kruskal–Wallis)  
- Purpose:  
  - Compare continuous variables (e.g., age, lesion size in mm, follow‑up time) across outcome groups when normality is doubtful or sample sizes are small.  
- Why appropriate:  
  1) Age and lesion size may be skewed and sample size per group modest; non‑parametric tests do not require normality.  
  2) Data are at least ordinal, and these tests are robust to outliers.  
- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - Less efficient than parametric tests when normality holds; only test for differences in distributions, not necessarily means.  
- When NOT to use:  
  - When sample sizes are extremely small (e.g., n<5 per group); test may have almost no power.  
- Alternatives if assumptions are violated:  
  - Exact versions of non‑parametric tests.  
  - Parametric tests (t‑test/ANOVA) with suitable transformations if data are approximately normal.  

B6. Sensitivity analyses for clustering (e.g., robust standard errors)  
- Purpose:  
  - Account for potential clustering of multiple teeth within the same patient in regression models.  
- Why appropriate:  
  1) Some patients may contribute more than one tooth (137 teeth, 128 patients), creating non‑independence.  
  2) Cluster‑robust standard errors or mixed‑effects models prevent underestimation of standard errors and inflated Type I error.  
- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - With only a small number of patients having multiple teeth, the clustering effect may be negligible; complex models may be unstable.  
- When NOT to use:  
  - When each patient contributes only one tooth (or almost all; clustering effect trivial).  
- Alternatives if assumptions are violated:  
  - Perform a sensitivity analysis using only the first or a randomly chosen tooth per patient.  
  - Use generalized estimating equations (GEE) with exchangeable correlation if enough clusters.  

CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL TESTS / ANALYSES  

C1. Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) analysis  
- Purpose:  
  - Evaluate predictive performance of continuous or combined predictors (e.g., time to sinus tract closure, PAI change, lesion size) for discriminating favorable vs unfavorable outcomes.  
- Why appropriate:  
  1) Provides global measure of predictive ability (AUC) for variables of prognostic interest.  
  2) Allows assessment of optimal cut‑points for clinical decision making (e.g., closure by X days predicting success).  
- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - Limited sample size and few failures reduce precision of AUC estimates.  
- When NOT to use:  
  - When event rate is extremely low or numbers are too small for meaningful performance evaluation.  
- Alternatives if assumptions are violated:  
  - Cross‑validation or bootstrapping to get optimism‑corrected performance; or simply report predictive probabilities without ROC.  

C2. Subgroup analyses  
- Purpose:  
  - Explore whether associations differ in key subgroups, e.g.:  
    - Initial treatment vs retreatment.  
    - Molars vs non‑molars.  
    - Maxillary vs mandibular teeth.  
- Why appropriate:  
  1) May reveal effect modification and inform clinical decision making (e.g., sinus tract closure predicting outcome differently in retreatment cases).  
  2) Supports external validity by assessing consistency across subgroups.  
- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - Retrospective design and limited sample reduce power; strong risk of false positives.  
- When NOT to use:  
  - When sample sizes in subsets are very small.  
- Alternatives if assumptions are violated:  
  - Include interaction terms in logistic regression instead of many separate subgroup tests; interpret cautiously.  

C3. Long‑term survival analysis of tooth retention (if data available)  
- Purpose:  
  - Evaluate time until extraction or loss of function as harder endpoints beyond radiographic healing.  
- Why appropriate:  
  1) Clinical endpoint of tooth survival is important.  
  2) Allows evaluation of predictors for tooth survival, not just periapical radiographic status.  
- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - Data on time and cause of extraction may not have been systematically recorded.  
- When NOT to use:  
  - When extraction dates are unknown or unreliable, or nearly all teeth retained.  
- Alternatives if assumptions are violated:  
  - Simple descriptive rates of extraction and reasons, without formal survival modeling.  

C4. Agreement between early sinus tract closure and long‑term radiographic outcome (e.g., kappa)  
- Purpose:  
  - Quantify concordance between early closure status (within 30 days) and long‑term favorable outcome (yes/no).  
- Why appropriate:  
  1) Direct measure of how well early closure predicts final outcome beyond chance.  
  2) Clinically relevant to evaluate prognostic value of early healing.  
- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - Kappa is sensitive to prevalence; skewed distributions can yield low kappa despite strong association.  
- When NOT to use:  
  - Extreme imbalance in categories (e.g., almost all favorable), making kappa hard to interpret.  
- Alternatives if assumptions are violated:  
  - Report predictive values (sensitivity, specificity, PPV, NPV) for early closure in predicting favorable outcome.

TASK 3 – INAPPROPRIATE TESTS AND WHY  

1. Student’s t‑test / ANOVA for primary outcome vs categorical predictors  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - The primary outcome (Healed/Healing/Diseased) is categorical, not continuous; t‑test/ANOVA requires continuous dependent variable.  
  - Distribution assumptions (normality, equal variances) do not hold for categorical outcomes.  
- Preferred alternatives:  
  - Chi‑square/Fisher’s exact; logistic/ordinal logistic regression.  

2. Pearson correlation coefficient (r) between categorical predictors and outcome  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Pearson’s r assumes continuous, approximately normally distributed variables; here both outcome and many predictors are categorical.  
- Preferred alternatives:  
  - Cramer’s V for association between categorical variables; logistic regression; chi‑square.  

3. Linear regression treating outcome as numeric (e.g., Healed=1, Healing=2, Diseased=3)  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - The numeric coding is arbitrary and does not reflect interval scaling; residuals will be non‑normal and heteroscedastic.  
  - Linear regression does not respect bounded nature or multinomial distribution of outcome.  
- Preferred alternatives:  
  - Logistic regression (binary) or ordinal/multinomial logistic regression.  

4. Paired t‑test for pre‑ and post‑PAI scores without considering ordinal nature  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - PAI is ordinal, not interval; paired t‑test assumes continuous, normally distributed difference scores.  
- Preferred alternatives:  
  - Wilcoxon signed‑rank test for paired ordinal data, or modeling outcome categories directly.  

5. Repeated‑measures ANOVA for serial sinus tract status (7,14,21,30 days)  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Sinus tract status is binary (closed/not closed), not continuous.  
  - Measurement times are few and discrete; repeated‑measures ANOVA assumptions (normality, sphericity) violated.  
- Preferred alternatives:  
  - Survival/interval‑censored models, or generalized estimating equations with binary outcome at each time point.  

6. Parametric methods assuming independence when multiple teeth per patient are common  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Teeth from the same patient are correlated; ignoring clustering may understate variance and give overly narrow confidence intervals.  
- Preferred alternatives:  
  - Cluster‑robust standard errors, GEE, or mixed‑effects models; or per‑patient analysis.  

7. Unadjusted multiple hypothesis testing without correction in extensive exploratory analyses  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Numerous comparisons increase risk of Type I error inflation; p‑values become misleading.  
- Preferred alternatives:  
  - Pre‑specify primary hypotheses; use FDR or Bonferroni correction for large sets of secondary tests; emphasize effect sizes and CIs over p‑values.

TASK 4 – SUMMARY AND JUSTIFICATION OF OVERALL ANALYTICAL APPROACH  

The study is a retrospective cohort of teeth with chronic apical abscess and sinus tract, with multiple categorical predictors and a categorical, ordered outcome assessed at long‑term follow‑up, plus short‑term sinus tract status.  

The core analytical strategy should therefore:  
1) Use descriptive statistics to characterize the cohort and treatment patterns.  
2) Use contingency‑table methods (chi‑square/Fisher’s exact, trend tests) to evaluate univariable associations between key factors (lesion size, root canal status, obturation quality, coronal restoration, sinus tract closure time) and outcome.  
3) Apply logistic regression (binary and, where feasible, ordinal/multinomial) to model the probability of favorable outcome while adjusting for confounders and key covariates; use robust or clustered standard errors to account for multiple teeth per patient if needed.  
4) For early sinus tract healing, apply time‑to‑event methods (Kaplan–Meier, Cox model, or interval‑censored alternatives) when time‑to‑closure data are sufficiently detailed, or logistic regression for closure by 30 days when only status at that time is available.  
5) Quantify reliability of radiographic assessments via kappa statistics.  

This approach aligns with the data structure (primarily categorical, ordered outcomes, time‑to‑event for sinus closure), respects key assumptions, allows adjustment for confounding, and keeps the analyses clinically interpretable for endodontic research.
