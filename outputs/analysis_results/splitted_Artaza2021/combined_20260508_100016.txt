## TASK 1 — Information extraction (study characteristics)

### Study design & setting
- **Design:** Retrospective observational cohort/case series with prognostic-factor evaluation (associations between covariates and outcomes).
- **Setting:** Single endodontic specialist private practice.
- **Period:** **1999–2019**.
- **Operator:** **Single experienced operator** performed all treatments/retreatments (reduces operator variability).
- **Ethics:** Institutional Board Review approval; patients consented for examination/treatment.

### Objectives
1. **Describe** clinical and radiographic outcomes after **root canal treatment or retreatment** of infected teeth **with an intraoral sinus tract**.
2. Report **demographics** of consecutive cases.
3. **Evaluate influence** of selected **preoperative, intraoperative, postoperative** variables on outcomes.

### Source population and sample
- Initially referred: **144 patients** (81 women, 63 men), age **15–86**, all referred due to **intraoral sinus tract**.
- Included/treated: **137 teeth from 128 patients** met inclusion criteria and were treated/retreated and followed.

**Unit of analysis:** Tooth (primary), but clustering exists because some patients contributed >1 tooth.

### Inclusion criteria (key eligibility)
- Tooth with **sinus tract** + **radiographic periradicular bone destruction**
- **Necrotic pulp** (confirmed by pulp tests) **or** previously root canal treated tooth
- Tooth **restorable** and indicated for treatment/retreatment
- Mobility score **<3**
- **No periodontal pockets >4 mm**
- Patient without **significant systemic disease**

### Data collection / variables

#### Preoperative variables (recorded)
- **Demographics:** age, sex
- **Sinus tract location:** buccal / distal / palatal-lingual
- **Lesion size** on periapical radiograph: largest diameter; categorized:
  - Small **≤5 mm**
  - Large **>5 to <10 mm**
  - Very large **≥10 mm**
- **Root canal status:** necrotic pulp vs previously treated (retreatment)
- **Tooth type and location:** tooth type; arch **maxillary vs mandibular**
- **Symptoms** (presence of symptoms; details not fully enumerated pre-op)
- **Periodontal probing**
- **Tooth mobility**

#### Intraoperative variables (recorded)
- **Number of sessions** (single vs multiple; possibly >2 with Ca(OH)₂ renewals)
- **Working length** (measured by apex locator, radiographically confirmed)
- **Obturation technique:** lateral compaction vs Schilder vertical compaction
- **Quality of root canal filling:** “adequate” if all canals obturated, no voids, terminus 0–1 mm short of radiographic apex
- **Filling of lateral canals** (yes/no)

#### Postoperative variables (recorded at follow-up)
- **Coronal restoration presence/type/quality** at follow-up:
  - “Adequate” if intact, no detectable defects or recurrent caries

### Outcomes

#### Outcome 1 (short-term): sinus tract closure
- Assessed at **7, 14, 21, 30 days** post-op.
- **Binary at each visit:** healed (closed) vs not healed (active).
- Additional derived outcomes:
  - **Persistent sinus tract:** not healed after **30 days**
  - **Recurrent sinus tract:** re-emerged after having healed (any time later)

#### Outcome 2 (long-term): periradicular (clinical + radiographic) outcome
- Follow-up frequency: periodic, **range 1–17 years**; annual telephone recall invitations.
- Failure could be declared even **before 1 year** if clinical signs/symptoms present.
- Clinical evaluation: sinus tract, spontaneous pain, tenderness to percussion/palpation, root fracture, loss of function, mobility, probing depths.
- Radiographic evaluation:
  - Periapical radiographs compared (pre, post, follow-up)
  - Scored using **Periapical Index (PAI)** by **2 blinded experienced evaluators**; disagreement resolved by discussion
  - Calibration: 100 reference radiographs
  - Multirooted teeth: categorized by **worst (highest) PAI root**
- **Composite outcome classification (ordinal, 3 levels):**
  1. **Healed:** asymptomatic/functional, sinus tract disappeared, **PAI 1–2**
  2. **Healing:** asymptomatic/functional, sinus tract disappeared, **PAI decreased but still >2**, and **follow-up <4 years**
  3. **Diseased:** symptomatic/nonfunctional, sinus tract **persisted or recurred**, and/or radiographic lesion unchanged/enlarged (**PAI 3–5**)

**Key implication:** the “Healing” category is partly defined by **follow-up duration (<4 years)**, so time is embedded in outcome definition (important for analysis).

## TASK 2 — Statistical analysis plan (tests & models)

### General analysis principles (apply to all categories)
- **Analysis unit:** tooth-level, but account for **within-patient clustering** (some patients have multiple teeth).
- **Two primary outcomes:**
  1. **Sinus tract closure within 30 days** (and/or time-to-closure using repeated assessments).
  2. **Final long-term outcome** (Healed / Healing / Diseased).
- **Covariate sets:** pre-, intra-, post-operative predictors as listed.
- **Effect size reporting (preferred):**
  - Odds ratios (OR) for binary/ordinal models
  - Hazard ratios (HR) for time-to-event
  - Risk differences/probabilities for interpretability
- **Missing data:** describe extent/pattern; if nontrivial, consider multiple imputation (see Recommended).

# CATEGORY A — MANDATORY (without these, analysis is incomplete)

## A1) Descriptive statistics (with appropriate summaries)
**Test/Method:** Descriptive summaries (no hypothesis test)  
**Purpose:** Characterize the cohort and distributions of predictors and outcomes.  
**Why appropriate (≥2):**
1. Retrospective cohort requires transparent reporting of case mix and baseline characteristics.
2. Many predictors are categorical/ordinal; simple counts/percentages are essential before modeling.  
**Why may not be appropriate (≥1):** Not sufficient for inference; cannot address associations/confounding.  
**When NOT to use:** Never omit; but avoid using only descriptives as the “analysis.”  
**Alternative if assumptions violated:** Not applicable (nonparametric summaries always possible).

Include:
- n teeth, n patients, teeth per patient distribution
- Follow-up time: median (IQR), range
- Outcome frequencies: closure by day 30; persistent/recurrent; 3-level long-term outcome
- Predictor distributions (age, lesion size categories, etc.)

---

## A2) Inter-rater reliability for PAI scoring
**Test:** Weighted kappa (or ICC depending on how PAI treated; PAI is ordinal → **weighted kappa** preferred)  
**Purpose:** Quantify agreement between the 2 blinded evaluators on PAI.  
**Why appropriate:**
1. PAI is an **ordinal** rating; weighted kappa accounts for partial disagreement.
2. Validates that outcome assessment is reliable and not dominated by measurement error.  
**Why may not be appropriate:** If only final consensus scores are retained and raw independent scores unavailable.  
**When NOT to use:** When only one rater or only consensus scores exist.  
**Alternative if assumptions violated:** Gwet’s AC2 (more stable than kappa under prevalence/imbalance issues).

---

## A3) Primary model for short-term sinus tract outcome at 30 days (binary)
**Test/Model:** Logistic regression with clustering handled via **GEE** (population-averaged) *or* mixed-effects logistic regression (subject-specific random intercept for patient)  
**Purpose:** Identify predictors associated with **non-closure/persistence at 30 days** (or closure).  
**Why appropriate:**
1. Outcome is **binary** (healed vs not at day 30; persistent defined accordingly).
2. Accounts for multiple predictors simultaneously (confounding control) and allows adjusted inference.
3. Can account for **patient-level clustering** (GEE or random effects).  
**Why may not be appropriate:**
- Small number of persistent cases can cause separation/unstable estimates (rare events).  
**When NOT to use:**
- When event count is too small for multivariable modeling (e.g., <~10 events per parameter as a rough rule).  
**Alternative if assumptions violated:**
- Penalized logistic regression (Firth) for rare events/separation (with cluster-robust SE if possible), or reduce predictors via prespecified limited model.

Predictors to consider (prespecified):
- Preop: age, sex, lesion size category, root canal status (necrotic vs retreatment), sinus tract location, tooth type/location, symptoms
- Intraop: number of sessions, obturation technique, filling quality, lateral canals filled, working length (if categorical/continuous available)
- Postop: coronal restoration adequacy (if assessed before/at 30 days; if only later, treat carefully)

---

## A4) Primary model for long-term outcome (3-level ordinal)
**Test/Model:** Ordinal logistic regression (proportional odds model) with clustering (GEE ordinal or mixed-effects cumulative logit)  
**Purpose:** Evaluate associations between predictors and ordered outcome **Healed > Healing > Diseased** (or reverse).  
**Why appropriate:**
1. Outcome is **ordinal (3 categories)** with clear ranking of health status.
2. More efficient than collapsing to binary (retains information).
3. Allows covariate adjustment and clustering control.  
**Why may not be appropriate:**
- Proportional odds assumption may be violated.
- “Healing” category depends on follow-up duration (<4 years), which can distort ordinal interpretation.  
**When NOT to use:**
- When proportional odds assumption is clearly violated and cannot be remedied.
- When follow-up time differs greatly and is strongly tied to category definition without adjustment.  
**Alternative if assumptions violated:**
- Partial proportional odds model / generalized ordered logit.
- Multinomial logistic regression (nominal) with cluster-robust SE.
- Or redefine outcome for sensitivity (e.g., healed vs not healed at ≥4y).

# CATEGORY B — RECOMMENDED (enhances rigor)

## B1) Time-to-sinus-tract closure analysis (repeated assessments)
**Test/Model:** Survival analysis for **time to closure**:
- Kaplan–Meier curve(s) + log-rank test (unadjusted)
- Cox proportional hazards model with robust SE for clustering or frailty model (adjusted)  
**Purpose:** Use full information from assessments at 7/14/21/30 days rather than only day-30 status.  
**Why appropriate:**
1. Closure is inherently **time-to-event**; uses censoring at last observed day (e.g., not closed by day 30).
2. Can compare closure speed by predictors (e.g., lesion size, retreatment vs initial).  
**Why may not be appropriate:**
- Event time is interval-censored (closure known only between visits), standard Cox approximates using visit day as event time.  
**When NOT to use:**
- When closure timing is too coarsely measured and interval censoring is substantial and must be modeled exactly.  
**Alternative if assumptions violated:**
- Discrete-time survival model (complementary log–log) using visit intervals.
- Interval-censored survival models (Turnbull / parametric).

---

## B2) Model diagnostics & assumptions
**Tests/Methods:**  
- Proportional odds test (Brant test or score tests) for ordinal models  
- Linearity in logit (restricted cubic splines for age)  
- Multicollinearity (VIF)  
**Purpose:** Validate model assumptions and improve specification.  
**Why appropriate:**
1. Ensures validity of inference (assumption checks are essential).
2. Improves predictive and explanatory accuracy.  
**Why may not be appropriate:** Some diagnostics (e.g., Brant) less straightforward with clustered/robust variance implementations.  
**When NOT to use:** When sample size too small to support flexible modeling/diagnostics.  
**Alternative if assumptions violated:** Use nonparametric terms/splines, categorize cautiously, or switch to multinomial/partial proportional odds.

---

## B3) Sensitivity analyses for outcome definition tied to follow-up time
**Test/Model:**  
- Binary outcome at a fixed horizon: e.g., among cases with **≥4-year** follow-up, model **Healed (PAI 1–2, asymptomatic)** vs **Diseased** (exclude “Healing” or recode). Use mixed/GEE logistic.  
**Purpose:** Reduce bias due to “Healing” being defined partly by follow-up duration.  
**Why appropriate:**
1. Makes outcome less dependent on follow-up length.
2. Provides robustness check for main conclusions.  
**Why may not be appropriate:** Subsetting to ≥4 years reduces sample size and power; may induce selection bias if long follow-up is informative.  
**When NOT to use:** When very few teeth have ≥4-year follow-up.  
**Alternative if assumptions violated:** Joint modeling or inverse probability weighting for follow-up/return.

---

## B4) Handling clustering and tooth-level vs patient-level inference
**Test/Model:** Mixed-effects models (random intercept for patient) or GEE with exchangeable correlation  
**Purpose:** Correct standard errors and inferences due to non-independence of teeth within patient.  
**Why appropriate:**
1. 137 teeth from 128 patients implies some clustering; ignoring it underestimates SE.
2. Clinically plausible shared patient factors (host response, behaviors).  
**Why may not be appropriate:** If almost all patients contribute only 1 tooth, clustering impact is negligible and mixed models may be unstable.  
**When NOT to use:** When clustering is absent (1 tooth per patient) or random-effects model fails to converge due to sparse data.  
**Alternative if assumptions violated:** Cluster-robust sandwich SE in standard regression.

---

## B5) Multiple imputation for missing covariates/outcomes (if present)
**Test/Method:** Multiple imputation by chained equations (MICE) + Rubin’s rules  
**Purpose:** Reduce bias and inefficiency from missing data.  
**Why appropriate:**
1. Retrospective charts often have missing predictors (e.g., symptoms, restoration quality).
2. Preserves sample size vs complete-case analysis.  
**Why may not be appropriate:** If missingness is MNAR and cannot be plausibly modeled; or if outcome missingness is extreme.  
**When NOT to use:** When missing proportion is tiny and complete-case is acceptable; or when variables needed for imputation are unavailable.  
**Alternative:** Sensitivity analyses (best/worst case), pattern-mixture models.

# CATEGORY C — OPTIONAL (supplementary insights)

## C1) Unadjusted bivariate association screens (with caution)
**Tests:** Chi-square/Fisher’s exact (categorical), t-test/Mann–Whitney (continuous vs binary), Kruskal–Wallis (continuous vs 3-level outcome)  
**Purpose:** Exploratory description of crude associations.  
**Why appropriate:**
1. Provides simple, interpretable unadjusted comparisons.
2. Helps detect data issues/outliers/imbalance before multivariable modeling.  
**Why may not be appropriate:** Can encourage p-value driven variable selection and inflate type I error; ignores clustering/confounding.  
**When NOT to use:** Do not use as the main evidence for prognostic claims.  
**Alternative if assumptions violated:** Fisher’s exact; nonparametric tests; or always prefer regression with robust SE.

## C2) Prediction performance (if building a prognostic model)
**Tests/Methods:**  
- Discrimination: C-statistic/AUC (binary), ordinal c-index  
- Calibration plots, calibration slope/intercept; Brier score  
- Internal validation: bootstrap or cross-validation  
**Purpose:** Quantify how well predictors predict outcomes (especially “closure predicts long-term healing”).  
**Why appropriate:**
1. Study conclusion suggests prognostic utility of early closure.
2. Distinguishes statistical significance from clinical predictiveness.  
**Why may not be appropriate:** Sample size may be limited for stable prediction modeling; risk of optimism.  
**When NOT to use:** When the goal is purely explanatory with limited events and no intent to predict.  
**Alternative:** Penalized regression + bootstrap validation.

---

## C3) Mediation-style exploratory analysis (early closure as predictor of long-term outcome)
**Test/Model:** Ordinal/binary regression of long-term outcome including **early closure status** (e.g., closed by 30 days) plus baseline covariates  
**Purpose:** Evaluate whether early closure predicts long-term healing after adjustment.  
**Why appropriate:**
1. Directly addresses concluding statement (“closure in first month may predict favorable long-term outcome”).
2. Adjusts for lesion size, retreatment status, filling quality, etc.  
**Why may not be appropriate:** Temporal ambiguity if some long-term outcomes are assessed early; also potential collider bias if only recalled patients are analyzed.  
**When NOT to use:** If closure status and long-term outcome are not available for the same teeth or follow-up is too short.  
**Alternative:** Landmark analysis (include only those at risk and evaluated after a defined time point), or inverse probability weighting for follow-up.

## TASK 3 — Inappropriate tests (should NOT be used)

1. **Independent-samples t-test / one-way ANOVA** to compare outcomes across groups  
   - **Why not:** Primary outcomes are binary/ordinal, not continuous; violates distributional assumptions and wastes information.

2. **Paired t-test / repeated-measures ANOVA** for sinus tract healing over days  
   - **Why not:** Outcome is binary at each time; repeated-measures ANOVA assumes normality/continuous response. Use GEE logistic or survival/discrete-time methods.

3. **Simple Pearson correlation** between predictors and outcome  
   - **Why not:** Outcomes are categorical; correlation is not meaningful and ignores confounding and clustering.

4. **Linear regression** with PAI or the 3-level outcome treated as continuous without justification  
   - **Why not:** Ordinal scale is not interval; linear model assumptions (normal residuals, equal spacing) are dubious.

5. **Chi-square tests as the sole inferential approach**  
   - **Why not:** Cannot adjust for confounding; does not handle clustering; multiple testing risk.

6. **Log-rank test alone** to claim predictor effects  
   - **Why not:** Unadjusted; cannot control confounders; also interval-censoring nuance.

---

## TASK 4 — Summary justification of the overall analytical approach

This is a retrospective cohort of consecutively treated teeth with sinus tracts, with outcomes measured short-term (closure by 30 days, repeatedly assessed) and long-term (ordinal clinical+radiographic status based on PAI and symptoms). The analysis should (i) describe the cohort, (ii) establish reliability of radiographic scoring, and (iii) quantify adjusted associations between pre/intra/postoperative factors and outcomes using regression models appropriate for binary/ordinal endpoints while accounting for clustering by patient and variable follow-up. Time-to-closure methods are recommended to fully leverage repeated early assessments, and sensitivity analyses are important because the “Healing” category is partially defined by follow-up duration.

### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS
1. **Chi-Square Test (or Fisher’s Exact Test)**  
   - **Purpose**: Assess associations between categorical variables (e.g., sinus tract location, lesion size) and treatment outcomes (healed, healing, diseased).  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. The study involves categorical variables (e.g., PAI scores, sinus tract healing status).  
     2. Large sample size allows for robust chi-square analysis.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Small sample sizes in subgroups may require Fisher’s Exact Test instead.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If expected frequencies in any cell are <5.  
   - **Alternative**: Fisher’s Exact Test.

2. **Logistic Regression**  
   - **Purpose**: Model the relationship between independent variables (e.g., lesion size, obturation technique) and dichotomous outcomes (e.g., healed vs. diseased).  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Allows for multivariable analysis of predictors of treatment outcomes.  
     2. Accounts for confounding variables.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Assumes linear relationship between predictors and log-odds of the outcome.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If multicollinearity exists among predictors.  
   - **Alternative**: Ridge regression or penalized logistic regression.

3. **Kaplan-Meier Survival Analysis**  
   - **Purpose**: Evaluate time-to-event data (e.g., time to sinus tract healing or recurrence).  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Suitable for follow-up data with varying durations.  
     2. Accounts for censored data (e.g., patients lost to follow-up).  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Assumes proportional hazards.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If sample size is insufficient for subgroup comparisons.  
   - **Alternative**: Cox proportional hazards model.

---

#### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED TESTS
1. **Cox Proportional Hazards Model**  
   - **Purpose**: Assess the impact of variables (e.g., lesion size, obturation technique) on time to healing or recurrence.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Accounts for time-dependent covariates.  
     2. Handles censored data effectively.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Assumes proportional hazards.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If proportional hazards assumption is violated.  
   - **Alternative**: Stratified Cox model.

2. **Repeated Measures ANOVA (or Mixed Effects Model)**  
   - **Purpose**: Analyze changes in radiographic PAI scores over time.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Accounts for within-subject correlations across repeated measures.  
     2. Suitable for longitudinal data.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Requires normality of residuals.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If data are non-normally distributed.  
   - **Alternative**: Nonparametric tests (e.g., Friedman test).

   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Does not account for confounding factors.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If variables are not continuous or ordinal.  
   - **Alternative**: Partial correlation.

2. **Cluster Analysis**  
   - **Purpose**: Identify patterns or subgroups within the dataset (e.g., patients with similar healing trajectories).  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Useful for exploratory analysis.  
     2. May reveal latent structures in the data.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Requires careful interpretation and validation.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If sample size is insufficient for meaningful clusters.  
   - **Alternative**: Principal component analysis (PCA).

TASK 1 – INFORMATION EXTRACTION  

1. Study design  
- Type: Retrospective observational cohort study.  
- Setting: Single private endodontic practice (specialist L.A.), Argentina.  
- Timeframe: Cases treated between 1999 and 2019; radiographic follow‑up 1–17 years.  
- Population source: 144 consecutive patients referred for endodontic evaluation due to presence of an intraoral sinus tract.  
- Included sample: 137 teeth from 128 patients that met the inclusion criteria and underwent root canal treatment or retreatment by a single operator.  
- Unit of analysis: Primarily tooth; some variables are at patient level (age, sex, systemic condition).  
- Blinding: Outcome radiographic evaluations performed by 2 blinded endodontists; operator not involved in outcome scoring.

2. Objectives  
- Primary:  
  - Describe clinical and radiographic outcome (healed/healing/diseased) of root canal treatment/retreatment in teeth with chronic apical abscess and intraoral sinus tract treated by a single experienced operator.  
- Secondary:  
  - Describe demographic and baseline characteristics of these consecutive cases.  
  - Evaluate the influence of preoperative, intraoperative and postoperative variables on treatment/retreatment outcome of teeth with sinus tracts.  
  - Evaluate short‑term healing/closure of the sinus tract (up to 30 days) and its relationship with long‑term periradicular healing.

3. Eligibility criteria  

3.1 Inclusion (tooth level)  
- Tooth with an intraoral sinus tract and radiographic evidence of periradicular bone destruction.  
- Pulpal status: necrotic pulp confirmed by thermal pulp tests or previously root‑canal treated tooth.  
- Tooth restorable and with indication for root canal treatment or retreatment.  
- Tooth mobility score <3.  
- Periodontal status: no periodontal pockets >4 mm.  
- Patient without significant systemic disease (assessed at patient level).  
- Patient gave informed consent.

3.2 Exclusion (implied)  
- Teeth not restorable.  
- Teeth without radiographic periradicular bone destruction.  
- Teeth with vital pulp.  
- Teeth with mobility ≥3 or periodontal pockets >4 mm.  
- Patients with significant systemic disease.  
- Referred patients with sinus tracts who did not meet the above inclusion criteria (7 teeth excluded: 144 referred → 137 treated).

4. Groups / categories  

4.1 Outcome categories (tooth level) – final periradicular outcome  
Based on clinical findings and periapical radiographs (PAI). For each tooth, the root with highest PAI used in multi‑rooted teeth.  

- Healed:  
  - Asymptomatic and functional.  
  - Sinus tract disappeared (closed).  
  - No radiographic apical periodontitis (PAI 1–2).  

- Healing:  
  - Asymptomatic and functional.  
  - Sinus tract disappeared.  
  - Radiographic lesion decreased in size (PAI score decreased but still >2).  
  - Follow‑up shorter than 4 years.  

- Diseased:  
  - Symptomatic and nonfunctional and/or  
  - Sinus tract persisted or recurred and/or  
  - Radiographic lesion unchanged or enlarged (PAI 3–5).

For many analyses, this can be dichotomized:  
- Favorable = Healed + Healing.  
- Unfavorable = Diseased.  

4.2 Short‑term sinus tract status (tooth level)  
- Time points: 7, 14, 21, 30 days after treatment.  
- Status at each visit: healed (closed) vs not healed (active).  
- Persistence: not healed after 30 days.  
- Recurrence: re‑emergence after prior closure at any time.

4.3 Lesion size classification (preoperative, tooth level)  
Measured on periapical radiograph (largest diameter):  
- Small: ≤5 mm.  
- Large: >5 to <10 mm.  
- Very large: ≥10 mm.

4.4 Tooth / sinus tract / treatment categories  
Preoperative:  
- Sinus tract location: buccal / distal / palatal–lingual.  
- Tooth type:  
  - Likely categories: anterior / premolar / molar, or more specific (incisor, canine, etc.), not explicitly detailed but recorded.  
- Arch: maxillary vs mandibular.  
- Root canal status:  
  - Necrotic pulp (no prior treatment).  
  - Previously root‑canal treated (retreatment).  
- Symptoms: presence/absence of symptoms pre‑treatment (not further classified in Methods).  
- Periodontal probing depth (continuous, later categorized as ≤4 mm by inclusion criteria; could still have variation below 4 mm).  
- Tooth mobility score: ordinal; all <3, but still potentially varying (e.g., 0–2).  
- Patient age (continuous); sex (male/female).

Intraoperative:  
- Number of treatment sessions: single vs multiple visits.  
- Working length (WL): continuous measurement, but in practice most likely analyzed as categorized if mis‑length occurs; Methods only say it was recorded.  
- Obturation technique:  
  - Lateral compaction.  
  - Schilder’s vertical compaction.  
- Quality of root canal filling:  
  - Adequate (all canals obturated; no voids; apical terminus 0–1 mm short of radiographic apex).  
  - Inadequate (any deviation).  
- Filling of lateral canals: present vs absent.

Implementation:

- Why it may not be appropriate:

Assumptions to check:

CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED TESTS  

B1. Ordinal/multinomial logistic regression  
- Purpose:

- Why appropriate:  
  1) The outcome is inherently ordered (Healed better than Healing better than Diseased); ordinal logistic regression exploits this ordering and is more efficient than treating it as nominal or binary.  
  2) Allows assessment of whether predictors shift the odds toward better vs worse outcome categories.  
- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  - Proportional odds assumption may be violated (effects of predictors not constant across cumulative splits).

- When NOT to use:  
  - Clear violation of proportional odds assumption, especially with small sample size.

- Alternatives if assumptions are violated:  
  - Multinomial (polytomous) logistic regression treating outcome as nominal (no ordering assumption).

B2. Survival analysis (Kaplan–Meier and log‑rank test) for time to sinus tract closure  
- Purpose:

TASK 3 – INAPPROPRIATE TESTS AND WHY  

1. Student’s t‑test / ANOVA for primary outcome vs categorical predictors  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - The primary outcome (Healed/Healing/Diseased) is categorical, not continuous; t‑test/ANOVA requires continuous dependent variable.  
  - Distribution assumptions (normality, equal variances) do not hold for categorical outcomes.  
- Preferred alternatives:  
  - Chi‑square/Fisher’s exact; logistic/ordinal logistic regression.  

2. Pearson correlation coefficient (r) between categorical predictors and outcome  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Pearson’s r assumes continuous, approximately normally distributed variables; here both outcome and many predictors are categorical.  
- Preferred alternatives:  
  - Cramer’s V for association between categorical variables; logistic regression; chi‑square.  

3. Linear regression treating outcome as numeric (e.g., Healed=1, Healing=2, Diseased=3)  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - The numeric coding is arbitrary and does not reflect interval scaling; residuals will be non‑normal and heteroscedastic.  
  - Linear regression does not respect bounded nature or multinomial distribution of outcome.  
- Preferred alternatives:  
  - Logistic regression (binary) or ordinal/multinomial logistic regression.  

4. Paired t‑test for pre‑ and post‑PAI scores without considering ordinal nature  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - PAI is ordinal, not interval; paired t‑test assumes continuous, normally distributed difference scores.  
- Preferred alternatives:  
  - Wilcoxon signed‑rank test for paired ordinal data, or modeling outcome categories directly.  

5. Repeated‑measures ANOVA for serial sinus tract status (7,14,21,30 days)  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Sinus tract status is binary (closed/not closed), not continuous.  
  - Measurement times are few and discrete; repeated‑measures ANOVA assumptions (normality, sphericity) violated.  
- Preferred alternatives:  
  - Survival/interval‑censored models, or generalized estimating equations with binary outcome at each time point.  

6. Parametric methods assuming independence when multiple teeth per patient are common  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Teeth from the same patient are correlated; ignoring clustering may understate variance and give overly narrow confidence intervals.  
- Preferred alternatives:  
  - Cluster‑robust standard errors, GEE, or mixed‑effects models; or per‑patient analysis.  

7. Unadjusted multiple hypothesis testing without correction in extensive exploratory analyses  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Numerous comparisons increase risk of Type I error inflation; p‑values become misleading.  
- Preferred alternatives:  
  - Pre‑specify primary hypotheses; use FDR or Bonferroni correction for large sets of secondary tests; emphasize effect sizes and CIs over p‑values.

### TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS

*   **Linear Regression:** Should NOT be used because the primary outcome (Healed/Healing/Diseased) is categorical, not continuous. Linear regression requires a continuous dependent variable.
*   **Paired t-test / Repeated Measures ANOVA:** Should NOT be used. Although patients are followed over time, the outcome is a clinical status (success/failure) rather than a continuous metric measured repeatedly (like blood pressure). 
*   **Pearson Correlation:** Should NOT be used because the vast majority of the variables in this study are categorical or ordinal. Pearson requires two continuous, normally distributed variables.

### TASK 4: SUMMARY

The analytical approach for this study must transition from validating the measurement tools to assessing crude associations, and finally to robust multivariable modeling. First, **Cohen’s Kappa** is essential to validate the reliability of the PAI scoring between the two blinded evaluators. Next, descriptive statistics and **Chi-square/Fisher's exact tests** will provide a baseline understanding of how individual clinical factors relate to treatment success. 

However, because the follow-up time varies massively (1 to 17 years), relying solely on standard Logistic Regression is statistically flawed, as it ignores the time patients were at risk of failure. Therefore, the core of the advanced analysis should rely on **Survival Analysis (Kaplan-Meier and Cox Proportional Hazards Regression)** to account for right-censored data and variable follow-up times. Finally, because a few patients contributed more than one tooth, applying a **Generalized Estimating Equation (GEE)** will ensure that intra-patient clustering does not artificially inflate statistical significance, ensuring the highest level of methodological rigor.
