### 1. Study design and setting

- **Design**: Historical controlled cohort study (retrospective cohort from clinical records and radiographs).
- **Time frame**: 2‑year follow‑up for periapical healing and restorative complications.
- **Setting**:  
  - The Endodontic Practice, Poole, UK  
  - Unit of Endodontology, UCL Eastman Dental Institute, London, UK
- **Unit of analysis**: Tooth (root‑treated tooth with full coverage restoration).

### 2. Objectives / hypotheses

**Primary aim**

- To determine **2‑year radiographic periapical healing** associated with NSRCT:
  - Group 1: NSRCT completed **through an existing full coverage restoration** (crown retained).
  - Group 2: NSRCT after **removal of existing restorations** (direct or indirect), placement of an immediate core, and subsequent **new full coverage restoration** (crown removed and restored).

**Null hypothesis**

- No significant difference in periapical healing between:
  - Teeth treated through pre‑existing full coverage restorations
  - Teeth treated after restoration removal and subsequent new crown.

**Secondary aim**

- To determine the **complication rate of accessing through existing extra‑coronal restorations** (porcelain fracture and de‑cementation) for up to 2 years.

### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS

#### A1. Descriptive statistics and group comparisons at baseline

**Tests**

- Means ± SD, medians (IQR), and frequencies (%) as appropriate.
- **ANOVA** or **Kruskal–Wallis** for continuous variables vs 3 groups.
- **Chi‑square test** (or Fisher’s exact) for categorical variables vs 3 groups.

**Purpose**

- Describe sample characteristics.
- Check for baseline imbalances between groups (potential confounding).

**Why appropriate**

1. Groups are independent (different teeth).
2. Variables are standard continuous (age, lesion size) and categorical (sex, tooth type, etc.).
3. Helps identify confounder distribution to inform multivariable modelling.

**Why may not be appropriate**

- ANOVA and chi‑square rely on distributional assumptions that may not be met (e.g. skewed continuous data, sparse contingency table cells).

**When NOT to use**

- ANOVA:
  - When continuous variable is highly skewed with outliers and transformation/robust methods are not planned.
  - When group variances are grossly unequal and sample sizes extremely unbalanced.
- Chi‑square:
  - When expected cell counts <5 in >20% of cells.

**Alternatives if assumptions violated**

- ANOVA → **Kruskal‑Wallis** (non‑parametric).
- Chi‑square → **Fisher’s exact test** or **Monte Carlo exact test**.

---

#### A2. Inter‑observer agreement for radiographic healing

**Test**

- **Weighted Kappa statistic** (κw) for ordinal healing categories.
- Possibly **95% CI** for κw.

**Purpose**

- Quantify inter‑examiner agreement on healing category assignment for 30% of teeth.

**Why appropriate**

1. Healing outcome is ordinal (4 ordered categories).
2. Two raters categorizing the same teeth.
3. Weighted kappa accounts for partial agreement (e.g. difference between “complete” vs “incomplete” smaller than “complete” vs “failure”).

**Why may not be appropriate**

- Requires adequate number of doubly rated cases in each category; very sparse categories can destabilize κw.

**When NOT to use**

- If healing outcome is dichotomized for reliability (then use unweighted kappa).
- If only one rater ultimately used and reliability data are insufficient (very small subsample).

**Alternatives if assumptions violated**

- If categories are collapsed to binary → **Cohen’s kappa** (unweighted).
- If >2 raters (not here) → **Fleiss’ kappa**.
- For small samples → report **percent agreement** with caution.

---

#### A3. Primary outcome comparison – unadjusted

**Tests**

- For **binary success/failure** healing endpoint:
  - **Chi‑square test** (or Fisher’s exact) comparing:
    - (a) Crown retained vs Crown removed.
    - (b) Crown retained vs Restoration removed.
    - (c) 3‑group comparison.
- For 4‑category ordinal outcome:
  - **Chi‑square test for independence** comparing healing categories across 3 groups.
  - Optionally **Cochran–Armitage trend test** if ordering is used (e.g. success → failure gradient).

**Purpose**

- Provide unadjusted comparison of healing distributions between groups.

**Why appropriate**

1. Outcome is categorical; groups are independent.
2. Provides straightforward, clinically interpretable differences in success rates.
3. Forms basis for later regression modelling (effect sizes).

**Why may not be appropriate**

- Sparseness in some healing categories (e.g. very few “uncertain” or “incomplete” cases) makes multi‑cell chi‑square unstable.

**When NOT to use**

- When any cell has zero or very small counts and combining categories is not clinically justifiable.
- For matched or paired data (not the case here).

**Alternatives if assumptions violated**

- Collapse healing categories to binary, then use **Fisher’s exact test**.
- Use **exact multinomial tests** or permutation tests if sample small and categories must remain.

---

#### A4. Multivariable analysis for periapical healing (primary analysis)

**Test**

- **Multivariable logistic regression** with binary healing outcome:

  - Dependent variable:
    - Example: “Healed” (complete + incomplete healing) vs “Not healed” (uncertain + failure) or “complete/incomplete vs failure only”, depending on clinical definition.

  - Key independent variable:
    - Group (crown retained; crown removed; restoration removed).  
      - Represented via dummy variables, “crown retained” as reference.

  - Covariates (candidate confounders / predictors):
    - Age (continuous or categorized).
    - Sex.
    - Tooth type (arch + category).
    - Pre‑operative lesion size (continuous).
    - Presence of sinus tract.
    - Primary vs secondary treatment.
    - Crown material.
    - Post‑RCT core material.
    - Quality of final coverage restoration (good vs poor).
    - Function as FPD abutment at review.

**Purpose**

- Estimate adjusted odds ratios (ORs) for healing associated with treatment group, controlling for confounding factors.
- Identify other predictors of healing.

**Why appropriate**

1. Binary outcome with multiple predictors – logistic regression is standard.
2. Cohort design with independent observations (teeth) and cross‑sectional outcome at 1–2 years fits logistic framework.
3. Allows adjustment for multiple confounders and reporting of adjusted effects (ORs with 95% CIs).

**Why may not be appropriate**

- Potential clustering: multiple teeth may belong to the same patient, violating independence.
- Outcome incidence might be very high or very low; ORs may overstate relative risk.

**When NOT to use**

- When there is severe quasi‑separation (e.g. one group has nearly 100% success or failure).
- When cluster structure is strong (many teeth per patient) and cluster‑robust or multilevel methods are not available.

**Alternatives if assumptions violated**

- Account for clustering:
  - **Generalized estimating equations (GEE)** with logit link and patient as cluster.
  - **Mixed‑effects logistic regression** (random intercept for patient).
- For common outcomes where risk ratio is preferred:
  - **Log‑binomial regression** or **Poisson regression with robust variance**.
- For separation issues:
  - **Firth’s penalized logistic regression**.

---

#### A5. Analysis of restorative complications (secondary outcome)

**Tests**

- If outcome is **binary** within 2‑year follow‑up (ever vs never):

  - **Chi‑square / Fisher’s exact** to compare complication proportions by:
    - Group (especially crown retained vs others; porcelain fracture mainly in crown‑retained).
    - Crown material.
    - Core material, etc.

  - **Logistic regression** to model probability of complication with:
    - Predictors: group, crown material, core material, age, tooth type, etc.

- If **time‑to‑complication** data (date of event or review) are available:
  - **Kaplan–Meier survival curves** by group and **log‑rank test**.
  - **Cox proportional hazards regression** with covariates.

**Purpose**

- Quantify and compare complication rates across groups and identify risk factors.

**Why appropriate**

1. Complication presence is binary; logistic models and contingency tests are suitable.
2. If time‑to‑event data exist, Cox and KM provide more informative estimates (hazards, survival probabilities).

**Why may not be appropriate**

- Small number of complications may limit model stability and power.
- Censoring times may be unclear if review times vary and are not recorded accurately.

**When NOT to use**

- Cox model when proportional hazards assumption is violated and cannot be reasonably addressed.
- Logistic regression when outcome is extremely rare (<5–10 events per predictor) → risk of overfitting.

**Alternatives if assumptions violated**

- For rare events: **Exact logistic regression** or limit predictors to avoid overfitting.
- For non‑proportional hazards: **time‑varying coefficients** or stratified Cox model; or use **pooled logistic regression** with discrete time.

---

### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED TESTS

#### B1. Trend or ordinal modelling of healing outcomes

**Test**

- **Ordinal logistic regression (proportional odds model)** treating healing as ordered categories:
  - 1 = complete; 2 = incomplete; 3 = uncertain; 4 = failure.

**Purpose**

- Use full ordinal information rather than collapsing to binary; estimate cumulative odds of better vs worse healing across categories.

**Why appropriate**

1. Healing categories are inherently ordered.
2. May increase statistical efficiency over binary dichotomy.
3. Can adjust for same covariates as in binary logistic regression.

**Why may not be appropriate**

- Proportional odds assumption (same effect across all category thresholds) may be violated.

**When NOT to use**

- When likelihood ratio test or Brant test shows strong violation of proportional odds and partial proportional odds models are not planned.
- If some categories are extremely sparse.

**Alternatives if assumptions violated**

- **Partial proportional odds models**.
- **Multinomial logistic regression** (treating categories as nominal, see B2).
- Collapse to binary and use standard logistic regression (A4).

---

#### B2. Multinomial logistic regression for healing

**Test**

- **Multinomial logistic regression** with 3 or 4 healing categories (e.g. complete/incomplete/uncertain/failure, or combined categories to ensure adequate counts).

**Purpose**

- Model probabilities of each healing category vs a reference category; explore more nuanced pattern of outcomes between groups.

**Why appropriate**

1. Does not require proportional odds assumption.
2. Allows different effect sizes for each category contrast.

**Why may not be appropriate**

- Less efficient; more parameters; requires larger sample size.
- Interpretation is more complex and may be less clinically intuitive.

**When NOT to use**

- When sample size is limited, especially if many covariates are included; risk of overfitting.
- When some categories are rare.

**Alternatives if assumptions violated**

- Collapse categories (e.g. success vs failure/uncertain) and use binary logistic regression.
- Use ordinal logistic regression (B1) if proportional odds holds.

---

#### B3. Sensitivity analyses for handling “uncertain” category

**Tests**

- Repeat logistic/ordinal/multinomial models under alternative coding:
  - “Uncertain” grouped with **failure**.
  - “Uncertain” grouped with **success** (if clinically justifiable).
  - “Uncertain” **excluded** (complete case).

**Purpose**

- Assess robustness of conclusions to how ‘uncertain’ category is handled.

**Why appropriate**

1. “Uncertain” outcome ambiguous; classification can materially influence success rates.
2. Sensitivity analyses improve credibility of findings.

**Why may not be appropriate**

- Multiple comparisons may increase type I error if not interpreted carefully.
- Reduction in sample size if “uncertain” excluded.

**When NOT to use**

- When “uncertain” category is extremely small and has negligible impact; results may be unstable.

**Alternatives**

- Report primary analysis with most defensible classification (e.g. uncertain as failure) and clearly describe rationale, with limited sensitivity checks.

---

#### B4. Assessment of confounding and interaction

**Tests**

- Within logistic/ordinal models:
  - Assess **confounding** by change‑in‑estimate (>10% change in group OR when covariate added/removed).
  - Test **interaction terms**:
    - e.g. Group × quality of final restoration.
    - Group × previous RCT.
    - Group × tooth type.

**Purpose**

- Determine whether effect of group differs across subgroups.
- Adjust models appropriately.

**Why appropriate**

1. Clinical plausibility of effect modification (e.g. impact of keeping crown may depend on quality of final restoration).
2. Static cohort design allows such modelling.

**Why may not be appropriate**

- Multiple interaction tests can produce unstable estimates and low power.

**When NOT to use**

- If sample is insufficient for multiplicative interaction terms (rule of thumb: ≥10–15 events per parameter).
- If there is no strong a priori clinical rationale.

**Alternatives**

- Pre‑specify a small number of key interactions; avoid data‑driven fishing.
- Report stratified analyses (e.g. stratify by good vs poor restoration quality) rather than formal interaction tests where sample is limited.

---

#### B5. Model diagnostics and goodness‑of‑fit

**Tests**

- For logistic regression:
  - **Hosmer–Lemeshow test** (with caution) and/or calibration plots.
  - **ROC curve** and area under the curve (AUC).
  - Check for influential observations (e.g. Cook’s distance).
- For Cox models (if used for complications):
  - **Schoenfeld residuals** for proportional hazards.
  - Graphical checks of log(–log) survival curves.

**Purpose**

- Ensure models adequately fit data and assumptions are reasonable.

**Why appropriate**

1. Regression models are central to primary inference; lack‑of‑fit can mislead.
2. Diagnostics help refine model (e.g. transformation of continuous covariates).

**Why may not be appropriate**

- Hosmer–Lemeshow has limitations: can be oversensitive in large samples and underpowered in small samples.

**When NOT to use**

- Relying solely on p‑values from goodness‑of‑fit tests to accept/reject models.

**Alternatives**

- Graphical diagnostics (calibration plots, observed vs predicted).
- Use flexible modelling of continuous predictors (restricted cubic splines) if non‑linear relationships suspected.

---

#### B6. Adjustment for clustering (if multiple teeth per patient)

**Tests**

- **Cluster‑robust standard errors** or
- **GEE logistic regression** with patient as cluster and exchangeable correlation.
- Alternatively, **mixed‑effects logistic regression** with random intercept for patient.

**Purpose**

- Correct standard errors and confidence intervals for intra‑patient correlation.

**Why appropriate**

1. Many patients may have more than one treated tooth.
2. Ignoring clustering underestimates variability → artificially narrow CIs.

**Why may not be appropriate**

- If nearly all patients have only one tooth; clustering negligible.

**When NOT to use**

- When number of clusters (patients) is very small (<30); some methods become unstable.

**Alternatives**

- If cluster numbers small, use **cluster bootstrap** for robust CIs.
- Aggregate to patient‑level analysis (e.g. restrict to first tooth per patient) as sensitivity analysis.

### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL TESTS

#### C1. Subgroup analyses

**Tests**

- Stratified logistic regression or chi‑square tests within subgroups:
  - e.g. high vs low lesion size (≥6 mm vs <6 mm).
  - Primary vs secondary RCT.
  - Good vs poor final restoration quality.

**Purpose**

- Explore whether effect of group varies in clinically meaningful strata.

**Why appropriate**

1. May generate hypotheses about specific clinical scenarios.
2. Helps interpret whether certain subsets benefit more/less from crown retention.

**Why may not be appropriate**

- Multiple subgroup analyses → risk of spurious results; limited power.

**When NOT to use**

- When strata are small; unstable estimates.

**Alternatives**

- Reserve subgroup analyses for pre‑specified, clinically justified questions.
- Present as exploratory with appropriate caveats.

---

#### C2. Propensity score methods

**Tests**

- **Propensity score logistic regression** (probability of receiving crown retained vs removed/restoration removed).
- Use propensity score for:
  - Matching.
  - Stratification (quintiles).
  - Inverse probability weighting.
  - Covariate adjustment in outcome model.

**Purpose**

- Address confounding by indication in non‑randomized, historical cohort.

**Why appropriate**

1. Treatment decision (keep vs remove crown) was based on clinical judgment and patient preference (non‑random).  
2. Propensity methods can balance measured covariates between groups and assess robustness.

**Why may not be appropriate**

- Sample size may not support matching or weighting without substantial loss of data.
- Only adjusts for measured confounders; unmeasured confounding remains.

**When NOT to use**

- When PS models are poorly specified or show poor overlap (e.g. PS distributions non‑overlapping between groups).
- When maximum sample retention is critical and matching would discard many observations.

**Alternatives**

- Standard multivariable outcome regression (as in A4).
- Doubly robust estimators (if capacity exists) combining PS and outcome models.

#### C3. Continuous lesion size change (if measured)

If pre‑operative and follow‑up lesion sizes are recorded as continuous values:

**Tests**

- **Paired t‑test** or **Wilcoxon signed‑rank test** to evaluate within‑tooth change.
- **Linear regression** modelling absolute or relative change in lesion size with group and covariates.

**Purpose**

- Quantify magnitude of radiolucency reduction beyond categorical healing.

**Why appropriate**

1. Provides more granular outcome; may detect subtle differences.
2. Within‑tooth paired design for change analyses enhances power.

**Why may not be appropriate**

- Follow‑up lesion measures might not be available or reliable for all cases.
- Measurement error and non‑normal residuals.

**When NOT to use**

- If lesion measurements are semi‑quantitative or ordinal; treat as categorical instead.

**Alternatives**

- Use **ordinal regression** on lesion size categories (e.g. small, medium, large).

#### C4. Power or post‑hoc detectable difference calculations

**Test**

- Use standard formulae for proportions in two/three independent groups to estimate:
  - Detectable difference in healing rates given n=106, 57, 82.
  - Or evaluate post‑hoc achieved power.

**Purpose**

- Inform interpretability of non‑significant findings; contextualize risk of type II error.

**Why appropriate**

1. Historical cohort – sample size fixed; helpful to understand limitations.
2. Common in clinical literature to report approximate detectable differences.

**Why may not be appropriate**

- Post‑hoc “observed power” adds little beyond confidence intervals and can be misleading.

**When NOT to use**

- If misinterpreted as evidence about the truth of null hypothesis.

**Alternatives**

- Emphasize **effect sizes with 95% CIs** rather than power.

TASK 3 – INAPPROPRIATE TESTS
-----------------------------

Below are tests that **should not be used** in this context and reasons why.

1. **Paired t‑test / McNemar’s test to compare groups**

   - Inappropriate because groups are **independent** (different teeth/patients).
   - Paired tests assume matched or repeated measures in the same unit.

2. **Independent samples t‑test for healing outcome**

   - Healing is **categorical**, not continuous; t‑test assumes continuous normally distributed outcome.

3. **Pearson correlation between group and healing**

   - Group is nominal categorical; Pearson correlation assumes both variables are continuous and linear relationship.

4. **One‑way ANOVA on ordinal healing scores coded as 1–4**

   - Treats ordinal categories as interval‑scaled with equal spacing, which is questionable.
   - Violates ANOVA assumptions in many cases; ordinal or multinomial models are better.

5. **Linear regression with healing as continuous (1–4)**

   - Same issue as above: ordinal outcome treated as continuous can bias estimates and interpretation.

6. **Student’s t‑test or ANOVA to compare proportions (e.g., % healing)**

   - Proportions are bounded (0–1) and binomially distributed; chi‑square or logistic regression is more appropriate.

7. **Repeated measures ANOVA for follow‑up healing**

   - Only one relevant post‑treatment time point (final 1–2 year outcome) is used for primary outcome; repeated‑measures structure not present unless multiple standardized time points exist.

8. **Parametric survival analysis (e.g., exponential or Weibull) without time‑to‑event data**

   - Not appropriate unless exact event times (e.g. complication dates) are recorded and assumptions of parametric form are justifiable.

9. **Multiple t‑tests across many subgroups without correction**

   - Inflates type I error; use appropriate multivariable modelling and limited pre‑specified subgroup analyses.

10. **Unadjusted comparisons interpreted as causal without considering confounding**

   - Not a statistical test per se, but an analytical approach that would be inappropriate in a non‑randomized historical cohort where treatment assignment is confounded.

TASK 4 – SUMMARY OF ANALYTICAL APPROACH
----------------------------------------

- The study is a **retrospective cohort** comparing periapical healing after NSRCT performed through existing crowns versus after restoration removal and re‑crowing, with additional interest in restorative complications.
- The primary outcome, **periapical healing**, is categorical and likely dichotomized for main analysis; thus **logistic regression** is central, with unadjusted chi‑square tests and, ideally, **cluster‑robust or multilevel methods** if multiple teeth per patient.
- Covariate adjustment (age, sex, tooth type, lesion size, sinus tract, previous RCT, crown/core material, restoration quality, abutment function) is necessary due to **non‑random group allocation** and observed differences in baseline characteristics (e.g. previous treatment, core material).
- Inter‑observer reliability for radiographic scoring is quantified using **weighted kappa**, underpinning the credibility of the radiographic outcome assessment.
- Restorative complications are analysed as **binary outcomes** using chi‑square and logistic regression, or **time‑to‑event models** if dates are available.
- Optional ordinal or multinomial models for healing, sensitivity analyses around “uncertain” outcomes, and propensity score methods strengthen robustness and address the non‑randomized design.

This integrated plan provides a coherent, assumption‑aware framework to evaluate both the primary and secondary aims and to control for confounding inherent in a historical controlled cohort.
