### TASK 1: INFORMATION EXTRACTION

#### Study Characteristics:
1. **Study Design**: Historical controlled cohort study.
2. **Objectives**:
   - **Primary Aim**: To determine the 2-year radiographic periapical healing associated with nonsurgical root canal treatment (NSRCT) performed through existing crowns compared to teeth where crowns were removed prior to NSRCT.
   - **Secondary Aim**: To assess the complication rates (e.g., porcelain fracture, de-cementation) of accessing through existing crowns over a 2-year period.
3. **Groups**:
   - **Group 1**: NSRCT performed through existing crowns.
   - **Group 2**: NSRCT performed after crown removal, followed by new crown placement.
4. **Sample Size**:
   - Group 1: 106 teeth.
   - Group 2: 57 teeth.
   - Additional group (restoration removed): 82 teeth (not the primary focus).
5. **Variables**:
   - **Primary Outcome**: Radiographic periapical healing (categorized as complete, incomplete, uncertain).
   - **Secondary Outcomes**:
     - Complication rates (e.g., porcelain fracture, de-cementation).
     - Quality of final restoration (good vs. poor).
   - **Independent Variables**:
     - Group (crown retained vs. crown removed).
     - Demographics (age, sex).
     - Tooth-related factors (tooth type, size of pre-operative periapical radiolucency, presence of sinus tract, previous treatment, crown material, core material).
6. **Measurements**:
   - Radiographic periapical healing assessed by independent observers using pre-defined categories.
   - Quality of final restoration assessed clinically and radiographically.
7. **Data Collection Methods**:
   - Retrospective review of clinical records and radiographs.
   - Radiographic evaluation using a calibrated digital imaging system.
   - Clinical examination of restoration quality using a dental explorer and radiographs.

### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY
1. **Chi-Square Test/Fisher’s Exact Test**:
   - **Purpose**: To compare categorical variables (e.g., radiographic healing outcomes, complication rates, restoration quality) between groups.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. The primary outcomes (healing and complications) are categorical.
     2. The groups are independent.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Small cell counts in contingency tables may violate assumptions.
   - **When NOT to Use**: If expected cell counts are <5 in >20% of cells.
   - **Alternative**: Fisher’s Exact Test for small sample sizes.

2. **Independent Samples t-Test/Mann-Whitney U Test**:
   - **Purpose**: To compare continuous variables (e.g., size of periapical radiolucency, age) between groups.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. The groups are independent.
     2. Continuous data are being analyzed.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: If data are not normally distributed or variances are unequal.
   - **When NOT to Use**: If normality or homogeneity of variance assumptions are violated.
   - **Alternative**: Mann-Whitney U Test for non-parametric data.

3. **Logistic Regression**:
   - **Purpose**: To assess the association between independent variables (e.g., group, demographic, and tooth-related factors) and binary outcomes (e.g., healing status, restoration quality).
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Allows adjustment for confounding variables.
     2. Suitable for binary outcomes.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: If sample size is insufficient for the number of predictors.
   - **When NOT to Use**: If multicollinearity exists among predictors.
   - **Alternative**: Exact logistic regression for small sample sizes.

---

#### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED
1. **Kaplan-Meier Survival Analysis**:
   - **Purpose**: To analyze complication-free survival over the 2-year follow-up period.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Accounts for time-to-event data (e.g., time to porcelain fracture or de-cementation).
     2. Censoring (e.g., loss to follow-up) can be handled.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: If follow-up times are not accurately recorded.
   - **When NOT to Use**: If event times are not available for all participants.
   - **Alternative**: Cox proportional hazards regression for multivariable analysis.

2. **Cochran-Armitage Trend Test**:
   - **Purpose**: To assess trends in healing outcomes across ordered categories (e.g., complete, incomplete, uncertain healing).
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Suitable for ordinal categorical data.
     2. Detects trends rather than differences.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: If sample sizes in some categories are too small.
   - **When NOT to Use**: If data are not ordinal.
   - **Alternative**: Chi-Square Test for independence.

---

#### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL
1. **Multinomial Logistic Regression**:
   - **Purpose**: To model the association between independent variables and the three-category healing outcome (complete, incomplete, uncertain).
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Suitable for nominal outcomes with more than two categories.
     2. Adjusts for confounders.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Requires a larger sample size for stable estimates.
   - **When NOT to Use**: If sample size is insufficient for the number of predictors.
   - **Alternative**: Collapsing categories into binary outcomes and using logistic regression.

2. **Generalized Estimating Equations (GEE)**:
   - **Purpose**: To account for clustering (e.g., multiple teeth from the same patient).
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Accounts for within-patient correlation.
     2. Suitable for repeated measures or clustered data.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: If clustering is minimal or negligible.
   - **When NOT to Use**: If data are truly independent.
   - **Alternative**: Standard regression models.
