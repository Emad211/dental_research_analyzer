TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

Overall approach:  
- Main focus: Compare radiographic healing (continuous and ordinal) across the three materials over time, accounting for repeated measures and small sample size.
- Primary analysis: Mixed-effects models for continuous lesion size (or change from baseline), plus nonparametric validation; and ordinal regression for subjective healing scores.
- Secondary analyses: Dichotomous success/failure, intraobserver reliability, sensitivity analyses for dropouts.

Below, tests/methods are grouped into Category A (mandatory), B (recommended), and C (optional).

CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS / ANALYSES

A1. Descriptive statistics and baseline comparability

Test / method:
- Descriptive statistics (means/medians, SD/IQR, counts, percentages).
- Baseline comparability tests:
  - For continuous baseline variables (e.g., baseline lesion size): ANOVA or Kruskal–Wallis.
  - For categorical baseline variables (e.g., tooth type, jaw if available): Chi-square or Fisher’s exact test.

Purpose:
- Summarize patient, tooth, and lesion characteristics by treatment group.
- Check whether randomization produced comparable groups at baseline, especially regarding baseline lesion size.

Why appropriate:
1) Randomized trials require demonstration (or at least reporting) of baseline comparability to interpret between-group differences as treatment effects rather than confounding.
2) Baseline lesion size is a strong prognostic factor; imbalances could bias observed outcomes and justify adjustment.

Why it may not be appropriate:
- Over-emphasis on formal baseline hypothesis testing in randomized trials is controversial; imbalance is best addressed by adjusted analysis rather than by concluding “imbalance significant”.

When NOT to use:
- Do not interpret baseline tests as proof of randomization success or failure; small p-values do not invalidate randomization, especially with small n.

Alternative if assumptions violated:
- For non-normal continuous baseline variables: use Kruskal-Wallis instead of ANOVA.
- For sparse categorical data: use Fisher’s exact instead of Chi-square.

A2. Primary continuous outcome analysis: Mixed-effects model for lesion size (or change)

Test / method:
- Linear mixed-effects model (LMM) for repeated measures.
  - Outcome: lesion size (mm) at each time point, or change from baseline.
  - Fixed effects: material (3 levels), time (preop/postop/2/4/6 months), and material × time interaction.
  - Random effects: random intercept for patient (and optionally random slope for time).

Purpose:
- Evaluate the trajectory of lesion size over time and compare healing across materials, accounting for within-patient correlation and differing follow-up patterns.

Why appropriate:
1) Repeated measures: Each patient contributes multiple lesion size measurements; LMM properly models correlation among repeated measures and accommodates irregularly spaced measurements and missing time points (assuming missing at random).
2) Small, unbalanced sample: LMM can handle unequal numbers of observations per patient and unequal group sizes (13, 14, 8 at 6 months), without discarding partial follow-up data.
3) Allows explicit testing of:
   - Main effect of material (average difference).
   - Material × time interaction (differences in healing trajectories).

Why it may not be appropriate:
- Assumes approximate normality of residuals and homoscedasticity; lesion size distributions or changes may be skewed, especially with small sample size and many near-zero values as healing progresses.

When NOT to use:
- If outcome is clearly non-normal, with heavy skew and outliers, and transformations do not improve distribution.
- If data are heavily zero-inflated near later time points and the measurement scale is not continuous enough for LMM.

Alternative if assumptions violated:
- Use log transformation of lesion size or add 1 and log-transform if zeros occur.
- Use rank-based methods for repeated measures:
  - Nonparametric repeated measures tests (e.g., Brunner–Langer nonparametric ANOVA, aligned rank transforms).
- Use generalized linear mixed model (GLMM) with appropriate distribution (e.g., Gamma with log link) if lesion size remains positive and skewed.

A3. Cross-sectional group comparison of lesion size at predefined time points

Test / method:
- Nonparametric tests per time point:
  - Kruskal–Wallis test to compare lesion size among 3 materials at each time point (especially 6 months).
  - Post-hoc pairwise Mann–Whitney U tests with multiple comparison adjustment (e.g., Bonferroni or Holm).

Purpose:
- Provide simple, interpretable comparisons of final lesion size (or residual radiolucency) across materials at clinically important time points (e.g., 6 months), complementing the mixed model.

Why appropriate:
1) Small sample size and non-normal distributions: Kruskal–Wallis and Mann–Whitney do not require normality and are robust to outliers.
2) Independent groups: Each patient receives only one material, satisfying independence assumption across groups.
3) Nonparametric results provide a robustness check for the parametric mixed model.

Why it may not be appropriate:
- Ignores within-patient correlation across time; per-time analysis does not exploit repeated measures structure.
- Reduced power due to multiple testing (2, 4, 6 months separately), and smaller n at later times, especially in the Biodentine group.

When NOT to use:
- If repeated measures model adequately addresses primary question and power is very low; additional cross-sectional tests may be redundant.

Alternative if assumptions violated:
- If data are truly categorical (e.g., dichotomized success/failure): use Chi-square or Fisher’s exact instead of Kruskal-Wallis.
- If many tied ranks (e.g., multiple zeros), consider permutation-based tests.

A4. Primary ordinal outcome analysis: Subjective healing classification

Test / method:
- Ordinal logistic regression (proportional odds model) or cumulative link mixed model (CLMM) if using all time points.
  - Outcome: 4-level healing score (0=no healing, 1=some, 2=moderate, 3=good).
  - Predictors: material, time, and possibly material × time.

Purpose:
- Compare the distribution of healing categories between materials, accounting for ordinal nature, and optionally repeated measures.

Why appropriate:
1) Healing score is clearly ordinal; ordinal models exploit rank ordering without assuming equal intervals.
2) Allows estimation of odds ratios for better healing in one material vs others, adjusting for time.

Why it may not be appropriate:
- Proportional odds assumption may be violated (differences vary across cut-points).
- Sample size is small, especially with 4 categories and three groups; sparse cells may produce unstable estimates.

When NOT to use:
- If categories are very sparsely populated (e.g., few cases in “no healing” at later time points).
- If convergence issues arise due to small n or quasi-complete separation.

Alternative if assumptions violated:
- Collapsing categories into fewer levels (e.g., “no/some” vs “moderate/good”) and using:
  - Binary logistic regression (per time point).
  - GLMM (logit link) for repeated measurements across time.
- Nonparametric tests:
  - Jonckheere–Terpstra test or Cochran–Mantel–Haenszel statistics on ordered categories (per time point).

A5. Intraobserver reliability assessment for lesion size measurement

Test / method:
- Intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC), two-way random-effects, absolute agreement, for continuous lesion size measurements.
- Additionally, Bland–Altman plots for agreement (optional but useful).

Purpose:
- Quantify repeatability and reliability of lesion size measurements made twice per scan by the same radiologist.

Why appropriate:
1) ICC is standard for continuous measurements from the same rater, measuring both correlation and agreement.
2) Critical because outcome depends entirely on radiographic measurements; poor reliability undermines conclusions.

Why it may not be appropriate:
- Requires sufficient number of repeated measurements; if repeated measures exist for all scans, fine; if only for a small subset, ICC precision may be limited.

When NOT to use:
- If only a trivial number of scans were double-measured or if repeated measurements were mean-aggregated before storing (raw repeats unavailable).

Alternative if assumptions violated:
- If distribution is skewed or outliers present: use nonparametric agreement metrics (e.g., concordance correlation coefficient) or at least report absolute differences and limits of agreement.

A6. Handling missing data and dropouts: Sensitivity analyses

Test / method:
- Primary analysis under mixed-effects model (implicitly handles missing at random).
- Sensitivity analyses:
  - Complete-case analysis (only patients with 6-month data).
  - LOCF (last observation carried forward) is generally discouraged but can be briefly reported if used historically.
  - Pattern analysis (compare baseline characteristics and early outcomes between completers vs dropouts using Chi-square/Mann–Whitney).

Purpose:
- Assess robustness of conclusions to loss to follow-up and missing outcomes.

Why appropriate:
1) Substantial attrition (59 → 35 at 6 months, ~41% drop) may bias results; evaluating patterns in missingness is essential.
2) Mixed models rely on missing at random; sensitivity analyses assess plausibility and impact.

Why it may not be appropriate:
- Very small final sample might limit interpretability of sensitivity analyses; they may mainly be descriptive.

When NOT to use:
- LOCF should not be used as the primary method due to bias; use only for comparison against more appropriate methods.

Alternative if assumptions violated:
- If missing-not-at-random is suspected (e.g., symptomatic failures more likely to drop), consider pattern-mixture models or worst-case/best-case bounds (e.g., assume noncompleters in a given group failed vs succeeded).

CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED TESTS / ANALYSES (ENHANCE RIGOR)

B1. Adjusted analysis including baseline lesion size

Test / method:
- ANCOVA-like extension within LMM:
  - Include baseline (pre-operative) lesion size as covariate; analyze change from baseline or final absolute size.
- Or cross-sectional ANCOVA at 6 months:
  - Outcome: lesion size at 6 months.
  - Predictor: material.
  - Covariate: baseline lesion size.

Purpose:
- Improve precision and account for baseline heterogeneity in lesion size across groups.

Why appropriate:
1) Baseline lesion size strongly influences healing; adjusting removes “noise” and reduces bias due to any random imbalance.
2) ANCOVA has greater power than analyzing change scores alone if baseline and follow-up correlate.

Why it may not be appropriate:
- Small sample size may make models with many parameters unstable, especially if additional covariates (age, tooth type) are added.

When NOT to use:
- If baseline lesion size is measured with low reliability or is missing for many patients.

Alternative if assumptions violated:
- If ANCOVA assumptions fail (non-linearity, non-homogeneous slopes), allow baseline-by-material interaction or stratify by baseline lesion size (e.g., small vs large lesions) and perform stratified analyses.

B2. Dichotomous “success/failure” analysis

Test / method:
- Define radiographic “success” (e.g., moderate/good healing, or >X% reduction in lesion size).
- Per time point (especially 6 months):
  - Compare success proportions using Chi-square or Fisher’s exact test.
- Logistic regression (or GLMM if repeated measures):
  - Outcome: success vs failure.
  - Predictor: material, adjusted for baseline covariates as feasible.

Purpose:
- Provide clinically intuitive outcome (success/failure) for clinicians and support statements like “no significant difference among materials”.

Why appropriate:
1) Clinicians interpret results more readily as success rates than as continuous mm changes.
2) Logistic models handle binary outcomes appropriately and allow covariate adjustment.

Why it may not be appropriate:
- Dichotomization loses information and reduces power; with small sample at 6 months (notably only 8 Biodentine), estimates may be unstable.

When NOT to use:
- If the number of events per group is very small (e.g., <5 successes or <5 failures), logistic regression may not converge; rely on Fisher’s exact instead.

Alternative if assumptions violated:
- Exact logistic regression for small sample sizes.
- Bayesian logistic models with weakly informative priors to stabilize estimates.

B3. Time-to-event analysis (if time to “adequate healing” is measurable)

Test / method:
- Define event: first time point at which healing reaches or exceeds a specified level (e.g., “moderate” or “good” healing).
- Kaplan–Meier curves by material.
- Log-rank test to compare survival curves.
- Cox proportional hazards model for hazard ratios.

Purpose:
- Account for varying follow-up duration and evaluate speed of healing across materials.

Why appropriate:
1) Patients have multiple evaluations; time to a particular healing threshold is clinically meaningful and uses all available information.
2) Can incorporate censoring for patients lost before event.

Why it may not be appropriate:
- Follow-up limited to 6 months; some cases may not reach threshold; sample size may be inadequate for survival analysis with 3 arms.
- Time resolution is coarse (2, 4, 6 months) and may not warrant full survival modeling.

When NOT to use:
- If the number of events is too low or if event definition is too subjective and unstable.

Alternative if assumptions violated:
- If proportional hazards assumption fails, use restricted mean survival time (RMST) comparisons or nonparametric tests for interval-censored data.

B4. Multiple comparison adjustment

Test / method:
- Adjust p-values for multiple pairwise group comparisons:
  - Bonferroni, Holm, or Benjamini–Hochberg (FDR).

Purpose:
- Control false positive rate when performing multiple comparisons among three materials and across multiple time points.

Why appropriate:
1) Multiple pairwise tests (EndoSequence vs MTA, EndoSequence vs Biodentine, MTA vs Biodentine) risk increased type I error.
2) Transparency in handling multiplicity improves interpretability.

Why it may not be appropriate:
- Very conservative corrections (Bonferroni) could render the study underpowered, especially given small sample size.

When NOT to use:
- If the number of comparisons is strictly limited and a hierarchical testing plan is pre-specified (e.g., primary comparison MTA vs Biodentine first, then EndoSequence vs best performer).

Alternative if assumptions violated:
- Use FDR control (Benjamini–Hochberg) to maintain power while controlling expected false discoveries.

B5. Subgroup analyses (exploratory)

Test / method:
- Stratified analysis or interaction testing for:
  - Jaw (maxilla vs mandible).
  - Tooth type (anterior vs posterior).
- Interaction terms in LMM: material × (subgroup variable).

Purpose:
- Assess whether material effect varies across clinically relevant subgroups.

Why appropriate:
1) Biological plausibility that healing differs by anatomical region.
2) May identify contexts where one material performs better or worse.

Why it may not be appropriate:
- Sample size likely too small; subgroup analyses have low power and risk spurious findings.

When NOT to use:
- If data are very sparse per subgroup; if pre-specified subgroups do not exist and exploratory analyses would be overinterpreted.

Alternative if assumptions violated:
- Present results descriptively without formal hypothesis testing.

TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS (SHOULD NOT BE USED)

1. Repeated-measures ANOVA on complete cases only
- Why not:
  - Requires complete data for all time points; would exclude all patients without full 5 scans, reducing n dramatically and inducing bias.
  - Assumes sphericity and normality; both dubious in small, skewed lesion size data.
  - Less flexible than LMM for unbalanced data.

2. Simple paired t-tests or multiple independent t-tests for repeated times
- Why not:
  - Multiple t-tests across times and groups inflate type I error.
  - Paired t-tests ignore multi-level structure and repeated measures; cannot examine time × treatment interactions properly.
  - Normality assumption unlikely with small n and non-normal distributions.

3. One-way ANOVA on ordinal healing scores
- Why not:
  - Treats ordinal categories as interval-scaled numeric; inappropriate because distances between categories are unknown.
  - Violates assumptions of linearity and equal variance; p-values unreliable.

4. Parametric tests on non-transformed, heavily skewed data without diagnostics
- Examples:
  - Standard ANOVA on lesion size if obviously skewed and with outliers.
- Why not:
  - Small sample sizes make parametric tests sensitive to violations; results may be misleading.

5. LOCF as primary missing data method
- Why not:
  - Carries forward earlier lesion size as 6-month value; likely underestimates healing and biases comparisons (especially if dropout pattern differs by group).
  - Violates the principle that later outcomes depend on unobserved processes and may misrepresent true trajectories.

6. Chi-square tests with expected counts <5 without correction
- Why not:
  - With small group sizes (e.g., 8 Biodentine at 6 months), some cells will have low expected counts; Chi-square approximations become invalid.
- Alternative:
  - Fisher’s exact or exact tests.

7. Multiple unadjusted post-hoc tests without correction for multiplicity
- Why not:
  - Increases type I error; with three materials and multiple time points, chance findings are likely.

8. Stepwise variable selection for models given small sample size
- Why not:
  - Automatic selection methods (forward/backward stepwise) produce overfitting, unstable models, and inflated type I error, especially with n ~35 at final time point.
