### TASK 1: INFORMATION EXTRACTION

#### Study Characteristics:
1. **Study Design**:  
   - Randomized, double-blind, parallel positive control, multicenter clinical trial.
   - Allocation ratio: 1:1.

2. **Objectives**:  
   - **Primary Objective**: Evaluate the efficacy of regenerative endodontic therapy (RET) with i-PRF versus blood clots (BCs) for mature permanent teeth with pulp necrosis.  
   - **Secondary Objective**: Investigate factors influencing clinical outcomes and explore a standard RET protocol for mature permanent teeth.

3. **Groups**:  
   - **Test Group**: RET with injectable platelet-rich fibrin (i-PRF).  
   - **Control Group**: RET with blood clots (BC).

4. **Sample Size**:  
   - Total: 346 patients (173 per group).

5. **Variables**:  
   - **Primary Outcome**: Success rates of RET (defined by clinical and radiographic outcomes).  
   - **Secondary Outcomes**: Crown discoloration, pocket depth, percussion, palpation, sinus tract, tooth mobility, periapical swelling, maxillofacial swelling, general symptoms, cold/heat test, vitality test, periapical film, and CBCT findings.  
   - **Safety Outcomes**: Adverse events during the study period.

6. **Measurements**:  
   - Clinical examination and imaging (CBCT, periapical film) at baseline and follow-up intervals (1, 3, 6, 12, 18, and 24 months).  
   - Subjective symptoms and clinical signs (e.g., tooth mobility, sinus tract presence).

7. **Data Collection Methods**:  
   - Randomization using SPSS 20.0 software.  
   - Blinding: Double-blind (patients and outcome evaluators).  
   - Follow-up assessments at specified intervals.  
   - Data management overseen by a coordinating center.

8. **Intervention Procedures**:  
   - **First Appointment**: Infection control and inflammation relief (standardized for both groups).  
   - **Second Appointment**: RET procedures differ by group:  
     - Test Group: i-PRF injected into the root canal.  
     - Control Group: Blood clot revascularization.

9. **Inclusion Criteria**:  
   - Age: 10–59 years.  
   - Diagnosis: Mature permanent tooth with pulp necrosis (confirmed by CBCT).  
   - Single root necrotic tooth with a single root canal.

10. **Exclusion Criteria**:  
    - Teeth with severe coronal defects, root fractures, or periodontitis.  
    - Patients with systemic diseases, dental phobia, or mental disorders.  
    - Pregnant/lactating women.  
    - Participation in other clinical trials within the last 3 months.

11. **Dropout Criteria**:  
    - Serious adverse events (e.g., severe root resorption or tooth fracture).  
    - Poor clinical compliance or withdrawal of consent.

### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS
1. **Chi-Square Test**:  
   - **Purpose**: Compare success rates between the test and control groups.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     - Primary outcome is categorical (success vs. failure).  
     - Groups are independent and randomized.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:  
     - Assumes large sample sizes; may be less reliable for small subgroups.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If expected cell counts are <5.  
   - **Alternative**: Fisher’s Exact Test for small sample sizes.

2. **Logistic Regression**:  
   - **Purpose**: Adjust for confounders affecting success rates and calculate odds ratios (OR).  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     - Accounts for baseline differences between groups.  
     - Provides adjusted ORs for predictors.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:  
     - Requires large sample sizes for reliable estimates.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If multicollinearity exists among predictors.  
   - **Alternative**: Ridge regression or penalized logistic regression.

3. **McNemar’s Test**:  
   - **Purpose**: Analyze intra-group changes over time.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     - Suitable for paired categorical data (e.g., success rates at different time points).  
     - Evaluates longitudinal changes within groups.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:  
     - Assumes paired observations; not suitable for unpaired data.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If data are not paired or if sample size is insufficient.  
   - **Alternative**: Generalized estimating equations (GEE) for repeated measures.

---

#### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED TESTS
1. **One-Way ANOVA**:  
   - **Purpose**: Compare baseline quantitative variables (e.g., age, apical hole size) between groups.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     - Tests mean differences across groups for continuous variables.  
     - Assumes normal distribution and homogeneity of variance.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:  
     - Sensitive to violations of normality and homogeneity assumptions.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If data are skewed or variances are unequal.  
   - **Alternative**: Kruskal-Wallis test for non-parametric data.

2. **Principal Component Analysis (PCA)**:  
   - **Purpose**: Identify factors influencing clinical outcomes and reduce dimensionality of predictors.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     - Useful for exploring relationships among multiple variables.  
     - Reduces multicollinearity in regression models.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:  
     - Interpretation can be complex.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If sample size is insufficient for reliable factor extraction.  
   - **Alternative**: Factor analysis.

---

#### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL TESTS
1. **Kaplan-Meier Survival Analysis**:  
   - **Purpose**: Assess time-to-event outcomes (e.g., time to treatment failure).  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     - Accounts for censored data.  
     - Provides survival curves for each group.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:  
     - Requires time-to-event data, which may not be collected.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If follow-up times are inconsistent.  
   - **Alternative**: Cox proportional hazards model.

2. **Repeated Measures ANOVA**:  
   - **Purpose**: Analyze changes in continuous outcomes (e.g., pocket depth) over time.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     - Suitable for longitudinal data with repeated measurements.  
     - Tests for time and group effects.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:  
     - Assumes sphericity; sensitive to violations of this assumption.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If sphericity is violated or data are non-normal.  
   - **Alternative**: Linear mixed models.

### TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS

   - **Why NOT Appropriate**:  
     - Assumes continuous data; primary outcome is categorical.  
     - Does not account for confounders.  
   - **Alternative**: Chi-square test or logistic regression.

2. **Pearson Correlation**:  
   - **Why NOT Appropriate**:  
     - Assumes linear relationships between continuous variables; not suitable for categorical outcomes.  
   - **Alternative**: Spearman correlation for ordinal data.
