### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY
*Tests without which the analysis would be incomplete.*

**1. Shapiro-Wilk Test**
*   **Test name:** Shapiro-Wilk Test of Normality
*   **Purpose:** To assess whether the continuous dependent variable (SBS) follows a normal distribution within each of the three groups.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) It is highly powerful and accurate for small sample sizes (n=15 per group). 
    2) It is a strict prerequisite to determine whether parametric or non-parametric inferential tests should be used for the primary analysis.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It can be overly sensitive to trivial deviations from normality if the sample size is extremely large, though this is not an issue for n=15.
*   **When this test should NOT be used:** When dealing with categorical or ordinal data (e.g., Adhesive Failure Index scores).
*   **Alternative if assumptions are violated:** Visual inspection of Q-Q plots and histograms, or the Kolmogorov-Smirnov test.

**2. One-Way Analysis of Variance (ANOVA)**
*   **Test name:** One-Way ANOVA
*   **Purpose:** To determine if there are any statistically significant differences in the mean shear bond strength across the three independent adhesive groups.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) The study design compares three distinct, independent groups. 
    2) The dependent variable (SBS) is continuous and (assuming it passes the Shapiro-Wilk test) normally distributed.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It assumes homogeneity of variances; if the variances between groups are vastly different, the F-statistic becomes unreliable.
*   **When this test should NOT be used:** When the data is heavily skewed, non-normal, or when comparing only two groups (where a t-test is sufficient).
*   **Alternative if assumptions are violated:** Kruskal-Wallis H Test (if normality is violated) or Welch’s ANOVA (if variance is unequal).

#### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED
*Tests that enhance the quality and rigor of the analysis.*

**1. Levene’s Test**
*   **Test name:** Levene’s Test for Equality of Variances
*   **Purpose:** To test the assumption of homoscedasticity (homogeneity of variances) across the three groups prior to running the ANOVA.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) It is less sensitive to departures from normality than other variance tests (like Bartlett's test). 
    2) It mathematically justifies the use of a standard ANOVA versus a robust ANOVA.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** With very small sample sizes, it may lack the statistical power to detect true heterogeneity of variance (Type II error).
*   **When this test should NOT be used:** When analyzing paired or repeated-measures data.
*   **Alternative if assumptions are violated:** If Levene's test is significant (variances are unequal), proceed directly to Welch’s ANOVA.

**2. Tukey’s Honestly Significant Difference (HSD)**
*   **Test name:** Tukey’s HSD Post-Hoc Test
*   **Purpose:** To perform pairwise comparisons to identify exactly *which* specific adhesives differ from one another, provided the overall ANOVA is significant.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) It strictly controls the Family-Wise Error Rate (Type I error) when making multiple comparisons. 
    2) It is highly reliable and optimal when group sample sizes are equal (n=15).
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It is slightly conservative and may lack power if the sample sizes are unequal.
*   **When this test should NOT be used:** When Levene's test indicates that variances are unequal across groups.
*   **Alternative if assumptions are violated:** Games-Howell post-hoc test (which does not assume equal variances).

#### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL
*Tests that provide supplementary insights.*

**1. Weibull Reliability Analysis**
*   **Test name:** Weibull Distribution / Survival Analysis
*   **Purpose:** To evaluate the probability of bond failure at given stress levels and determine the structural reliability of the adhesives.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) It is the gold standard in dental materials research for assessing the predictability of material failure. 
    2) It provides the Weibull modulus (m), where a higher number indicates fewer critical flaws and more predictable clinical performance.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** Weibull analysis typically requires a larger sample size (ideally n ≥ 30 per group) for highly accurate parameter estimation; n=15 may yield wide confidence intervals.
*   **When this test should NOT be used:** When the failure modes (adhesive, cohesive, mixed) are not properly categorized, as mixing failure types confounds the reliability modulus.
*   **Alternative if assumptions are violated:** Kaplan-Meier survival analysis (if tracking time-to-failure in a dynamic fatigue test rather than static load-to-failure).

### TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS
*   **Independent Samples t-test:** Inappropriate because there are *three* groups. Conducting multiple t-tests (A vs B, B vs C, A vs C) inflates the Type I error rate, increasing the chance of a false positive.
*   **Paired t-test / Repeated Measures ANOVA:** Inappropriate because the groups are independent (different teeth were used for different adhesives). These tests are reserved for when the *same* specimens are tested multiple times.
*   **Chi-Square Test of Independence:** Inappropriate for the primary outcome because the dependent variable (Shear Bond Strength) is continuous. Chi-square is only used for categorical frequency data (e.g., comparing the frequency of adhesive vs. cohesive failure modes).
