A1. Descriptive statistics of baseline and outcomes

Why appropriate:  
1) Essential to interpret and contextualize inferential tests.  
2) Clarifies skewness / discreteness of VAS and analgesic use, informing choice of parametric vs nonparametric methods.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- N/A (description is always appropriate)  

When not to use:  
- Not applicable.  

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- Use robust summary measures (medians, IQR, trimmed means) if distributions are highly skewed or bounded.  

---

A2. Baseline comparability of groups  

Tests:  
- Age: Independent samples t-test or Mann–Whitney U test (depending on distribution).  
- Sex: Chi-square test (or Fisher’s exact if small counts).  

Purpose:  
- To verify success of randomization with respect to key baseline covariates (age, sex).  

Why appropriate:  
1) Randomization should eliminate systematic differences; these tests check balance and help interpret results.  
2) Age and sex are potential confounders of pain perception; confirming balance increases confidence that observed differences are due to the intervention.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Strictly, testing baseline differences is not necessary in randomized trials and can be misleading; imbalance can occur by chance.  

When NOT to use:  
- Do not interpret statistically significant baseline differences as evidence that randomization “failed”; instead consider covariate adjustment in primary analysis.  

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- If age is non-normal or variances unequal, use Mann–Whitney U or Welch’s t-test.  
- For sparse sex counts, use Fisher’s exact.  

---

A3. Primary comparison of pain intensity between groups over time (repeated measures)  

Preferred Test:  
- Linear mixed-effects model (LMM) on VAS scores or generalized linear mixed model (GLMM) on ordinal pain categories.  

Option 1: LMM on continuous VAS  

Model specification:  
- Outcome: VAS (0–10) per patient per timepoint per visit.  
- Fixed effects:  
  - Group (Control vs Laser)  
  - Visit (1 vs 2)  
  - Time after visit (8 h, 24 h, 48 h, 7 d; can be factor)  
  - Group × Visit  
  - Group × Time  
  - Visit × Time (optional)  
  - Group × Visit × Time (only if needed; otherwise treat interaction more simply).  
- Random effects:  
  - Random intercept for patient (and optionally random slope for time after visit).  
- Correlation structure:  
  - Within-subject correlation handled by the random effects; can also specify an AR(1) residual structure if needed.  

Purpose:  
- To assess whether the trajectory of pain over time differs between laser and control groups across visits and time points.  

Why appropriate:  
1) Accounts for correlated repeated measurements within patients (multiple time points and visits).  
2) Handles unbalanced data and missing observations without resorting to complete-case analysis.  
3) Models the whole time-course, increasing power and reducing type I error compared with multiple pairwise tests.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- VAS is bounded (0–10) and often skewed; LMM assumes residuals are approximately normal and homoscedastic, which may be violated.  

When this test should NOT be used:  
- If VAS scores are extremely skewed with many zeros, or if residual diagnostics show severe non-normality and heteroscedasticity that cannot be resolved via transformations.  

Alternative if assumptions are violated:  
- Use GLMM with appropriate distribution:  
  - Ordinal logistic mixed model for categorized pain (none/mild/moderate/severe)  
  - Or a nonparametric repeated-measures method (see A4).  

Option 2: GLMM for ordinal pain  

Model:  
- Cumulative logit mixed model (proportional odds) with same fixed and random effects structure, outcome = pain category.  

Advantages:  
- Respects ordinal nature of pain categories; does not assume equal intervals.  

---

A4. Nonparametric repeated-measures alternative (if LMM not feasible)  

Test:  
- For each visit separately, use a nonparametric approach across time:  
  - Friedman test (within each group) to test change over time (8 h, 24 h, 48 h, 7 d)  
  - Mann–Whitney U at each time point for between-group comparisons, with multiplicity adjustment (e.g., Holm).  

Purpose:  
- To compare repeated pain scores across time within and between groups without assuming normality.  

Why appropriate:  
1) VAS pain scores in this study are skewed and include many zeros, making nonparametric tests attractive.  
2) Friedman test explicitly accounts for within-subject dependency for time within a visit.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Does not jointly model time and group interactions; limited in interpreting complex patterns; multiple tests inflate type I error.  

When NOT to use:  
- When sample size is large and a mixed model can be properly specified and interpreted; nonparametric tests become less efficient and harder to interpret.  

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- If Friedman’s test assumptions (e.g., data at all time points) are violated due to missing data, use LMM/GLMM that can handle missingness under MAR (missing at random).  

---

A5. Primary analysis of analgesic use between groups  

Test:  
- For each time point and visit:  
  - Fisher’s exact test (or chi-square) comparing proportion of patients taking analgesics in control vs laser group.  

Purpose:  
- To evaluate whether laser treatment reduces the need for analgesics.  

Why appropriate:  
1) Analgesic use is binary; Fisher’s exact test/chi-square is standard for comparing proportions.  
2) Small event numbers (especially in laser group) favor Fisher’s exact test.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Multiple independent tests for each time point ignore repeated measures and correlations (same patients contributing multiple time points).  

When NOT to use:  
- As the only analysis if you want a unified estimate of treatment effect over time; repeated-measures logistic models are superior.  

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- Use a mixed-effects logistic regression:  
  - Outcome: analgesic use (yes/no)  
  - Fixed: group, visit, time, interactions  
  - Random intercept for patient.  

This model should be preferred if enough events occur; Fisher’s exact can be used as a sensitivity analysis for key time points (e.g., 24 h).  

---

A6. Within-group change in pain between visits  

Test:  
- Wilcoxon signed-rank test (or paired t-test if distribution ~normal) comparing VAS at visit 1 vs visit 2 at each time point, within each group.  

Purpose:  
- To assess whether pain after obturation (visit 2) differs from pain after first-stage disinfection (visit 1) within each treatment group.  

Why appropriate:  
1) Directly addresses the secondary question: whether the second visit is less painful than the first.  
2) Within-patient paired comparison is well captured by Wilcoxon signed-rank when data are non-normal.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Does not adjust for multiple comparisons across time points and groups; potential type I error inflation.  

When NOT to use:  
- When the primary mixed model already includes visit as a factor and shows no interaction; repeated simple tests may be redundant and confusing.  

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- Use nonparametric repeated-measures models (e.g., all timepoints in a mixed model) where visit is a factor; interpret visit’s main effect.  

---

A7. Multiplicity control  

Method:  
- Adjust p-values for multiple comparisons when conducting multiple pairwise tests (e.g., Bonferroni or Holm).  

Purpose:  
- To control family-wise error rate due to multiple timepoint-specific tests.  

Why appropriate:  
1) The study assesses multiple time points and visits; uncorrected multiple testing increases false positives.  
2) Enhances credibility of reported significant findings.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Overly conservative corrections (e.g., Bonferroni) may reduce power in small RCT.  

When NOT to use:  
- If the primary analysis is a single mixed model with a prespecified primary contrast (e.g., difference at 24 h after visit 1), and other analyses are explicitly labelled exploratory.  

Alternative:  
- Use hierarchical testing or focus on prespecified primary endpoint (e.g., 24 h after first visit).

CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED TESTS  
--------------------------------  

B1. Mixed-effects logistic regression for analgesic use over time  

Test:  
- Generalized linear mixed model (GLMM) with logit link.  

Model:  
- Outcome: analgesic use (yes/no) at each time point and visit.  
- Fixed: group, visit, time, and interaction(s).  
- Random: patient intercept.  

Purpose:  
- To estimate overall and time-specific odds ratios for analgesic use comparing laser vs control, accounting for repeated measures.  

Why appropriate:  
1) Analgesic use is sparse and repeated within patient; GLMM appropriately models correlated binary data.  
2) Provides interpretable effect sizes (odds ratios with CI) over the whole trajectory.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- If events are extremely rare (e.g., zero events in one group at most times), model may not converge or yield unstable estimates.  

When NOT to use:  
- With small N and very low event counts; simpler Fisher’s tests at key timepoints are more stable.  

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- Use penalized logistic regression (e.g., Firth’s correction) at key time points or exact logistic regression for single-time analyses.

Purpose:

Why appropriate:

Why it may not be appropriate:

When NOT to use:  
- Never skip entirely, but interpret cautiously with small samples.  

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- Use robust methods (e.g., rank-based methods, bootstrapped SEs), or switch to GLMM with appropriate distribution.

TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS AND WHY  

Below are examples of tests that should not be used (or should be avoided as primary methods) for this study, with rationale.

1. Repeated-measures ANOVA (rm-ANOVA) without modelling between visits and unbalanced data  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Assumes sphericity and normally distributed residuals; VAS with many zeros and skewness, plus small N, violate these assumptions.  
  - Does not handle missing data well; requires complete cases across all time points, leading to loss of data.  
- When NOT to use:  
  - As the main analysis; only consider if all assumptions hold strictly and data are complete (unlikely here).  
- Alternative: LMM as described above.  

2. Simple unadjusted multiple independent t-tests at each time point  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Ignores within-subject correlation and multiplicity; inflated type I error.  
  - VAS may not satisfy normality; sample size modest.  
- Alternative: Mann–Whitney U or mixed models with multiplicity control.  

3. One-way ANOVA or Kruskal–Wallis treating each timepoint as independent observations  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Repeated measures from the same individual are not independent; violates independence assumption.  
- Alternative: Friedman test or mixed models.  

4. Pearson correlation between time and pain within group treated as cross-sectional  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Treats repeated measurements as independent; non-independent errors.  
- Alternative: LMM with time as fixed effect and patient random effect.  

5. Logistic regression ignoring clustering (patient-level repeated measures)  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - If applied to pooled repeated time points as if independent, underestimates standard errors and inflates type I error.  
- Alternative: mixed-effects logistic regression or GEE.  

6. Parametric tests on raw VAS assuming equal intervals and perfect normality without checking  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - VAS is bounded; distribution often skewed; parametric assumptions may be violated.  
- Alternative: Nonparametric tests or generalized models.  

7. Using McNemar’s test for between-group comparisons  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - McNemar’s is for paired binary data (same subjects measured under two conditions); here, groups are independent.  
- Alternative: Fisher’s exact/chi-square, or mixed logistic models.  

8. MANOVA treating timepoints as independent outcome variables with no correlation structure  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - MANOVA requires multivariate normality and large sample sizes; rare for this type of outcome.  
- Alternative: LMM/GLMM.

TASK 4: SUMMARY – JUSTIFICATION OF OVERALL ANALYTICAL APPROACH  

- The study is a two-arm parallel RCT with repeated measures of pain (VAS) at multiple time points after two visits within the same patients.  
- Pain scores are bounded, likely skewed, and measured repeatedly within patients, so standard independent-sample tests at each time point are suboptimal.  
- A mixed-model framework (LMM or GLMM) naturally accommodates:  
  - The correlation among repeated measures within each patient.  
  - Unbalanced or missing data if present.  
  - Multiple timepoints and visits, allowing estimation of group × time and group × visit effects in a single coherent model.  
- Nonparametric alternatives (Mann–Whitney, Wilcoxon, Friedman, Fisher’s exact) remain useful as supplemental or sensitivity analyses, particularly given the modest sample size and non-normal distributions.  
- The planned analyses prioritize:  
  - A single, prespecified primary analysis (mixed model for VAS, with focus on 24 h pain as key endpoint).  
  - Complementary analyses for analgesic use and for pain category distributions.  
  - Appropriate multiplicity control and model diagnostics.  

This approach yields statistically sound and clinically interpretable conclusions about whether intra-canal 980-nm diode laser treatment reduces post-operative pain and analgesic use after RCT in single-rooted teeth with apical periodontitis.
