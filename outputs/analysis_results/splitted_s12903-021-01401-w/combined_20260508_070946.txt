## TASK 1 — Information extraction (study characteristics)

### Study design and setting
- **Design:** Parallel-group **randomized controlled clinical trial** (allocation ratio 1:1).
- **Setting:** Department of Endodontics, Biruni University (Istanbul, Turkey).
- **Operator:** All procedures performed by the **same operator** over ~5 months.
- **Trial registration:** NCT04486196 (retrospectively registered).
- **Follow-up:** Pain assessed after **each of two visits** at **8 h, 24 h, 48 h, 7 d**.

### Objective and hypothesis
- **Primary objective:** Evaluate whether **980‑nm diode laser irradiation** after conventional final irrigation reduces **post-operative pain (PP)** after RCT in eligible teeth.
- **Null hypothesis:** No difference in PP between laser vs control groups.

### Population
- **Screened:** 395 patients.
- **Enrolled:** 60 healthy patients (age **18–65**).
- **Teeth:** **Asymptomatic, single-rooted teeth** with apical periodontitis defined by **PAI score 3 or 4** (radiographic).
- **Exclusion criteria (key):** recent antibiotics (1 month), anti-inflammatory analgesics (5 days), systemic disorders, pregnancy/lactation, traumatic occlusion, other teeth needing RCT, previously treated/calcified canals, resorption, periodontal disease/sinus tract, severe crown destruction preventing rubber dam.

### Randomization / blinding
- **Randomization:** “Simple randomization” (method details not provided).
- **Blinding:** Patients likely **not blinded** (explicit limitation: patients aware of group assignment may influence reporting). Assessor/operator collected outcomes by contacting patients (not blinded).

### Interventions (two-visit RCT for all)
All patients received:
- Chemomechanical preparation + **Ca(OH)₂** intracanal medicament at visit 1.
- Visit 2 at **7 days**: removal of Ca(OH)₂, obturation (cold lateral condensation, AH Plus), composite restoration.

**Control group (n=30):**
- Final irrigation: **5 ml 2.5% NaOCl**, then **5 ml 17% EDTA for 3 min**, then **5 ml distilled water**.

**Laser group (n=30):**
- Same final irrigation as control **plus** intracanal **980‑nm diode laser irradiation** after final irrigation at **both visits**:
  - 200 μm fiber, pulsed mode; output power 2.4 W, energy 12 J/cycle; 10s irradiation + 10s pause = 1 cycle; **4 cycles**.
  - Inserted to working length; apical-to-coronal movement at ~2 mm/s with circling.

### Outcomes and measurements

**Primary outcome (continuous/ordinal):**
- **Post-operative pain level** measured using **VAS 0–10** at:
  - 8 h, 24 h, 48 h, 7 d **after visit 1**
  - 8 h, 24 h, 48 h, 7 d **after visit 2**
- Also categorized as: none (0), mild (1–3), moderate (4–7), severe (8–10) (derived from VAS).

**Secondary outcomes:**
- **Analgesic use** at 8 h, 24 h, 48 h, 7 d after each visit (binary at each time point).
- **Time interval to medication intake** (time-to-event type variable; unclear unit/precision, but recorded by patient).

**Baseline covariates recorded:**
- Age (years), Sex (male/female).
- PAI eligibility (3 or 4), single-rooted, asymptomatic (selection criteria).

**Data collection:**
- Patients trained to complete VAS at home.
- Operator contacted patients at scheduled times and recorded PP and analgesic intake on forms.

### Sample size calculation
- Based on pilot study; G*Power:
  - Effect size = 0.82, alpha = 0.05, power = 80%.
  - Minimum 25/group; inflated to **30/group** for dropouts (total 60).

# CATEGORY A — MANDATORY (without these analysis is incomplete)

## A1) Mixed-effects model for repeated measures (MMRM) / GEE for VAS pain over time
**Test name:**  
- Preferred: **Linear mixed-effects model (LMM/MMRM)** for VAS (or **GEE**), with subject-level repeated measures.  
- If treating VAS as ordinal/zero-inflated: **mixed-effects ordinal logistic model** (cumulative link mixed model) as an alternative primary.

**Purpose:**  
Assess overall **treatment effect** on pain while accounting for **within-patient correlation** across time points and across both visits, and estimate effects at specific times (e.g., 24h).

**Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):**
1. Pain is measured **repeatedly in the same patient** (8h/24h/48h/7d) after **two visits** → observations are correlated; mixed/GEE models handle this properly.
2. Provides a **single global test** (group, time, group×time; optionally visit and interactions), reducing reliance on many separate Mann–Whitney tests and limiting multiplicity inflation.
3. Can incorporate **incomplete follow-up** without discarding participants (valid under MAR for LMM).

**Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason):**
- VAS is bounded (0–10) and often **zero-inflated**, possibly violating normality/homoscedasticity for LMM.

**When this test should NOT be used:**
- If sample size is too small for model complexity (e.g., sparse cells at later times) and model fails to converge, or if VAS distribution is extremely discrete with heavy piling at 0 and 10 making linear modeling misleading without transformation/robustness.

**Alternatives if assumptions are violated:**
- **Robust LMM** (Huber/ sandwich SEs), or **GEE with robust SEs**.
- **Mixed-effects ordinal logistic regression** on categorized pain (none/mild/moderate/severe).
- **Two-part model** (logistic for any pain + conditional model for >0 pain) if extreme zero inflation.

**Suggested model specification (minimal):**
- Outcome: VAS (0–10) at each follow-up time after each visit.
- Fixed effects: group (laser/control), time (8h/24h/48h/7d), visit (1 vs 2), and key interactions: group×time, group×visit (and optionally group×time×visit).
- Random effect: subject intercept (and possibly random slope for time if stable).
- Covariates (optional but reasonable): age, sex (pre-specified adjustment improves precision).

## A2) Between-group comparison of analgesic use at each time point (binary outcome)
**Test name:** **Fisher’s exact test** (or chi-square if expected counts adequate).

**Purpose:**  
Compare proportions of patients taking analgesics in laser vs control at each follow-up time (particularly early time points).

**Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):**
1. Outcome is **binary** (use vs no use) per time point.
2. Some cells are **sparse/zero** (e.g., 0% in laser group) → Fisher’s exact maintains validity.

**Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason):**
- Doing it separately at many time points ignores correlation and increases multiplicity.

**When this test should NOT be used:**
- As the sole approach if the goal is overall inference across repeated times; also not appropriate if you need adjusted estimates (covariates).

**Alternatives if assumptions are violated / better approach:**
- **GEE logistic regression** or **mixed-effects logistic regression** for repeated binary outcomes across time/visit (global test + time-specific contrasts).
- If quasi-separation due to many zeros: **Firth penalized logistic regression** for adjusted comparisons.

## A3) Baseline comparability and descriptive checks (not “hypothesis testing” driven)
**Test name:** Descriptive summary; optionally **standardized mean differences (SMD)**.

**Purpose:**  
Assess balance of baseline covariates (age, sex) across randomized groups and describe sample.

**Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):**
1. Randomization should balance groups; SMDs quantify balance without overinterpreting p-values.
2. Helps identify covariates for precision adjustment and informs external validity.

**Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason):**
- Formal significance testing of baseline variables is often discouraged (p-values reflect chance and sample size).

**When this test should NOT be used:**
- Do not use baseline p-values to decide whether randomization “worked” or to select covariates purely by significance.

**Alternatives if assumptions are violated:**
- For continuous covariates, if still testing: **Welch t-test** or **Mann–Whitney**; but prefer SMD reporting.

# CATEGORY B — RECOMMENDED (improves rigor)

## B1) Ordinal regression for categorized pain severity (none/mild/moderate/severe) with repeated measures
**Test name:** **Mixed-effects ordinal logistic regression** (cumulative link mixed model) or **GEE ordinal model**.

**Purpose:**  
Model pain as an **ordered categorical outcome**, aligned with clinically used categories.

**Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):**
1. Pain categories are **ordinal** and not interval-scaled; ordinal models respect ordering without assuming equal spacing.
2. Handles **ceiling/floor effects** (e.g., many zeros at 7 days) better than linear models.

**Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason):**
- **Proportional odds assumption** may be violated; sparse categories (e.g., severe) can destabilize estimates.

**When this test should NOT be used:**
- If categories are too sparse (e.g., almost no moderate/severe in one group/time), causing convergence issues.

**Alternatives if assumptions are violated:**
- **Partial proportional odds model**, or collapse categories (e.g., none vs any; none/mild vs moderate/severe).
- **Binary mixed model** for “any pain” or “moderate/severe pain”.

---

## B2) Time-to-first-analgesic intake analysis
**Test name:** **Kaplan–Meier curves + log-rank test**; and/or **Cox proportional hazards model**.

**Purpose:**  
Compare **time until first ibuprofen intake** between groups after each visit.

**Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):**
1. Outcome is inherently **time-to-event** with potential **censoring** (no medication during follow-up).
2. Uses more information than a binary “took analgesic” indicator at fixed time points.

**Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason):**
- If almost nobody takes analgesics in one group (as here), estimates may be unstable; Cox may face separation.

**When this test should NOT be used:**
- If time is not recorded accurately (heaping/rounding) or if event counts are extremely low (e.g., 0 events in laser group) making hazard ratio estimation unreliable.

**Alternatives if assumptions are violated:**
- If Cox PH violated: **restricted mean survival time (RMST)** difference.
- If zero events in a group: report **KM curves descriptively**; consider **exact methods** or **Poisson/negative binomial** for counts (if multiple intakes recorded) but here it’s “first intake”.

## B3) Adjusted treatment effect estimation (covariate adjustment)
**Test name:** Same primary models (LMM/GEE/ordinal mixed) but **adjusted** for age and sex (pre-specified).

**Purpose:**  
Increase precision and address any chance imbalance.

**Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):**
1. Even in RCTs, adjustment can improve **efficiency** and precision.
2. Age/sex may relate to pain perception; adjustment supports robustness.

**Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason):**
- Overfitting risk if too many covariates relative to sample size.

**When this test should NOT be used:**
- Do not add many post-randomization variables or data-driven covariate fishing.

**Alternatives if assumptions are violated:**
- Use **penalized regression** or keep adjustment minimal (age, sex only).

---

## B4) Multiple comparison control / hierarchical endpoint strategy
**Test name:** **Holm–Bonferroni** (family-wise) or **Benjamini–Hochberg** (FDR), or a **pre-specified hierarchy**.

**Purpose:**  
Control false positives given many time-point contrasts.

**Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):**
1. There are multiple time points and two visits → inflated Type I error if many unadjusted tests.
2. Improves credibility of “significant at 24h/48h” findings.

**Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason):**
- Can be conservative, reducing power in small samples.

**When this test should NOT be used:**
- If a single global mixed-model interaction test is pre-specified as primary and only limited contrasts are confirmatory, strict correction across all exploratory contrasts may be unnecessary.

**Alternatives if assumptions are violated:**
- Use **model-based** estimation with planned contrasts and limited family definitions; report **effect sizes with CIs** and treat other comparisons as exploratory.

## TASK 3 — Tests that should NOT be used (and why)

1. **Independent-samples t-test at each time point for VAS** (as the main approach)  
   - VAS is bounded and often non-normal with many zeros; repeated testing inflates Type I error; ignores within-patient correlation across time and visits.

2. **Multiple paired t-tests within each group across time (e.g., 8h vs 24h vs 48h)**  
   - Strong multiplicity problem; violates independence; does not directly test the treatment×time effect.

3. **One-way ANOVA treating each time point as independent groups**  
   - Repeated measurements are not independent; violates ANOVA assumptions unless using repeated-measures ANOVA (and even then, sphericity issues).

4. **Pearson correlation between group (coded 0/1) and pain**  
   - Not an appropriate inferential method for group comparisons; does not handle repeated measures or non-normal outcomes.

5. **Chi-square test when expected counts are small/zero (analgesic use)**  
   - Invalid approximation; Fisher’s exact or logistic models are needed.

6. **Linear regression on time-to-analgesic (raw times) without censoring methods**  
   - Ignores censoring (many patients may never take analgesic), violating model structure.

---

## TASK 4 — Summary justification of the overall approach

The study is a 2-arm RCT with **repeated pain measurements** after **two treatment visits**, creating correlated longitudinal data. The primary analysis should therefore use a **repeated-measures framework (mixed-effects or GEE)** to estimate the treatment effect over time (and by visit) while properly accounting for within-patient correlation and reducing multiplicity. Analgesic use should be analyzed with **exact or logistic repeated-measures methods**, and medication timing, when informative, should be handled with **survival methods** to incorporate censoring. Effect sizes with CIs and sensitivity analyses (ordinal/binary pain endpoints) strengthen interpretability and robustness.

### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS
1. **Mann–Whitney U Test**  
   - **Purpose**: Compare VAS pain scores between the laser and control groups at each time point.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. VAS pain scores are ordinal data, making non-parametric tests suitable.  
     2. Small sample size (n=30 per group) may not meet normality assumptions.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Assumes independent observations, which may not hold if patients' responses are correlated across time points.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If data are normally distributed and sample size is large.  
   - **Alternative**: Independent samples t-test if normality is met.

2. **Wilcoxon Signed-Rank Test**  
   - **Purpose**: Compare within-group changes in VAS pain scores across time points.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Non-parametric test suitable for paired ordinal data.  
     2. Accounts for repeated measures within the same group.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Sensitive to tied ranks in small datasets.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If data are normally distributed.  
   - **Alternative**: Paired t-test if normality is met.

3. **Fisher’s Exact Test**  
   - **Purpose**: Compare proportions of analgesic use between groups.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Suitable for small sample sizes and categorical data.  
     2. Provides exact p-values for 2x2 contingency tables.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Limited to binary outcomes.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: For larger datasets where chi-square test is more efficient.  
   - **Alternative**: Chi-square test for larger sample sizes.

#### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED TESTS
1. **Repeated Measures ANOVA (or Mixed-Effects Model)**  
   - **Purpose**: Analyze VAS scores over time, accounting for within-subject correlations.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Accounts for repeated measures across multiple time points.  
     2. Allows for interaction effects between group and time.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Assumes normality of residuals and sphericity.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If data are non-normal or sphericity is violated.  
   - **Alternative**: Non-parametric Friedman test.

2. **Logistic Regression**  
   - **Purpose**: Assess factors (e.g., group, age, sex) associated with analgesic use.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Suitable for binary outcomes (analgesic use: yes/no).  
     2. Adjusts for potential confounders.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Requires large sample size for stable estimates.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If sample size is too small.  
   - **Alternative**: Fisher’s exact test for unadjusted comparisons.

#### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL TESTS
1. **Friedman Test**  
   - **Purpose**: Non-parametric alternative to repeated measures ANOVA for comparing VAS scores across time points.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Suitable for ordinal data.  
     2. Does not assume normality.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Limited to within-group comparisons.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If between-group comparisons are required.  
   - **Alternative**: Repeated measures ANOVA.

2. **Effect Size Calculation (e.g., Cohen’s d)**  
   - **Purpose**: Quantify the magnitude of differences between groups.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Provides clinically meaningful interpretation of results.  
     2. Complements p-values.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Not inferential; does not test hypotheses.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If focus is solely on hypothesis testing.  
   - **Alternative**: Confidence intervals for mean differences.

TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN  

Below is an improved plan structured around the actual design, grouped by mandatory, recommended, and optional analyses.  

Important: Because pain is measured repeatedly within the same patient (four time points after each of two visits), the primary analysis should explicitly account for within-patient correlation and multiple time points.

CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS  
--------------------------------  

A1. Descriptive statistics of baseline and outcomes  

Test / Method:  
- Descriptive statistics (not a formal “test” but mandatory):  
  - Means, SDs, medians, IQRs for continuous/ordinal variables (age, VAS)  
  - Frequencies and percentages for categorical variables (sex, pain categories, analgesic use).  

Purpose:  
- To describe sample characteristics and outcome distributions by group and time.  

Why appropriate:  
1) Essential to interpret and contextualize inferential tests.  
2) Clarifies skewness / discreteness of VAS and analgesic use, informing choice of parametric vs nonparametric methods.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- N/A (description is always appropriate)  

When not to use:  
- Not applicable.  

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- Use robust summary measures (medians, IQR, trimmed means) if distributions are highly skewed or bounded.  

---

A2. Baseline comparability of groups  

Tests:  
- Age: Independent samples t-test or Mann–Whitney U test (depending on distribution).  
- Sex: Chi-square test (or Fisher’s exact if small counts).  

Purpose:  
- To verify success of randomization with respect to key baseline covariates (age, sex).  

Why appropriate:  
1) Randomization should eliminate systematic differences; these tests check balance and help interpret results.  
2) Age and sex are potential confounders of pain perception; confirming balance increases confidence that observed differences are due to the intervention.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Strictly, testing baseline differences is not necessary in randomized trials and can be misleading; imbalance can occur by chance.  

When NOT to use:  
- Do not interpret statistically significant baseline differences as evidence that randomization “failed”; instead consider covariate adjustment in primary analysis.  

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- If age is non-normal or variances unequal, use Mann–Whitney U or Welch’s t-test.  
- For sparse sex counts, use Fisher’s exact.  

---

A3. Primary comparison of pain intensity between groups over time (repeated measures)  

Preferred Test:  
- Linear mixed-effects model (LMM) on VAS scores or generalized linear mixed model (GLMM) on ordinal pain categories.  

Option 1: LMM on continuous VAS  

Model specification:  
- Outcome: VAS (0–10) per patient per timepoint per visit.  
- Fixed effects:  
  - Group (Control vs Laser)  
  - Visit (1 vs 2)  
  - Time after visit (8 h, 24 h, 48 h, 7 d; can be factor)  
  - Group × Visit  
  - Group × Time  
  - Visit × Time (optional)  
  - Group × Visit × Time (only if needed; otherwise treat interaction more simply).  
- Random effects:  
  - Random intercept for patient (and optionally random slope for time after visit).  
- Correlation structure:  
  - Within-subject correlation handled by the random effects; can also specify an AR(1) residual structure if needed.  

Purpose:  
- To assess whether the trajectory of pain over time differs between laser and control groups across visits and time points.  

Why appropriate:  
1) Accounts for correlated repeated measurements within patients (multiple time points and visits).  
2) Handles unbalanced data and missing observations without resorting to complete-case analysis.  
3) Models the whole time-course, increasing power and reducing type I error compared with multiple pairwise tests.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- VAS is bounded (0–10) and often skewed; LMM assumes residuals are approximately normal and homoscedastic, which may be violated.  

When this test should NOT be used:  
- If VAS scores are extremely skewed with many zeros, or if residual diagnostics show severe non-normality and heteroscedasticity that cannot be resolved via transformations.  

Alternative if assumptions are violated:  
- Use GLMM with appropriate distribution:  
  - Ordinal logistic mixed model for categorized pain (none/mild/moderate/severe)  
  - Or a nonparametric repeated-measures method (see A4).  

Option 2: GLMM for ordinal pain  

Model:  
- Cumulative logit mixed model (proportional odds) with same fixed and random effects structure, outcome = pain category.  

Advantages:  
- Respects ordinal nature of pain categories; does not assume equal intervals.  

---

A4. Nonparametric repeated-measures alternative (if LMM not feasible)  

Test:  
- For each visit separately, use a nonparametric approach across time:  
  - Friedman test (within each group) to test change over time (8 h, 24 h, 48 h, 7 d)  
  - Mann–Whitney U at each time point for between-group comparisons, with multiplicity adjustment (e.g., Holm).  

Purpose:  
- To compare repeated pain scores across time within and between groups without assuming normality.  

Why appropriate:  
1) VAS pain scores in this study are skewed and include many zeros, making nonparametric tests attractive.  
2) Friedman test explicitly accounts for within-subject dependency for time within a visit.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Does not jointly model time and group interactions; limited in interpreting complex patterns; multiple tests inflate type I error.  

When NOT to use:  
- When sample size is large and a mixed model can be properly specified and interpreted; nonparametric tests become less efficient and harder to interpret.  

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- If Friedman’s test assumptions (e.g., data at all time points) are violated due to missing data, use LMM/GLMM that can handle missingness under MAR (missing at random).  

---

A5. Primary analysis of analgesic use between groups  

Test:  
- For each time point and visit:  
  - Fisher’s exact test (or chi-square) comparing proportion of patients taking analgesics in control vs laser group.  

Purpose:  
- To evaluate whether laser treatment reduces the need for analgesics.  

Why appropriate:  
1) Analgesic use is binary; Fisher’s exact test/chi-square is standard for comparing proportions.  
2) Small event numbers (especially in laser group) favor Fisher’s exact test.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Multiple independent tests for each time point ignore repeated measures and correlations (same patients contributing multiple time points).  

When NOT to use:  
- As the only analysis if you want a unified estimate of treatment effect over time; repeated-measures logistic models are superior.  

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- Use a mixed-effects logistic regression:  
  - Outcome: analgesic use (yes/no)  
  - Fixed: group, visit, time, interactions  
  - Random intercept for patient.  

This model should be preferred if enough events occur; Fisher’s exact can be used as a sensitivity analysis for key time points (e.g., 24 h).  

---

A6. Within-group change in pain between visits  

Test:  
- Wilcoxon signed-rank test (or paired t-test if distribution ~normal) comparing VAS at visit 1 vs visit 2 at each time point, within each group.  

Purpose:  
- To assess whether pain after obturation (visit 2) differs from pain after first-stage disinfection (visit 1) within each treatment group.  

Why appropriate:  
1) Directly addresses the secondary question: whether the second visit is less painful than the first.  
2) Within-patient paired comparison is well captured by Wilcoxon signed-rank when data are non-normal.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Does not adjust for multiple comparisons across time points and groups; potential type I error inflation.  

When NOT to use:  
- When the primary mixed model already includes visit as a factor and shows no interaction; repeated simple tests may be redundant and confusing.  

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- Use nonparametric repeated-measures models (e.g., all timepoints in a mixed model) where visit is a factor; interpret visit’s main effect.  

---

A7. Multiplicity control  

Method:  
- Adjust p-values for multiple comparisons when conducting multiple pairwise tests (e.g., Bonferroni or Holm).  

Purpose:  
- To control family-wise error rate due to multiple timepoint-specific tests.  

Why appropriate:  
1) The study assesses multiple time points and visits; uncorrected multiple testing increases false positives.  
2) Enhances credibility of reported significant findings.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Overly conservative corrections (e.g., Bonferroni) may reduce power in small RCT.  

When NOT to use:  
- If the primary analysis is a single mixed model with a prespecified primary contrast (e.g., difference at 24 h after visit 1), and other analyses are explicitly labelled exploratory.  

Alternative:  
- Use hierarchical testing or focus on prespecified primary endpoint (e.g., 24 h after first visit).

B1. Mixed-effects logistic regression for analgesic use over time  

Test:  
- Generalized linear mixed model (GLMM) with logit link.  

Model:  
- Outcome: analgesic use (yes/no) at each time point and visit.  
- Fixed: group, visit, time, and interaction(s).  
- Random: patient intercept.  

Purpose:  
- To estimate overall and time-specific odds ratios for analgesic use comparing laser vs control, accounting for repeated measures.  

Why appropriate:  
1) Analgesic use is sparse and repeated within patient; GLMM appropriately models correlated binary data.  
2) Provides interpretable effect sizes (odds ratios with CI) over the whole trajectory.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- If events are extremely rare (e.g., zero events in one group at most times), model may not converge or yield unstable estimates.  

When NOT to use:  
- With small N and very low event counts; simpler Fisher’s tests at key timepoints are more stable.  

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- Use penalized logistic regression (e.g., Firth’s correction) at key time points or exact logistic regression for single-time analyses.

TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS AND WHY  

Below are examples of tests that should not be used (or should be avoided as primary methods) for this study, with rationale.

1. Repeated-measures ANOVA (rm-ANOVA) without modelling between visits and unbalanced data  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Assumes sphericity and normally distributed residuals; VAS with many zeros and skewness, plus small N, violate these assumptions.  
  - Does not handle missing data well; requires complete cases across all time points, leading to loss of data.  
- When NOT to use:  
  - As the main analysis; only consider if all assumptions hold strictly and data are complete (unlikely here).  
- Alternative: LMM as described above.  

2. Simple unadjusted multiple independent t-tests at each time point  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Ignores within-subject correlation and multiplicity; inflated type I error.  
  - VAS may not satisfy normality; sample size modest.  
- Alternative: Mann–Whitney U or mixed models with multiplicity control.  

3. One-way ANOVA or Kruskal–Wallis treating each timepoint as independent observations  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Repeated measures from the same individual are not independent; violates independence assumption.  
- Alternative: Friedman test or mixed models.  

4. Pearson correlation between time and pain within group treated as cross-sectional  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Treats repeated measurements as independent; non-independent errors.  
- Alternative: LMM with time as fixed effect and patient random effect.  

5. Logistic regression ignoring clustering (patient-level repeated measures)  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - If applied to pooled repeated time points as if independent, underestimates standard errors and inflates type I error.  
- Alternative: mixed-effects logistic regression or GEE.  

6. Parametric tests on raw VAS assuming equal intervals and perfect normality without checking  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - VAS is bounded; distribution often skewed; parametric assumptions may be violated.  
- Alternative: Nonparametric tests or generalized models.  

7. Using McNemar’s test for between-group comparisons  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - McNemar’s is for paired binary data (same subjects measured under two conditions); here, groups are independent.  
- Alternative: Fisher’s exact/chi-square, or mixed logistic models.  

8. MANOVA treating timepoints as independent outcome variables with no correlation structure  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - MANOVA requires multivariate normality and large sample sizes; rare for this type of outcome.  
- Alternative: LMM/GLMM.

TASK 4: SUMMARY – JUSTIFICATION OF OVERALL ANALYTICAL APPROACH  

- The study is a two-arm parallel RCT with repeated measures of pain (VAS) at multiple time points after two visits within the same patients.  
- Pain scores are bounded, likely skewed, and measured repeatedly within patients, so standard independent-sample tests at each time point are suboptimal.  
- A mixed-model framework (LMM or GLMM) naturally accommodates:  
  - The correlation among repeated measures within each patient.  
  - Unbalanced or missing data if present.  
  - Multiple timepoints and visits, allowing estimation of group × time and group × visit effects in a single coherent model.  
- Nonparametric alternatives (Mann–Whitney, Wilcoxon, Friedman, Fisher’s exact) remain useful as supplemental or sensitivity analyses, particularly given the modest sample size and non-normal distributions.  
- The planned analyses prioritize:  
  - A single, prespecified primary analysis (mixed model for VAS, with focus on 24 h pain as key endpoint).  
  - Complementary analyses for analgesic use and for pain category distributions.  
  - Appropriate multiplicity control and model diagnostics.  

This approach yields statistically sound and clinically interpretable conclusions about whether intra-canal 980-nm diode laser treatment reduces post-operative pain and analgesic use after RCT in single-rooted teeth with apical periodontitis.
