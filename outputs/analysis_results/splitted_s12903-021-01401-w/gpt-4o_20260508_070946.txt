### TASK 1: INFORMATION EXTRACTION

#### Study Characteristics:
1. **Study Design**:  
   - Parallel randomized controlled trial (RCT) with an allocation ratio of 1:1.
   - Followed CONSORT guidelines.

2. **Objective**:  
   - To evaluate the effect of a 980-nm diode laser following conventional irrigation on post-operative pain (PP) after root canal treatment (RCT) in asymptomatic single-rooted teeth with a periapical index (PAI) score of 3 or 4.

3. **Groups**:  
   - **Control Group**: Final irrigation with 2.5% sodium hypochlorite (NaOCl), 17% ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid (EDTA), and distilled water, followed by calcium hydroxide (Ca(OH)₂) dressing.  
   - **Laser Group**: Same irrigation protocol as the control group, followed by root canal irradiation using a 980-nm diode laser.

4. **Sample Size**:  
   - Total: 60 patients (30 per group).  
   - Sample size calculation: Effect size = 0.82, Power = 80%, Significance level = 0.05.

5. **Participants**:  
   - Inclusion criteria: Healthy patients aged 18–65 years with asymptomatic single-rooted teeth, PAI score of 3 or 4, no systemic disorders, no recent antibiotic or anti-inflammatory use, and no other dental issues (e.g., root canal fillings, periodontal diseases).  
   - Exclusion criteria: Systemic disorders, pregnancy/lactation, recent antibiotic or anti-inflammatory use, traumatic occlusions, multiple teeth requiring RCT, calcified canals, root resorption, severe crown destruction, or sinus tracts.

6. **Variables**:  
   - **Primary Outcome**: Post-operative pain (PP) measured using a Visual Analogue Scale (VAS) at 8 hours, 24 hours, 48 hours, and 7 days post-treatment.  
   - **Secondary Outcome**: Analgesic use (600 mg ibuprofen) and time to medication intake.  
   - **Covariates**: Age, sex.

7. **Measurements**:  
   - Pain levels: Assessed using a 10-point VAS (0 = no pain, 10 = worst pain). Pain categorized as none (0), mild (1–3), moderate (4–7), and severe (8–10).  
   - Analgesic use: Recorded at 8 hours, 24 hours, 48 hours, and 7 days post-treatment.  
   - Demographics: Age and sex.

8. **Data Collection Methods**:  
   - Pain levels and analgesic use recorded by patients on a VAS form at home.  
   - Patients trained on how to complete the VAS form.  
   - Demographic data collected at baseline.

9. **Randomization**:  
   - Simple randomization method used to allocate patients to groups.  
   - No significant differences in age or sex between groups.

10. **Statistical Analysis Methods**:  
    - Mann–Whitney U test: Between-group comparisons of VAS scores.  
    - Wilcoxon signed-rank test: Within-group comparisons of VAS scores over time.  
    - Student’s t-test: Age and sex distribution between groups.  
    - Fisher’s exact chi-square test: Analgesic use differences.

### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS
1. **Mann–Whitney U Test**  
   - **Purpose**: Compare VAS pain scores between the laser and control groups at each time point.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. VAS pain scores are ordinal data, making non-parametric tests suitable.  
     2. Small sample size (n=30 per group) may not meet normality assumptions.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Assumes independent observations, which may not hold if patients' responses are correlated across time points.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If data are normally distributed and sample size is large.  
   - **Alternative**: Independent samples t-test if normality is met.

2. **Wilcoxon Signed-Rank Test**  
   - **Purpose**: Compare within-group changes in VAS pain scores across time points.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Non-parametric test suitable for paired ordinal data.  
     2. Accounts for repeated measures within the same group.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Sensitive to tied ranks in small datasets.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If data are normally distributed.  
   - **Alternative**: Paired t-test if normality is met.

3. **Fisher’s Exact Test**  
   - **Purpose**: Compare proportions of analgesic use between groups.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Suitable for small sample sizes and categorical data.  
     2. Provides exact p-values for 2x2 contingency tables.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Limited to binary outcomes.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: For larger datasets where chi-square test is more efficient.  
   - **Alternative**: Chi-square test for larger sample sizes.

#### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED TESTS
1. **Repeated Measures ANOVA (or Mixed-Effects Model)**  
   - **Purpose**: Analyze VAS scores over time, accounting for within-subject correlations.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Accounts for repeated measures across multiple time points.  
     2. Allows for interaction effects between group and time.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Assumes normality of residuals and sphericity.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If data are non-normal or sphericity is violated.  
   - **Alternative**: Non-parametric Friedman test.

2. **Logistic Regression**  
   - **Purpose**: Assess factors (e.g., group, age, sex) associated with analgesic use.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Suitable for binary outcomes (analgesic use: yes/no).  
     2. Adjusts for potential confounders.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Requires large sample size for stable estimates.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If sample size is too small.  
   - **Alternative**: Fisher’s exact test for unadjusted comparisons.

#### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL TESTS
1. **Friedman Test**  
   - **Purpose**: Non-parametric alternative to repeated measures ANOVA for comparing VAS scores across time points.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Suitable for ordinal data.  
     2. Does not assume normality.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Limited to within-group comparisons.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If between-group comparisons are required.  
   - **Alternative**: Repeated measures ANOVA.

2. **Effect Size Calculation (e.g., Cohen’s d)**  
   - **Purpose**: Quantify the magnitude of differences between groups.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Provides clinically meaningful interpretation of results.  
     2. Complements p-values.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Not inferential; does not test hypotheses.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If focus is solely on hypothesis testing.  
   - **Alternative**: Confidence intervals for mean differences.

### TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS
1. **Independent Samples t-Test**  
   - **Why Not Appropriate**: VAS scores are ordinal, and the sample size may not meet normality assumptions.  
   - **Alternative**: Mann–Whitney U test.

2. **Chi-Square Test**  
   - **Why Not Appropriate**: Less accurate than Fisher’s exact test for small sample sizes.  
   - **Alternative**: Fisher’s exact test.

3. **Pearson Correlation**  
   - **Why Not Appropriate**: VAS scores are ordinal, and correlation does not test group differences.  
   - **Alternative**: Mann–Whitney U test or Wilcoxon signed-rank test.
