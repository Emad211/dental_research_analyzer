## CATEGORY A — MANDATORY tests/analyses

### A1) Mann–Whitney U test (Wilcoxon rank-sum) — between-group comparisons on Likert/ordinal outcomes
- **Purpose:** Compare distributions/central tendency of ordinal survey responses between:
  - students with 3rd-year clinical exercises vs online-only.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1) Likert responses are **ordinal** and often non-normal; Mann–Whitney does not assume normality.
  2) Groups are **independent** (different students in each group).
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - If distributions have very different shapes, the test is not strictly a “median difference” test and interpretation can be tricky.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When responses are **paired/repeated** (same students measured twice) or when there are **>2 groups**.
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
  - **Ordinal logistic regression** (proportional odds) with robust SEs (handles covariates and differing distributions); or **Brunner–Munzel test** if stochastic dominance assumption concerns.

### A2) Wilcoxon signed-rank test — within-student paired comparisons (only if questionnaire contains paired items)
- **Purpose:** Compare two related ordinal ratings within the same student (e.g., rating of e-learning vs clinical classes) **if** such paired constructs are analyzed.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1) Handles **paired ordinal** data without normality assumption.
  2) Uses within-student ranking → controls for subject-level differences.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Requires **paired measurements** on the same construct/time; not appropriate for independent groups.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If pairing is absent, or differences are extremely tied/zero-heavy (power/interpretation issues).
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
  - **Sign test** (fewer assumptions, lower power) or **paired ordinal regression/GEE** for more complex paired structures.

### A3) Chi-square test of independence / Fisher’s exact test — between-group comparisons on binary/categorical items
- **Purpose:** Compare proportions (e.g., yes/no safety perception; selection of a given advantage/disadvantage) between the two student subgroups.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1) Outcomes are **categorical**.
  2) Directly tests association between group membership and response category.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Chi-square requires adequate expected cell counts; sparse tables invalidate approximation.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When expected counts are small (use Fisher) or when data are paired.
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
  - **Fisher’s exact** (2×2 or small tables), or **exact multinomial tests**; for adjusted analysis use **logistic regression**.

### A4) Pre-pandemic vs pandemic comparison of standardized procedure counts (primary clinical activity endpoint)
**Preferred primary approach:** **Negative binomial regression** (or Poisson with overdispersion handling) with an offset.
- **Test/model:** Negative binomial GLM with:
  - outcome = procedure count per unit time,
  - **offset** = log(student-pair-days) if using raw counts, or analyze standardized rates directly,
  - predictor = period (pre vs pandemic).
- **Purpose:** Quantify change in clinical procedure activity between periods (rate ratio).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1) Procedure data are **counts/rates** and commonly skewed; NB handles overdispersion.
  2) Allows explicit handling of **different exposure/denominators** via offset (student-pair-days), matching the described standardization.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - If the analysis dataset is very small (few blocks/days), model estimates may be unstable.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If data are continuous and approximately normal; or if there is no meaningful exposure denominator.
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
  - **Quasi-Poisson** (robust variance), **zero-inflated NB** if many zeros, or **Mann–Whitney** on block-level standardized rates (less parametric but loses denominator modeling).

*(If the investigators insist on the paper’s simpler approach, Mann–Whitney on standardized counts is acceptable as a nonparametric comparison, but NB regression is the methodologically stronger “mandatory” choice for rates.)*

## CATEGORY B — RECOMMENDED tests/analyses

### B1) Ordinal logistic regression (proportional odds model) for key Likert outcomes
- **Purpose:** Estimate adjusted association between subgroup (3rd-year clinical vs online-only) and ordinal outcomes (e.g., overall blended-learning evaluation), optionally adjusting for covariates if available (e.g., gender, age, prior experience—none provided in excerpt).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1) Preserves **ordinal nature** (better than treating Likert as continuous).
  2) Produces interpretable **odds ratios** and supports covariate adjustment and interaction tests.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Proportional odds assumption may be violated; also requires adequate sample size across categories.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When outcome has very sparse categories (collapse categories or use alternative).
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
  - **Partial proportional odds model**, **multinomial logistic regression**, or **adjacent-category models**.

### B2) Reliability analysis for multi-item constructs (if scales are formed)
- **Test/indices:** **McDonald’s omega** (preferred) and/or **Cronbach’s alpha**.
- **Purpose:** Assess internal consistency of questionnaire sections (self-evaluation, safety, etc.) if items are combined into subscale scores.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1) The instrument is **author-created**; reliability evidence is needed.
  2) Supports defensible creation of composite scores and reduces multiplicity.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - If items are not intended to measure a single latent construct (low unidimensionality).
- **When NOT to use:**
  - For heterogeneous item sets or dichotomous items without appropriate method.
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
  - **Ordinal alpha/omega** using polychoric correlations; or avoid scale creation and analyze items separately.

### B3) Exploratory factor analysis (EFA) / Confirmatory factor analysis (CFA) for questionnaire structure
- **Purpose:** Evaluate whether the 5-part structure is supported empirically; define latent domains.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1) Strengthens validity evidence for an author questionnaire beyond “pre-validated on 5 volunteers”.
  2) Enables more coherent reporting via domain scores.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Requires adequate sample size; with small N results are unreliable.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If respondent N is too low relative to item count (common rule: ≥5–10 per item, though context-dependent).
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
  - **Parallel analysis + EFA on reduced item sets**, or **item response theory (graded response)** if sample allows.

### B4) Multiple-comparisons control (Benjamini–Hochberg FDR or Holm)
- **Purpose:** Control Type I error inflation across many questionnaire item tests.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1) 39 items → many p-values; unadjusted testing is misleading.
  2) FDR maintains power while limiting false discoveries.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - If only a small pre-specified set of primary endpoints is tested, adjustment may be unnecessary.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When analyses are purely descriptive/exploratory and clearly labeled as such (still report cautiously).
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
  - **Hierarchical testing** with pre-specified endpoints; or **family-wise error** control via Holm.

### B5) Effect sizes with confidence intervals (reporting standard)
- **Measures:**
  - Mann–Whitney: **rank-biserial correlation** or **Cliff’s delta**
  - Binary: **risk difference**, **risk ratio**, **odds ratio**
  - Count models: **rate ratio** (NB/Poisson)
- **Purpose:** Provide magnitude, not only significance.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1) Educational significance depends on effect size.
  2) CI conveys precision and supports interpretation independent of N.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Some effect sizes are unfamiliar; requires careful explanation.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Never “not use”; at minimum provide CIs for key estimates.
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
  - Use **bootstrap CIs** for nonstandard estimands.

### B6) Multidimensional correspondence analysis (MCA) — as stated, with safeguards
- **Purpose:** Explore association patterns between categorical selections of advantages/disadvantages and overall evaluation categories.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1) Designed for **multivariate categorical** data.
  2) Provides visual, low-dimensional representation of relationships.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Results are exploratory and sensitive to sparse categories; interpretation can be subjective.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When categories are extremely sparse or when the goal is confirmatory inference.
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
  - **Multiple correspondence analysis with regularization**, or **log-linear models**, or **latent class analysis**.

## CATEGORY C — OPTIONAL (supplementary insights)

### C1) Sensitivity analyses treating Likert outcomes as continuous (t-test/linear regression)
- **Purpose:** Check robustness of conclusions under approximate interval assumptions.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1) Often yields similar inference with large samples; easy to interpret mean differences.
  2) Enables standard regression diagnostics and covariate adjustment.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Likert is ordinal; mean differences may be misleading with skew/ceiling effects.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - With very skewed distributions, small N, or few categories used.
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
  - Stick to **ordinal models** or nonparametric methods.

### C2) Qualitative analysis of open-ended responses (thematic analysis)
- **Purpose:** Summarize perceived strengths/weaknesses of blended learning beyond closed items.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1) Open questions require a transparent qualitative method.
  2) Adds context to quantitative findings.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - If responses are too brief/sparse for reliable coding.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When only a handful of responses exist.
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
  - Simple **content analysis** with frequency of key themes.

### C3) Cluster-aware modeling for clinical activity (GEE or mixed models)
- **Purpose:** Account for clustering by block/group/day (non-independence).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1) Procedures within the same block/day share staffing and scheduling → correlation.
  2) Produces more valid SEs/CIs than assuming independence.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Needs enough clusters; with few blocks, variance components are unstable.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - With extremely small number of clusters.
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
  - **Cluster-robust SEs** with simple NB/Poisson; or aggregate to block-level.

## TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS (should NOT be used)

1) **Paired t-test** for subgroup comparisons (3rd-year clinical vs online-only)  
- **Why not:** groups are independent; paired t-test assumes matched/paired observations.

2) **One-way ANOVA** on individual Likert items as primary analysis  
- **Why not:** Likert ordinal; ANOVA assumes interval scale and (approx.) normal residuals/homoscedasticity; also ignores ceiling/floor effects.

3) **Multiple unadjusted t-tests across many items**  
- **Why not:** severe multiplicity; inflated Type I error and selective reporting risk.

4) **Pearson correlation on ordinal Likert items as the main association measure**  
- **Why not:** assumes continuous, linear relationship; **Spearman** or ordinal models are more suitable.

5) **Simple comparison of raw procedure counts without accounting for exposure (student-pair-days)**  
- **Why not:** confounds clinical activity with differing clinical capacity; violates the study’s own standardization concept.

6) **Linear regression on count outcomes (procedures) without transformation/modeling counts**  
- **Why not:** count data are non-normal, heteroscedastic, non-negative; predictions can be negative.

## TASK 4: SUMMARY (overall analytical approach)

The analysis should treat the project as two linked evaluations: (1) **student-perceived effectiveness and safety** measured mostly on **ordinal/binary survey data**, best analyzed using **nonparametric and/or ordinal regression methods** with multiplicity control and effect sizes; and (2) **objective clinical activity change** between semesters measured as **procedure counts/rates**, best analyzed using **count regression (negative binomial/Poisson with offsets)** and, where feasible, **cluster-aware inference**. This combination aligns with the data types, preserves correct measurement level, and yields interpretable estimates of both perceived and operational “effectiveness” of blended learning.
