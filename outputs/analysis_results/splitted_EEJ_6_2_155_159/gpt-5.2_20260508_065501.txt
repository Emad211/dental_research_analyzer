# CATEGORY A — MANDATORY tests/analyses

## A1) Independent-samples t-test (or Welch t-test)
- **Purpose**: Compare **mean** primary outcome between laser vs control **post-intervention** (if outcome is continuous, approximately normal).
- **Why appropriate**
  1. RCT with **two independent groups**.
  2. Directly estimates mean difference; aligns with common continuous endpoints (e.g., EPT threshold, VAS).
- **Why may not be appropriate**
  - Outcome is **ordinal**, strongly skewed, bounded, or has floor/ceiling effects (common with pain).
- **When NOT to use**
  - Severe non-normality with small n; clear ordinal scale; unequal variances without Welch; clustered observations (multiple teeth per patient).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated**
  - **Welch t-test** (unequal variances), or **Mann–Whitney U** (ordinal/non-normal), or **linear mixed model** if clustering/repeated measures.

## A2) Mann–Whitney U test (Wilcoxon rank-sum)
- **Purpose**: Compare distributions (often interpreted as location shift) of pain/EPT outcomes between groups when outcome is **ordinal or non-normal**.
- **Why appropriate**
  1. Robust for small samples and skewed distributions.
  2. Appropriate for ordinal pain scores (if that is the scale).
- **Why may not be appropriate**
  - Does not directly estimate mean difference; interpretation depends on distributional assumptions; ties are common in bounded scales.
- **When NOT to use**
  - When the estimand is explicitly the **mean difference**; when there is heavy clustering or repeated measures without adjustment.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated**
  - **Permutation test** for difference in means/medians; **ordinal logistic regression** for ordinal outcomes; **mixed models** for clustered/repeated.

## A3) Chi-square test or Fisher’s exact test (for binary “anesthesia success”)
- **Purpose**: Compare proportion with “successful pulpal anesthesia / no pain response” between groups (if outcome is binary).
- **Why appropriate**
  1. Two-group RCT with categorical endpoint.
  2. Provides hypothesis test for difference in proportions; can pair with risk difference/ratio.
- **Why may not be appropriate**
  - Expected counts may be small (likely with n≈10/group at analysis), making chi-square invalid.
- **When NOT to use**
  - Any cell expected count <5 → avoid chi-square.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated**
  - **Fisher’s exact test**; estimate **risk ratio** with exact or mid-P CI; or **logistic regression**.

## A4) Primary effect size + 95% CI (mandatory reporting)
- **Purpose**: Quantify clinical magnitude (not just p-values).
- **Why appropriate**
  1. CONSORT-aligned; essential with small trials.
  2. Supports clinical interpretation and future sample size planning.
- **Why may not be appropriate**
  - If scale is poorly defined or outcome distribution makes standard effect sizes misleading.
- **When NOT to use**
  - Do not report standardized mean difference without clarifying units/scale and distribution.
- **Alternative**
  - Report **Hodges–Lehmann median difference** (with CI) for nonparametric; **risk ratio/risk difference** for binary.

# CATEGORY B — RECOMMENDED analyses

## B1) ANCOVA (linear regression adjusting for baseline EPT/pain, if measured)
- **Purpose**: Compare post-intervention outcome between groups **adjusting for baseline** value (and possibly other prespecified covariates).
- **Why appropriate**
  1. Increases precision vs analyzing change scores, especially in small RCTs.
  2. Accounts for any baseline imbalance due to small sample.
- **Why may not be appropriate**
  - Baseline not measured; or baseline measure has poor reliability; or outcome is ordinal/non-normal.
- **When NOT to use**
  - If baseline outcome is not available or measured after randomization.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated**
  - **Rank ANCOVA**, **robust regression**, **quantile regression**, or **ordinal logistic regression** with baseline as covariate.

## B2) Logistic regression (binary endpoint) / Ordinal logistic regression (ordinal pain)
- **Purpose**: Estimate adjusted treatment effect (OR) and allow covariates (age/sex/baseline anxiety, etc.).
- **Why appropriate**
  1. Provides effect estimate with CI and adjustment, useful with small imbalances.
  2. Handles categorical outcomes more naturally than dichotomizing continuous outcomes.
- **Why may not be appropriate**
  - With very small n and sparse events, standard logistic regression can be biased/unstable.
- **When NOT to use**
  - Separation or quasi-separation; too many covariates relative to events.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated**
  - **Firth penalized logistic regression** (binary), **exact logistic**, or reduce covariates (pre-specify minimal set).

## B3) Mixed-effects model / GEE (if repeated EPT measurements or multiple teeth per subject)
- **Purpose**: Account for within-subject correlation (repeated time points or clustered teeth).
- **Why appropriate**
  1. Prevents underestimated SEs if multiple correlated observations exist.
  2. Can model time-by-treatment interaction if multiple post-treatment time points exist.
- **Why may not be appropriate**
  - If truly one observation per subject and one tooth only, this adds complexity without benefit.
- **When NOT to use**
  - When design is clearly independent with single endpoint per participant.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated**
  - **Cluster-robust SEs**, or analyze one prespecified tooth/summary per subject.

## B4) Multiple imputation + sensitivity analyses for missing outcomes
- **Purpose**: Reduce bias from attrition (2/12 and 3/13 lost).
- **Why appropriate**
  1. Attrition is non-trivial (~20% overall if complete-case), risking bias.
  2. MI yields valid inference under MAR and uses all available data.
- **Why may not be appropriate**
  - With extremely small sample, MI models may be unstable.
- **When NOT to use**
  - When missingness is minimal and demonstrably MCAR, or no auxiliary variables exist.
- **Alternative**
  - **Best/worst-case bounds**, **pattern-mixture sensitivity**, or clearly labeled complete-case as sensitivity (not primary).

## B5) Baseline comparability (descriptive + standardized differences)
- **Purpose**: Assess balance of key baseline variables (not for “significance testing”).
- **Why appropriate**
  1. Small RCTs can have chance imbalances affecting precision.
  2. Standardized differences are more informative than p-values.
- **Why may not be appropriate**
  - If baseline variables are not collected.
- **When NOT to use**
  - Avoid null-hypothesis “baseline p-value testing” as decision rule.
- **Alternative**
  - Present descriptive table and adjust for key prognostic covariates in ANCOVA/regression.

# CATEGORY C — OPTIONAL analyses

## C1) Permutation (randomization) test
- **Purpose**: Provide exact/near-exact inference consistent with randomization, useful in small samples.
- **Why appropriate**
  1. Does not rely on normality assumptions.
  2. Particularly attractive with n≈10/group.
- **Why may not be appropriate**
  - More complex to implement/report; needs clear estimand (difference in means/medians).
- **When NOT to use**
  - When clustering/repeated measures unless permutation scheme respects dependence.
- **Alternative**
  - Nonparametric tests or bootstrap CIs.

## C2) Bootstrap confidence intervals for effect size
- **Purpose**: Robust CI for mean/median differences with small samples or non-normality.
- **Why appropriate**
  1. Handles skewness and unknown distributions.
  2. Produces CIs for statistics without closed-form SEs (e.g., median difference).
- **Why may not be appropriate**
  - With very small n and many ties, bootstrap can be unstable.
- **When NOT to use**
  - Highly discrete outcomes with extreme sparsity.
- **Alternative**
  - Exact methods (Fisher/exact CI), or asymptotic CI with caution.

## C3) Subgroup / interaction analysis (pre-specified only)
- **Purpose**: Explore heterogeneity (e.g., by sex, baseline anxiety, tooth type/side).
- **Why appropriate**
  1. Can generate hypotheses for future trials.
  2. Uses interaction terms rather than separate within-group tests.
- **Why may not be appropriate**
  - Severe underpowering; high false-positive risk.
- **When NOT to use**
  - Any post hoc subgrouping without prespecification and multiplicity control.
- **Alternative**
  - Report descriptively; plan adequately powered future study.

## C4) Multiplicity adjustment (if multiple outcomes/time points)
- **Purpose**: Control family-wise error or FDR when testing multiple endpoints.
- **Why appropriate**
  1. Prevents inflated type I error.
  2. Encourages clear primary endpoint selection.
- **Why may not be appropriate**
  - If only one primary endpoint and few secondary endpoints (adjustment may be unnecessary if clearly labeled exploratory).
- **When NOT to use**
  - Do not adjust in a way that obscures the single primary endpoint analysis.
- **Alternative**
  - Hierarchical testing or report unadjusted with clear interpretation.

## TASK 3 — Inappropriate tests (should NOT be used)

1. **Paired t-test** (unless there is true within-subject pairing, e.g., split-mouth or pre/post on same subject being analyzed as paired difference only)  
   - Rationale: Trial appears **parallel-group**; groups are independent.

2. **One-way ANOVA (single factor, >2 groups)** as the primary analysis  
   - Rationale: Only **two groups**; ANOVA is equivalent to t-test but adds no value; if repeated measures exist, simple ANOVA is wrong.

3. **Repeated-measures ANOVA** without verifying repeated measures and sphericity, or without mixed-model alternative  
   - Rationale: Not justified by provided text; also fragile with missing data and small n.

4. **Multiple unadjusted t-tests across time points/outcomes**  
   - Rationale: Inflates type I error; should use mixed models or multiplicity strategy.

5. **“Baseline significance testing” to decide whether to adjust**  
   - Rationale: Adjustment should be prespecified based on prognostic relevance, not baseline p-values.

6. **Correlation/regression ignoring clustering** (if multiple teeth per participant)  
   - Rationale: Violates independence; underestimates SEs.
