### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS

These are necessary to properly address the main research question.

#### A1. Baseline comparison of groups

**Tests:**
- For continuous baseline variables (age, baseline EPT, baseline pain):
  - Test: Independent-samples t-test.
  - If assumptions violated or N small with skewed data: Mann–Whitney U test.
- For categorical baseline variables (sex, tooth type, etc.):
  - Test: Chi-square test or Fisher’s exact test (if cell counts <5).

**Purpose:**
- To check balance between randomized groups at baseline.

**Why appropriate (t-test/Chi-square):**
1. Randomization should create comparable groups, but baseline comparisons help identify imbalances that may require adjustment.
2. t-test and Chi-square are standard, easily interpretable tests for differences in means and proportions between two groups.

**Why may not be appropriate:**
- t-test assumes normality and homogeneity of variances; with n ≈ 10 per group, heavy skew or outliers can distort results.
- Chi-square requires expected cell counts generally ≥5; small sample may violate this.

**When NOT to use:**
- t-test: if data are markedly non-normal, with clear outliers, or if variables are ordinal with few categories.
- Chi-square: when expected frequencies are very small.

**Alternatives if assumptions violated:**
- t-test → Mann–Whitney U test (for continuous/ordinal variables).
- Chi-square → Fisher’s exact test (for 2×2 or small contingency tables).

#### A2. Primary outcome analysis – between-group comparison post-intervention

There are two main structural scenarios:

1. Only post-treatment values recorded (no baseline).
2. Both baseline and post-treatment values recorded.

Given typical clinical protocols, I assume scenario 2 (baseline + post) is likely. I provide both options.

##### Scenario 2A: Baseline and post-treatment continuous outcome

**Primary Test: Analysis of covariance (ANCOVA)**

- Dependent variable: Post-treatment EPT threshold (or post-treatment pain score).
- Fixed factor: Group (Laser vs Control).
- Covariate: Baseline EPT (or baseline pain score).

**Purpose:**
- To compare post-treatment outcome between groups while adjusting for baseline values.

**Why appropriate:**
1. Adjusting for baseline improves precision and power, especially in small randomized trials, by accounting for baseline variability.
2. ANCOVA directly models the treatment effect on the final outcome, providing adjusted mean differences and CIs.

**Why may not be appropriate:**
- Requires approximate linear relationship between baseline and outcome, homogeneity of regression slopes, and normally distributed residuals. With small sample, these assumptions are harder to check and may be unstable.

**When NOT to use:**
- If the baseline measurement is missing for a large fraction of participants.
- If the relationship between baseline and outcome differs dramatically between groups (interaction) and sample is too small to model this interaction.

**Alternative if assumptions violated:**
- Use non-parametric analysis of change scores:
  - Calculate change = Post − Baseline for each subject.
  - Compare change between groups using Mann–Whitney U test.
  - Also report median and IQR of change.

##### Scenario 2B: Only post-treatment outcome available

**Primary Test: Independent-samples t-test**

- Dependent variable: Post-treatment EPT (or pain) value.
- Grouping variable: Laser vs Control.

**Purpose:**
- To compare mean post-treatment outcome between the two groups.

**Why appropriate:**
1. Straightforward comparison of continuous outcomes between two independent groups.
2. Widely used; effect sizes (mean difference, Cohen’s d) are easily interpretable.

**Why may not be appropriate:**
- Violates assumptions if data are heavily skewed, non-normal, or variance differs significantly between groups.
- Very small sample size increases sensitivity to outliers.

**When NOT to use:**
- When data are non-normal and transformations do not help.
- When outcome is ordinal or heavily censored.

**Alternative if assumptions violated:**
- Mann–Whitney U test for between-group comparison of ordinal or non-normally distributed outcomes.
- Bootstrap confidence intervals for the mean difference.

#### A3. Within-group pre–post comparison (if pre/post outcome)

**Tests:**
- Paired t-test (for continuous, approximately normal change).
- Alternatively, Wilcoxon signed-rank test (if non-normal).

**Purpose:**
- To assess whether laser and control groups each show significant within-group change in EPT or pain from baseline to post-treatment.

**Why appropriate:**
1. Paired t-test accounts for within-subject correlation between baseline and post measures.
2. Provides insight into magnitude of change within each group, complementing between-group analysis.

**Why may not be appropriate:**
- Paired t-test assumes normal distribution of change scores; violations common with small samples.

**When NOT to use:**
- When the distribution of differences (post − baseline) is highly skewed, or data are ordinal with few categories.

**Alternative if assumptions violated:**
- Wilcoxon signed-rank test for paired non-parametric data.

**Tests:**

  - Fixed effects: Group, Time, Group×Time interaction.

**Purpose:**
- To evaluate overall time-course of effect and whether the effect differs over time between groups.

**Why appropriate:**

**Why may not be appropriate:**

**When NOT to use:**

- When data are highly unbalanced or many missing values relative to sample size.

**Alternative if assumptions violated:**
- Non-parametric repeated measures methods (e.g., Friedman test) for within-group comparisons plus stratified between-group comparisons at each time point with adjusted α.

## TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS

These should NOT be used in this study, with reasons.

### 1. One-way ANOVA for two-group comparisons

- Reason: ANOVA collapses to t-test for two groups; using ANOVA is unnecessary and can confuse interpretation. With very small sample, simpler tests are preferable.

### 2. Repeated measures MANOVA for single outcome with 2 time points

- Reason: MANOVA for repeated measures is over-complex for a simple pre/post design and small sample; assumptions (multivariate normality) are unrealistic, and power is low.

### 3. Non-paired tests for paired data (e.g., independent t-test on pre vs post within same group)

- Reason: Pre and post data within the same participants are correlated; treating them as independent violates assumptions and misestimates variance.

### 4. Multiple logistic regression with many predictors

- Reason: With ~22 participants, models with more than 2–3 predictors will be severely overfitted, producing unreliable estimates and inflated type I errors.

### 5. Time-to-event (survival) analyses (e.g., Kaplan–Meier, Cox regression)

- Reason: Outcome is immediate pain/anesthesia during EPT; there is no follow-up time or event-time variable, so survival methods are inappropriate.

### 6. Complex multilevel/hierarchical models (beyond simple random intercept for subject)

- Reason: Experimental unit is likely single tooth per subject; no nested clusters such as multiple teeth per subject or centers. With small sample and simple structure, multilevel modeling is unnecessary and unstable.

### 7. Multiple comparison procedures for numerous outcomes/time points without clear hypothesis

- Reason: Sample size is small; performing many tests with Bonferroni or similar corrections will drastically reduce power without adding meaningful insights, unless pre-specified outcomes/time points exist.

### 8. Parametric tests on ordinal scales with very few categories (e.g., 4-point pain scale)

- Reason: If pain/anxiety is measured on a 3–4 category scale, treating it as continuous for t-tests may be inappropriate; better to use ordinal methods.
