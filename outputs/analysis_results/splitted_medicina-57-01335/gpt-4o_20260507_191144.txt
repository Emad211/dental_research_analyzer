### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS
1. **Test Name**: Independent Samples t-Test (or Mann-Whitney U Test for non-normal data).
   - **Purpose**: Compare mean pain scores between the two groups (traditional syringe vs. Dentapen®).
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. The study involves two independent groups.
     2. Pain scores are likely continuous data.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:
     - Assumes normal distribution of pain scores; if violated, Mann-Whitney U Test should be used.
   - **When NOT to Use**:
     - If pain scores are categorical or ordinal.
   - **Alternative**: Mann-Whitney U Test for non-normal data.

2. **Test Name**: Chi-Square Test of Independence.
   - **Purpose**: Assess the association between syringe type and categorical outcomes (e.g., type of procedure performed).
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Categorical data (procedure type) is compared across syringe types.
     2. Randomization ensures independence of observations.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:
     - Small cell counts may violate assumptions.
   - **When NOT to Use**:
     - If expected frequencies are <5 in any cell.
   - **Alternative**: Fisher’s Exact Test for small sample sizes.

#### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED TESTS
1. **Test Name**: Repeated Measures ANOVA (or Linear Mixed Models).
   - **Purpose**: Analyze pain scores across multiple interventions per patient.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Accounts for repeated measures within the same patient.
     2. Allows adjustment for covariates (e.g., age, sex, procedure type).
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:
     - Assumes sphericity; if violated, corrections like Greenhouse-Geisser should be applied.
   - **When NOT to Use**:
     - If pain scores are not continuous.
   - **Alternative**: Generalized Estimating Equations (GEE) for non-normal data.

2. **Test Name**: Logistic Regression.
   - **Purpose**: Predict likelihood of high pain scores based on syringe type and procedure type.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Pain scores can be dichotomized (e.g., high vs. low pain).
     2. Allows adjustment for covariates.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:
     - Requires large sample size for reliable estimates.
   - **When NOT to Use**:
     - If pain scores cannot be dichotomized.
   - **Alternative**: Multinomial Logistic Regression for more than two categories.

#### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL TESTS
1. **Test Name**: Subgroup Analysis (e.g., stratified t-tests or ANOVA).
   - **Purpose**: Explore differences in pain scores across specific procedures (e.g., extractions vs. fillings).
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Provides insights into procedure-specific effects.
     2. Enhances clinical relevance of findings.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:
     - Risk of type I error due to multiple comparisons.
   - **When NOT to Use**:
     - If subgroup sample sizes are too small.
   - **Alternative**: Bonferroni correction for multiple comparisons.

2. **Test Name**: Correlation Analysis (e.g., Spearman’s Rank Correlation).
   - **Purpose**: Assess relationships between pain scores and continuous variables (e.g., age).
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Useful for exploratory analysis.
     2. Does not assume linearity (Spearman’s).
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:
     - Does not establish causation.
   - **When NOT to Use**:
     - If data are not paired or continuous.
   - **Alternative**: Partial correlation controlling for covariates.
