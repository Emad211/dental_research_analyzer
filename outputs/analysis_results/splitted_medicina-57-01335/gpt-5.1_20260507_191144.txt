==================================  

Key design implications:  
- Randomised comparison of two methods (Dentapen® vs conventional).  
- Repeated/clustered observations per patient (287 procedures in 178 patients).  
- Outcome(s) are likely continuous (pain score, pulse rate); must account for non‑independence.  

Below, tests are grouped into Mandatory, Recommended, Optional. For each: purpose, appropriateness, possible issues, when not to use, and alternatives.

CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS  
-----------------------------  

A1. Descriptive statistics and baseline comparability  
**Test/Methods:**  
- Descriptive statistics: means ± SD or medians (IQR), counts (%) by group.  
- Between‑group comparisons for baseline variables:  
  - t‑test or Mann–Whitney U for continuous variables (e.g., age).  
  - Chi‑square or Fisher’s exact test for categorical variables (sex, procedure type).  

**Purpose:**  
- Describe sample and procedures.  
- Assess balance across treatment groups (Dentapen® vs conventional) despite randomization, particularly for age, sex, and procedure type.  

**Why appropriate:**  
1. Essential to characterize the population and context of the trial; journals expect this.  
2. Allows evaluation of the success of randomization and identification of potential imbalances that may deserve adjustment in multivariable models.  

**Why it may not be appropriate:**  
- Formal hypothesis testing for baseline differences is sometimes discouraged in RCTs (randomization ensures comparability on expectation), so some reviewers prefer descriptive balance statements without p‑values.  

**When NOT to use:**  
- Avoid over‑interpreting baseline p‑values as evidence of systematic bias; they are often not needed for RCTs with sufficient sample size.  

**Alternative if assumptions violated:**  
- For non‑normal continuous variables (e.g., skewed age): report medians and IQR; use Mann–Whitney U instead of t‑test.  
- For sparse categorical data: use Fisher’s exact instead of Chi‑square.

---

A2. Primary analysis: comparison of pain scores between Dentapen® and conventional syringe  

Given multiple observations per patient, the primary analysis must account for clustering. Two main options:

### Option A2.1: Linear mixed‑effects model (LMM)  

**Test name:** Linear mixed‑effects regression (random intercept for patient).  

**Purpose:**  
- Compare mean pain scores between Dentapen® and conventional syringe while accounting for repeated procedures per patient and adjusting for relevant covariates (e.g., procedure type, sex, age).  

**Why appropriate:**  
1. Accounts for intra‑patient correlation (clustered data), yielding correct standard errors and valid inference.  
2. Flexible to include fixed effects for procedure type, injection site, or other covariates, improving precision and allowing adjusted comparisons.  

**Why it may not be appropriate:**  
- If pain scores are markedly non‑normal or strongly skewed with heavy ceiling/floor effects, LMM’s normality and homoscedasticity assumptions might be violated.  

**When NOT to use:**  
- When outcome is ordinal with a small number of categories (e.g., Likert 1–5) and clearly non‑interval; LMM may not be ideal.  
- When there is a substantial number of outliers and residuals are strongly non‑normal even after reasonable transformation.  

**Alternative if assumptions are violated:**  
- Transform outcome (e.g., log, square root) and re‑fit LMM;  
- Or use generalized linear mixed model (GLMM) with appropriate link:  
  - e.g., ordinal mixed model (cumulative logit) if outcome is ordinal.  
  - Or robust regression / quantile mixed models (if available).  

Model specification example:  
- Pain_ij = β0 + β1*(Dentapen_ij) + β2*(ProcedureType_ij) + β3*(Sex_i) + β4*(Age_i) + u_i + ε_ij  
  - i = patient, j = procedure.  
  - u_i ~ N(0, σ_u²) patient random intercept.  

### Option A2.2: Generalized estimating equations (GEE)  

**Test name:** GEE with identity link (for continuous pain outcome).  

**Purpose:**  
- Estimate marginal (population‑averaged) difference in mean pain between Dentapen® and conventional syringe accounting for within‑patient correlation.  

**Why appropriate:**  
1. Provides robust, large‑sample‑consistent standard errors even if correlation structure is misspecified.  
2. Gives population‑level effect (mean difference) which is often the primary interest in RCTs.  

**Why it may not be appropriate:**  
- Less efficient in smaller samples than LMM; 178 patients is moderate, but not large if many parameters are included.  
- Requires correct specification of link function and reasonable continuous outcome.  

**When NOT to use:**  
- When cluster size is extremely unbalanced or very small N at the cluster level; GEE may perform poorly.  
- When interest is in subject‑specific effects rather than average effects.  

**Alternative if assumptions violated:**  
- If outcome highly skewed: use GEE with appropriate link (e.g., log link) and family (e.g., Gaussian with identity/log or Gamma, depending on scale), or use rank‑based methods with clustering (e.g., clustered Wilcoxon, see below).  

Note: Choose either LMM or GEE as primary approach; the other can be used as sensitivity analysis.

A3. Secondary analysis: pulse rate changes  

Assuming pulse rate measured at baseline and during/after injection:  

**Test name:** Linear mixed‑effects model for repeated measures of pulse rate (or change scores), or GEE equivalent.  

**Purpose:**  
- Compare change in pulse rate from baseline between Dentapen® and conventional syringe.  

**Why appropriate:**  
1. Repeated measures within procedures and/or patients: mixed models/GEE handle this correlation.  
2. Continuous outcome; these models can directly estimate mean differences in change, adjusting for baseline and other covariates (procedure type, age, sex).  

**Why it may not be appropriate:**  
- If pulse measurements are few and inconsistent across patients, resulting in sparse or poorly structured repeated measures.  

**When NOT to use:**  
- If only “single” pulse reading per procedure is available (no baseline), then use simpler clustered models (LMM/GEE on a single time point, not repeated measures).  
- If measurement error is high and data quality poor.  

**Alternative if assumptions violated:**  
- Use non‑parametric approaches for paired/clustered data (e.g., cluster‑bootstrap differences in pulse change, or Wilcoxon signed rank for properly paired data); or GEE with robust standard errors.  

---

A4. Procedure‑type–adjusted comparison  

**Test name:** LMM or GEE with interaction or stratification by procedure type.  

**Purpose:**  
- Investigate whether the effect of Dentapen® on pain differs by procedure type (extraction vs filling vs root canal vs surgery vs stump prep).  

**Why appropriate:**  
1. Procedure type is strongly related to expected pain; adjusting controls confounding and improves precision.  
2. Interaction terms detect effect modification (e.g., Dentapen® more effective in palatal injection‑heavy procedures).  

**Why it may not be appropriate:**  
- Some procedure categories have small sample sizes (e.g., specific surgeries), leading to unstable estimates and unreliable interaction tests.  

**When NOT to use:**  
- When cell counts are extremely low (e.g., <10 per method per category); interaction tests will have very low power and risk overfitting.  

**Alternative if assumptions violated:**  
- Collapse similar procedures into broader categories (e.g., restorative vs surgical; simple vs complex) to increase group sizes.  
- Perform stratified analyses with descriptive comparisons rather than formal interaction tests.

CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED TESTS  
-------------------------------  

B1. Simple (non‑cluster‑adjusted) comparisons as secondary / sensitivity  

**Test name:**  
- Independent samples t‑test or Mann–Whitney U test comparing pain scores between Dentapen® and conventional at the procedure level, ignoring clustering.  

**Purpose:**  
- Provide straightforward, easily interpretable comparisons; useful as sensitivity analyses or for readers unfamiliar with mixed models/GEE.  

**Why appropriate:**  
1. Simple implementation; widely understood.  
2. Serves as a check on the robustness of the primary cluster‑adjusted findings; large differences are usually consistent.  

**Why it may not be appropriate:**  
- Ignores within‑patient correlation; standard errors likely underestimated, p‑values anti‑conservative.  

**When NOT to use:**  
- As the *primary* analysis, given clearly clustered design.  

**Alternative if assumptions violated:**  
- For non‑normal distributions or outliers: Mann–Whitney U test.  
- For matched/bilateral split‑mouth subset: use paired t‑test or Wilcoxon signed‑rank (see B2).

---

B2. Split‑mouth/bilateral subset: paired tests  

For those patients with bilateral treatments where one side received Dentapen® and the other conventional:  

**Test name:**  
- Paired t‑test (for continuous pain scores) or Wilcoxon signed‑rank test.  

**Purpose:**  
- Compare within‑patient pain for Dentapen® vs conventional in bilateral cases, which naturally control for patient‑level factors (pain threshold, anxiety).  

**Why appropriate:**  
1. Within‑patient pairing eliminates inter‑subject variability, increasing power.  
2. Consistent with random allocation of side to method, yielding unbiased paired effect estimates.  

**Why it may not be appropriate:**  
- Number of bilateral cases may be small; results may be unstable or unrepresentative of whole sample.  

**When NOT to use:**  
- When side allocation is not random or when both sides use same syringe type.  
- If many bilateral procedures differ in type or site across sides (non‑comparable).  

**Alternative if assumptions violated:**  
- Use sign test (less powerful but robust to non‑normality/blunt measurement).  

---

B3. Ordinal models if pain measured on Likert‑type scale  

If pain is recorded on an ordinal scale (e.g., 0–3, 0–5 categories) rather than continuous:  

**Test name:**  
- Cumulative logit (proportional odds) mixed model or GEE for ordinal outcomes.  

**Purpose:**  
- Assess odds of higher pain category with Dentapen® vs conventional.  

**Why appropriate:**  
1. Respect the ordinal nature of the outcome, avoiding inappropriate treatment as continuous.  
2. Mixed model/GEE still accommodate clustered data (procedures within patients).  

**Why it may not be appropriate:**  
- Proportional odds assumption may be violated (effect differs by threshold).  

**When NOT to use:**  
- When categories are very sparse or some categories rarely used.  

**Alternative if assumptions violated:**  
- Partial proportional odds models.  
- Collapsing categories (e.g., low vs high pain) and using binomial mixed model / GEE.

---

B4. Logistic regression for high vs low pain  

**Test name:**  
- Mixed‑effects logistic regression or GEE logistic regression.  

**Purpose:**  
- Evaluate the odds of “clinically relevant” pain (e.g., pain score ≥ some threshold) with Dentapen® vs conventional, adjusting for covariates.  

**Why appropriate:**  
1. Clinically interpretable: probability/odds of experiencing unpleasant pain.  
2. Handles binary outcome with clustering via random effects or GEE.  

**Why it may not be appropriate:**  
- Dichotomizing continuous pain can reduce statistical power and discard information.  

**When NOT to use:**  
- As sole primary analysis when you have a well‑measured continuous pain scale; better as secondary.  

**Alternative if assumptions violated:**  
- Use GLMM with robust standard errors;  
- Or keep pain continuous and use LMM/GLMM.

---

B5. Subgroup analyses (sex, age category, procedure type)  

**Test name:**  
- Mixed model or GEE with interaction terms (e.g., method*sex, method*procedure type).  

**Purpose:**  
- Explore whether the effect of Dentapen® differs by sex (male vs female), age category (e.g., <40 vs ≥40), or procedure type.  

**Why appropriate:**  
1. Patient pain perception and anxiety may differ by sex and age; important to check for effect heterogeneity.  
2. Provides information for tailored clinical recommendations (e.g., greater benefit in surgeries).  

**Why it may not be appropriate:**  
- Multiple testing; risk of spurious findings if not pre‑specified and corrected.  

**When NOT to use:**  
- When sample size in subgroups is too small to give interpretable results.  

**Alternative if assumptions violated:**  
- Descriptive subgroup analyses without formal interaction tests; present means and CIs.

TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS (TO AVOID)  
======================================  

T3.1. Simple independent t‑test or ANOVA ignoring clustering as the *primary* analysis  

**Why not:**  
- Procedures are clustered within patients; ignoring this leads to underestimated standard errors and inflated Type I error (p‑values too small).  

**Better alternative:**  
- Linear mixed‑effects model or GEE accounting for patient‑level clustering.

---

T3.2. Repeated measures ANOVA treating each procedure as repeated measure within patient (with equal number of observations)  

**Why not:**  
- Repeated measures ANOVA assumes equal number and spacing of observations per subject; here number of procedures per patient varies (mean 1.8 ± 1.9).  
- Handles only fixed, balanced time points; not appropriate for unbalanced, irregular number of procedures.  

**Better alternative:**  
- Mixed‑effects models with random intercepts (and slopes if needed) easily handle unbalanced repeated data.

---

T3.3. Simple paired t‑test as global method  

**Why not:**  
- Not all patients have paired (bilateral) treatments with both methods; applying paired tests to whole sample is invalid.  

**Better alternative:**  
- Use paired tests only in subset of true bilateral matched procedures (B2), and use mixed models/GEE for overall data.

---

T3.4. Multiple unadjusted comparisons across procedure types with no multiplicity control  

**Why not:**  
- Testing many procedure‑specific comparisons without adjustment inflates family‑wise error and increases chance of false positives.  

**Better alternative:**  
- Pre‑specify key subgroups; use interaction terms in a single model, or adjust p‑values (e.g., Holm) if many post‑hoc tests.

---

T3.5. Use of parametric tests for clearly ordinal outcomes with few categories without justification  

**Examples:**  
- Using t‑test on a 4‑point categorical pain scale.  

**Why not:**  
- Violates assumption of interval scale; may misrepresent differences.  

**Better alternative:**  
- Ordinal logistic mixed models or non‑parametric tests (e.g., Wilcoxon, sign test).

---

T3.6. Stepwise variable selection based purely on p‑values  

**Why not:**  
- Increases risk of overfitting and biased estimates; results unstable and difficult to interpret, especially with moderate sample size.  

**Better alternative:**  
- Pre‑specify covariates based on clinical reasoning (sex, age, procedure type) and keep the model parsimonious.
