## CATEGORY A – MANDATORY tests/models

### A1) Linear Mixed-Effects Model (LMM) for repeated OHIP-14
- **Purpose:** Primary comparison of OHIP-14 trajectory over time between groups; estimate **group × time** interaction (difference-in-differences).
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Handles **correlated repeated measures** within individuals (baseline/follow-ups).
  2. Uses all available timepoints (especially important because groups have **different number of measurements**: 2 vs 3), without needing to discard intermediate data.
  3. Provides directly interpretable estimates: change from baseline within each group and **between-group difference in change** at obturation.
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - If OHIP-14 residuals are highly non-normal with strong floor effects, LMM assumptions may be poor (especially with small n).
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If outcome is extremely skewed/zero-inflated and transformation/robust methods are insufficient; or if timepoints are not comparable/clearly defined (e.g., “follow-up visit” timing varies drastically and is not recorded).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Generalized Linear Mixed Model (GLMM)** with appropriate family (e.g., ordinal or Poisson/negative binomial if treated as counts), or **rank-based mixed models**, or **GEE with robust SE**.

**Model specification (recommended minimum):**
- Fixed effects: group (single vs multiple), time (categorical: baseline, extirpation [multi only], obturation), and **group×time**.
- Random effects: subject-level random intercept; consider random slope for time if supported.
- Primary contrast: difference between groups at obturation adjusted for baseline (via interaction/estimated marginal means).

---

### A2) Baseline comparability: two-sample tests for key baseline variables
- **Purpose:** Describe baseline balance; identify major imbalances to consider as covariates (not for “proving” randomization).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Small samples can have chance imbalance; documenting it supports interpretability.
  2. Helps justify covariate-adjusted sensitivity models (e.g., adjusting for age/sex/baseline OHIP).
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Significance testing of baseline differences can be misleading; emphasis should be on **standardized differences** rather than p-values.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Do not use baseline p-values to decide whether randomization “worked” or to select covariates mechanically.
- **Alternatives if assumptions violated:**
  - For continuous: **Wilcoxon rank-sum** instead of t-test.  
  - For categorical: **Fisher’s exact** instead of chi-square.

### A3) Within-group pre–post change (paired analysis to support interpretation)
- **Test name:** Paired t-test (baseline vs obturation within each group) *or* model-based estimated change from LMM
- **Purpose:** Quantify whether OHRQoL improved from baseline within each treatment strategy.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Directly evaluates the clinical question “does OHRQoL improve after RCT?”
  2. Accounts for within-person pairing (if paired t-test) or uses all data (if LMM-derived).
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Paired t-test ignores intermediate timepoint (multiple-visit group) and cannot compare trajectories.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Do not use paired t-tests as the *primary* method for between-group inference.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Wilcoxon signed-rank** test (within-group) or rely on robust/mixed modeling.

## CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED tests/models

### B1) Effect size estimation with confidence intervals
- **Test/measure:** Mean change difference (Δ) with **95% CI** from LMM; optionally standardized effect (Hedges g for change scores).
- **Purpose:** Provide magnitude/precision, not only p-values.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Clinical interpretability of OHIP improvement requires effect size.
  2. Small trials benefit from CI-focused reporting.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Standardized effects can be unstable with small samples and non-normality.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Avoid over-interpreting standardized effects if distribution is highly non-normal.
- **Alternative:**
  - Report **median change** and CI via bootstrap if needed.

### B2) Model diagnostics / assumption checks
- **Tests/tools:** Residual plots; QQ-plots; check influential points; assess heteroscedasticity.
- **Purpose:** Validate LMM assumptions.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. OHIP totals may have floor effects; diagnostics guide robust alternatives.
  2. Protects against misleading inference in small samples.
- **Why not appropriate:**
  - Formal normality tests (e.g., Shapiro–Wilk) have low interpretability in small n; should not drive decisions alone.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Don’t use Shapiro–Wilk as a strict gatekeeper for parametric models.
- **Alternative:**
  - Robust SE, transformation, GLMM, or nonparametric/bootstrap.

### B3) Covariate-adjusted mixed model (sensitivity / precision gain)
- **Test/model:** LMM including prespecified covariates (e.g., age, sex, baseline OHIP, tooth type if collected).
- **Purpose:** Improve precision and address chance imbalance.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Increases efficiency in small samples when covariates are prognostic.
  2. Addresses potential residual confounding from convenience sampling and any imperfect randomization implementation.
- **Why not appropriate:**
  - Risk of overfitting with small n and many covariates.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Do not include many predictors relative to sample size; avoid data-driven covariate fishing.
- **Alternative:**
  - Limit to 1–3 prespecified covariates; or use penalized regression (optional).

### B4) Multiple comparisons control (if analyzing OHIP domains or items)
- **Test:** Holm–Bonferroni (family-wise) or Benjamini–Hochberg (FDR).
- **Purpose:** Control false positives if multiple OHIP subscales/items are tested.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. OHIP-14 contains multiple items/domains; item-level testing inflates Type I error.
  2. Encourages clear hierarchy: primary outcome first, secondary controlled.
- **Why not appropriate:**
  - Can reduce power in small samples.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Don’t adjust the primary outcome; adjust only secondary/exploratory families.
- **Alternative:**
  - Pre-specify a small number of secondary endpoints; or use multivariate models.

### B5) Categorical/ordinal responder analysis (clinically interpretable)
- **Test/model:** Define “clinically meaningful improvement” (e.g., ≥X-point reduction) and compare proportions via Fisher’s exact; or logistic regression.
- **Purpose:** Translate score changes into patient-level benefit.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. OHIP change may be easier to interpret as “improved vs not”.
  2. Robust to non-normal distributions.
- **Why not appropriate:**
  - Requires a validated minimal important difference (MID), which is not provided here.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If MID is arbitrary or post hoc.
- **Alternative:**
  - Use distribution-based thresholds (e.g., 0.5 SD) explicitly labeled exploratory.

## CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL (supplementary) analyses

### C1) Generalized Estimating Equations (GEE)
- **Purpose:** Population-averaged effect of group, time, and interaction with robust SE.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Robust to some misspecification of random effects distribution.
  2. Directly estimates average marginal effects.
- **Why not appropriate:**
  - With small n, robust SE can be unreliable; and unequal timepoints across groups complicate correlation structure.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Very small sample with sparse repeated measures and missingness.
- **Alternative:**
  - LMM/GLMM with small-sample corrections (Kenward–Roger).

### C2) Nonparametric longitudinal approach
- **Test/model:** Rank-based longitudinal model (e.g., Brunner–Langer) or aligned-rank transform for interaction.
- **Purpose:** Inference on group×time without normality.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Suitable when OHIP totals are strongly non-normal.
  2. Less sensitive to outliers.
- **Why not appropriate:**
  - Less straightforward effect-size interpretation on original OHIP scale.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If primary goal is estimating mean differences on original scale.
- **Alternative:**
  - Robust LMM or bootstrap CIs.

### C3) Bootstrap confidence intervals
- **Purpose:** Obtain CIs for mean change difference with fewer parametric assumptions.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Small samples and non-normality.
  2. Flexible for derived quantities (e.g., change differences).
- **Why not appropriate:**
  - With very small n, bootstrap may be unstable; needs careful resampling at the **participant level**.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Extremely small samples or heavy missingness.
- **Alternative:**
  - Profile likelihood CIs; robust sandwich estimators.

### C4) Reliability/consistency of OHIP-14 in this sample
- **Test:** Cronbach’s alpha or preferably McDonald’s omega (baseline).
- **Purpose:** Document internal consistency of Arabic OHIP-14 in this trial sample.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Supports validity of summing items into a total score.
  2. Useful if population differs from validation studies.
- **Why not appropriate:**
  - Not an outcome effect test; alpha can be misleading (tau-equivalence assumption).
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If sample size too small for stable reliability estimates.
- **Alternative:**
  - Omega; item-total correlations (descriptive).

### C5) Time-to-event / pain analyses
- Not available in provided methods (pain described in background only). Only consider if pain outcomes were actually collected.

## TASK 3: Inappropriate tests (should NOT be used)

1. **Independent (unpaired) t-test comparing post-treatment OHIP between groups without adjusting for baseline**
   - Ignores baseline differences and within-person change; lower power and potentially biased if baseline imbalanced.

2. **Multiple separate t-tests at each timepoint (baseline, extirpation, obturation) without a repeated-measures framework**
   - Inflates Type I error; ignores correlation; does not test the key **group×time** effect.

3. **One-way ANOVA on change scores across more than two “times”**
   - Change scores are not independent across time; also groups have different measurement schedules.

4. **Chi-square tests applied to OHIP-14 total score (treating it as categorical without rationale)**
   - Discards information and depends on arbitrary cutpoints.

5. **Simple linear regression treating repeated observations as independent**
   - Violates independence; underestimates SEs and inflates significance.

6. **MANOVA with many OHIP items/domains as outcomes in this small sample**
   - Requires large sample and multivariate normality; high risk of overfitting and unstable estimates.

## TASK 4: Summary justification of overall approach

The primary scientific question is whether **change in OHRQoL over treatment** differs between **single-visit and multiple-visit RCT**. Because OHRQoL is measured **repeatedly within the same individuals** (and with **unequal numbers of assessments across groups**), a **mixed-effects repeated-measures model with a group×time interaction** is the most appropriate primary analysis: it accounts for within-subject correlation, uses all observed data, and directly estimates the between-group difference in improvement at obturation while allowing sensitivity analyses for non-normality and missingness.

- Graphical: line plots of mean OHIP over time by group; boxplots; histograms.

Purpose  
- Characterize sample and distribution of OHIP scores; check balance between randomized groups; inspect trajectories over time.

Why appropriate (at least two reasons)  
1) Essential to understand data, identify outliers, missingness patterns, and baseline comparability between groups.  
2) Helps assess plausibility of normality and homoscedasticity, guiding choice between parametric and nonparametric methods.  

Why it may not be appropriate  
- Not a limitation per se; but descriptive summaries alone cannot support inferential conclusions.

When NOT to use  
- Never omitted; descriptions are always needed, although exact form can vary.

Alternatives if assumptions violated  
- If distributions are highly skewed or multi-modal, emphasize medians/IQR and nonparametric visualizations (e.g., kernel density plots).

A2. Primary inferential model: Linear Mixed-Effects Model (LMM) for repeated measures  

Test name  
- Linear Mixed-Effects Model (e.g., using restricted maximum likelihood).  

Purpose  
- Evaluate the effect of treatment group (single vs multiple visit), time, and group-by-time interaction on OHIP-14 scores, accounting for repeated measures within patients.  
- Provide adjusted estimates of mean difference in change in OHRQoL between groups.

Model specification (conceptual)  
- Outcome: OHIP_total_it (patient i, time t).  
- Fixed effects:  
  - Group (single vs multiple)  
  - Time (categorical: T0, T1, T2; T2 missing for single-visit)  
  - Group × Time interaction  
  - Optional: sociodemographic covariates if imbalances observed (age, sex, etc.).  
- Random effects:  
  - Random intercept for patient (and optionally random slope for time if data allow).  
- Correlation structure: unstructured or AR(1), depending on fit and data.  
- Analysis strategies for different time structures:  
  - Primary comparison: final follow-up vs baseline (T_final – T0) for both groups (simpler model with two time-points per subject; T_final for single-visit = post-obturation; for multiple-visit = post-obturation).  
  - Secondary: include intermediate timepoint for multiple-visit group only, possibly using a three-level time factor and allowing missing at T2 for single-visit, handled by mixed model’s capability for unbalanced repeated measures.

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) Handles repeated measures and within-subject correlation properly, avoiding inflated type I error from treating observations as independent.  
2) Accommodates unequal number of observations per subject (single-visit has 2 time-points; multiple-visit has 3) without discarding data.  
3) Permits inclusion of covariates and random effects (e.g., operator as random effect) to adjust for potential confounders and clustering.  

Why it may not be appropriate  
- Sample size is modest; complex random-effects structures may be unstable or fail to converge, particularly with more parameters (group×time interactions, multiple random effects).  
- OHIP-14 is bounded and mildly skewed; linear models assume approximate normality and homoscedastic residuals; if these are grossly violated, LMM may not be ideal.

When this test should NOT be used  
- When sample size is too small to support model complexity (e.g., <10 subjects per group) or convergence issues arise.  
- If outcome is heavily skewed, with many zero scores (“floor effect”), and transformations do not improve normality; parametric LMM assumptions would be untenable.

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Use a Generalized Linear Mixed Model (GLMM) with appropriate link and distribution (e.g., Poisson or negative binomial for count-like OHIP scores, or ordinal mixed model if item-level analysis).  
- Use nonparametric longitudinal methods (e.g., Brunner–Langer rank-based approaches) if GLMM is not feasible.  
- For simplified analysis, compute individual change scores and use nonparametric tests (e.g., Mann–Whitney U) as described below.

A3. Baseline comparison between groups  

Test name  
- Independent samples t-test (or Mann–Whitney U) for baseline OHIP-14 total score;  
- Chi-square (or Fisher’s exact) tests for categorical sociodemographic variables;  
- t-test or Mann–Whitney U for continuous covariates (e.g., age).

Purpose  
- Check randomization success and describe baseline equivalence between single- and multiple-visit groups.

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) Ensures groups are comparable at baseline; imbalance may suggest need for covariate adjustment.  
2) Helps interpret differences in change scores—if baseline differences are large, simple post-treatment comparisons can be misleading.  

Why it may not be appropriate  
- With small sample sizes, statistical significance tests for baseline comparability are often underpowered and not strictly necessary; descriptive comparison may suffice.

When this test should NOT be used  
- Some argue baseline hypothesis testing in randomized trials is conceptually inappropriate; focus should be on descriptive comparisons and adjusted analyses.  
- If sample sizes are very small, p-values may be misleading; emphasizing effect sizes and confidence intervals is better.

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Use nonparametric tests (Mann–Whitney U, Fisher’s exact) when normality or expected cell count assumptions fail.  

---

A4. Within-group change analysis  

Test name  
- Paired t-test (for baseline vs final OHIP-14) within each group;  
- For multiple-visit group: repeated measures ANOVA (or LMM as above) to evaluate change across all three time-points.

Purpose  
- Assess whether OHRQoL improves significantly after treatment within each group.  

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) Directly addresses primary question “OHRQoL improved after endodontic treatment.”  
2) Provides simple, interpretable statistics (mean change, 95% CI) illustrating magnitude of improvement within each group.

Why it may not be appropriate  
- If OHIP differences are non-normal or sample size is small, paired t-test assumptions may not hold.

When this test should NOT be used  
- When distributions of change scores are severely skewed or contain many ties/outliers; parametric paired t-test is then questionable.

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Use Wilcoxon signed-rank test (nonparametric) for paired data.  
- Alternatively, rely on estimates from the LMM / GLMM rather than separate paired tests.

---

A5. Between-group comparison of change  

Test name  
- Independent samples t-test on change scores (Δ = final – baseline OHIP) or ANCOVA with baseline score as covariate;  
- Preferred: ANCOVA model: Final OHIP = Group + Baseline OHIP + (covariates).

Purpose  
- Compare magnitude of improvement in OHIP between single-visit and multiple-visit groups.

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) Directly tests the effect of visit pattern on net OHRQoL change.  
2) ANCOVA adjusts for baseline OHIP differences, improving precision and reducing bias compared with simple post-treatment comparison.

Why it may not be appropriate  
- Assumes:  
  - Linear relationship between baseline and outcome.  
  - Homogeneity of regression slopes across groups.  
- With small samples, ANCOVA estimates may be unstable.

When this test should NOT be used  
- If there is strong evidence of interaction between baseline OHIP and group (nonparallel slopes).  
- If baseline distributions differ markedly and overlap is limited, making adjustment unreliable.

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Use nonparametric methods:  
  - Mann–Whitney U test on change scores.  
  - Or rank ANCOVA (e.g., van Elteren or aligned-rank ANCOVA).  
- Use LMM as described in A2, which inherently adjusts for baseline and repeated measures.

B1. Mixed-Effects Model including sociodemographic covariates  

Test name  
- Extended LMM (or GLMM) including covariates (age, sex, education, etc.).

Purpose  
- Explore whether sociodemographic factors influence baseline OHRQoL, magnitude of improvement, or modify the treatment effect.  

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) OHRQoL is known to be influenced by sociodemographic variables; adjusting provides more accurate estimates of treatment effect.  
2) Identifies subgroups (e.g., younger vs older) where treatment may have different impact, which may inform clinical practice.  

Why it may not be appropriate  
- Small sample size may not support multiple covariates (risk of overfitting, wide CIs, unstable estimates).

When this test should NOT be used  
- When events per parameter are too low (rule-of-thumb: <10–15 subjects per parameter).  
- If covariate data are incomplete or of poor quality.

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Limit to a few key covariates (e.g., age, sex) selected a priori.  
- Use stratified analyses (e.g., descriptive comparisons by age group) instead of multivariable modeling.

B2. Nonparametric sensitivity analyses  

Test name  
- Wilcoxon signed-rank for within-group change;  
- Mann–Whitney U (Wilcoxon rank-sum) for between-group differences in change;  
- Friedman test for repeated measures in multiple-visit group.

Purpose  
- Provide robustness checks when normality assumptions are questionable, and confirm that conclusions do not depend on parametric assumptions.

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) OHIP-14 is an ordinal composite score; nonparametric tests respect ordinal nature and are robust to non-normality.  
2) Small samples benefit from distribution-free methods to avoid misleading parametric results.

Why it may not be appropriate  
- Less powerful than parametric tests when parametric assumptions hold.  
- Do not readily adjust for covariates or complex designs (though some nonparametric ANCOVA-type methods exist).

When this test should NOT be used  
- When data are clustered beyond simple pairing (e.g., multi-level clusters) and nonparametric options cannot accommodate clustering.

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Use permutation tests that can handle small N and non-normal distributions.  
- Use GLMM if treating OHIP-14 as count/ordinal.

B3. Item-level or domain-level analyses  

Test name  
- For binary/ordinal items:  
  - Repeated measures ordinal logistic mixed models;  
  - McNemar test or Cochran’s Q for dichotomized items;  
  - Nonparametric tests for ordered responses.  

Purpose  
- Investigate which specific aspects (e.g., physical pain, functional limitation) drive changes in OHRQoL.

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) OHIP-14 has defined domains; analyzing domain or item-level changes gives clinically interpretable insights (e.g., pain vs psychological discomfort).  
2) Identifies specific domains where single- vs multiple-visit treatments differ (if any).  

Why it may not be appropriate  
- Item- or domain-level tests increase multiplicity and risk of type I error; sample size may be insufficient for reliable item-level modeling.

When this test should NOT be used  
- When sample size is very small or item response distributions are extremely sparse (e.g., most zeros).

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Present descriptive changes in domain scores without formal hypothesis testing, or use composite domain scores with nonparametric tests.

B4. Internal consistency (reliability) of OHIP-14 in this sample  

Test name  
- Cronbach’s alpha (and if possible, McDonald’s omega).

Purpose  
- Verify internal consistency reliability of the OHIP-14 Arabic version in this specific sample.

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) Even though a validated version exists, confirming reliability in the current context (endodontic patients in Saudi Arabia) adds credibility.  
2) Demonstrates that the total score is a coherent construct, justifying use of a summed score.

Why it may not be appropriate  
- Sample size may be at the lower end for stable reliability estimates, particularly if data are highly skewed.

When this test should NOT be used  
- If item responses are highly restricted (e.g., almost all “never”), alpha will be artificially low; interpretation becomes difficult.

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Report item-total correlations; consider Spearman–Brown or Guttman lambda coefficients.

B5. Effect size and confidence intervals  

Test name  
- Calculation of standardized effect sizes:  
  - Cohen’s d for within- and between-group differences.  
  - Partial eta-squared for ANOVA/LMM;  
  - 95% confidence intervals for all primary estimates.

Purpose  
- Quantify the magnitude, not just significance, of treatment effects and OHRQoL improvements.

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) Clinically useful: helps determine whether observed differences are meaningful, not just statistically significant or non-significant.  
2) Facilitates comparison with previous literature and meta-analyses.

Why it may not be appropriate  
- With small samples, effect size estimates may be imprecise (wide confidence intervals).

When this test should NOT be used  
- Not applicable; effect sizes are generally recommended but interpretation must consider sample size.

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Use nonparametric effect sizes (e.g., Cliff’s delta) if using rank-based tests.

C1. Mediation analysis via pain-related items  

Test name  
- Exploratory mediation models (e.g., path analysis) where physical pain items mediate the relationship between treatment and overall OHRQoL change.

Purpose  
- Explore whether reduction in pain (specific OHIP items) explains most of the improvement in OHRQoL.

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)

C2. Sensitivity analyses excluding protocol deviations  

Test name  
- Re-run primary analyses excluding patients with protocol deviations (e.g., incomplete treatment, major complications, missing follow-up).

Purpose  
- Assess robustness of conclusions to deviations and missing data.

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) Ensures treatment effect estimates are not driven by anomalous or incomplete cases.  
2) Differentiates intention-to-treat vs per-protocol perspectives.

Why it may not be appropriate  
- Small sample size limits ability to exclude cases without losing power; risk of biased per-protocol estimates.

When this test should NOT be used  
- If exclusion would remove a large fraction of sample (>20–30%), interpretations become unreliable.

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Use multiple imputation for missing OHIP scores; present ITT as primary and note limitations.

C3. Stratified analyses by age or gender

- Separate analyses (descriptive or inferential) within age groups (e.g., ≤30 vs >30) or by sex.

- Explore whether treatment effects differ across key demographic strata.

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) OHRQoL perceptions vary by age/gender; differential effects might be clinically important.  
2) Generates hypotheses for future, larger trials.

Why it may not be appropriate  
- With small N, stratification leads to very small subgroup sizes and uninformative, unstable estimates.

When this test should NOT be used

Alternative if assumptions violated

TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS  
----------------------------------

Tests that should NOT be used for this study, with reasons:

1. Multiple independent-samples t-tests at each time-point without accounting for repeated measures  
- Why not:  
  - Treats repeated measures from the same patient as independent, violating independence assumption and inflating type I error.  
  - Ignores within-subject correlations and changes over time; less efficient and can misrepresent longitudinal pattern.  
- Preferred alternative:  
  - LMM/GLMM for repeated measures; or analyze change scores with appropriate paired tests.

2. Simple one-way ANOVA comparing groups using only final OHIP scores without adjusting for baseline  
- Why not:  
  - Baseline imbalance, even if small, can bias inference about treatment effect.  
  - Randomization justifies adjusting for baseline to increase precision; unadjusted final comparisons are suboptimal and potentially misleading.  
- Preferred alternative:  
  - ANCOVA with baseline OHIP as covariate; or LMM including baseline as part of the repeated measures.

3. Parametric repeated measures ANOVA assuming sphericity with complete-case data only  
- Why not:  
  - Requires sphericity and complete data; this design has unequal time-points and likely missing data; RM-ANOVA would drop partially observed cases.  
  - Mixed models handle missingness and unequal spacing better.  
- Preferred alternative:  
  - LMM with appropriate covariance structure; if needed, nonparametric longitudinal methods.

4. Treating each OHIP item as continuous and running multiple separate t-tests or chi-square tests without multiplicity control  
- Why not:  
  - Inflates type I error due to multiple testing; sample too small to support multiple comparisons.  
  - Item-level data are ordinal and often sparse; simple t-tests/chi-square may be inappropriate.  
- Preferred alternative:  
  - Limit to pre-specified domains or summary scores; if items are analyzed, apply appropriate ordinal models and adjust for multiple comparisons.

5. Simple Pearson correlations between group assignment (binary 0/1) and OHIP scores  
- Why not:  
  - Correlation does not account for repeated measures or covariates; yields a crude and potentially misleading measure of association.  
- Preferred alternative:  
  - Use regression or mixed models that reflect the design.

6. Logistic regression treating OHIP-14 total as binary (e.g., “improved vs not improved”) with arbitrary cut-offs  
- Why not:  
  - Dichotomizing continuous/ordinal outcomes reduces information and power; arbitrary thresholds can bias results.  
- Preferred alternative:  
  - Analyze continuous OHIP scores; if categorization is clinically essential, choose pre-specified, validated thresholds and interpret cautiously.

7. Student’s t-test for raw Likert item responses  
- Why not:  
  - Item-level responses are ordinal with 5 levels; t-tests assume continuous, approximately normal variables.  
- Preferred alternative:  
  - Use ordinal models (e.g., proportional odds) or nonparametric tests; or focus on total score which is closer to continuous.

TASK 4: SUMMARY OF ANALYTICAL APPROACH  
----------------------------------

This trial compares OHRQoL outcomes (OHIP-14) in patients undergoing single- vs multiple-visit root canal treatment, with repeated measurements over time. The primary analytic approach should:

- Treat OHIP-14 total score as the main continuous outcome, with careful descriptive analysis.  
- Use linear mixed-effects models (or generalized mixed models) to account for:  
  - Repeated measures within patients;  
  - Unequal number and timing of measurements;  
  - Random variability between patients (and potentially operators).  
- Focus primary inference on:  
  - Within-group improvement in OHRQoL from baseline to post-obturation;  
  - Between-group differences in change (whether single vs multiple visits lead to different magnitudes of improvement).  
- Use ANCOVA / LMM approaches that adjust for baseline OHIP and, where feasible, key sociodemographic covariates.  
- Conduct assumption checks and supplement with nonparametric sensitivity analyses to ensure robustness.  
- Avoid simplistic independent t-tests at each time-point and unadjusted end-point comparisons, which ignore the longitudinal and randomized nature of the data.

This analytical plan aligns with the trial’s objectives, respects the structure of the OHIP-14 outcome and the repeated-measures design, and provides interpretable, clinically meaningful estimates of the effect of visit pattern on OHRQoL.
