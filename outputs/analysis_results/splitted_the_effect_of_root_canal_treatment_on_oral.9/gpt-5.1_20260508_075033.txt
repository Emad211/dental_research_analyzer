A1. Descriptive statistics and graphical summaries  

Test / Step

- Graphical: line plots of mean OHIP over time by group; boxplots; histograms.

Purpose

Why it may not be appropriate

When NOT to use

Alternatives if assumptions violated

A2. Primary inferential model: Linear Mixed-Effects Model (LMM) for repeated measures  

Test name  
- Linear Mixed-Effects Model (e.g., using restricted maximum likelihood).  

Purpose  
- Evaluate the effect of treatment group (single vs multiple visit), time, and group-by-time interaction on OHIP-14 scores, accounting for repeated measures within patients.  
- Provide adjusted estimates of mean difference in change in OHRQoL between groups.

Model specification (conceptual)  
- Outcome: OHIP_total_it (patient i, time t).  
- Fixed effects:  
  - Group (single vs multiple)  
  - Time (categorical: T0, T1, T2; T2 missing for single-visit)  
  - Group × Time interaction  
  - Optional: sociodemographic covariates if imbalances observed (age, sex, etc.).  
- Random effects:  
  - Random intercept for patient (and optionally random slope for time if data allow).  
- Correlation structure: unstructured or AR(1), depending on fit and data.  
- Analysis strategies for different time structures:  
  - Primary comparison: final follow-up vs baseline (T_final – T0) for both groups (simpler model with two time-points per subject; T_final for single-visit = post-obturation; for multiple-visit = post-obturation).  
  - Secondary: include intermediate timepoint for multiple-visit group only, possibly using a three-level time factor and allowing missing at T2 for single-visit, handled by mixed model’s capability for unbalanced repeated measures.

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) Handles repeated measures and within-subject correlation properly, avoiding inflated type I error from treating observations as independent.  
2) Accommodates unequal number of observations per subject (single-visit has 2 time-points; multiple-visit has 3) without discarding data.  
3) Permits inclusion of covariates and random effects (e.g., operator as random effect) to adjust for potential confounders and clustering.  

Why it may not be appropriate  
- Sample size is modest; complex random-effects structures may be unstable or fail to converge, particularly with more parameters (group×time interactions, multiple random effects).  
- OHIP-14 is bounded and mildly skewed; linear models assume approximate normality and homoscedastic residuals; if these are grossly violated, LMM may not be ideal.

When this test should NOT be used  
- When sample size is too small to support model complexity (e.g., <10 subjects per group) or convergence issues arise.  
- If outcome is heavily skewed, with many zero scores (“floor effect”), and transformations do not improve normality; parametric LMM assumptions would be untenable.

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Use a Generalized Linear Mixed Model (GLMM) with appropriate link and distribution (e.g., Poisson or negative binomial for count-like OHIP scores, or ordinal mixed model if item-level analysis).  
- Use nonparametric longitudinal methods (e.g., Brunner–Langer rank-based approaches) if GLMM is not feasible.  
- For simplified analysis, compute individual change scores and use nonparametric tests (e.g., Mann–Whitney U) as described below.

A3. Baseline comparison between groups  

Test name  
- Independent samples t-test (or Mann–Whitney U) for baseline OHIP-14 total score;  
- Chi-square (or Fisher’s exact) tests for categorical sociodemographic variables;  
- t-test or Mann–Whitney U for continuous covariates (e.g., age).

Purpose  
- Check randomization success and describe baseline equivalence between single- and multiple-visit groups.

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) Ensures groups are comparable at baseline; imbalance may suggest need for covariate adjustment.  
2) Helps interpret differences in change scores—if baseline differences are large, simple post-treatment comparisons can be misleading.  

Why it may not be appropriate  
- With small sample sizes, statistical significance tests for baseline comparability are often underpowered and not strictly necessary; descriptive comparison may suffice.

When this test should NOT be used  
- Some argue baseline hypothesis testing in randomized trials is conceptually inappropriate; focus should be on descriptive comparisons and adjusted analyses.  
- If sample sizes are very small, p-values may be misleading; emphasizing effect sizes and confidence intervals is better.

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Use nonparametric tests (Mann–Whitney U, Fisher’s exact) when normality or expected cell count assumptions fail.  

---

A4. Within-group change analysis  

Test name  
- Paired t-test (for baseline vs final OHIP-14) within each group;  
- For multiple-visit group: repeated measures ANOVA (or LMM as above) to evaluate change across all three time-points.

Purpose  
- Assess whether OHRQoL improves significantly after treatment within each group.  

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) Directly addresses primary question “OHRQoL improved after endodontic treatment.”  
2) Provides simple, interpretable statistics (mean change, 95% CI) illustrating magnitude of improvement within each group.

Why it may not be appropriate  
- If OHIP differences are non-normal or sample size is small, paired t-test assumptions may not hold.

When this test should NOT be used  
- When distributions of change scores are severely skewed or contain many ties/outliers; parametric paired t-test is then questionable.

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Use Wilcoxon signed-rank test (nonparametric) for paired data.  
- Alternatively, rely on estimates from the LMM / GLMM rather than separate paired tests.

---

A5. Between-group comparison of change  

Test name  
- Independent samples t-test on change scores (Δ = final – baseline OHIP) or ANCOVA with baseline score as covariate;  
- Preferred: ANCOVA model: Final OHIP = Group + Baseline OHIP + (covariates).

Purpose  
- Compare magnitude of improvement in OHIP between single-visit and multiple-visit groups.

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) Directly tests the effect of visit pattern on net OHRQoL change.  
2) ANCOVA adjusts for baseline OHIP differences, improving precision and reducing bias compared with simple post-treatment comparison.

Why it may not be appropriate  
- Assumes:  
  - Linear relationship between baseline and outcome.  
  - Homogeneity of regression slopes across groups.  
- With small samples, ANCOVA estimates may be unstable.

When this test should NOT be used  
- If there is strong evidence of interaction between baseline OHIP and group (nonparallel slopes).  
- If baseline distributions differ markedly and overlap is limited, making adjustment unreliable.

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Use nonparametric methods:  
  - Mann–Whitney U test on change scores.  
  - Or rank ANCOVA (e.g., van Elteren or aligned-rank ANCOVA).  
- Use LMM as described in A2, which inherently adjusts for baseline and repeated measures.

Test name

Purpose

Alternative if assumptions violated

B2. Nonparametric sensitivity analyses  

Test name  
- Wilcoxon signed-rank for within-group change;  
- Mann–Whitney U (Wilcoxon rank-sum) for between-group differences in change;  
- Friedman test for repeated measures in multiple-visit group.

Purpose  
- Provide robustness checks when normality assumptions are questionable, and confirm that conclusions do not depend on parametric assumptions.

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) OHIP-14 is an ordinal composite score; nonparametric tests respect ordinal nature and are robust to non-normality.  
2) Small samples benefit from distribution-free methods to avoid misleading parametric results.

Why it may not be appropriate  
- Less powerful than parametric tests when parametric assumptions hold.  
- Do not readily adjust for covariates or complex designs (though some nonparametric ANCOVA-type methods exist).

When this test should NOT be used  
- When data are clustered beyond simple pairing (e.g., multi-level clusters) and nonparametric options cannot accommodate clustering.

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Use permutation tests that can handle small N and non-normal distributions.  
- Use GLMM if treating OHIP-14 as count/ordinal.

B3. Item-level or domain-level analyses  

Test name  
- For binary/ordinal items:  
  - Repeated measures ordinal logistic mixed models;  
  - McNemar test or Cochran’s Q for dichotomized items;  
  - Nonparametric tests for ordered responses.  

Purpose  
- Investigate which specific aspects (e.g., physical pain, functional limitation) drive changes in OHRQoL.

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) OHIP-14 has defined domains; analyzing domain or item-level changes gives clinically interpretable insights (e.g., pain vs psychological discomfort).  
2) Identifies specific domains where single- vs multiple-visit treatments differ (if any).  

Why it may not be appropriate  
- Item- or domain-level tests increase multiplicity and risk of type I error; sample size may be insufficient for reliable item-level modeling.

When this test should NOT be used  
- When sample size is very small or item response distributions are extremely sparse (e.g., most zeros).

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Present descriptive changes in domain scores without formal hypothesis testing, or use composite domain scores with nonparametric tests.

B4. Internal consistency (reliability) of OHIP-14 in this sample  

Test name

Purpose  
- Verify internal consistency reliability of the OHIP-14 Arabic version in this specific sample.

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)  
1) Even though a validated version exists, confirming reliability in the current context (endodontic patients in Saudi Arabia) adds credibility.  
2) Demonstrates that the total score is a coherent construct, justifying use of a summed score.

Why it may not be appropriate  
- Sample size may be at the lower end for stable reliability estimates, particularly if data are highly skewed.

When this test should NOT be used  
- If item responses are highly restricted (e.g., almost all “never”), alpha will be artificially low; interpretation becomes difficult.

Alternative if assumptions violated  
- Report item-total correlations; consider Spearman–Brown or Guttman lambda coefficients.

TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS  
----------------------------------

Tests that should NOT be used for this study, with reasons:

1. Multiple independent-samples t-tests at each time-point without accounting for repeated measures  
- Why not:  
  - Treats repeated measures from the same patient as independent, violating independence assumption and inflating type I error.  
  - Ignores within-subject correlations and changes over time; less efficient and can misrepresent longitudinal pattern.  
- Preferred alternative:  
  - LMM/GLMM for repeated measures; or analyze change scores with appropriate paired tests.

2. Simple one-way ANOVA comparing groups using only final OHIP scores without adjusting for baseline  
- Why not:  
  - Baseline imbalance, even if small, can bias inference about treatment effect.  
  - Randomization justifies adjusting for baseline to increase precision; unadjusted final comparisons are suboptimal and potentially misleading.  
- Preferred alternative:  
  - ANCOVA with baseline OHIP as covariate; or LMM including baseline as part of the repeated measures.

3. Parametric repeated measures ANOVA assuming sphericity with complete-case data only  
- Why not:  
  - Requires sphericity and complete data; this design has unequal time-points and likely missing data; RM-ANOVA would drop partially observed cases.  
  - Mixed models handle missingness and unequal spacing better.  
- Preferred alternative:  
  - LMM with appropriate covariance structure; if needed, nonparametric longitudinal methods.

4. Treating each OHIP item as continuous and running multiple separate t-tests or chi-square tests without multiplicity control  
- Why not:  
  - Inflates type I error due to multiple testing; sample too small to support multiple comparisons.  
  - Item-level data are ordinal and often sparse; simple t-tests/chi-square may be inappropriate.  
- Preferred alternative:  
  - Limit to pre-specified domains or summary scores; if items are analyzed, apply appropriate ordinal models and adjust for multiple comparisons.

5. Simple Pearson correlations between group assignment (binary 0/1) and OHIP scores  
- Why not:  
  - Correlation does not account for repeated measures or covariates; yields a crude and potentially misleading measure of association.  
- Preferred alternative:  
  - Use regression or mixed models that reflect the design.

6. Logistic regression treating OHIP-14 total as binary (e.g., “improved vs not improved”) with arbitrary cut-offs  
- Why not:  
  - Dichotomizing continuous/ordinal outcomes reduces information and power; arbitrary thresholds can bias results.  
- Preferred alternative:  
  - Analyze continuous OHIP scores; if categorization is clinically essential, choose pre-specified, validated thresholds and interpret cautiously.

7. Student’s t-test for raw Likert item responses  
- Why not:  
  - Item-level responses are ordinal with 5 levels; t-tests assume continuous, approximately normal variables.  
- Preferred alternative:  
  - Use ordinal models (e.g., proportional odds) or nonparametric tests; or focus on total score which is closer to continuous.

TASK 4: SUMMARY OF ANALYTICAL APPROACH  
----------------------------------

This trial compares OHRQoL outcomes (OHIP-14) in patients undergoing single- vs multiple-visit root canal treatment, with repeated measurements over time. The primary analytic approach should:

- Treat OHIP-14 total score as the main continuous outcome, with careful descriptive analysis.  
- Use linear mixed-effects models (or generalized mixed models) to account for:  
  - Repeated measures within patients;  
  - Unequal number and timing of measurements;  
  - Random variability between patients (and potentially operators).  
- Focus primary inference on:  
  - Within-group improvement in OHRQoL from baseline to post-obturation;  
  - Between-group differences in change (whether single vs multiple visits lead to different magnitudes of improvement).  
- Use ANCOVA / LMM approaches that adjust for baseline OHIP and, where feasible, key sociodemographic covariates.  
- Conduct assumption checks and supplement with nonparametric sensitivity analyses to ensure robustness.  
- Avoid simplistic independent t-tests at each time-point and unadjusted end-point comparisons, which ignore the longitudinal and randomized nature of the data.

This analytical plan aligns with the trial’s objectives, respects the structure of the OHIP-14 outcome and the repeated-measures design, and provides interpretable, clinically meaningful estimates of the effect of visit pattern on OHRQoL.
