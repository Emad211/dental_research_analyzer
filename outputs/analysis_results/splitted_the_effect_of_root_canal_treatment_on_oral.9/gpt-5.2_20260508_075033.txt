## TASK 1: Information extraction (study characteristics)

### Study overview
- **Design:** Parallel-group clinical trial with **repeated measures** of the same outcome over time (pre/post within person), comparing **single-visit vs multiple-visit** root canal treatment (RCT).
- **Setting/period:** Dental teaching hospital, Makkah, Saudi Arabia; **Sep 2019–Jan 2020**.
- **Primary objective:** Assess the impact of RCT on **oral health-related quality of life (OHRQoL)** and compare change between **one-visit** vs **multiple-visit** treatment.
- **Ethics:** IRB approved (132-19); written informed consent.

### Participants
- **Sampling:** Convenience sample.
- **Planned sample size:** 16 per group (power calculation described).
- **Enrolled sample size:** 32 total (stated explicitly).  
  *Note:* Text also inconsistently states “assigned randomly into two groups of fifty patients each” (likely an error; plan should assume **n=32** unless full paper clarifies).
- **Age range:** 16–60 years.
- **Inclusion criteria:** Need RCT; diagnosed with **symptomatic irreversible pulpitis** and **normal periapical tissue**.
- **Exclusion criteria:** Calcified canals; systemic diseases that may complicate RCT (e.g., endocarditis); any resorption.

### Groups / interventions
- **Group 1: Single-visit RCT**
  - OHIP-14 completed **twice**:
    1) at beginning of treatment (baseline)  
    2) at the **second follow-up visit after obturation** (post-treatment)
- **Group 2: Multiple-visit RCT**
  - OHIP-14 completed **three times**:
    1) baseline (beginning)  
    2) next visit after access cavity + pulp extirpation  
    3) after obturation

### Outcomes, variables, measurements
- **Primary outcome:** **OHIP-14 total score** (0–56), higher = worse OHRQoL.
  - Each item coded 0–4 (never → very often), summed across 14 items.
- **Secondary/other variables mentioned:** “Sociodemographic factors” were verified via questionnaire/interview (specific variables not listed in provided text).
- **Measurement method:** Face-to-face interview using **Arabic OHIP-14**; “interview form” used to reduce information bias.

### Randomization / bias control
- **Allocation:** “Assigned randomly” to single vs multiple visits (method not described).
- **Operator bias control:** Two undergraduate operators with similar performance; supervised by one endodontist.
- **Selection bias:** Authors state they used **mixed-effect models** to reduce selection bias (more accurately: to account for correlation/repeated measures and possibly covariate imbalance).

### Sample size / power basis
- Targeted detection of **difference in mean changes = 8.0** OHIP points, **SD=6.0**, **power=82%**, **alpha=0.05** → **16 per group**.

## CATEGORY A – MANDATORY tests/models

### A1) Linear Mixed-Effects Model (LMM) for repeated OHIP-14
- **Purpose:** Primary comparison of OHIP-14 trajectory over time between groups; estimate **group × time** interaction (difference-in-differences).
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Handles **correlated repeated measures** within individuals (baseline/follow-ups).
  2. Uses all available timepoints (especially important because groups have **different number of measurements**: 2 vs 3), without needing to discard intermediate data.
  3. Provides directly interpretable estimates: change from baseline within each group and **between-group difference in change** at obturation.
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - If OHIP-14 residuals are highly non-normal with strong floor effects, LMM assumptions may be poor (especially with small n).
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If outcome is extremely skewed/zero-inflated and transformation/robust methods are insufficient; or if timepoints are not comparable/clearly defined (e.g., “follow-up visit” timing varies drastically and is not recorded).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Generalized Linear Mixed Model (GLMM)** with appropriate family (e.g., ordinal or Poisson/negative binomial if treated as counts), or **rank-based mixed models**, or **GEE with robust SE**.

**Model specification (recommended minimum):**
- Fixed effects: group (single vs multiple), time (categorical: baseline, extirpation [multi only], obturation), and **group×time**.
- Random effects: subject-level random intercept; consider random slope for time if supported.
- Primary contrast: difference between groups at obturation adjusted for baseline (via interaction/estimated marginal means).

---

### A2) Baseline comparability: two-sample tests for key baseline variables
- **Purpose:** Describe baseline balance; identify major imbalances to consider as covariates (not for “proving” randomization).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Small samples can have chance imbalance; documenting it supports interpretability.
  2. Helps justify covariate-adjusted sensitivity models (e.g., adjusting for age/sex/baseline OHIP).
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Significance testing of baseline differences can be misleading; emphasis should be on **standardized differences** rather than p-values.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Do not use baseline p-values to decide whether randomization “worked” or to select covariates mechanically.
- **Alternatives if assumptions violated:**
  - For continuous: **Wilcoxon rank-sum** instead of t-test.  
  - For categorical: **Fisher’s exact** instead of chi-square.

### A3) Within-group pre–post change (paired analysis to support interpretation)
- **Test name:** Paired t-test (baseline vs obturation within each group) *or* model-based estimated change from LMM
- **Purpose:** Quantify whether OHRQoL improved from baseline within each treatment strategy.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Directly evaluates the clinical question “does OHRQoL improve after RCT?”
  2. Accounts for within-person pairing (if paired t-test) or uses all data (if LMM-derived).
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Paired t-test ignores intermediate timepoint (multiple-visit group) and cannot compare trajectories.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Do not use paired t-tests as the *primary* method for between-group inference.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Wilcoxon signed-rank** test (within-group) or rely on robust/mixed modeling.

## CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED tests/models

### B1) Effect size estimation with confidence intervals
- **Test/measure:** Mean change difference (Δ) with **95% CI** from LMM; optionally standardized effect (Hedges g for change scores).
- **Purpose:** Provide magnitude/precision, not only p-values.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Clinical interpretability of OHIP improvement requires effect size.
  2. Small trials benefit from CI-focused reporting.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Standardized effects can be unstable with small samples and non-normality.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Avoid over-interpreting standardized effects if distribution is highly non-normal.
- **Alternative:**
  - Report **median change** and CI via bootstrap if needed.

### B2) Model diagnostics / assumption checks
- **Tests/tools:** Residual plots; QQ-plots; check influential points; assess heteroscedasticity.
- **Purpose:** Validate LMM assumptions.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. OHIP totals may have floor effects; diagnostics guide robust alternatives.
  2. Protects against misleading inference in small samples.
- **Why not appropriate:**
  - Formal normality tests (e.g., Shapiro–Wilk) have low interpretability in small n; should not drive decisions alone.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Don’t use Shapiro–Wilk as a strict gatekeeper for parametric models.
- **Alternative:**
  - Robust SE, transformation, GLMM, or nonparametric/bootstrap.

### B3) Covariate-adjusted mixed model (sensitivity / precision gain)
- **Test/model:** LMM including prespecified covariates (e.g., age, sex, baseline OHIP, tooth type if collected).
- **Purpose:** Improve precision and address chance imbalance.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Increases efficiency in small samples when covariates are prognostic.
  2. Addresses potential residual confounding from convenience sampling and any imperfect randomization implementation.
- **Why not appropriate:**
  - Risk of overfitting with small n and many covariates.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Do not include many predictors relative to sample size; avoid data-driven covariate fishing.
- **Alternative:**
  - Limit to 1–3 prespecified covariates; or use penalized regression (optional).

### B4) Multiple comparisons control (if analyzing OHIP domains or items)
- **Test:** Holm–Bonferroni (family-wise) or Benjamini–Hochberg (FDR).
- **Purpose:** Control false positives if multiple OHIP subscales/items are tested.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. OHIP-14 contains multiple items/domains; item-level testing inflates Type I error.
  2. Encourages clear hierarchy: primary outcome first, secondary controlled.
- **Why not appropriate:**
  - Can reduce power in small samples.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Don’t adjust the primary outcome; adjust only secondary/exploratory families.
- **Alternative:**
  - Pre-specify a small number of secondary endpoints; or use multivariate models.

### B5) Categorical/ordinal responder analysis (clinically interpretable)
- **Test/model:** Define “clinically meaningful improvement” (e.g., ≥X-point reduction) and compare proportions via Fisher’s exact; or logistic regression.
- **Purpose:** Translate score changes into patient-level benefit.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. OHIP change may be easier to interpret as “improved vs not”.
  2. Robust to non-normal distributions.
- **Why not appropriate:**
  - Requires a validated minimal important difference (MID), which is not provided here.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If MID is arbitrary or post hoc.
- **Alternative:**
  - Use distribution-based thresholds (e.g., 0.5 SD) explicitly labeled exploratory.

## CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL (supplementary) analyses

### C1) Generalized Estimating Equations (GEE)
- **Purpose:** Population-averaged effect of group, time, and interaction with robust SE.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Robust to some misspecification of random effects distribution.
  2. Directly estimates average marginal effects.
- **Why not appropriate:**
  - With small n, robust SE can be unreliable; and unequal timepoints across groups complicate correlation structure.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Very small sample with sparse repeated measures and missingness.
- **Alternative:**
  - LMM/GLMM with small-sample corrections (Kenward–Roger).

### C2) Nonparametric longitudinal approach
- **Test/model:** Rank-based longitudinal model (e.g., Brunner–Langer) or aligned-rank transform for interaction.
- **Purpose:** Inference on group×time without normality.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Suitable when OHIP totals are strongly non-normal.
  2. Less sensitive to outliers.
- **Why not appropriate:**
  - Less straightforward effect-size interpretation on original OHIP scale.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If primary goal is estimating mean differences on original scale.
- **Alternative:**
  - Robust LMM or bootstrap CIs.

### C3) Bootstrap confidence intervals
- **Purpose:** Obtain CIs for mean change difference with fewer parametric assumptions.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Small samples and non-normality.
  2. Flexible for derived quantities (e.g., change differences).
- **Why not appropriate:**
  - With very small n, bootstrap may be unstable; needs careful resampling at the **participant level**.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Extremely small samples or heavy missingness.
- **Alternative:**
  - Profile likelihood CIs; robust sandwich estimators.

### C4) Reliability/consistency of OHIP-14 in this sample
- **Test:** Cronbach’s alpha or preferably McDonald’s omega (baseline).
- **Purpose:** Document internal consistency of Arabic OHIP-14 in this trial sample.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Supports validity of summing items into a total score.
  2. Useful if population differs from validation studies.
- **Why not appropriate:**
  - Not an outcome effect test; alpha can be misleading (tau-equivalence assumption).
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If sample size too small for stable reliability estimates.
- **Alternative:**
  - Omega; item-total correlations (descriptive).

## TASK 3: Inappropriate tests (should NOT be used)

1. **Independent (unpaired) t-test comparing post-treatment OHIP between groups without adjusting for baseline**
   - Ignores baseline differences and within-person change; lower power and potentially biased if baseline imbalanced.

2. **Multiple separate t-tests at each timepoint (baseline, extirpation, obturation) without a repeated-measures framework**
   - Inflates Type I error; ignores correlation; does not test the key **group×time** effect.

3. **One-way ANOVA on change scores across more than two “times”**
   - Change scores are not independent across time; also groups have different measurement schedules.

4. **Chi-square tests applied to OHIP-14 total score (treating it as categorical without rationale)**
   - Discards information and depends on arbitrary cutpoints.

5. **Simple linear regression treating repeated observations as independent**
   - Violates independence; underestimates SEs and inflates significance.

6. **MANOVA with many OHIP items/domains as outcomes in this small sample**
   - Requires large sample and multivariate normality; high risk of overfitting and unstable estimates.

## TASK 4: Summary justification of overall approach

The primary scientific question is whether **change in OHRQoL over treatment** differs between **single-visit and multiple-visit RCT**. Because OHRQoL is measured **repeatedly within the same individuals** (and with **unequal numbers of assessments across groups**), a **mixed-effects repeated-measures model with a group×time interaction** is the most appropriate primary analysis: it accounts for within-subject correlation, uses all observed data, and directly estimates the between-group difference in improvement at obturation while allowing sensitivity analyses for non-normality and missingness.
