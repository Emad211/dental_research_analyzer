# CATEGORY A — MANDATORY tests/models

### A1) Pearson Chi-square test (or Fisher’s exact) for unit vs outcome
- **Purpose:** Compare **success/failure proportions** between Endodontic vs Oral Surgery units.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Outcome is **binary** (success/failure) and exposure is **binary** (unit).
  2. Directly answers the primary comparative question as an unadjusted analysis.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - If expected cell counts are small (sparse table), chi-square approximation may be invalid.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When any expected cell count is too low (rule of thumb: <5 in ≥20% of cells), or sample size is very small.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Fisher’s exact test** (exact inference) for sparse tables.

### A2) Effect size estimation for binary outcomes (RR/OR with 95% CI)
- **Purpose:** Quantify the magnitude and direction of differences between units beyond p-values.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Clinical interpretation requires **effect size + CI**.
  2. Facilitates comparison with prior literature and supports equivalence/non-inferiority discussions (even if not designed as such).
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - OR can exaggerate effect when outcomes are common; RR may be preferable but may require specific modeling.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Do not present **only** ORs if the outcome is common without also giving absolute risks.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Use **log-binomial regression** for RR; if it fails to converge, use **Poisson regression with robust SE**.

### A3) Multivariable logistic regression (or GEE/mixed-effects logistic) for adjusted comparison
- **Purpose:** Estimate association of **unit** with success/failure **adjusting** for filling quality and filling material (and any other collected covariates).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Outcome is binary; logistic model is standard for adjustment.
  2. Allows simultaneous evaluation of **unit**, **quality of filling**, and **filling material** as prognostic factors (as implied by the conclusion).
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - If sample size is small with few failures, estimates may be unstable (separation/overfitting).
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When events are too few for the number of predictors (e.g., very low failures), or strong separation exists.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Penalized logistic regression** (Firth) for small-sample/separation.
  - If clustering by patient exists: **GEE logistic** (population-averaged) or **mixed-effects logistic** (random intercept for patient).

# CATEGORY B — RECOMMENDED tests/models

### B1) Baseline comparability: t-test / Mann–Whitney U (continuous) and Chi-square/Fisher (categorical)
- **Purpose:** Describe whether groups differ on baseline characteristics (potential confounding).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Non-randomized comparisons require checking imbalance.
  2. Guides which covariates are important to adjust for.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - “Significance testing baseline” can be misleading; standardized differences are often better.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Do not use baseline p-values as the sole criterion for confounder selection.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Use **standardized mean differences (SMD)**; use **Mann–Whitney** if non-normal/ordinal.

### B2) Interaction testing (effect modification): Unit × Filling quality; Unit × Filling material
- **Purpose:** Determine if the unit’s effect differs by filling quality/material.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Clinically plausible that outcomes depend on technique/material differently across units.
  2. Supports nuanced interpretation (e.g., unit difference only when filling quality is poor).
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Low power; can generate false positives if many interactions tested.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When sample size is insufficient for interaction terms (sparse subgroups).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Pre-specify a small number of interactions; or perform **stratified analyses** with exact methods if sparse.

### B3) Model diagnostics for logistic regression
- **Purpose:** Validate model adequacy and reporting quality.
- **Recommended components:**
  - **Calibration:** Hosmer–Lemeshow (with caution), calibration plot.
  - **Discrimination:** AUC/ROC.
  - **Influence/linearity:** check continuous covariates with splines (if any).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Ensures regression conclusions are credible.
  2. Identifies misspecification/outliers.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Hosmer–Lemeshow is sample-size dependent; can mislead.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Avoid over-interpreting HL p-values; don’t use AUC as “model validity” alone.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Use **bootstrapped calibration**, **Brier score**, and **penalization**.

### B4) Sensitivity analysis for clustering (tooth within patient)
- **Purpose:** Assess robustness if multiple teeth per patient were included.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Dental data often have within-patient correlation.
  2. Ignoring clustering inflates type I error (too-narrow CIs).
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - If only one tooth per patient, clustering correction is unnecessary.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When clustering cannot occur by design.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Use **cluster-robust SE**, **GEE**, or **mixed models**.

# CATEGORY C — OPTIONAL (supplementary insights)

### C1) Time-to-event analysis (Kaplan–Meier + Cox regression) *if follow-up times vary and failures occur over time*
- **Purpose:** Compare **survival/failure over time** between units.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Handles unequal follow-up and censoring.
  2. More informative than binary “ever failed” when follow-up duration differs.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - If outcome is assessed at a fixed timepoint for everyone (e.g., 12 months), survival methods are unnecessary.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When failure time is unknown or only final status is recorded.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - If proportional hazards fails: **restricted mean survival time (RMST)** or stratified Cox.

### C2) Propensity score methods (matching/weighting) *for non-random unit assignment*
- **Purpose:** Reduce confounding due to systematic differences in case-mix between units.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Unit assignment is not randomized; PS can improve comparability.
  2. Provides an alternative to regression adjustment and can be used as sensitivity analysis.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Requires rich covariate data; unmeasured confounding remains.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When sample size is small or overlap/positivity is poor.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Use **doubly robust estimation** or stick to carefully specified multivariable models with sensitivity analyses.

### C3) Agreement between clinical and radiographic outcome (Cohen’s kappa)
- **Purpose:** Quantify concordance between clinical and radiographic success classifications.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. The paper mentions both clinical and radiographic outcomes; they may disagree.
  2. Helps interpret whether both endpoints capture the same construct.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - If outcomes are not measured on the same cases/timepoints or are not categorical.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When one measure is continuous or categories are ordered with weights needed.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Weighted kappa** (ordered categories) or **ICC** (continuous).

## TASK 3 — Tests that should NOT be used (and why)

1. **Paired t-test / Wilcoxon signed-rank**
   - Not appropriate because groups are **independent** (different unit cases), not paired/repeated on same tooth/patient (unless explicitly matched).

2. **One-way ANOVA for the primary outcome**
   - Primary outcome is **binary**; ANOVA assumes continuous normally distributed residuals.

3. **Multiple independent t-tests on proportions**
   - Proportions require binomial methods; t-tests on 0/1 outcomes are poor practice and yield incorrect SEs in small/moderate samples.

4. **Simple linear regression with binary outcome**
   - Can predict outside [0,1] and violates homoscedasticity/normality; logistic/binomial models are more appropriate.

5. **Correlation (Pearson/Spearman) between “unit” and outcome**
   - Correlation is not the correct framework for a binary exposure and binary outcome; contingency-table or regression methods are required.
