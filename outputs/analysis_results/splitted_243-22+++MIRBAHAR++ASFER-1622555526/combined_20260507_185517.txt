- **Why it may not be appropriate:**

   - Primary outcome is **binary**; ANOVA assumes continuous normally distributed residuals.

3. **Multiple independent t-tests on proportions**

5. **Correlation (Pearson/Spearman) between “unit” and outcome**
   - Correlation is not the correct framework for a binary exposure and binary outcome; contingency-table or regression methods are required.

- Purpose: Evaluate reliability of clinical success/failure classification between different examiners.  
- Why appropriate:
  1) Clinical outcomes often involve judgment; assessing agreement is key to credibility.  
  2) Kappa adjusts for agreement expected by chance, improving interpretation beyond raw percentage agreement.  
- Why it may not be appropriate:
  - Requires that at least some cases were rated independently by ≥2 examiners; if not done, cannot be applied.  
- When this should NOT be used:
  - When each case is assessed by only one examiner; or when sample for reliability is extremely small (<20).  
- Alternative:
  - Simple percent agreement (descriptive) if kappa cannot be estimated; or intra-class correlation (ICC) if measuring continuous scores instead of binary categories.

5.2 Radiographic outcome assessment agreement  
- Name: Cohen’s kappa / weighted kappa (if ordinal radiographic scores used).  
- Purpose: Evaluate reliability of radiographic healing assessment.  
- Why appropriate:
  1) Radiographic interpretation can be subjective; kappa is standard for categorical imaging scores.  
  2) Weighted kappa handles ordinal categories (e.g., complete, incomplete, uncertain, failure).  
- Why it may not be appropriate:
  - Same constraints as above; requires multiple raters and sufficient shared cases.  
- When this should NOT be used:
  - If only one radiographic assessor.  
- Alternative:
  - ICC for continuous periapical index scores; or percent agreement.

6. Subgroup analyses  

6.1 Unit-specific analyses by tooth type or filling material  
- Name: Stratified chi-square or Mantel–Haenszel test.  
- Purpose:  
  - Determine whether unit effect differs across strata, e.g., anterior vs posterior teeth or different materials.  
- Why appropriate:
  1) Allows exploration of effect modification (interaction) without fitting overly complex models.  
  2) Useful if the distribution of tooth types or materials is very different between units.  
- Why it may not be appropriate:
  - Multiple subgroup analyses increase type I error risk; may be underpowered.  
- When this should NOT be used:
  - When sample size per stratum is small and results will be highly unstable; or if no pre-specified subgroups exist.  
- Alternative:
  - Include interaction terms in logistic regression (unit × tooth type, unit × material); interpret cautiously.

7. Sensitivity analyses  

7.1 Alternative definitions of success  
- Name: Repeated chi-square / logistic regression with different success definitions (e.g., success = full healing vs success = full + partial healing).  
- Purpose: Examine robustness of findings to different clinically plausible outcome definitions.  
- Why appropriate:
  1) Clinical success criteria can vary; demonstrating consistent results enhances credibility.  
  2) Helps understand if “borderline” categories drive results.  
- Why it may not be appropriate:
  - Requires adequate sample sizes; multiple analyses risk overinterpretation.  
- When this should NOT be used:
  - In very small samples; or when outcome definition is clearly pre-specified and consensus-based.  
- Alternative:
  - Pre-register one primary outcome definition and treat others as purely exploratory.

7.2 Analysis by follow-up duration (if variable)  
- Name: Stratified analysis by follow-up time (e.g., ≤1 year vs >1 year) using chi-square / logistic regression.  
- Purpose: Assess whether unit differences or failure rates depend on length of follow-up.  
- Why appropriate:
  1) Outcome of periapical surgery can evolve over time; early success may fail later.  
  2) Balancing follow-up time across units reduces bias.  
- Why it may not be appropriate:
  - May not be possible if follow-up times are homogeneous or not recorded.  
- When this should NOT be used:
  - When follow-up duration information is missing or unreliable.  
- Alternative:
  - Time-to-event methods (Kaplan–Meier) if exact failure dates known (see below).

8. Time-to-event analysis (if sufficient data available)  

8.1 Survival analysis of tooth/root survival or time to failure  
- Name: Kaplan–Meier survival curves and log-rank test; Cox proportional hazards model.  
- Purpose:  
  - Evaluate time-dependent outcome such as time to failure or time to extraction; compare survival between units.  
- Why appropriate:
  1) If follow-up varies, survival methods correctly account for censoring.  
  2) Cox model can incorporate filling material, quality, etc., as covariates.  
- Why it may not be appropriate:
  - Requires known dates of surgery and failure/censoring; such data may not be available in a retrospective chart review.  
- When this should NOT be used:
  - When only “status at last follow-up” is known without time information; or when sample size is too small with few events.  
- Alternative:
  - Logistic regression on success at a fixed follow-up point; or simple cross-sectional analysis.

CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL TESTS  

9. Effect size estimation and confidence intervals  

9.1 Risk difference, relative risk, odds ratio with CIs  
- Name: Calculation of effect sizes and 95% confidence intervals (e.g., Newcombe method for risk difference).  
- Purpose: Provide magnitude and precision of differences, not just p-values.  
- Why appropriate:
  1) Effect size quantification is more interpretable for clinicians than p-values alone.  
  2) Confidence intervals show uncertainty, crucial for “no significant difference” conclusions.  
- Why it may not be appropriate:
  - None in principle; but in very small samples CIs can be very wide and unstable.  
- When this should NOT be used:
  - Rarely; but be cautious about interpretation when N is tiny.  
- Alternative:
  - Report exact binomial CIs for proportions if necessary.

10. Exploratory modeling of radiographic score as ordinal outcome  
- Name: Ordinal logistic regression (proportional odds model) if radiographic healing is scored on an ordinal scale (e.g., healed, improving, unchanged, worse).  
- Purpose: Model the entire spectrum of radiographic healing, not just binary success/failure.  
- Why appropriate:
  1) Uses more information than dichotomization, potentially more power.  
  2) Clinically relevant if partial healing categories are important.  
- Why it may not be appropriate:
  - Requires assumption of proportional odds; if violated, interpretation becomes complex.  
- When this should NOT be used:
  - If sample size is small, or counts in some categories are low; or if assumption tests indicate strong non-proportionality.  
- Alternative:
  - Collapsing the scale into binary outcome; or using multinomial logistic regression, though more parameter-hungry.

11. Exploratory association between filling material and specific complications  
- Name: Chi-square/Fisher’s exact for association of specific complications (e.g., overfill, extrusion, persistent sinus) with material.  
- Purpose: Explore whether certain materials are associated with specific adverse radiographic or clinical signs.  
- Why appropriate:
  1) May highlight clinically important safety differences beyond success/failure.  
  2) Uses standard contingency methods for binary categorical relationships.  
- Why it may not be appropriate:
  - Multiple testing increases false-positive risk; results may be underpowered.  
- When this should NOT be used:
  - When sample size per complication is very low or complications are rare.  
- Alternative:
  - Descriptive reporting only; or summarizing complications qualitatively.

TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS  

Given the design and outcome structure, the following tests/methods should be avoided:

1. Paired tests (paired t-test, McNemar’s test)  
- Why inappropriate:
  - The study compares independent groups (different patients/teeth in two units). There is no pairwise matching of the same tooth or patient pre- and post-intervention across units.  
- When might they be considered but still wrong:
  - If someone misconstrues “before vs after surgery” as paired between units rather than within a single tooth, they might incorrectly use paired tests across units.

2. Repeated-measures ANOVA / mixed models for longitudinal repeated outcomes  
- Why inappropriate:
  - Although there may be follow-up over time, the published conclusion is purely about success/failure at follow-up; repeated time points or continuous repeated measures are not described.  
- When not to use:
  - When only a single outcome assessment per tooth is available or retained for analysis.

3. One-way or two-way ANOVA for primary success comparison  
- Why inappropriate:
  - Primary outcome is binary (success/failure), not continuous. ANOVA assumes continuous, roughly normal outcomes.  
- When not to use:
  - For any analysis where the dependent variable is dichotomous.

4. Pearson correlation for main binary outcomes  
- Why inappropriate:
  - Correlation is not the primary method for binary outcomes; association between binary variables is better measured by odds ratio/risk ratio or logistic regression.  
- When not to use:
  - For assessing association between unit and success, or between filling quality/material and success.

5. Parametric tests without checking assumptions  
- Example: Using standard t-test on highly skewed or small-sample continuous variables (if any).  
- Why inappropriate:
  - Violated assumptions compromise validity; robust or non-parametric alternatives should be used.

6. Complex machine-learning models (e.g., random forests, SVM) for this small comparative study  
- Why inappropriate:
  - Overfitting, lack of interpretability, and likely inadequate sample size make them unsuitable and unjustified for a straightforward clinical comparison.

TASK 4: SUMMARY – OVERALL ANALYTICAL APPROACH  

- The study’s main question is simple and comparative: Are periapical surgery outcomes (clinical and radiographic success/failure) different between endodontic and oral surgery units in a teaching hospital?  
- The design is observational with two independent groups and binary outcomes. The backbone of the analysis is thus:
  - Robust descriptive statistics.
  - 2×2 comparisons via chi-square/Fisher’s exact tests for primary outcomes (clinical, radiographic, combined success) between units.
  - Assessment of key predictors (filling quality, filling material) using contingency methods and multivariable logistic regression to control for confounding.
- Reliability of outcome assessment (kappa) and sensitivity analyses for outcome definitions and follow-up duration improve methodological rigor.
- If time-to-failure information is available, survival analysis can add depth; if not, cross-sectional binary analyses suffice.
- The recommended plan balances:
  - Simplicity and interpretability (critical for clinical readers),
  - Appropriate handling of binary outcomes and confounding,
  - Awareness of sample size and assumption constraints.

This structure will allow clear, statistically sound conclusions about whether there is a meaningful difference between units and how filling quality/material influence the success of periapical surgery.
