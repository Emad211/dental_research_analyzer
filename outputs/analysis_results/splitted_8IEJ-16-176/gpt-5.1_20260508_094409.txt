A1. Descriptive statistics

When NOT to use:

Alternative if assumptions violated:
- Use medians/IQR and nonparametric summaries when distributions are skewed; graphical methods (histograms, boxplots).

A2. Kaplan–Meier (KM) survival analysis

Test / Method:
- Kaplan–Meier survival curves with log-rank tests.
  - Primary comparison: DRC vs FCC.
  - Optionally by other key factors (e.g., ≥1:1:1 vs <1:1:1 dentin ratio).

Purpose:
- Estimate and visualize survival functions (time to unrestorable fracture) for DRC and FCC.  
- Obtain cumulative survival probabilities at clinically relevant time points (e.g., 1, 3, 5, 10 years).
- Use log-rank test to compare survival curves.

Why appropriate:
1) Outcome is time-to-event (unrestorable fracture), with censoring; KM is standard nonparametric estimator for such data.  
2) Groups are defined by restoration type at baseline (exposure), with no time-varying group assignment; KM/log-rank is valid for between-group comparisons under non-informative censoring.  

Why it may not be appropriate:
- KM assumes independent censoring and does not adjust for covariates; if groups differ substantially in prognostic factors, results are confounded.

When NOT to use:
- If event times are interval-censored or only known as “occurred sometime between visits” without approximated times (then use interval-censored methods).  
- If there is heavy informative censoring (e.g., many extractions for reasons related to fracture risk) without appropriate modeling.

Alternative if assumptions violated:
- Use Cox proportional hazards model (possibly with time-dependent covariates or stratification) for adjusted comparison.  
- Use parametric survival models (Weibull, log-logistic) if proportional hazards fails and parametric form is reasonable.

A3. Log-rank test for group comparison

Test / Method:
- Log-rank test comparing survival distributions of DRC vs FCC.

Purpose:
- Test null hypothesis of no difference in survival experience between DRC and FCC.

Why appropriate:
1) Nonparametric test designed for comparing KM survival curves between groups.  
2) Handles right-censored data and does not require distributional assumptions about survival times beyond equal hazards under H0.

Why it may not be appropriate:
- Assumes proportional hazards between groups over time; if hazards cross substantially, log-rank may miss differences or be misleading.

When NOT to use:
- When sample size in one group is very small with few events (e.g., 1 event in 106 FCC teeth, as in their data); p-values become unstable and test has low power.  
- When proportional hazards strongly violated and time-varying effects are obvious.

Alternative if assumptions violated:
- Use Wilcoxon (Breslow) test if early differences are of main interest.  
- Use time-varying Cox model or restricted mean survival time (RMST) comparisons.

A4. Cox proportional hazards regression (univariable and multivariable)

Test / Method:
- Cox proportional hazards (PH) model.

  Model 1 (unadjusted):  
  - Outcome: time to unrestorable fracture.  
  - Predictor: restoration type (DRC=ref, FCC).

  Model 2 (adjusted):  
  - Include clinically relevant covariates / potential confounders:
    - Age, gender.
    - Tooth type, location.
    - Remaining cervical walls (categorical).
    - Number of restorative surfaces (1–3 vs 4–5).
    - Cervical dentin ratio (<1:1:1 vs ≥1:1:1).
    - Posterior support (0/1/2).
    - Adjacent teeth (0/1/2).
    - Parafunction (yes/no).
    - Post type, post length vs bone level (for FCC, but also consider full data with an appropriate coding).

Purpose:
- Estimate hazard ratios (HR) for unrestorable fracture associated with DRC vs FCC.  
- Assess independent effects of predisposing factors while adjusting for confounders.

Why appropriate:
1) Standard multivariable method for time-to-event analysis in observational cohorts.  
2) Handles censored data and allows inclusion of multiple covariates, enabling adjustment and identification of independent predictors.

Why it may not be appropriate:
- Assumes proportional hazards for each covariate; if violated, hazard ratios are not constant over time.  
- With very low number of events (e.g., in FCC; 1 failure), model may be unstable, particularly for multi-parameter models.

When NOT to use:
- When the number of events is too small relative to number of predictors (rule-of-thumb: ~≥10 events per parameter; here events may be <25 total).  
- When PH assumption is clearly violated and cannot be remedied with stratification or time-varying terms.

Alternative if assumptions violated:
- Use stratified Cox model (e.g., stratify by variables violating PH).  
- Use parametric survival models (e.g., Weibull with AFT formulation).  
- Use penalized Cox regression (Firth, ridge, LASSO) under small-event conditions.  
- Use cause-specific modeling of the few predictors deemed most crucial; keep model parsimonious.

A5. Chi-square or Fisher’s exact tests for baseline comparability

Test / Method:
- Chi-square test of independence or Fisher’s exact test (for small expected counts).

Purpose:
- Compare distributions of categorical covariates between DRC and FCC groups (e.g., tooth type, location, cervical walls, dentin ratio, parafunction).

Why appropriate:
1) Retrospective allocation (not randomized); assessing baseline imbalances is important to interpret confounding in the Cox models.  
2) Helps demonstrate whether restoration type is preferentially chosen for more compromised teeth.

Why it may not be appropriate:
- Provides p-values but does not adjust for multiple covariates or account for clustering; significant differences do not automatically imply causation.

When NOT to use:
- When expected cell counts are very small (<5) in more than 20% of cells; Chi-square then invalid.

Alternative if assumptions violated:
- Use Fisher’s exact test for 2×2 or small tables.  
- Use logistic regression with restoration type as outcome and covariates as predictors to model selection mechanisms more formally.

A6. Assessment and reporting of follow-up time

Method:
- Summarize follow-up time distribution (median, IQR; min–max).  
- Summarize number of events and censoring by group.

Purpose:
- Essential to interpret survival rates and hazard ratios; ensures that group differences are not due to strongly different follow-up durations.

Why appropriate:
1) Provides context for KM and Cox estimates; differences in censoring patterns can bias apparent survival.  
2) Needed to understand event rate and justify model complexity.

Why it may not be appropriate:
- Not a hypothesis test; interpretive only.

When NOT to use:
- Always necessary.

Alternative:
- Graphical representation of number at risk over time (already present under KM).

TASK 3 – INAPPROPRIATE TESTS AND WHY

1. Student’s t-test / ANOVA comparing mean “survival time” without censoring
- Why inappropriate:
  - Survival time is right-censored; standard t-test/ANOVA assumes complete data and normal distribution of outcomes.  
  - Censoring will bias mean comparisons and invalidate inference.
- Proper alternative:
  - KM survival analysis and log-rank test; Cox PH model; RMST.

2. Simple chi-square test comparing proportion “fractured” vs “not fractured” without accounting for follow-up time
- Why inappropriate:
  - Ignores different follow-up durations between teeth; teeth followed longer naturally accumulate more failures.  
  - Risk estimates biased if DRC vs FCC have different follow-up distributions or censoring patterns.
- Proper alternative:
  - KM/log-rank or Cox PH using time-to-event data.

3. Paired tests (paired t-test, McNemar’s test) treating teeth within same patient as paired observations
- Why inappropriate:
  - Teeth are not necessarily paired by design; many patients may have only one tooth.  
  - Pairing assumptions not met; mis-specified data structure.
- Proper alternative:
  - Use robust SEs or frailty models if clustering by patient is to be addressed.

4. Repeated measures ANOVA / linear mixed models for survival time
- Why inappropriate:
  - Outcome is time-to-event, not repeated continuous measures over time; structure and distribution assumptions violated.
- Proper alternative:
  - Survival analysis methods (KM, Cox, parametric survival).

5. Pearson correlation between continuous predictors and “survival time”
- Why inappropriate:
  - Censored outcome; standard correlation mis-handles censored observations and is biased.
- Proper alternative:
  - Use survival regression (Cox or parametric) including continuous predictors appropriately.

6. Non-time-aware logistic regression as the primary method
- Why inappropriate:
  - If used as primary method, it ignores differential follow-up; cannot distinguish between early and late failures.  
  - Odds ratios may misrepresent relative hazard over time.
- Proper alternative:
  - Use Cox PH for primary time-to-event analysis; logistic can be reported only as a supplementary sensitivity analysis.
