### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS

A1. Descriptive Statistics

Test/procedure:

- Tabulation and descriptive summaries (no formal “test”):
  - For each group: frequencies and percentages for:
    - Frankl categories (4 levels).
    - Venham scores (0–5).
    - Wong-Baker scores (0–5).
  - For covariates: age (mean ± SD or median [IQR]), sex distribution, tooth-type distribution if available.

Purpose:

- Characterize each group.
- Check balance after randomization.
- Provide absolute numbers and distributions for interpretation.

Why appropriate:

1) Essential in RCTs to show baseline characteristics and distributions of outcomes.
2) Ordinal outcomes summarized via frequency tables and medians allow understanding without assuming normality.

Why it may not be appropriate:

- Not a hypothesis test; alone, it does not provide evidence of statistical significance.

When not to use:

- Never; descriptive statistics are always required.

Alternative if assumptions violated:

- Not applicable; purely descriptive.

---

A2. Comparison of Frankl Behavior Categories Between Groups

Primary test:

- Chi-square test of independence (manual vs rotary by 4-category Frankl scale) as the main categorical comparison.
- Additionally: Ordinal association test such as Cochran–Armitage trend test or nonparametric Mann–Whitney U (see A3) treating Frankl scores as 1–4.

Purpose:

- Test whether distribution of behavior (definitely negative → definitely positive) differs between manual and rotary instrumentation.

Why appropriate (Chi-square):

1) Outcome is categorical; groups are independent and mutually exclusive.
2) Sample size is moderate (44 per group), suitable for Chi-square contingency analysis.

Why it may not be appropriate:

- If some cells (e.g., “definitely negative”) have very small counts (<5), Chi-square approximation may be inaccurate.

When NOT to use:

- When expected cell counts in more than ~20% of cells are <5 or any expected count <1.

Alternative if assumptions are violated:

- Fisher’s exact test for a 2×4 table (exact p-value).
- Or collapse categories into fewer levels (e.g., positive vs negative) and then use Chi-square or Fisher’s test.

---

A3. Comparison of Frankl Scores Using a Nonparametric Test

Test:

- Mann–Whitney U test (a.k.a. Wilcoxon rank-sum test) treating Frankl scale as ordered 1–4.

Purpose:

- Compare central tendency of behavior (as ordinal scores) between manual and rotary groups, considering ordering.

Why appropriate:

1) Frankl is an ordinal scale; Mann–Whitney uses ranking and does not assume normality.
2) Two independent groups with a single post-intervention measure each; ideal setting for this test.

Why it may not be appropriate:

- If there is a high proportion of tied values and distributions are very skewed in different shapes, interpretation of the Mann–Whitney “shift in distribution” may be less straightforward.

When NOT to use:

- For nominal (unordered) categories; or if sample size in one group is extremely small (not the case here).

Alternative if assumptions are violated:

- Ordinal logistic regression (proportional odds model) with group as predictor, which can handle ties and adjust for covariates (see B3).
- Permutation test on ranks as a robust alternative.

---

A4. Comparison of Venham Anxiety Scores Between Groups

Test:

- Mann–Whitney U test.

Purpose:

- Compare anxiety levels (0–5) between manual and rotary groups.

Why appropriate:

1) Venham scale is ordinal (0–5); Mann–Whitney is suitable for comparing two independent groups on ordinal outcomes.
2) Does not assume normal distribution or equal variances, appropriate for small ordinal scales.

Why it may not be appropriate:

- Similar to A3: different distribution shapes and many ties may complicate interpretation.

When NOT to use:

- If Venham were treated as categorical without clear ordering (not the case here).
- For paired/repeated measurements (not present here).

Alternative if assumptions are violated:

- Ordinal logistic regression with group as predictor (see B3).
- Exact Mann–Whitney or permutation test if sample is small or distribution is extremely nonstandard.

---

A5. Comparison of Wong-Baker Pain Scores Between Groups

Test:

- Mann–Whitney U test.

Purpose:

- Compare pain intensity (ordinal faces scale) between manual and rotary groups.

Why appropriate:

1) Wong-Baker FACES is inherently ordinal; Mann–Whitney respects ranking and avoids normality assumptions.
2) Two independent groups; outcomes measured once per child.

Why it may not be appropriate:

- Interpretation of a numeric “median difference” on a faces scale may not be intuitive to clinicians, though the test is statistically valid.

When NOT to use:

- For data that are binary/categorical without order.
- For repeated time points (not present).

Alternative if assumptions are violated:

- Ordinal logistic regression with group as predictor.
- Nonparametric permutation test on ranks.

A6. Familywise Considerations / Multiple Primary Outcomes

Test/procedure:

- Control for multiple comparisons (behavior, anxiety, pain) using one of:
  - Bonferroni correction (α = 0.05/3 ≈ 0.0167 per test).
  - Or clearly declare one primary endpoint and two key secondary endpoints.

Purpose:

- Maintain overall Type I error given multiple primary outcomes.

Why appropriate:

1) Behavior, anxiety, and pain are related but distinct, and the study conclusions emphasize all three; formal multiplicity control improves rigor.
2) Randomization tests each separately; controlling error avoids overstating significance.

Why it may not be appropriate:

- Bonferroni can be overly conservative and reduce power, especially with correlated outcomes.

When NOT to use:

- If the study protocol pre-specified one primary and others as exploratory; in that case, only the primary needs strict α=0.05.

Alternative if assumptions are violated:

- Holm–Bonferroni procedure (less conservative).
- Multivariate model (e.g., multivariate ordinal model), but this is complex and rarely necessary here.

### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED TESTS (ENHANCE RIGOR)

B1. Baseline Comparability Tests

Tests:

- Age: t-test or Mann–Whitney U (depending on distribution) to check age balance.
- Sex: Chi-square or Fisher’s exact test.
- Tooth type (if available): Chi-square.

Purpose:

- Document that randomization produced comparable groups in important characteristics.

Why appropriate:

1) Common practice in RCT reporting; helps identify potential confounding imbalances (even if random).
2) Simple tests; clarify whether any covariates should be adjusted for in further analyses.

Why it may not be appropriate:

- Conceptually, significance testing of baseline variables in properly randomized trials is not strictly necessary; imbalance is due to chance, not systematic bias.

When NOT to use:

- Not needed if sample size is very large and baseline balancing is guaranteed; or if the trial protocol discourages significance testing of baseline covariates, favoring descriptive comparison only.

Alternative if assumptions are violated:

- Use nonparametric tests or exact tests if sample sizes/cell counts are small.

B2. Effect Size Estimation

Measures:

  - 95% confidence intervals for effect sizes.

Purpose:

- Quantify magnitude of differences beyond p-values.

Why appropriate:

1) Clinically interpretable: “X% more children had positive behavior with rotary.”
2) Helps assess clinical relevance even when p-values are significant or borderline.

Why it may not be appropriate:

When NOT to use:

B3. Ordinal Logistic Regression (Proportional Odds Model)

Tests:

- For each ordinal outcome (Frankl, Venham, Wong-Baker), fit a proportional odds model:
  - Outcome: ordinal scale.
  - Predictor: group (rotary vs manual).
  - Optional covariates: age, sex, tooth type, baseline behavior if available.

Purpose:

- Estimate adjusted odds ratios for better behavior, lower anxiety, and lower pain with rotary vs manual.
- Check robustness of simple two-sample analyses.

Why appropriate:

1) Ordinal platforms: properly captures ordering without assuming equal intervals or normality.
2) Allows adjustment for covariates, helpful if any baseline imbalance (e.g., age difference) is discovered.

Why it may not be appropriate:

- Proportional odds assumption (same odds ratio across all cut-points) may be violated.

When NOT to use:

- When the proportional odds assumption is clearly violated (e.g., goodness-of-fit tests show poor fit).
- When sample size is insufficient relative to the number of covariates (rule-of-thumb: at least ~10 events per parameter for each cumulative split; here likely fine with 1–2 covariates).

Alternative if assumptions are violated:

- Partial proportional odds model (relax assumption for some covariates).
- Multinomial logistic regression treating outcome as unordered (loses ordering information).
- Nonparametric models (Mann–Whitney plus stratification).

Purpose:

Why it may not be appropriate:

When NOT to use:

- When there is no clinically meaningful threshold; or when sample size is small and dichotomization leads to sparse categories.

Alternative if assumptions are violated:

- Use ordered categories in ordinal logistic regression instead of dichotomization.

TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS
----------------------------------

Tests that should NOT be used and why:

1) Independent-samples t-test for Venham or Wong-Baker scores (treating them as continuous)

- Why inappropriate:
  - These scales are ordinal with few categories; assuming interval-scale and normal distribution is questionable.
  - Differences between adjacent categories (e.g., 0 to 1 vs 4 to 5) are not guaranteed equal.
- When not to use:
  - When sample size is moderate and appropriate ordinal/nonparametric methods are available.
- Appropriate alternative:
  - Mann–Whitney U test or ordinal logistic regression.

2) One-way ANOVA for Frankl, Venham, or Wong-Baker scores

- Why inappropriate:
  - Same reasons as above; ordinal outcomes with non-normal distribution and limited categories.
  - ANOVA assumes normally distributed residuals and homoscedasticity.
- Alternative:
  - Mann–Whitney U or ordinal logistic regression.

3) Paired t-test or Wilcoxon signed-rank test for between-group comparisons

- Why inappropriate:
  - Design is parallel groups; observations are independent, not paired.
- Alternative:
  - Independent-sample tests (Mann–Whitney U, Chi-square).

4) Repeated-measures ANOVA

- Why inappropriate:
  - Only one measurement time point per outcome; no repeated measures over time are described.
- Alternative:
  - Simple between-group tests as outlined in Category A.

5) Pearson correlation for scale associations

- Why inappropriate:
  - Venham, Wong-Baker, and Frankl are ordinal; Pearson assumes interval/ratio-scaled variables and linear relationships.
- Alternative:
  - Spearman or Kendall rank correlation.

6) Linear regression treating ordinal outcomes as continuous dependent variables

- Why inappropriate:
  - Ordinal outcomes with few points violate assumptions of linearity and normal residuals.
  - Interpretation of regression coefficients is less meaningful.
- Alternative:
  - Ordinal logistic regression (proportional odds model).

7) McNemar test

- Why inappropriate:
  - McNemar is for paired dichotomous data (pre/post in same subjects or matched pairs). Here we have independent groups and multi-category outcomes.
- Alternative:
  - Chi-square or Fisher’s exact tests for between-group comparisons.

8) Survival analysis (Kaplan–Meier, Cox regression)

- Why inappropriate:
  - No time-to-event outcomes; all outcomes are measured during a single visit.
- Alternative:
  - Not applicable; use cross-sectional analyses.
