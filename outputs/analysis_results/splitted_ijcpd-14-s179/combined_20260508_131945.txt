## TASK 2: Statistical analysis plan

### General principles (apply to all analyses)
- **Analysis set:** Intention-to-treat (ITT) as primary; per-protocol as sensitivity if protocol deviations exist.
- **Descriptives:**
  - Continuous (if any): mean±SD (and median/IQR if skewed)
  - Ordinal: median (IQR) and full category distributions
- **Alpha:** 0.05 (two-sided).
- **Multiplicity:** 3 outcomes → control family-wise error (e.g., Holm) or pre-specify one primary endpoint and treat others as secondary.
- **Effect sizes:** report alongside p-values
  - Ordinal comparisons: **rank-biserial r**, **Cliff’s delta**, **odds ratios** (for ordinal models)
  - Categorical distributions: **risk differences**, **relative risks**, or **common OR**
- **Missing data:** quantify; if >5–10%, consider multiple imputation (ordinal models) and do sensitivity analyses.
- **Baseline comparability:** describe and test cautiously (randomization should balance on average).

### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY tests

#### A1) Mann–Whitney U test (Wilcoxon rank-sum)
- **Purpose:** Compare **distributions/central tendency** of ordinal outcomes between manual vs rotary.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Outcomes (Venham 0–5; pain faces; Frankl as ordered) are **ordinal**, not interval-scaled.
  2. Does **not require normality**; robust for skewed ordinal scores.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - If the goal is to compare **category probabilities** (e.g., “negative vs positive”) rather than overall rank distribution, MWU may be less interpretable clinically.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When there is **substantial tie inflation** (very common with small ordinal scales), MWU can lose interpretability/power; also not ideal if distributions differ mainly in shape (not location).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated / better fit:**
  - **Ordinal logistic regression (proportional odds)** for an effect estimate (common OR), or
  - **Permutation version** of MWU if small sample/exact inference desired.

*(Apply separately to Venham score and Wong-Baker pain score; for Frankl either treat as ordinal ranks or use A2 below depending on the analysis choice.)*

#### A2) Chi-square test of independence (or Fisher’s exact test)
- **Purpose:** Compare **proportions across Frankl categories** (4-level) between groups (and/or anxiety/pain categorized).
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Frankl is naturally **categorical/ordinal** and often analyzed via **frequency tables**.
  2. Directly addresses clinically meaningful differences in **behavior category distribution**.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - If expected cell counts are small (likely with 4 categories and n=44/group), chi-square approximation may be invalid.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When **>20% of cells have expected count <5** (rule of thumb), or any expected count <1.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Fisher’s exact test** (r×c) or **Monte Carlo exact chi-square**.
  - If you want to preserve ordering and estimate effect: **ordinal logistic regression**.

---

### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED tests (to improve rigor)

#### B1) Ordinal logistic regression (proportional odds model)
- **Purpose:** Estimate the **treatment effect** of rotary vs manual on each ordinal outcome as an **odds ratio** (shift toward better behavior/lower anxiety/lower pain), optionally adjusting for covariates.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Outcomes are **ordered**; model uses ordering efficiently and yields interpretable **common OR**.
  2. Allows adjustment for **important prognostic factors** (e.g., age, tooth type if recorded) and provides **CIs**, improving clinical interpretability beyond p-values.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - The **proportional odds assumption** may be violated (effect not constant across thresholds).
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When sample size is too small relative to outcome categories leading to **sparse cells/separation**, or when proportional odds is strongly violated and cannot be remedied.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Partial proportional odds model** or **generalized ordered logit**.
  - **Multinomial logistic regression** (drops ordering but flexible).
  - Collapse categories (pre-specified) + binary logistic regression.

*(Recommended as the primary inferential framework if the authors want effect sizes and covariate adjustment.)*

#### B2) Fisher’s exact / exact trend test (Cochran–Armitage trend)
- **Purpose:** For ordered categorical outcomes (esp. Frankl), test for a **monotonic shift** toward more positive categories with rotary.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Leverages **ordering** (more power than nominal chi-square when shift is directional).
  2. Works well when distributions are sparse; exact versions mitigate small expected counts.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - If the effect is not monotonic (e.g., changes only in one category), trend tests can miss differences.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When category ordering is ambiguous or when the alternative is not a monotone trend.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **r×c Fisher’s exact**, **ordinal logistic regression**, or **MWU**.

#### B3) Inter-rater reliability / measurement bias assessment (if feasible)
- **Test:** **Weighted Cohen’s kappa** (or intraclass correlation for ordinal treated as numeric—less ideal)
- **Purpose:** Quantify reliability of behavior/anxiety scoring if a second blinded assessor scores a subset from video/audio.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Outcomes (Frankl, Venham) are **ordinal observer ratings**; reliability is crucial.
  2. Addresses a key threat to validity: the operator was also the assessor.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - Not possible if only one rater and no recordings/second ratings exist.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When ratings are not independent or when categories are extremely imbalanced (kappa paradox).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Gwet’s AC2** (more stable with prevalence imbalance).

#### B4) Multiple comparison control (Holm–Bonferroni)
- **Purpose:** Control family-wise error across the 3 outcomes (or across multiple tests per outcome).
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Prevents inflation of Type I error due to multiple endpoints.
  2. Holm is more powerful than classic Bonferroni and easy to report.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - If one endpoint is clearly pre-specified as **primary**, multiplicity control across secondary outcomes may be unnecessary (but still defensible).
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When analyses are strictly exploratory and clearly labeled as such (still report unadjusted + caution).
- **Alternative:**
  - **Benjamini–Hochberg FDR** control (if many exploratory comparisons).

### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL tests (supplementary insight)

#### C1) Mediation-style exploration: does anxiety/pain relate to behavior?
- **Test/model:** Ordinal regression models assessing association of anxiety and pain with Frankl, including group as predictor (exploratory).
- **Purpose:** Explore whether **pain/anxiety** may explain behavior differences.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Clinically plausible pathways (instrumentation → pain/anxiety → behavior).
  2. Helps interpret findings beyond group differences.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - Temporality and measurement timing are limited; strong causal mediation claims are not justified.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When sample size is insufficient for multi-parameter models or when this was not pre-specified.
- **Alternative:**
  - Simple **Spearman correlation** between scales + stratified summaries.

#### C2) Sensitivity analyses with dichotomized outcomes (clinically interpretable)
- **Test:** Risk ratio / chi-square or Fisher after collapsing:
  - Frankl: positive (definitely positive/positive) vs negative (negative/definitely negative)
  - Venham: low (0–1) vs higher (≥2) (threshold must be justified)
- **Purpose:** Provide easily communicated clinical contrasts.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Clinicians often reason in **binary** outcomes (cooperative vs not).
  2. Allows reporting **RR/RD** and NNT-like measures.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - Dichotomization **loses information** and can reduce power / introduce threshold artifacts.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - As the only analysis of ordinal outcomes.
- **Alternative:**
  - Keep ordinal via **ordinal logistic regression** and report predicted probabilities.

#### C3) Nonparametric effect size CIs via bootstrap
- **Test:** Bootstrap CI for median difference / Cliff’s delta
- **Purpose:** Provide robust uncertainty quantification without normality assumptions.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Outcomes are ordinal with ties; bootstrap can be robust.
  2. Complements rank tests with **confidence intervals**.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - With heavy ties and small n, bootstrap distributions may be unstable.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Very small samples or extreme discreteness.
- **Alternative:**
  - Exact methods; model-based ordinal regression CIs.

## TASK 3: Inappropriate tests (should NOT be used)

1. **Independent-samples t-test** (for Frankl/Venham/Wong-Baker raw scores)
   - Why inappropriate: outcomes are **ordinal**, bounded, and likely non-normal with many ties; mean differences may be misleading.

2. **One-way ANOVA / repeated-measures ANOVA**
   - Why inappropriate: only **two independent groups** and outcomes are ordinal; no repeated measures described.

3. **Pearson correlation** between ordinal scales
   - Why inappropriate: assumes interval scale and linear relationship; use **Spearman** or ordinal association measures.

4. **Linear regression treating ordinal outcomes as continuous** (without strong justification)
   - Why inappropriate: violates distributional assumptions; predicted values can fall outside scale; variance non-constant.

5. **Paired tests (paired t-test, Wilcoxon signed-rank)**
   - Why inappropriate: groups are **independent** (not split-mouth / not crossover).

6. **Kaplan–Meier / Cox regression**
   - Why inappropriate: no time-to-event outcome.
