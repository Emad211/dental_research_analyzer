### TASK 1: INFORMATION EXTRACTION

*   **Study Design:** Retrospective cohort study (with a cross-sectional survey component for the pandemic cohort).
*   **Objectives:** 
    1. To evaluate the consequences of emergency remote teaching modalities on student learning satisfaction.
    2. To compare the academic performance of students taught via emergency remote teaching with those taught face-to-face in the pre-pandemic year.
*   **Study Groups:**
    *   *Group 1 (Pandemic Cohort):* 39 undergraduate 3rd-year dental students (2019–2020 academic year) exposed to emergency remote teaching (4 face-to-face, 11 online-synchronized, 4 online-asynchronized lectures).
    *   *Group 2 (Pre-pandemic Cohort):* 37 undergraduate dental students from the previous academic year exposed entirely to conventional face-to-face lectures.
*   **Sample Size:** Total N = 76 (39 in Group 1; 37 in Group 2).
*   **Variables & Measurements:**
    *   *Outcome 1: Academic Performance (Continuous/Ratio).* Measured via overall scores from two summative multiple-choice examinations (ExamSoft platform).
    *   *Outcome 2: Student Satisfaction (Ordinal/Categorical).* Measured via an online questionnaire (Pandemic cohort only). Rated on a 3-point scale (Satisfied, Neutral, Unsatisfied).
    *   *Outcome 3: Lecture Delivery Preference (Nominal).* Measured via a single multiple-choice question selecting the best method among the three modalities.
*   **Data Collection Methods:** 
    *   Retrospective extraction of exam scores from the ExamSoft platform.
    *   Online questionnaire distributed via email prior to summative assessments.

### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS
*Tests without which the analysis would be incomplete.*

**1. Independent Samples t-test**
*   **Purpose:** To compare the mean summative examination scores between the pandemic cohort and the pre-pandemic cohort.
*   **Why appropriate:** 1) The dependent variable (exam scores) is continuous. 2) The comparison involves two distinct, independent groups of students.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It assumes the exam scores are normally distributed and that the variances between the two groups are equal.
*   **When this test should NOT be used:** When the data is heavily skewed, contains extreme outliers, or if the two groups are not independent (e.g., same students measured twice).
*   **Alternative if assumptions are violated:** Mann-Whitney U test.

**2. Friedman Test**
*   **Purpose:** To compare student satisfaction ratings across the three different teaching methods (face-to-face, online-synchronized, online-asynchronized) within the pandemic cohort.
*   **Why appropriate:** 1) The data is ordinal (Satisfied, Neutral, Unsatisfied). 2) The samples are dependent (the *same* 39 students rated all three methods).
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It only tells you that a difference exists somewhere among the three methods, not exactly which methods differ from one another.
*   **When this test should NOT be used:** When comparing independent groups (different students rating different methods).
*   **Alternative if assumptions are violated:** N/A (This is the robust non-parametric standard for this data structure. If data were continuous and normal, Repeated Measures ANOVA would be used).

**3. Descriptive Statistics (Frequencies, Percentages, Means, Standard Deviations)**
*   **Purpose:** To summarize the demographic/baseline characteristics, exam scores, and survey responses.
*   **Why appropriate:** 1) Provides a baseline understanding of the data spread. 2) Essential for reporting categorical survey data (e.g., % of students who preferred synchronized lectures).
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** Descriptive statistics do not test hypotheses or determine statistical significance.
*   **When this test should NOT be used:** As a replacement for inferential statistics when comparing groups.
*   **Alternative:** N/A (Mandatory first step in all analyses).

#### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED TESTS
*Tests that enhance the quality and rigor of the analysis.*

**1. Shapiro-Wilk Test**
*   **Purpose:** To assess the normality of the summative examination scores in both cohorts.
*   **Why appropriate:** 1) The sample sizes are relatively small (< 50 per group), making Shapiro-Wilk highly accurate. 2) It dictates whether parametric or non-parametric tests should be used for performance comparison.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** With very small samples, it may lack the power to detect non-normality; with very large samples, it is overly sensitive.
*   **When this test should NOT be used:** On categorical or ordinal data (e.g., the satisfaction survey results).
*   **Alternative if assumptions are violated:** Visual inspection using Q-Q plots and histograms.

**2. Wilcoxon Signed-Rank Test (with Bonferroni Correction)**
*   **Purpose:** To act as a post-hoc test following a significant Friedman test to identify exactly *which* teaching methods significantly differed in satisfaction.
*   **Why appropriate:** 1) It accounts for the paired nature of the data (same students). 2) It is designed for ordinal data.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** Conducting multiple pairwise comparisons increases the risk of Type I errors (false positives) if p-values are not adjusted.
*   **When this test should NOT be used:** Without a p-value adjustment (like Bonferroni) when doing multiple comparisons, or on independent samples.
*   **Alternative if assumptions are violated:** Sign test.

**3. Cronbach’s Alpha**
*   **Purpose:** To measure the internal consistency (reliability) of the 18-item satisfaction questionnaire.
*   **Why appropriate:** 1) The questionnaire is a custom instrument used to measure an underlying construct (satisfaction). 2) It ensures the 18 items are reliably measuring the same concept.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It assumes unidimensionality; if the 18 questions cover vastly different constructs (e.g., internet issues vs. cognitive comprehension), the alpha might be artificially low.
*   **When this test should NOT be used:** On single-item questions (like the overall preference question).
*   **Alternative if assumptions are violated:** McDonald’s Omega.

#### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL TESTS
*Tests that provide supplementary insights.*

**1. Spearman’s Rank-Order Correlation**
*   **Purpose:** To determine if there is a relationship between a student's overall satisfaction level (ordinal) and their summative exam score (continuous) within the pandemic cohort.
*   **Why appropriate:** 1) It can handle the mix of continuous and ordinal data. 2) It does not assume a linear relationship, only a monotonic one.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** Correlation does not equal causation; a positive correlation wouldn't prove that high satisfaction *caused* high grades.
*   **When this test should NOT be used:** If both variables are nominal/categorical.
*   **Alternative if assumptions are violated:** Kendall’s Tau.

### TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS

*   **Paired Samples t-test (for exam scores):** Should NOT be used because the pandemic cohort and pre-pandemic cohort consist of completely different students. The groups are independent, not paired.
*   **Repeated Measures ANOVA (for satisfaction):** Should NOT be used because the satisfaction data is measured on a 3-point ordinal scale (Satisfied, Neutral, Unsatisfied). ANOVA requires continuous, normally distributed dependent variables.
*   **Pearson Correlation (for satisfaction vs. scores):** Should NOT be used because Pearson requires both variables to be continuous and normally distributed. Satisfaction is an ordinal variable.
*   **Chi-Square Test of Independence (for comparing satisfaction across the 3 teaching methods):** Should NOT be used because the observations are not independent. The *same* 39 students rated all three methods, violating the core assumption of the Chi-Square test.

### TASK 4: SUMMARY

The analytical approach for this study must be bifurcated due to the distinct nature of the two primary objectives. 

For the **academic performance objective**, a *between-subjects* approach is required. Because we are comparing two independent groups (pandemic vs. pre-pandemic cohorts) on a continuous variable (exam scores), parametric testing (Independent t-test) is the gold standard, provided the data passes normality checks (Shapiro-Wilk). 

For the **student satisfaction objective**, a *within-subjects* (repeated measures) approach is required. Because the same group of 39 students evaluated three different teaching modalities using an ordinal 3-point scale, non-parametric tests are mandatory. The Friedman test, followed by Bonferroni-adjusted Wilcoxon Signed-Rank tests, perfectly accommodates the dependent, ordinal nature of this survey data. 

By combining rigorous normality testing, appropriate non-parametric alternatives for survey data, and internal consistency checks for the questionnaire, this statistical plan ensures high methodological validity and protects against common biostatistical errors such as treating ordinal data as continuous or dependent samples as independent.
