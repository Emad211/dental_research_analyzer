A2. Kaplan–Meier (KM) survival analysis
---------------------------------------

- Test/method name:  
  - Kaplan–Meier estimator of the survival function.  

- Purpose:  
  - Estimate the probability of tooth survival over time (overall and stratified by key covariates, particularly age categories).  
  - Derive survival probabilities at specific time points (e.g., 1-, 3-, 5‑year survival).  

- Why appropriate:  
  1. Primary outcome is time‑to‑event with right‑censoring; KM is the standard nonparametric survival estimator in this setting.  
  2. Accommodates varying follow‑up times and censoring without strong parametric assumptions about the shape of the hazard or survival function.  

- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  1. It does not directly adjust for multiple covariates; it gives unadjusted survival curves.  
  2. KM assumes non‑informative censoring and independent observations; teeth from the same patient violate strict independence.  

- When this method should NOT be used:  
  - If there is heavy competing risk (e.g., if other causes of loss of tooth dominate and we specifically care about cause‑specific cumulative incidence), KM can overestimate event probability.  
  - If time origin is ambiguous or not consistently defined across subjects.  

- Alternative if assumptions are violated:  
  - If independence of observations is a major concern and clustering is strong but number of teeth per patient is small, one can still use KM descriptively but supplement with:  
    - Cluster‑bootstrap confidence intervals for survival estimates; or  
    - A marginal model (e.g., Cox with robust variance) for covariate adjustment.  
  - If there are clinically meaningful competing risks (e.g., tooth loss due to trauma unrelated to NSRCT outcome), use the cumulative incidence function (Fine–Gray model) instead of KM.  

A3. Log‑rank test for comparing survival curves
-----------------------------------------------

- Test name:  
  - Log‑rank test (Mantel–Cox test).  

- Purpose:  
  - Compare the survival experience of different groups (e.g., age categories, gender, insurance type, tooth type, jaw type) in an unadjusted manner.  

- Why appropriate:  
  1. Designed specifically to test equality of survival functions between two or more groups with censored data.  
  2. Nonparametric and widely understood; useful for preliminary assessment of group differences before modeling.  

- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  1. Assumes proportional hazards between groups; if hazards cross strongly, the test’s power and interpretation are compromised.  
  2. Treats each tooth as an independent observation; clustering within patients can inflate Type I error.  

- When this test should NOT be used:  
  - When sample size per group is very small (few events), especially if the event counts are <5 per group.  
  - When there is strong violation of proportional hazards and pre‑specified time intervals or weighted tests would be more suitable.  

- Alternative if assumptions are violated:  
  - Use weighted log‑rank tests (e.g., Tarone–Ware, Peto–Peto) if early or late events are of primary interest and proportional hazard assumption is doubtful.  
  - Use Cox regression (with robust sandwich standard errors or frailty terms) to compare hazards with adjustment for clustering and covariates.  

A4. Cox proportional hazards regression (primary multivariable model)
--------------------------------------------------------------------

- Test/method name:  
  - Cox proportional hazards (PH) regression model.  
  - Model the hazard of ETT failure as a function of covariates.  

- Purpose:  
  - Quantify the association between age, gender, insurance type, tooth type, jaw type, and hazard of failure of ETT, adjusting for other covariates.  
  - Provide adjusted hazard ratios (HRs) with 95% confidence intervals.  

- Why appropriate:  
  1. The objective explicitly involves estimating effect of predictors on time‑to‑event; Cox PH is the standard semi‑parametric method for this.  
  2. Handles right‑censoring and does not require specifying baseline hazard.  
  3. Well suited for categorical predictors and interpretation via hazard ratios.  

- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  1. Assumes proportional hazards (relative hazards constant over time) for each covariate; if this is strongly violated, estimates may be biased or hard to interpret.  
  2. Standard Cox models assume independence of observations; teeth within the same patient may have correlated failure times, leading to underestimated standard errors if not addressed.  

- When this test should NOT be used:  
  - If there is severe violation of proportional hazards that cannot be remedied (e.g., non‑proportional and complex time‑varying effects) and flexible modeling is not considered.  
  - If events are extremely sparse for some categories (e.g., very few failures in one age group), leading to unstable HR estimates.  

- Alternatives if assumptions are violated:  
  1. For non‑independence/clustering (teeth within patients):  
     - Cox model with robust sandwich variance estimator clustered by patient ID (marginal model, same HR estimates but corrected SEs).  
     - Shared frailty Cox model with a random effect (frailty term) for patient.  
  2. For non‑proportional hazards:  
     - Cox model with time‑varying coefficients (include interactions with log(time) or piecewise constant hazard).  
     - Stratified Cox model (e.g., stratify by tooth type or age group when PH is violated for that factor but remains a potential confounder).  
     - Flexible parametric survival models (e.g., Royston–Parmar models) or fully parametric models (Weibull, log‑normal) that allow non‑PH.  

Recommendation:  
- Use a Cox model with robust standard errors clustered by patient ID as default. Test PH assumption (see B1). If necessary, adopt time‑varying effects or stratified models.

B1. Diagnostics for proportional hazards (PH) assumption
--------------------------------------------------------

- Test/approach:  
  - Schoenfeld residuals test and plots.  
  - Log‑log survival plots by category.  

- Purpose:  
  - Assess whether PH assumption holds for each covariate in the Cox model.  

- Why appropriate:  
  1. Ensures validity of Cox model conclusions; if PH is violated, hazard ratios may vary over time and interpretation changes.  
  2. Helps decide whether to modify the model (e.g., include time‑varying covariates or stratification).  

- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  1. With small numbers of events, tests can have low power; a “non‑significant” test may not guarantee PH holds.  
  2. PH tests and plots are less informative for continuous covariates when modeled as categories with sparse groups.  

- When NOT to use:  
  - When using an explicitly non‑PH model (e.g., fully parametric model chosen for time‑varying hazards) as the primary analysis.  

- Alternatives if assumptions are violated:  
  - As above: time‑varying coefficients, stratified Cox, flexible parametric models.  

B2. Model for clustering: robust variance or frailty model
----------------------------------------------------------

- Method names:  
  1. Cox model with robust (sandwich) variance estimates clustered by patient.  
  2. Shared frailty Cox model (e.g., gamma frailty on patient ID).  

- Purpose:  
  - Address non‑independence of teeth within patients, improving standard error and confidence interval estimates, and optionally modeling between‑patient heterogeneity.  

- Why appropriate:  
  1. Data structure has multiple teeth per patient; ignoring clustering can underestimate SEs, leading to inflated Type I error.  
  2. Frailty models can provide insight into unobserved patient‑level factors influencing risk of failure (random effect variance).  

- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  1. With very few clustered units (i.e., if most patients have only one tooth), the gain in robustness may be minimal and frailty variance poorly estimated.  
  2. Frailty models make additional distributional assumptions (e.g., gamma frailty); misspecification can mislead.  

- When NOT to use:  
  - Frailty model:  
    - If nearly all patients contribute only one tooth or there are too few clusters; model may be unstable or non‑identifiable.  
  - Cluster‑robust variance:  
    - If number of clusters (patients) is extremely small (<30); robust SEs become unreliable. Here, we have ~341 patients, so it is appropriate.  

- Alternatives if assumptions are violated:  
  - Use marginal models with generalized estimating equations (GEE) for discrete‑time survival (if time is grouped into intervals) with robust SEs.  
  - Conduct sensitivity analyses treating one tooth per patient (random selection or first treated) to assess influence of clustering.  

B3. Multivariable model building and assessment

- Methods:

- Purpose:

- Why appropriate:  
  1. Reduces confounding compared with univariate models or crude survival curves.

- Why it may not be appropriate:  
  1. With limited events, including too many parameters (especially interactions) risks overfitting.

- When NOT to use:  
  - Do not fit complex interaction models when total number of events is small or distribution of covariates across events is highly imbalanced.  

- Alternatives if assumptions are violated:  
  - Use penalized Cox regression (e.g., Firth penalized likelihood or ridge) to reduce small‑sample bias.

- Methods:

- Purpose:

TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS AND WHY
===================================

Below are tests that should NOT be used for this study’s primary objectives, with reasons.

1. Simple t‑tests or ANOVA on “time to failure” without censoring
-----------------------------------------------------------------

- Why inappropriate:  
  - Time‑to‑event data are right‑censored; many teeth have not failed by last follow‑up. Treating survival time as a complete continuous outcome (and ignoring censoring) biases estimates and invalidates standard errors.  
  - Survival distributions are typically skewed, not normal.  

- When might they be used incorrectly:  
  - Comparing mean follow‑up time or mean time‑to‑failure between age groups using t‑test or ANOVA.  

- Proper alternative:  
  - Kaplan–Meier survival analysis and log‑rank test; Cox regression for covariate adjustment.  

2. Standard logistic regression on “failure yes/no” ignoring time
-----------------------------------------------------------------

- Why inappropriate for the main analysis:  
  - Dichotomizing into “ever failed vs never failed” discards timing information and treats all censored cases as “survivors,” leading to bias, especially when follow‑up duration differs substantially between teeth.  
  - Does not handle right‑censoring appropriately.  

- When might it be used incorrectly:  
  - Modeling risk of failure at last follow‑up using simple logistic regression, ignoring varying follow‑up times.  

- Proper alternative:  
  - Cox regression (time‑to‑event).  
  - If one is interested in risk at a fixed time point (e.g., 5‑year failure), use time‑to‑event methods and then derive risk estimates at 5 years.  

3. Chi‑square tests of “failure proportion” without accounting for time and censoring
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

- Why inappropriate:  
  - Comparing crude failure proportions across groups using chi‑square ignores the fact that some groups may have shorter follow‑up and more censoring.  
  - Over‑ or underestimates differences in survival.  

- When might they be used incorrectly:  
  - Testing association between age group and failure using only a 2×3 contingency table of failure yes/no.  

- Proper alternative:  
  - Log‑rank test for survival curves; Cox regression for adjusted comparisons.  

4. Paired tests (paired t‑test, McNemar’s test) treating multiple teeth per patient as paired data
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

- Why inappropriate:  
  - Teeth within a patient are not “paired measurements” of the same variable over time; they are multiple units of analysis with possibly correlated outcomes.  
  - Paired designs assume specific within‑pair structure (e.g., before/after on same tooth); that is not the case here.  

- Proper alternative:  
  - Clustered Cox models (robust SEs or frailty).  

5. Ordinary least squares (OLS) regression on survival time
-----------------------------------------------------------

- Why inappropriate:  
  - OLS cannot handle censoring; censored observations would need to be dropped or artificially assigned times, leading to bias.  
  - Survival times are typically non‑normal and heteroscedastic; OLS assumptions are violated.  

- Proper alternative:  
  - Cox or parametric survival models that handle censoring.  

6. Repeated‑measures ANOVA or linear mixed models for survival outcome
-----------------------------------------------------------------------

- Why inappropriate:  
  - Repeated measures ANOVA is for continuous outcomes measured repeatedly in the same unit; here the primary outcome is time‑to‑single event per tooth.  
  - Mixed models time structure is not aligned with event‑time data, and censoring is not properly treated.  

- Proper alternative:  
  - Cox model with frailty (random effects at patient level) if one wants to account for between‑patient variability.  

7. Non‑survival nonparametric tests (Mann–Whitney U, Kruskal–Wallis) on survival times
---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

- Why inappropriate:  
  - Similar to t‑tests, these tests do not handle censoring; restricting analysis to uncensored observations introduces bias.  

- Proper alternative:  
  - Log‑rank test for comparison across groups.
