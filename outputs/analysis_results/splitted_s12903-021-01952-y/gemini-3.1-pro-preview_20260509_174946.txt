### TASK 1: INFORMATION EXTRACTION

*   **Study Design:** Retrospective cohort study.
*   **Study Objective:** To examine the survival rates of nonsurgical root canal treatments (NSRCT) performed on permanent teeth of children and to assess predictors of failure.
*   **Null Hypothesis:** Age, gender, insurance type, tooth type, and jaw type have no effect on treatment outcome (survival).
*   **Setting:** Boston University Henry M. Goldman School of Dental Medicine (BUGSDM) (Records from Jan 1, 2007, to Dec 31, 2015).
*   **Sample Size:** 341 patients with 424 teeth (after excluding 432 patients/507 teeth due to lack of follow-up).
*   **Primary Outcome Variable:** Time-to-event (Time until failure of endodontically treated teeth [ETT]).
    *   *Failure definition:* Tooth became symptomatic OR required extraction, endodontic retreatment, or apical surgery.
    *   *Survival (Censored) definition:* Absence of clinical signs/symptoms and no treatment intervention.
*   **Covariates (Independent Variables):**
    *   *Age at treatment:* Categorical (6–11, 12–14, and 15–18 years).
    *   *Gender:* Categorical (Male/Female).
    *   *Insurance type (Proxy for SES):* Categorical (Medicaid/MassHealth vs. private/self-paid).
    *   *Tooth type:* Categorical (Posterior vs. Anterior).
    *   *Jaw type:* Categorical (Maxillary vs. Mandibular).
*   **Data Collection Method:** Extraction from deidentified electronic dental records using a study ID.

### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

Given that the primary outcome is "time until failure" and includes patients with varying lengths of follow-up (right-censored data), Survival Analysis is the required statistical framework. Furthermore, because there are 424 teeth among 341 patients, there is a clustering effect (some patients contributed more than one tooth) that must be accounted for.

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY (Core tests required for the analysis)

**1. Kaplan-Meier (KM) Estimator with Log-Rank Test**
*   **Purpose:** To estimate the survival probability of ETT over time and compare survival curves across categorical covariates (e.g., age groups, tooth type).
*   **Why appropriate:** 1) It effectively handles right-censored time-to-event data. 2) It is non-parametric, meaning it does not assume an underlying probability distribution for survival times.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It cannot adjust for multiple confounding variables simultaneously (univariable only).
*   **When this test should NOT be used:** When analyzing continuous predictor variables or when survival curves cross significantly (violating the assumption of proportional hazards).
*   **Alternative if assumptions are violated:** Parametric survival models (e.g., Weibull distribution) or generalized Wilcoxon test (if early failures are more clinically relevant than late failures).

**2. Multivariable Cox Proportional Hazards Regression with Cluster-Robust Standard Errors (or Shared Frailty Model)**
*   **Purpose:** To evaluate the independent effect of multiple covariates (age, gender, insurance, etc.) on the hazard of ETT failure simultaneously.
*   **Why appropriate:** 1) It allows for multivariable adjustment to isolate the independent effect of each predictor. 2) Using cluster-robust standard errors accounts for the intra-subject correlation (the fact that multiple teeth within the same patient are not statistically independent).
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It relies heavily on the assumption that the hazard ratio remains constant over time (Proportional Hazards assumption).
*   **When this test should NOT be used:** If the proportional hazards assumption is severely violated (e.g., the effect of age changes drastically between year 1 and year 5).
*   **Alternative if assumptions are violated:** Time-dependent Cox regression (incorporating time-varying covariates) or an Accelerated Failure Time (AFT) model.

#### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED (Enhances quality and rigor)

**1. Schoenfeld Residuals Test**
*   **Purpose:** To statistically test the Proportional Hazards (PH) assumption of the Cox regression model.
*   **Why appropriate:** 1) It provides mathematical validation that the core assumption of the mandatory Cox model is met. 2) It allows for graphical plotting to visually inspect how covariate effects behave over time.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It is a diagnostic tool, not an inferential test to answer the clinical research question.
*   **When this test should NOT be used:** When using non-proportional models (like AFT models or logistic regression).
*   **Alternative if assumptions are violated:** Log-minus-log (LML) survival plots for categorical variables.

**2. Descriptive Statistics with Chi-Square / Fisher’s Exact Tests**
*   **Purpose:** To summarize the baseline characteristics of the cohort and compare the distribution of covariates between failed and survived teeth.
*   **Why appropriate:** 1) Provides a clear clinical picture of the study population. 2) Identifies potential baseline imbalances that need careful attention in the multivariable model.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It treats the outcome as binary and completely ignores the "time-to-event" and censoring aspects of the data.
*   **When this test should NOT be used:** To draw final conclusions about the survival or failure rate of the teeth.
*   **Alternative if assumptions are violated:** N/A (Standard exploratory data analysis).

#### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL (Supplementary insights)

**1. Competing Risks Regression (Fine-Gray Model)**
*   **Purpose:** To account for teeth that may have been lost due to reasons unrelated to endodontic failure (e.g., trauma, orthodontic extraction).
*   **Why appropriate:** 1) In pediatric dentistry, teeth are frequently extracted for orthodontics; treating this as standard censoring in a Kaplan-Meier model overestimates the risk of endodontic failure. 2) It provides a more accurate real-world cumulative incidence of endodontic failure.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** The dataset may not have recorded the specific reasons for extraction, making it impossible to differentiate competing risks from true failures.
*   **When this test should NOT be used:** If there are no competing events, or if all extractions are strictly defined as endodontic failures.
*   **Alternative if assumptions are violated:** Cause-specific Cox regression.

### TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS

*   **Standard Binary Logistic Regression:** Should NOT be used for the primary outcome. Logistic regression treats the outcome as a simple binary (success vs. failure) and ignores the time at risk. It treats a tooth that failed at 1 month the same as a tooth that failed at 5 years, and it cannot properly handle censored patients (those lost to follow-up).
*   **Independent Samples t-test / ANOVA:** Should NOT be used to compare time-to-failure between groups. Time-to-event data is rarely normally distributed (it is usually highly skewed) and these tests cannot incorporate censored data, leading to heavily biased estimates.
*   **Standard (Unadjusted) Cox Proportional Hazards Model:** Should NOT be used because it assumes all 424 teeth are completely independent. Since 341 patients provided the 424 teeth, there is a clustering effect. Ignoring this violates the assumption of independence, artificially deflates standard errors, and increases the risk of Type I errors (false positives).

### TASK 4: SUMMARY

The overall analytical approach must be rooted in **Survival Analysis** because the primary outcome is a time-to-event variable containing right-censored data (patients with varying follow-up times who did not experience failure). The Kaplan-Meier method is ideal for visualizing and estimating unadjusted survival probabilities across different age groups and tooth types. However, to test the null hypothesis rigorously, a multivariable Cox Proportional Hazards model is required to adjust for confounding variables. Crucially, because the dataset contains multiple teeth from single patients (424 teeth / 341 patients), the biostatistical approach *must* utilize cluster-robust standard errors or a shared frailty model to account for intra-patient correlation; failing to do so would compromise the validity of the p-values and confidence intervals.
