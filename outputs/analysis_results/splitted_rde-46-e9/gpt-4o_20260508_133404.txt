### TASK 1: INFORMATION EXTRACTION

#### Study Characteristics:
1. **Study Design**:  
   - Prospective, double-blind, randomized clinical trial.

2. **Objective**:  
   - To evaluate the efficacy of buccal infiltration with piroxicam on the success rate of inferior alveolar nerve block (IANB) in patients with irreversible pulpitis.

3. **Groups**:  
   - **Control Group**: Buccal infiltration with normal saline (syringe A).  
   - **Test Group**: Buccal infiltration with 20 mg/mL of piroxicam (syringe B).

4. **Sample Size**:  
   - Total: 56 patients (28 per group).  
   - Sample size calculation: Effect size = 0.82, α = 0.05, power = 0.95.

5. **Participants**:  
   - Inclusion criteria: Patients aged 15–65 years with mandibular molars exhibiting irreversible pulpitis, initial pain score > 54 on Heft-Parker VAS, exaggerated response to cold testing lasting > 45 seconds, and negative response to electric pulp testing (EPT).  
   - Exclusion criteria: Multiple infected teeth, failure to achieve lip numbness, hypersensitivity to piroxicam, history of peptic ulcers, renal/liver/bleeding disorders, pregnancy/lactation.

6. **Variables**:  
   - **Independent Variable**: Type of buccal infiltration (normal saline vs. piroxicam).  
   - **Dependent Variables**:  
     - Pain scores during caries removal (CR), access opening procedure (AOP), and working length measurement (WLM) using Heft-Parker VAS.  
     - Success of anesthesia (absence of pain or mild/slight pain during procedures).

7. **Measurements**:  
   - Pain scores: Heft-Parker VAS (0–170 mm).  
   - Anesthesia success: Absence of pain or mild/slight pain during CR, AOP, and WLM, with no need for additional anesthetic doses.

8. **Data Collection Methods**:  
   - Pain assessment using Heft-Parker VAS and EPT.  
   - Randomized allocation of patients into groups using coded syringes (A and B).  
   - Blinding: Both patients and the postgraduate student administering the injections were blinded to the group allocation.

### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS:
1. **Test Name**: Independent Samples t-Test  
   - **Purpose**: Compare mean pain scores between the control and test groups.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     - The study involves two independent groups (control vs. test).  
     - Pain scores are continuous variables measured on the Heft-Parker VAS.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Assumes normal distribution of pain scores and equal variances between groups.  
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**: If pain scores are not normally distributed or variances are unequal.  
   - **Alternative**: Mann-Whitney U test (non-parametric alternative).

2. **Test Name**: Chi-Square Test of Independence  
   - **Purpose**: Compare the proportion of patients achieving successful anesthesia between the two groups.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     - Success of anesthesia is a categorical variable (success vs. failure).  
     - The study involves two independent groups.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Requires a sufficient sample size in each cell of the contingency table.  
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**: If expected cell counts are < 5.  
   - **Alternative**: Fisher’s Exact Test.

---

#### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED TESTS:
1. **Test Name**: Repeated Measures ANOVA  
   - **Purpose**: Analyze pain scores across multiple time points (CR, AOP, WLM) within each group.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     - Pain scores are measured repeatedly for each patient during different procedures.  
     - Allows comparison of trends over time between groups.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Assumes sphericity (equal variances of differences between time points).  
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**: If sphericity is violated.  
   - **Alternative**: Greenhouse-Geisser correction or Linear Mixed Models.

2. **Test Name**: Logistic Regression  
   - **Purpose**: Assess the impact of group allocation (control vs. test) on the likelihood of successful anesthesia, adjusting for covariates like age and sex.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     - Outcome (success of anesthesia) is binary.  
     - Allows adjustment for potential confounders.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Requires a large sample size for reliable estimates.  
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**: If the sample size is too small.  
   - **Alternative**: Exact Logistic Regression.

---

#### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL TESTS:
1. **Test Name**: Effect Size Calculation (Cohen’s d)  
   - **Purpose**: Quantify the magnitude of difference in pain scores between groups.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     - Provides a standardized measure of group differences.  
     - Useful for interpreting clinical significance.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Does not account for confounding variables.  
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**: If the sample size is insufficient to reliably estimate effect size.  
   - **Alternative**: Glass’s Delta (if variances are unequal).

2. **Test Name**: Subgroup Analysis  
   - **Purpose**: Explore differences in outcomes within subgroups (e.g., age or sex).  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     - Identifies potential effect modifiers.  
     - Enhances understanding of differential effects.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Increases risk of Type I error due to multiple comparisons.  
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**: If the sample size within subgroups is too small.  
   - **Alternative**: Bootstrap methods for small samples.
