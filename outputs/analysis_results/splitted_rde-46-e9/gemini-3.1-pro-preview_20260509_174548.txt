### TASK 1: INFORMATION EXTRACTION

*   **Study Design:** Prospective, double-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled clinical trial.
*   **Objective:** To evaluate the efficacy of buccal piroxicam infiltration on the success of Inferior Alveolar Nerve Block (IANB) and buccal infiltration using 2% lignocaine with adrenaline in patients with irreversible pulpitis.
*   **Participants:** 56 patients (aged 15 to 65 years) with ASA Class I or II medical history.
*   **Sample Size:** 28 patients per group (Calculated sample size was 52 based on 80% power and 0.82 effect size; rounded to 56 to account for manual errors/dropouts).
*   **Study Groups:**
    *   **Group 1 (Control):** Standard IANB + buccal lignocaine + buccal infiltration with normal saline (Syringe A).
    *   **Group 2 (Test):** Standard IANB + buccal lignocaine + buccal infiltration with 20 mg/mL piroxicam (Syringe B).
*   **Independent Variable:** Type of supplemental buccal infiltration (Piroxicam vs. Normal Saline).
*   **Dependent Variables (Outcomes):**
    *   *Pain Scores:* Measured using the 170-mm Heft-Parker Visual Analogue Scale (HP-VAS).
    *   *Anesthetic Success:* Categorical/Binary (Success = absence of pain or mild pain [HP-VAS 0–54] during procedures with no extra anesthetic needed; Failure = moderate/severe pain requiring additional anesthetic).
*   **Time Points for Measurement:** Initial Pain Score (IPS), during Caries Removal (CR), Access Opening Procedure (AOP), and Working Length Measurement (WLM).
*   **Data Collection Methods:** HP-VAS for subjective pain; Electric Pulp Tester (EPT) and lip numbness assessment every 5 minutes for objective pulpal anesthesia.

### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY (Essential Tests)

**1. Chi-Square Test (or Fisher’s Exact Test)**
*   **Purpose:** To compare the primary outcome (Anesthetic Success rates: Success vs. Failure) between the two groups, and to compare baseline sex distribution.
*   **Why appropriate:** 1) Anesthetic success and sex are categorical (binary) variables. 2) It determines if the difference in proportions between two independent groups is statistically significant.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** If the expected frequency in any cell of the 2x2 contingency table is less than 5, the Chi-square approximation breaks down.
*   **When NOT to use:** When the data is paired/matched (e.g., same patient receiving both treatments on different sides), or if expected cell counts are < 5.
*   **Alternative:** Fisher’s Exact Test (if expected cell counts are < 5).

**2. Mann-Whitney U Test**
*   **Purpose:** To compare HP-VAS pain scores between the Piroxicam and Saline groups at specific procedural stages (IPS, CR, AOP, WLM).
*   **Why appropriate:** 1) HP-VAS pain scores are typically skewed and do not follow a normal distribution. 2) It is the ideal non-parametric test for comparing continuous/ordinal data between two independent groups.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It tests for differences in mean ranks, not absolute means, which can sometimes be less intuitive for clinical interpretation.
*   **When NOT to use:** If the data is perfectly normally distributed and variances are equal, this test loses statistical power compared to its parametric counterpart.
*   **Alternative:** Independent Samples t-test (if Shapiro-Wilk test confirms normal distribution of VAS scores).

**3. Independent Samples t-test**
*   **Purpose:** To compare the baseline age of patients between the two groups to ensure successful randomization.
*   **Why appropriate:** 1) Age is a continuous variable. 2) It effectively tests the null hypothesis that the mean age of the two independent groups is equal.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** If the age distribution is highly skewed (e.g., mostly young patients with a few elderly outliers), the mean becomes a poor measure of central tendency.
*   **When NOT to use:** When the continuous data violates the assumption of normality or homogeneity of variances.
*   **Alternative:** Mann-Whitney U Test (if age data is non-normally distributed).

#### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED (Enhances Quality and Rigor)

**1. Shapiro-Wilk Test**
*   **Purpose:** To assess the normality of continuous variables (Age, HP-VAS scores).
*   **Why appropriate:** 1) It is highly sensitive to deviations from normality for small to medium sample sizes (n=56). 2) It provides statistical justification for choosing between parametric and non-parametric tests.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** In very small sample sizes, it may lack the power to detect non-normality.
*   **When NOT to use:** When dealing with strictly categorical or nominal data (e.g., Sex, Success/Failure).
*   **Alternative:** Kolmogorov-Smirnov test or visual inspection of Q-Q plots.

**2. Friedman Test**
*   **Purpose:** To evaluate the change in HP-VAS pain scores within the *same* group across the four time points (IPS, CR, AOP, WLM).
*   **Why appropriate:** 1) It accounts for the repeated-measures nature of the data (same patients measured 4 times). 2) It does not require the assumption of normality, which is ideal for pain scores.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It only tells you that a difference exists somewhere among the time points, requiring post-hoc tests (like Wilcoxon signed-rank with Bonferroni correction) to find exactly where the difference lies.
*   **When NOT to use:** When comparing data between the two different treatment groups (it is strictly for within-group analysis).
*   **Alternative:** Repeated Measures ANOVA (if the pain scores are normally distributed and meet the assumption of sphericity).

**3. Relative Risk (RR) / Odds Ratio (OR) with 95% Confidence Intervals**
*   **Purpose:** To quantify the clinical effect size of piroxicam on anesthetic success.
*   **Why appropriate:** 1) Provides a clinically meaningful metric (e.g., "Patients receiving piroxicam are X times more likely to achieve successful anesthesia"). 2) Confidence intervals provide insight into the precision of the estimate.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** If the event rate is 0 in one group, the OR/RR calculation will result in an error (division by zero) without statistical adjustments.
*   **When NOT to use:** When analyzing continuous outcomes (like exact VAS scores).
*   **Alternative:** Absolute Risk Reduction (ARR) and Number Needed to Treat (NNT).

#### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL (Supplementary Insights)

**1. Generalized Estimating Equations (GEE) or Mixed-Effects Linear Models**
*   **Purpose:** To analyze the interaction between "Treatment Group" and "Time" on HP-VAS scores simultaneously.
*   **Why appropriate:** 1) It handles repeated measures robustly while allowing for missing data (if any patients dropped out). 2) It can adjust for baseline covariates like Age or Initial Pain Score.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It is highly complex and may overcomplicate the analysis for a straightforward 2-group RCT with a relatively small sample size.
*   **When NOT to use:** If the sample size is too small to support complex modeling without overfitting.
*   **Alternative:** Two-way Mixed ANOVA (if normality and sphericity assumptions are met).

### TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS

*   **Paired Samples t-test / Wilcoxon Signed-Rank Test (for between-group analysis):** These tests are for matched pairs or repeated measures on the *same* subjects. Group 1 and Group 2 consist of completely independent, different patients.
*   **Pearson Correlation Coefficient:** Inappropriate for evaluating the primary outcome (Anesthetic Success), as success is a binary categorical variable, not a continuous variable.
*   **One-Way ANOVA (for between-group VAS comparison):** ANOVA is used to compare means across *three or more* independent groups. Since there are only two groups (Piroxicam vs. Saline), an Independent t-test or Mann-Whitney U test is the correct choice. Furthermore, ANOVA assumes normality, which HP-VAS data often violates.

### TASK 4: SUMMARY

The overall analytical approach for this study should be divided into three phases: **Baseline Equivalence, Primary Outcome Analysis, and Secondary Outcome Analysis.** 

First, baseline demographics (Age, Sex) and Initial Pain Scores (IPS) must be compared using t-tests/Mann-Whitney and Chi-square tests to ensure the randomization successfully created comparable groups. Second, the primary clinical outcome—Anesthetic Success (a binary variable)—should be analyzed using a Chi-square test, supplemented by Relative Risk calculations to provide clinical context. Finally, the secondary outcome—HP-VAS pain scores at various procedural stages—should be analyzed using non-parametric tests (Mann-Whitney U for between-group comparisons and Friedman test for within-group trends over time), as pain scales are bounded and typically skewed. This approach ensures rigorous handling of both categorical and non-normal continuous data, directly answering the study's core hypothesis.
