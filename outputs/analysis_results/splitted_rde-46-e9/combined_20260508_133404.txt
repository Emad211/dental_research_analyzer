## TASK 2: Statistical analysis plan (SAP)

### General analysis principles (apply to all outcomes)
- **Analysis population (mandatory):**
  - **Intention-to-treat (ITT):** analyze all randomized participants in assigned groups.
  - **Key correction to published exclusions:** participants requiring additional anesthesia or failing anesthesia criteria should be coded as **anesthesia failure**, not excluded.
- **Descriptive statistics:**
  - Continuous: mean±SD (if ~normal) and median [IQR] (robust)
  - Categorical: n (%)
- **Two-sided α = 0.05**, report **95% confidence intervals (CI)**.
- **Multiplicity:** if comparing pain at multiple procedural stages (CR/AOP/WLM), control family-wise error (e.g., Holm) or model jointly using mixed models.
- **Assumption checks:** normality of residuals, equal variances, influential points; for ordinal/censored pain distributions consider nonparametric/ordinal models.
- **Missing data:** document reasons; if nontrivial, use multiple imputation (MI) or likelihood-based mixed models (MAR assumption), with sensitivity analyses.

### CATEGORY A – Mandatory tests/models

#### A1) Pearson chi-square test (or Fisher’s exact test)
- **Purpose:** Compare **anesthetic success rate** (success vs failure) between groups.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Primary endpoint is **binary** (success defined clinically).
  2. Parallel 2-group RCT → independent proportions comparison is standard.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - If expected cell counts are small (e.g., very high success in both groups), chi-square approximation may be poor.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When any expected cell count <5 (rule of thumb) → use Fisher’s exact.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Fisher’s exact test**; and/or **log-binomial regression** / **Poisson regression with robust SE** to estimate risk ratio with CI.

#### A2) Effect size estimation for binary endpoint: Risk Ratio (RR) or Risk Difference (RD) with 95% CI (via regression)
- **Purpose:** Quantify treatment effect beyond p-values.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. RR/RD are clinically interpretable (probability of success improvement).
  2. Supports CONSORT-style reporting; enables adjusted analyses.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Log-binomial models may fail to converge if probabilities near 1.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Don’t rely on **odds ratio** alone if outcome common (OR overstates effect).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Modified Poisson regression (robust SE)** for RR; or **logistic regression** with marginal standardization to report adjusted risks.

#### A3) Linear mixed-effects model (LMM) *or* GEE for repeated HP‑VAS pain (CR, AOP, WLM)
- **Purpose:** Compare groups on **pain trajectories across procedural stages** accounting for within-person correlation.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Same participant provides multiple pain scores → independence violated; LMM/GEE handles correlation.
  2. Allows testing **group**, **stage**, and **group×stage interaction** (whether piroxicam effect differs by procedure).
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - HP‑VAS pain may be skewed/zero-inflated; LMM assumes approximately normal residuals.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If distributions are highly non-normal and transformations/robust methods are inadequate.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Ordinal mixed model** (if using pain categories), or **rank-based methods** (e.g., aligned rank transform for repeated measures), or **GEE with robust SE** and suitable link (e.g., gamma with log link if modeling positive pain).

### CATEGORY B – Recommended tests/models

#### B1) Baseline comparability: t-test (or Mann–Whitney U) for age/IPS; chi-square/Fisher for sex
- **Purpose:** Describe baseline characteristics and check for gross imbalance.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Confirms randomization plausibly achieved balance (descriptive quality control).
  2. Identifies covariates to consider in adjusted models if imbalance is clinically meaningful.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Hypothesis testing of baseline differences is often discouraged (randomization justifies balance in expectation).
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Do not use baseline p-values to decide inclusion/exclusion of covariates mechanically.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - For continuous non-normal: **Mann–Whitney U**; for small categorical counts: **Fisher’s exact**.

#### B2) Multivariable regression for primary endpoint (adjusted analysis)
- **Test/model:** Modified Poisson (robust) or logistic regression
- **Purpose:** Estimate treatment effect adjusted for **preoperative pain (IPS)**, age, sex (and potentially tooth-related factors if available).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Improves precision and addresses chance imbalance in strong predictors (IPS likely prognostic).
  2. Produces adjusted effect sizes with CI.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Overfitting risk if events (failures) are few.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If number of failures is too small for stable estimates (e.g., <10 failures per parameter).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Penalized regression (Firth logistic) for rare events; or unadjusted RR/RD as primary with adjusted as sensitivity.

#### B3) Holm-adjusted pairwise comparisons (if analyzing each stage pain separately)
- **Purpose:** Control type I error across multiple pain outcomes (CR, AOP, WLM).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Multiple endpoints increase false positives.
  2. Holm is more powerful than Bonferroni and valid under dependence.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - If you use a single repeated-measures model with a prespecified primary contrast, multiplicity burden is reduced.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Avoid redundant corrections when the primary analysis is a single global mixed model.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - False discovery rate (Benjamini–Hochberg) for exploratory settings.

#### B4) Per-protocol (PP) sensitivity analysis
- **Purpose:** Assess robustness to protocol deviations (e.g., exclusions described in the article).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Helps interpret efficacy under ideal adherence.
  2. Complements ITT (which addresses effectiveness).
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - PP is subject to selection bias (post-randomization exclusions related to outcome).
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Do not replace ITT with PP as the primary analysis.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Use ITT as primary; perform **complier average causal effect** methods if compliance information exists.

## TASK 3: Tests that should NOT be used (and why)

1. **Paired t-test** (between groups)  
   - Data are **independent** between participants; pairing does not exist.

2. **Multiple separate independent-samples t-tests** for CR, AOP, WLM without multiplicity control  
   - Inflates type I error and ignores **within-subject correlation**.

3. **One-way ANOVA** (single-factor) on pooled pain scores without modeling repeated measures  
   - Violates independence; stage-specific pain is repeated within subject.

4. **Using only odds ratios from logistic regression as primary effect** (without RR/RD)  
   - Outcome (success) is likely common; OR can be misleadingly large vs RR.

5. **Excluding “failures” post-randomization and analyzing only those completing treatment** as the main analysis  
   - Introduces **attrition/selection bias**; undermines randomization.

## TASK 4: Summary justification of overall analytical approach

A two-arm parallel RCT requires (i) a **between-group comparison of the primary binary clinical success endpoint** with effect sizes and CIs (ITT principle), and (ii) appropriate modeling of **repeated HP‑VAS pain measurements** across procedural stages using mixed models/GEE to account for within-patient correlation and to test whether piroxicam changes pain patterns during treatment. Sensitivity/adjusted analyses improve precision and assess robustness, while avoiding post-randomization exclusions preserves the causal interpretation of randomization.

  - Quantify and test whether piroxicam buccal infiltration increases the success of IANB + buccal lignocaine compared with saline.

- **Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)**
  1. Primary outcome is binary: success vs. failure of anesthesia based on HP‑VAS and need for supplemental anesthetic. χ²/Fisher’s compare proportions between two independent groups appropriately.
  2. Groups are randomized and independent, with fixed sample sizes and simple 2×2 contingency tables.
  3. The test is easy to interpret clinically (e.g., percentage success difference, relative risk).

- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)**
  - If there are very few failures or very small counts in some cells, the χ² approximation may be inaccurate.

- **When this test should NOT be used**
  - Not appropriate for small expected cell frequencies (<5) in any cell of the 2×2 table.
  - Not appropriate if multiple categories (e.g., 4‑level pain categories) are collapsed into binary in a way that discards important information without clear justification.

- **Alternative if assumptions are violated**
  - Fisher’s exact test (exact p‑value) for 2×2 tables with small counts.
  - For adjusted analysis, exact logistic regression if sample size is small and sparse.

---

A3. **Comparison of mean HP‑VAS scores between groups at each procedure step**

- **Test name**
  - Independent samples t test (CR HP‑VAS, AOP HP‑VAS, WLM HP‑VAS), or Mann–Whitney U test if distributional assumptions are not met.

- **Purpose**
  - Compare the intensity of pain during CR, AOP, and WLM between groups.
  - Provide quantitative evidence of pain reduction beyond simple success/failure.

- **Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)**
  1. HP‑VAS is a continuous measure with reasonably fine resolution; often approximates a normal distribution in moderate samples.
  2. Groups are independent and randomized; t tests compare means between two independent groups.
  3. Pain scores at each procedural step are clinically meaningful continuous outcomes.

- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)**
  - HP‑VAS distributions are frequently skewed and bounded (0–170), especially when many values cluster near 0 or near high pain; normality and equal variance assumptions may fail.

- **When this test should NOT be used**
  - When distributions are heavily skewed or clearly non‑normal (e.g., many zeros) and sample size per group is small enough that CLT cannot ensure approximate normality.
  - When variances differ substantially between groups (Levene’s test significant) and not using a variance‑corrected version.

- **Alternative if assumptions are violated**
  - Mann–Whitney U test as a nonparametric alternative.
  - If transformation (e.g., log or square root) normalizes data, a t test on transformed scores.

---

A4. **Within‑group comparison: change from baseline pain (IPS) to intraoperative HP‑VAS**

- **Test name**
  - Paired t test or Wilcoxon signed‑rank test (within each group) for:
    - IPS vs CR pain.
    - IPS vs AOP pain.
    - IPS vs WLM pain.

- **Purpose**
  - Assess the magnitude of pain reduction within each group from preoperative pain (IPS) to pain at various procedural steps.

- **Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)**
  1. Same patients measured at multiple times: baseline and during procedures; paired data require within‑subject tests.
  2. HP‑VAS is continuous; t tests or Wilcoxon provide simple summaries of mean/median change.
  3. Helps demonstrate clinical effect of anesthesia in each group separately.

- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)**
  - Multiple tests across steps inflate type I error without adjustment.
  - Distribution of change scores may be skewed.

- **When this test should NOT be used**
  - When the distribution of differences is strongly non‑normal with many outliers and n is small; Wilcoxon is preferable.
  - If many patients have missing follow‑up pain scores at specific steps.

- **Alternative if assumptions are violated**
  - Wilcoxon signed‑rank test for non‑normal paired data.
  - Mixed‑effects models (see B5) can incorporate all time points and handle missing data more efficiently.

---

A5. **Effect size and confidence intervals for primary and key secondary outcomes**

- **Test name**
  - Estimation using:
    - Risk ratio (RR) and risk difference (RD) for success rates, with 95% CIs.
    - Mean difference (MD) in HP‑VAS, with 95% CIs.

- **Purpose**
  - Quantify the magnitude and precision of treatment effects, not just p‑values.

- **Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)**
  1. Clinical decision‑making relies on effect sizes and their precision, especially in analgesic/anesthetic trials.
  2. Confidence intervals are standard reporting in RCTs and required by many journals.
  3. They give more information than hypothesis tests alone.

- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)**
  - If event counts are very low, standard CI formulas may be inaccurate.

- **When this should NOT be used**
  - Never “not used”; effect size reporting is always recommended; but specific formulas (e.g., Wald CIs for RR) should be avoided with small samples.

- **Alternative if assumptions are violated**
  - Exact confidence intervals for proportions and RRs.
  - Bootstrap CIs for mean differences when normality is questionable.

B1. **Logistic regression for anesthetic success (adjusted and effect‑modification analyses)**

- **Test name**
  - Binary logistic regression with success (yes/no) as outcome; predictor: group (piroxicam vs saline), adjusted for relevant covariates (e.g., age, sex, baseline IPS if any imbalance).

- **Purpose**
  - Estimate adjusted odds ratio (OR) for success comparing piroxicam to saline.
  - Explore whether baseline variables modify treatment effect (interaction terms).

- **Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)**
  1. Outcome is binary; logistic regression is the standard modeling approach.
  2. Adjusts for any residual imbalance in prognostic factors (e.g., age, sex, baseline pain) even in randomized trials, improving precision.
  3. Allows examination of effect modification (e.g., stronger effect in severe baseline pain).

- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)**
  - Sample size is modest (n ≈ 56); with few failures, model may be unstable or overfit if too many covariates are included.

- **When this test should NOT be used**
  - When events per variable (EPV) are too low (rule of thumb: <10 events per predictor) leading to unreliable estimates.
  - When outcome is extremely common or rare in both groups, causing separation.

- **Alternative if assumptions are violated**
  - Exact logistic regression for small samples and sparse data.
  - Fisher’s exact test for unadjusted comparisons if multivariable modeling is not feasible.

B2. **Analysis of HP‑VAS as ordinal categories (no/slight/moderate/severe pain)**

- **Test name**
  - Ordinal logistic regression (proportional odds model) or nonparametric tests like Cochran–Armitage trend test or χ² test for trend.

- **Purpose**
  - Use the clinically defined HP‑VAS categories to compare distribution of pain severity between groups at each step.

- **Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)**
  1. Categories (none, slight, moderate, extreme) reflect clinically meaningful gradations, not just continuous mm differences.
  2. Ordinal models respect the ordered nature of the outcome and can be more robust than assuming normality of raw HP‑VAS scores.
  3. They may be easier to interpret clinically (e.g., “odds of being in a lower pain category”).

- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)**
  - Ordinal logistic regression assumes proportional odds (parallel lines); if violated, the model may misrepresent relationships.

- **When this test should NOT be used**
  - When sample sizes in some categories are very small (e.g., few extreme pain values), leading to sparse ordinal data.
  - When proportional odds assumption clearly fails.

- **Alternative if assumptions are violated**
  - Collapsing categories and using binary logistic regression (e.g., none/slight vs moderate/extreme).
  - Generalized ordered logit models if proportional odds is violated.

---

B3. **Repeated measures analysis for HP‑VAS over time (CR, AOP, WLM)**

- **Test name**
  - Repeated measures ANOVA or, preferably, linear mixed‑effects model (LMM) with random intercept for patients.
  
- **Purpose**
  - Account for within‑subject correlation across the three intraoperative time points and directly test:
    - Main effect of group.
    - Main effect of time.
    - Group × time interaction.

- **Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)**
  1. Each patient contributes repeated measures (CR, AOP, WLM), which are correlated; repeated measures methods handle this correlation explicitly.
  2. Mixed models can handle missing data at some time points under missing‑at‑random assumptions.
  3. More efficient and interpretable than running separate t tests at each time point.

- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)**
  - Sample size is modest; complex covariance structures may not be estimable.
  - HP‑VAS may violate normality and homoscedasticity assumptions.

- **When this test should NOT be used**
  - When distributions are highly skewed and transformations fail to fix assumptions.
  - When there is substantial differential missingness across time points that is clearly not missing at random.

- **Alternative if assumptions are violated**
  - Nonparametric repeated measures tests (e.g., Friedman test within group; then compare change scores between groups with Mann–Whitney).
  - Generalized estimating equations (GEE) with robust standard errors and appropriate link (identity for continuous, cumulative logit for ordinal).

---

B4. **Sensitivity analyses for excluded post‑randomization patients**

- **Test name**
  - Sensitivity analysis using worst‑case/best‑case imputation frameworks and re‑analysis of primary outcome (e.g., treat all excluded for pain as failures in their randomized groups; ITT‑like approach).
  - Compare with per‑protocol analysis.

- **Purpose**
  - Assess robustness of primary conclusions to exclusion of patients who required additional anesthesia (post‑randomization, likely informative).

- **Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)**
  1. Post‑randomization exclusions can bias treatment effect estimates; sensitivity analyses probe impact of such bias.
  2. ITT principle is gold standard in RCTs; even if strict ITT is not feasible, approximations via imputation help.

- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)**
  - If the number of excluded patients is extremely small and balanced between groups, the impact may be negligible, and complex sensitivity analyses may not change conclusions.

- **When this should NOT be used**
  - Not “not used,” but full ITT with actual outcome imputation may be misleading if outcome is impossible to define in excluded patients (e.g., no further pain measurements). However, simple classification as failure is reasonable and conservative.

- **Alternative if assumptions are violated**
  - Present both per‑protocol and modified ITT results and discuss differences qualitatively rather than over‑interpret imputed data.

B5. **Graphical data presentation**

- **Test name / method**

- **Purpose**
  - Visually summarize distributions and treatment differences.

- **Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)**

  2. Aid interpretation by clinicians and non‑statisticians.

TASK 3 – INAPPROPRIATE TESTS FOR THIS STUDY

1. **Paired t test for between‑group comparisons**
   - Inappropriate because:
     - Groups (piroxicam vs saline) are independent, not paired.
     - Using a paired test would artificially inflate power and yield invalid p‑values.

2. **Repeated measures ANOVA treating groups as repeated factor**
   - Inappropriate because:
     - Group is a between‑subjects factor, not within‑subjects.
     - Mis‑specification of design leads to incorrect error terms.

3. **One‑way ANOVA with more than two levels for group**
   - Inappropriate because:
     - Only two groups exist; ANOVA offers no advantage over t test, and multiple comparisons (if misused) could complicate interpretation.

4. **Correlation (Pearson/Spearman) between group and outcomes**
   - Inappropriate because:
     - Group is binary; correlation is not the right measure for testing treatment effects; binary outcomes need appropriate presentation (proportions, logistic regression).

5. **Linear regression with binary outcome treated as continuous**
   - Inappropriate because:
     - Success/failure is binary; linear regression yields predicted probabilities outside [0,1] and wrong error structure.

6. **Parametric tests ignoring repeated measures (e.g., ordinary least squares regression with multiple pain measures per subject treated as independent)**
   - Inappropriate because:
     - Violates independence assumption, underestimates standard errors.

7. **Time‑to‑event survival analysis (Kaplan–Meier, Cox)**
   - Inappropriate because:
     - No time‑to‑event outcome is specified (no follow‑up time, no event times recorded); focus is on immediate anesthetic success during a single visit.

8. **McNemar test for success differences between groups**
   - Inappropriate because:
     - McNemar is for paired binary data (e.g., pre/post in same individuals); this is a parallel‑group design.

9. **Multiple comparison post‑hoc tests designed for >2 groups (e.g., Tukey HSD)**
   - Inappropriate because:
     - Only two randomized groups; no multiple group factor requiring such procedures.

10. **Chi‑square test for trend when only two ordered categories**
    - Inappropriate because:
      - With two categories, a simple χ² is equivalent; trend test adds nothing and may be misinterpreted.

TASK 4 – SUMMARY AND JUSTIFICATION OF OVERALL ANALYTICAL APPROACH

The study is a two‑arm, parallel‑group, randomized, double‑blind clinical trial assessing whether buccal piroxicam infiltration improves the anesthetic efficacy of standard IANB plus buccal lignocaine in mandibular molars with irreversible pulpitis. The main outcomes are binary anesthetic success and continuous HP‑VAS pain scores during specific operative steps.

Accordingly, the core analytical strategy focuses on:

- Comparing **proportions of anesthetic success** between groups using χ²/Fisher’s exact tests, with effect size estimates (risk differences, risk ratios, and CIs), potentially supplemented by logistic regression for adjusted estimates.
- Comparing **HP‑VAS pain scores** between groups with appropriate two‑sample tests (t or Mann–Whitney), and modeling repeated measurements over CR, AOP, and WLM with a repeated measures/mixed‑effects approach to respect within‑patient correlation.
- Evaluating **within‑group changes** from baseline pain using paired methods to illustrate the clinical effect of anesthesia.

Recommended enhancements include ordinal modeling of pain categories, mixed models for repeated HP‑VAS measures, and sensitivity analyses for post‑randomization exclusions, all of which improve rigor, account for the longitudinal and hierarchical nature of the data, and support more robust conclusions while respecting the modest sample size and the randomized design.
