CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS  
-----------------------------  

A1. Descriptive statistics and baseline comparability  
-----------------------------------------------------  
**Test / Methods**  
- Descriptive summaries:  
  - Continuous/count outcomes: median, IQR; mean, SD if appropriate.  
  - Categorical variables: counts, percentages.  
- Baseline comparability:  
  - Age: independent-samples t-test or Mann–Whitney U.  
  - Sex, tooth location/type: chi-square test or Fisher’s exact test.  

**Purpose**  
- Describe the sample characteristics and baseline microbial loads.  
- Verify that randomization produced comparable groups at baseline (S1).  

**Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)**  
1. Essential for interpreting trial results and assessing risk of confounding.  
2. Microbial counts are often skewed; medians/IQR give robust summaries; t-test or Mann–Whitney allows checking for any important baseline imbalances.  

**Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)**  
- With small sample (n=15 per group), normality assumptions for t-tests may be violated and chi-square may be unstable if expected cell counts are low.  

**When NOT to use**  
- Do not use chi-square when any expected cell count <5; use Fisher’s exact instead.  
- Do not rely solely on means if distributions are heavily skewed or zero-inflated.  

**Alternative if assumptions violated**  
- Age: Mann–Whitney U test instead of t-test.  
- Categorical variables: Fisher’s exact instead of chi-square.

A2. Primary between-group comparison of antimicrobial effect  
------------------------------------------------------------  

Two reasonable primary analytic frameworks (choose one as primary, report the other as sensitivity):

**Option 1 (preferred): Repeated-measures analysis of log-transformed counts**  

**Test / Method**  
- Linear mixed-effects model (LMM) or repeated-measures ANOVA on log-transformed bacterial counts (e.g., log10(CFU + 1)).  
  - Fixed effects: Group (TAP vs propolis), Time (S1, S2, S3), and Group × Time interaction.  
  - Random effect: subject/tooth ID (to account for within-tooth correlation).  

**Purpose**  
- Evaluate overall antimicrobial trajectory over time and compare whether the reduction over time differs between TAP and propolis, focusing on the S2→S3 segment as the effect of the medicament.  

**Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)**  
1. Repeated measures from the same tooth (S1–S3) are correlated; LMM explicitly models this correlation.  
2. Log transformation stabilizes variance and makes distribution of microbial counts more symmetric, improving model assumptions.  
3. Group × Time interaction directly tests whether the medicament effect differs by group.  

**Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)**  
- Sample size is small; with 30 teeth and 3 time points, complex models (e.g., rich random effects structures) may not converge or may be underpowered to detect small interaction effects.  

**When NOT to use**  
- Do not use parametric repeated-measures ANOVA/LMM if even after transformation residuals are highly non-normal or variances grossly heteroscedastic, especially with many zero counts.  

**Alternative if assumptions violated**  
- Non-parametric alternative: Friedman test within each group plus Wilcoxon rank-sum test on change scores (see A3, A4).  
- Count model alternative: generalized linear mixed model (GLMM) with Poisson or negative binomial distribution and log link (see B1).  

**Effect size reporting**  
- Difference in mean log10 counts at S3 between groups and 95% CI.  
- Or difference in mean change (S2→S3) between groups on log scale (interpretable as ratio of geometric means).

A3. Within-group assessment of change over time  
-----------------------------------------------  

**Test / Method**  
- Primary (if parametric assumptions hold):  
  - Paired t-tests (S1 vs S2; S2 vs S3; S1 vs S3) on log-transformed counts within each group.  
- Non-parametric:  
  - Friedman test per group to test overall time effect (S1, S2, S3).  
  - If significant, post-hoc pairwise Wilcoxon signed-rank tests (with multiplicity adjustment).  

**Purpose**  
- Quantify and test whether there is significant reduction in microbial load:  
  - After irrigation (S1 → S2).  
  - After medicament period (S2 → S3) within each group.  

**Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)**  
1. Data are paired (same tooth at different times) – pairing is naturally handled by paired tests or within-subject factor.  
2. Assessing within-group efficacy is clinically relevant (does each medicament significantly reduce bacteria from S2 to S3).  

**Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)**  
- Multiple paired comparisons inflate type I error if no correction is applied; also, parametric paired t-test assumes roughly symmetric distribution of differences on log scale.  

**When NOT to use**  
- Do not use paired t-test on raw counts if distributions are severely skewed with many zeros.  

**Alternative if assumptions violated**  
- Use Wilcoxon signed-rank tests on raw counts or log-transformed counts.  
- Use Friedman test for overall within-group time effect.

A4. Between-group comparison of change scores  
---------------------------------------------  

**Test / Method**  
- Compute per tooth:  
  - ΔS2–S3 = (log-bacteria at S3) − (log-bacteria at S2), or percent reduction.  
- Compare ΔS2–S3 between TAP vs Propolis using independent-samples t-test (on Δ) or Mann–Whitney U test.  

**Purpose**  
- Directly test whether the reduction attributable to the medicament differs between groups.  

**Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)**  
1. Change scores reduce baseline inter-tooth variability; comparing changes is intuitive for clinicians.  
2. Independent-samples test matches parallel-group design.  

**Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)**  
- Change scores may be heteroscedastic or non-normal, especially when baseline levels vary widely; may lead to violation of t-test assumptions.  

**When NOT to use**  
- Do not use t-test on change scores if there is clear non-normality on log scale or strong dependence of change on baseline.  

**Alternative if assumptions violated**  
- Mann–Whitney U test on change scores.  
- ANCOVA on S3 adjusting for S2 as covariate (see B2).

CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED TESTS  
-------------------------------  

B1. Generalized linear mixed model for count data  
-------------------------------------------------  

**Test / Method**  
- GLMM with:  
  - Outcome: bacterial colony counts per sample.  
  - Distribution: negative binomial (preferable if overdispersion evident) or Poisson.  
  - Link: log.  
  - Fixed effects: Group, Time, Group × Time.  
  - Random intercept for tooth ID.  

**Purpose**  
- Model the count nature of data directly without transformation, account for overdispersion and within-tooth correlation, and estimate rate ratios.  

**Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)**  
1. Microbial colony counts are discrete and often overdispersed; negative binomial GLMM directly fits such data better than Gaussian models.  
2. Handles repeated measures and unbalanced data (if some time points missing).  
3. Provides interpretable effect measures (e.g., incident rate ratios for TAP vs propolis at S3).  

**Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)**  
- With small sample size, parameter estimates can have large uncertainty; convergence issues may arise, especially with zero-inflation or sparse counts.  

**When NOT to use**  
- Do not use Poisson GLMM if there is clear overdispersion (variance >> mean) without accounting for it; instead use negative binomial or quasi-Poisson.  

**Alternative if assumptions violated**  
- Zero-inflated Poisson/negative binomial models if large proportion of zero counts.  
- Non-parametric repeated-measures methods as described in A3 and A4.

B2. ANCOVA for S3 bacterial counts adjusting for S2 (or S1)  
------------------------------------------------------------  

**Test / Method**  
- Analysis of covariance (ANCOVA):  
  - Outcome: log-bacterial count at S3.  
  - Predictor: Group (TAP vs propolis).  
  - Covariate: log-bacterial count at S2 (or S1).  

**Purpose**  
- Compare groups at the primary endpoint (post-medicament) while adjusting for any baseline (or pre-medicament) differences.  

**Why appropriate (≥2 reasons)**  
1. Statistical efficiency: adjusting for baseline improves power and precision in randomized trials, especially with small sample size.  
2. Controls for any residual imbalance in pre-medicament microbial load that may remain despite randomization.  

**Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason)**  
- ANCOVA assumes a linear relationship between baseline and follow-up on log scale and homogeneity of regression slopes across groups.  

**When NOT to use**  
- Do not use ANCOVA if baseline and follow-up counts have strongly non-linear relationship or if variance of residuals is highly unequal across groups.  

**Alternative if assumptions violated**  
- Non-parametric: stratify by baseline category (e.g., low vs high) and compare S3 within strata or use rank ANCOVA methods; or rely on comparison of change scores with Mann–Whitney.

TASK 3 – INAPPROPRIATE TESTS AND WHY  
====================================  

1. **Independent-samples t-test on raw (untransformed) CFU counts at each time point without considering repeated measures**  
   - Why inappropriate:  
     - Ignores within-tooth correlation across S1–S3.  
     - Raw counts are typically skewed and zero-inflated; t-test normality assumptions likely violated.  

2. **Multiple independent-samples t-tests at each time point (S1, S2, S3) for group comparisons, ignoring baseline and change**  
   - Why inappropriate:  
     - Inflates type I error due to multiple testing.  
     - Does not focus on the clinical question (change over time due to medicament).  
     - S1 and S2 should be similar between groups by randomization; testing repeatedly is unnecessary and potentially misleading.  

3. **Chi-square test for CFU counts treated as categories with arbitrary cutoffs**  
   - Why inappropriate:  
     - Dichotomizing or categorizing a continuous count outcome discards information and reduces power.  
     - Small sample leads to small expected cell counts, violating chi-square assumptions.  

4. **Unpaired analysis of pre-post differences ignoring the paired nature (e.g., treating S2 and S3 as independent samples)**  
   - Why inappropriate:  
     - Violates independence assumption; underestimates standard errors; may lead to inflated type I error.  

5. **Simple linear regression of S3 on group without adjustment for baseline when baseline imbalance exists**  
   - Why inappropriate:  
     - If baseline counts differ materially between groups (despite randomization), ignoring them may bias treatment effect estimates.  

6. **Parametric repeated-measures ANOVA on raw counts without checking assumptions**  
   - Why inappropriate:  
     - Repeated-measures ANOVA assumes multivariate normality and sphericity; raw CFU counts likely violate both.  
     - Better to use log transformation or GLMM.  

7. **Correlation tests (e.g., Pearson) between time points treated as independent variables**  
   - Why inappropriate:  
     - Correlation of repeated measures within the same subject is trivial and not informative; focus should be on treatment effect, not correlation among repeated measures.  

8. **Equivalence or non-inferiority tests without pre-specified margins and design**  
   - Why inappropriate:  
     - This trial is described as comparing efficacy, not designed with pre-specified non-inferiority margins; performing post-hoc non-inferiority testing would be invalid.
