General principles

CATEGORY A – MANDATORY ANALYSES  

A1. Descriptive statistics and baseline comparability  

Test/Tool: Descriptive summaries + baseline comparisons (ANOVA or Kruskal–Wallis for continuous; χ²/Fisher’s exact for categorical).  

Purpose:  
- Summarize participant and tooth characteristics and baseline microbiological measures.  
- Assess whether randomization produced comparable groups at baseline (especially TBL at time point 1 and key demographics).

Why appropriate:  
1) Randomized trials still require documenting baseline balance; important for interpretation.  
2) TBL at time point 1 may already differ due to first-visit treatment; baseline comparability ensures observed differences later are less likely due to baseline imbalance.

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- For small sample sizes, formal hypothesis testing of baseline differences may be underpowered and not very informative; descriptive emphasis is preferred.

When NOT to use:  
- Overemphasis on p-values for baseline variables (use descriptive interpretation rather than inferential statements).

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- If normality for continuous variables is implausible, use nonparametric tests (Kruskal–Wallis) and report medians/IQR instead of means/SD.

A2. Transformation and assessment of TBL distribution  

Test/Tool:  
- Examine histograms, Q–Q plots, skewness.  
- Possibly apply log10-transformation: log10(TBL + c) (c small constant if zeros exist).  

Purpose:  
- Decide whether parametric tests (ANOVA/linear mixed models) are appropriate.  
- Stabilize variance and approximate normal distribution.

Why appropriate:  
1) Microbial CFU counts are typically log-normally distributed; log transformation is standard.  
2) Improves interpretability (differences on log scale correspond to ratios on original scale).

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- If a large proportion of values are zero (below detection), log transformation can be problematic without adding arbitrary constants.

When NOT to use:  
- If the majority of observations are zero or near zero (floor effect); modeling approaches for zero-inflated or count data may be more suitable.

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- Use nonparametric methods for repeated measures, or generalized linear mixed models (GLMM) with appropriate link (e.g., Poisson/negative binomial) for count data.

A3. Primary outcome analysis – repeated measures comparison of TBL  

Primary test (parametric): Two-way repeated-measures ANOVA or Linear Mixed-Effects Model (LMM) on log10 TBL.  

- Factors:  
  - Between-subject: Group (NaOCl vs Laser vs Combination).  
  - Within-subject: Time (3 levels: after temporary removal, after chemomechanical prep, after adjunct).  
- Outcomes:  
  - Group × Time interaction (primary): whether change over time differs between groups.  
  - Within-group time effects: degree of reduction over steps.  
  - Between-group differences at each time point, especially at time point 3.  

Purpose:  
- Evaluate if adjunctive laser (alone or combined with NaOCl) yields additional TBL reduction relative to NaOCl only.  
- Account for within-patient correlation across repeated measurements.

Why appropriate:  
1) Data structure is clearly repeated measures with same patients evaluated at three time points; ANOVA/LMM handles within-subject correlation.  
2) LMM can handle missing data at some time points and allows flexible modeling of covariance structure, which is important for clinical data.

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- With small sample in each group (n=19), assumptions of sphericity and normality may be difficult to verify; violations can affect ANOVA results.

When NOT to use:  
- If TBL distribution remains heavily non-normal even after transformation.  
- If many patients have missing time points or unbalanced design.  
- If sphericity is severely violated and corrections (Greenhouse–Geisser) still leave unstable estimates, particularly in small sample.

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- Nonparametric: Friedman test (within-group) + post-hoc Wilcoxon signed-rank, combined with between-group tests on change scores via Kruskal–Wallis/Mann–Whitney.  
- GLMM for counts (Poisson/negative binomial) or for log-transformed continuous with robust standard errors.  

Implementation details (recommended):  
- Prefer LMM over classical repeated-measures ANOVA:  
  - Model: logTBL_ij = β0 + β1*Group_i + β2*Time_j + β3*Group_i*Time_j + random intercept (patient) + ε_ij.  
  - Estimate marginal means at each time × group.  
  - Use appropriate covariance structure (e.g., unstructured or compound symmetry), guided by AIC/BIC.  
  - Report estimated mean differences and 95% CIs on log scale and back-transform to ratios.

A4. Analysis of TBL reduction/change between key steps  

Test: ANOVA (or Kruskal–Wallis) on change scores (ΔTBL).  

- Define pre-specified contrasts:  
  - ΔTBL_1-2: change from time 1 to time 2 (effect of chemomechanical preparation; same in all groups).  
  - ΔTBL_2-3: change from time 2 to time 3 (effect of adjunctive protocol; primary comparison among groups).  

Purpose:  
- Provide clear, clinically interpretable measure of additional reduction attributable to adjunctive step.  
- Serve as complementary analysis to LMM.

Why appropriate:  
1) Change scores are straightforward and directly reflect adjunct effect, especially ΔTBL_2-3.  
2) Between-group ANOVA on ΔTBL_2-3 is effectively testing the group × time interaction using a simpler approach.

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Change scores can have larger variance and may be less efficient than mixed models; potential regression to the mean when baseline differs between groups.

When NOT to use:  
- If baseline TBL (time point 2) is substantially imbalanced between groups; then an ANCOVA approach is preferable (post value adjusted for pre value).

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- ANCOVA: model logTBL_3 as outcome, group as factor, logTBL_2 as covariate.  
- Nonparametric: rank-based ANCOVA or compare rank changes (Kruskal–Wallis on Δ).

A5. Analysis of presence/absence of specific bacteria  

Test: McNemar’s test (within-group over time) and χ²/Fisher’s exact tests (between groups at time point 3).  

Purpose:  
- Evaluate whether specific pathogens (e.g., E. faecalis, P. gingivalis) are eradicated more frequently in certain adjunctive protocols.  
- Provide species-level insights complementing TBL.

Why appropriate:  
1) Presence/absence is a natural outcome when many counts are low or below detection.  
2) McNemar’s test is suitable for paired binary data (within patient, over time); χ²/Fisher’s suitable for comparing proportions across groups.

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- With small numbers of positives for some species, χ² approximations may be poor; very sparse data can limit interpretability.

When NOT to use:  
- When event counts are extremely low (e.g., species present in only 1–2 patients), tests become unstable and outputs misleading.

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- Fisher’s exact test for between-group comparisons.  
- For repeated binary outcomes, consider logistic mixed-effects models (GLMM) with random intercept for patient.

CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED ANALYSES  

B1. Logistic regression / GLMM for species presence  

Test: Logistic mixed-effects model for key bacteria (e.g., E. faecalis) over time and group.  

Purpose:  
- Quantify odds of persistence of specific bacteria at time 3 across groups, adjusting for baseline presence.  
- Handle repeated measures and correlated binary outcomes.

Why appropriate:  
1) Provides adjusted odds ratios for group differences in bacterial persistence, improving interpretability over simple proportions.  
2) Accounts for repeated measures and varying time effects; can incorporate covariates (e.g., tooth type, baseline TBL).

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Sample size may be insufficient for stable logistic regression, particularly if species prevalence is low.

When NOT to use:  
- If observed events per group per time are <5–10, models may not converge or estimates become unstable.

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- Collapse across time and report simple Fisher’s exact tests on presence at time point 3.

B2. ANCOVA for final TBL  

Test: ANCOVA on logTBL at time point 3, adjusting for logTBL at time point 2 (or 1).  

Purpose:  
- Compare final bacterial loads between groups while adjusting for any baseline differences.  
- Complement LMM by focusing on clinically relevant final status.

Why appropriate:  
1) Adjusts for any imbalance in bacterial load just before adjunctive step.  
2) Typically more powerful than ANOVA on change scores and interpretable as adjusted final difference.

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- With small sample sizes, ANCOVA may be sensitive to violation of homogeneity of regression slopes (group × baseline interaction).

When NOT to use:  
- If group × baseline interaction is significant (slopes differ) or baseline strongly non-linear relative to outcome.

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- Use rank-based ANCOVA or nonparametric ANCOVA approaches (e.g., Quade’s test) or revert to stratified analyses by baseline strata.

B3. Nonparametric repeated measures analysis  

Test:  
- Within-group: Friedman test for TBL across 3 time points.  
- Between-group: Kruskal–Wallis tests on change scores, with post-hoc pairwise Mann–Whitney U tests (Bonferroni-adjusted).  

Purpose:  
- Provide robustness to non-normality in TBL distributions, including outliers or heavy skewness.  
- Serve as sensitivity analyses confirming conclusions of parametric methods.

Why appropriate:  
1) Doesn’t assume normal distribution; suitable for small sample sizes and skewed outcomes.  
2) Simple and interpretable in terms of ranks and median changes.

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Loses information about magnitude of differences on original scale; less powerful compared to parametric models when assumptions hold.

When NOT to use:  
- If there are many ties or zero values, interpretation of rank tests can be less precise (but still generally acceptable).

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- Bootstrapped estimates of mean/median differences with confidence intervals, rather than formal nonparametric tests.

B4. Effect size estimation and confidence intervals  

Tool:  
- Report Cohen’s d (or f) for continuous outcomes and risk ratios/odds ratios for categorical outcomes, with 95% CIs.  

Purpose:  
- Quantify magnitude of differences and provide clinically meaningful interpretation beyond p-values.

Why appropriate:  
1) Effect sizes are crucial in clinical trials to assess practical significance.  
2) Allows readers to compare effect magnitudes across interventions and with previous studies.

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- In very small samples, effect size estimates can be unstable; CIs may be wide.

When NOT to use:  
- Never “not use”; but interpret cautiously when N is small and variability high.

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- Use nonparametric effect sizes (e.g., Hodges–Lehmann estimator, Cliff’s delta).

B5. Multiplicity control for multiple comparisons  

Test:  
- P-value adjustment (e.g., Bonferroni, Holm) for multiple pairwise comparisons (e.g., group comparisons at time 3, multiple species).  

Purpose:  
- Limit inflation of Type I error due to multiple hypothesis testing (e.g., multiple pairwise group comparisons, numerous bacterial species).

Why appropriate:  
1) The study evaluates several group contrasts and multiple bacterial species; multiplicity control helps prevent spurious significant findings.  
2) Holm or Benjamini–Hochberg procedures balance error control and power.

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Overly strict adjustment (e.g., Bonferroni) can reduce power in small studies, masking potential true effects.

When NOT to use:  
- For clearly pre-specified primary comparisons (e.g., primary outcome, primary contrast), strict adjustment is not necessary; but still recommended for exploratory secondary analyses.

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- Use false discovery rate (FDR) methods where appropriate.

B6. Sensitivity analyses for missing data  

Test/Approach:  
- LMM under MAR assumption; compare with complete case analysis.  
- If missingness appears non-random, conduct sensitivity analyses (e.g., worst-case imputation).  

Purpose:  
- Check robustness of primary conclusions to missing microbiological data (e.g., lost samples).

Why appropriate:  
1) In clinical settings, some samples might be lost or insufficient; proper handling is needed to avoid bias.  
2) Mixed models naturally handle unbalanced data if MAR holds.

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- If missing data are extensive or clearly MNAR (e.g., missing because of high pain), standard methods may be inadequate.

When NOT to use:  
- If missing data are negligible (<5%); then simple complete case analysis may suffice.

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- Pattern-mixture models, multiple imputation with sensitivity scenarios.

TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS  

Tests that should NOT be used and why:

1. Simple independent-samples t-test at each time point ignoring repeated measures  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Ignores within-subject correlation; inflates Type I error and discards within-patient information.  
  - Fails to jointly consider changes over time and group × time interaction.  

2. Paired t-test within each group only (without between-group comparison)  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Answers only whether each intervention reduces bacteria, not whether one is better than another.  
  - Does not exploit trial’s comparative design; fails to answer primary research question.  

3. One-way ANOVA on raw (non-transformed) TBL without checking assumptions  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - TBL is typically highly skewed; ANOVA on raw counts violates normality and homoscedasticity.  
  - P-values and confidence intervals become unreliable.  

4. Chi-square tests on small cell counts without exact methods  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - For species presence with small numbers, χ² approximations are poor; inflated Type I or II error.  
  - Fisher’s exact is needed.  

5. Multiple unadjusted pairwise tests on many species and time points  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Severe multiplicity issues; high false-positive rate.  
  - Misleading in a small trial, particularly for exploratory endpoints.  

6. Non-inferiority/superiority testing without pre-specified margins and design  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Trial appears designed as a superiority trial without explicit non-inferiority margins or power.  
  - Post-hoc non-inferiority testing is not valid.  

7. Logistic regression with numerous covariates relative to events  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Small N (19 per group) cannot support multivariable logistic models with many predictors; high risk of overfitting.  

8. Structural equation modeling or complex multivariate methods  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Sample size is insufficient; complexity not justified by data or objectives.
