### CATEGORY A — MANDATORY (analysis would be incomplete without)

#### A1) Descriptive statistics by group (with clustering-aware summaries)
- **Test/name:** Descriptive summaries (means/SD, medians/IQR; counts/%).
- **Purpose:** Characterize baseline and outcomes by restorative strategy and ferrule stratum.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1) Required to interpret clinical/economic comparability and generalizability.
  2) Cost data are skewed; reporting both mean and median is informative for decision-making and robustness.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):** Not inferential; cannot quantify uncertainty alone.
- **When NOT to use:** Never “not used,” but avoid relying solely on means if extreme skew/outliers dominate.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:** Not applicable (no assumptions), but add robust summaries (trimmed mean).

#### A2) Primary cost comparison using a regression model that accounts for clustering
- **Test/name:** **Generalized Linear Model (GLM) for costs** with **cluster-robust SEs** or **GEE** (e.g., Gamma family, log link).
- **Purpose:** Estimate adjusted mean cost differences between strategies while accounting for patient-level clustering and stratification factors.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1) **Costs are typically skewed and positive**; Gamma-log GLM is standard for cost outcomes.
  2) **Multiple teeth per patient** violates independence; cluster-robust/GEE addresses correlation.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):** Gamma may fit poorly if costs include many zeros or have heavy tails not well captured.
- **When this test should NOT be used:** If costs include **substantial zeros** (e.g., no follow-up/repair costs) requiring a two-part model; or if model diagnostics show severe misfit.
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
  - **Two-part (hurdle) model**: logistic for any-cost + GLM for positive costs.
  - **Log-normal model** with smearing estimator for retransformation.
  - **Nonparametric cluster bootstrap** for mean difference.

#### A3) Global comparison across four groups + pairwise comparisons with multiplicity control
- **Test/name:** **Wald test / likelihood ratio test** for overall group effect in the GLM, plus **planned pairwise contrasts** with multiplicity adjustment (e.g., Holm).
- **Purpose:** Determine whether costs differ among strategies and identify where differences lie.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1) Four-arm comparison requires a global test before/alongside pairwise inference.
  2) Controls type I error inflation from multiple contrasts.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):** Multiplicity control reduces power; if study is explicitly decision-analytic with pre-specified primary contrast only, broad pairwise testing may be unnecessary.
- **When NOT to use:** If only one **pre-specified primary comparison** is justified (e.g., GFP+CR vs all others) and others are exploratory.
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:** Bootstrap-based simultaneous CIs; permutation tests respecting clustering (less common but possible).

#### A4) Verification that cost-minimization framework is justified (clinical equivalence/non-inferiority evidence from RCT)
- **Test/name:** **Equivalence or non-inferiority analysis** for primary clinical outcome(s) (success/survival), consistent with the parent RCT margin (the text mentions an 18% margin context).
- **Purpose:** Cost-minimization is valid only if outcomes are **clinically equivalent** (or non-inferior) across strategies.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1) Cost-minimization requires demonstrating no meaningful clinical differences, not merely “non-significant.”
  2) The methods cite a margin-like concept (>18% difference excluded), aligning with equivalence framing.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):** The excerpt does not provide complete endpoint definitions/time horizon; equivalence margin must be clinically pre-specified.
- **When NOT to use:** If the parent RCT was not designed for equivalence/non-inferiority and no credible margin exists; then use cost-effectiveness instead.
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:** If equivalence cannot be supported, switch to **cost-effectiveness analysis** (incremental cost per failure avoided; ICER).

### CATEGORY B — RECOMMENDED (improves rigor)

#### B1) Mixed-effects model for costs (patient random intercept)
- **Test/name:** **Generalized linear mixed model (GLMM)** (e.g., Gamma-log with random intercept for patient).
- **Purpose:** Model tooth-level costs while explicitly modeling within-patient correlation.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1) Naturally handles **multiple teeth per participant**.
  2) Provides subject-level variance estimates; may improve efficiency vs robust SEs.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):** Can be unstable with small cluster sizes or convergence issues under Gamma/log.
- **When NOT to use:** If many patients contribute only one tooth and convergence/fit is poor; or if model complexity is not supported by data.
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:** GEE with robust SE; cluster bootstrap.

#### B2) Cluster bootstrap for mean cost differences and CIs
- **Test/name:** **Nonparametric bootstrap resampling at patient level**.
- **Purpose:** Obtain CIs for mean/median cost differences without heavy distributional assumptions and preserving clustering.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1) Cost data are skewed; bootstrap CIs are often more reliable than normal approximations.
  2) Resampling by patient preserves correlation among teeth.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):** With few clusters, bootstrap performance degrades.
- **When NOT to use:** Very small number of patients (clusters) or extreme leverage points dominating.
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:** Use model-based robust SEs; Bayesian models.

#### B3) Adjustment for stratification factors and prognostic covariates
- **Test/name:** Multivariable GLM/GLMM including **tooth type**, ferrule stratum, and potentially other baseline factors available (not listed here).
- **Purpose:** Improve precision and correct for any chance imbalance despite randomization.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1) Randomization was stratified by **tooth type**; including it respects design and improves efficiency.
  2) Costs may differ by tooth type and ferrule, reducing residual variance.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):** Overfitting if too many covariates relative to sample.
- **When NOT to use:** If covariate data are missing extensively or measured post-randomization.
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:** Penalized regression; covariate adjustment via ANCOVA-style models on transformed costs.

#### B4) Interaction testing (effect modification by ferrule and tooth type)
- **Test/name:** **Interaction terms** (group×ferrule, group×tooth type) in cost model.
- **Purpose:** Evaluate whether cost differences depend on ferrule presence or tooth type.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1) Treatment options differ by ferrule status (two randomization pathways), so heterogeneity is plausible.
  2) Cost drivers (crown vs composite) likely vary by tooth type.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):** Limited sample within subgroups yields low power and unstable estimates.
- **When NOT to use:** If subgroup sizes are too small or not pre-specified.
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:** Stratified analyses with cautious interpretation; Bayesian hierarchical partial pooling.

#### B5) Missing data handling with multiple imputation (if applicable)
- **Test/name:** **Multiple imputation** (preferably multilevel MI if tooth-level and patient-level variables).
- **Purpose:** Reduce bias and improve efficiency if cost/outcome data are missing.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1) Missing follow-up/repair costs can bias mean costs if not MCAR.
  2) MI can incorporate predictors of missingness and outcome.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):** If missingness is MNAR and no sensitivity analysis is done.
- **When NOT to use:** Very high missingness with weak predictors; structural missingness.
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:** Pattern-mixture or selection models; best/worst-case sensitivity analyses.

## TASK 3 — INAPPROPRIATE TESTS (should NOT be used)

1) **Multiple independent-samples t-tests without multiplicity control**
- **Why not:** Inflates type I error; ignores four-arm design and clustering of teeth within patients.

2) **One-way ANOVA on raw costs assuming normality and independence**
- **Why not:** Cost data are typically right-skewed/heteroscedastic; independence violated due to multiple teeth per participant.

3) **Chi-square tests on tooth-level outcomes ignoring clustering**
- **Why not:** Teeth within the same patient are correlated; standard chi-square yields incorrect SEs/p-values.

4) **Simple linear regression on log(cost) with naive back-transformation**
- **Why not:** Produces biased mean estimates unless a smearing correction (or appropriate model) is used; also must address clustering.

5) **Stepwise variable selection as primary analytic strategy**
- **Why not:** Unstable inference, inflated error rates, and undermines randomized design principles.

  - Kaplan–Meier survival curves + log-rank test; Cox proportional hazards regression.  

- Purpose:  
  - Compare time-to-failure (loss of restoration, catastrophic failure, etc.) between the four restorative strategies and confirm clinical equivalence.  

- Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):  
  1) Time-to-event data with censoring (patients lost to follow-up or event not yet occurred) are best handled with survival methods; simple proportions would misrepresent follow-up differences.  
  2) Cox models allow adjustment for covariates (tooth type, ferrule status, patient-level factors), providing hazard ratios and confidence intervals.  

- Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason):  
  - If follow-up time is uniform and very short, with few outcomes, survival methods may be underpowered or unstable.  

- When NOT to use:  
  - When event counts are extremely low (e.g., <5 per group), leading to non-convergence or highly unstable hazard ratio estimates.  

- Alternatives if assumptions violated:  
  - Non-proportional hazards in Cox:
    * Use time-varying coefficients or stratified Cox.  
  - Very sparse events:
    * Use exact methods (exact log-rank) or compare cumulative incidence at fixed time point using risk differences with exact CIs.

A3. Primary economic outcome: comparison of mean cost per tooth among the four arms

- Test name:  
  - Generalized linear model (GLM) for cost data (e.g., log-link gamma regression) or nonparametric bootstrap of mean cost differences.  

- Purpose:  
  - Estimate and compare mean costs per tooth (and per patient if relevant) between groups; identify which strategy has lowest mean cost.  

- Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):  
  1) Cost data are typically right-skewed and heteroscedastic; GLM with gamma distribution and log-link (or log-normal model) accommodates this better than ordinary linear regression.  
  2) Regression framework allows adjustment for clustering (multiple teeth per patient), tooth type, ferrule status, and follow-up duration, improving precision and accounting for design.  

- Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason):  
  - In small samples, GLM distributional assumptions (e.g., gamma) may not fit well, leading to biased standard errors or poor convergence.  

- When NOT to use:  
  - Very small sample sizes per group (e.g., <15) with highly irregular cost distributions; GLM parameters may be poorly estimated.  

- Alternatives if assumptions violated:  
  - Nonparametric bootstrap:
    * Compare mean costs using simple group means and bootstrap confidence intervals for pairwise differences.  
  - Transform cost (e.g., log-cost) and use linear mixed models; interpret carefully.

A4. Accounting for clustering (multiple teeth per patient)

- Test name:  
  - Multilevel (mixed-effects) models or generalized estimating equations (GEE).  
  - For cost outcomes: multilevel GLM; for binary success: multilevel logistic regression.  

- Purpose:  
  - Adjust for non-independence of teeth within the same patient, ensuring valid standard errors and CIs.  

- Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):  
  1) Each patient can have multiple teeth, generating intra-patient correlation; ignoring this violates independence assumptions and underestimates variance.  
  2) Mixed or GEE models provide patient-level random effects or robust standard errors, which are standard in clustered RCTs and economic evaluations.  

- Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason):  
  - If most patients contribute only one tooth, or if clustering is minimal (intraclass correlation near zero), complex hierarchical models may offer minimal gain and complicate interpretation.  

- When NOT to use:  
  - When each patient has exactly one tooth (no clustering) or cluster size is so small and variable that multilevel models fail to converge.  

- Alternatives if assumptions violated:  
  - Use cluster-robust (sandwich) standard errors in standard regression models with patient as clustering unit.

A5. Cost-minimization conclusion (equivalence assessment and cost comparison)

- Test name:  
  - Equivalence/noninferiority approach for clinical outcome (risk difference CI) + cost comparison.  

- Purpose:  
  - Formally demonstrate that clinical outcomes are sufficiently similar (within predefined equivalence margin) to justify cost-minimization, then identify the lowest-cost strategy.  

- Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):  
  1) Cost-minimization is only justified when outcomes are clinically equivalent; equivalence tests clarify this assumption beyond “no significant difference.”  
  2) Using risk differences and pre-set margin (e.g., ±18% as mentioned in sample size) aligns with initial power calculations and supports the economic interpretation.  

- Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason):  
  - If the original trial was not explicitly designed as an equivalence trial (e.g., margin not prespecified in protocol), formal equivalence claims may be controversial.  

- When NOT to use:  
  - If substantial differences in survival/success are observed (CI well outside any reasonable equivalence margin); then cost–effectiveness (cost per success) rather than cost-minimization is required.  

- Alternatives if assumptions violated:  
  - Perform cost-effectiveness analysis (incremental cost per additional success) using incremental cost-effectiveness ratios and cost-effectiveness planes.

CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED ANALYSES

B1. Sensitivity analyses on cost assumptions

- Test name:  
  - One-way and probabilistic sensitivity analysis; scenario analyses.  

- Purpose:  
  - Assess robustness of cost-minimization results to variation in key parameters (unit costs, discount rates, failure rates, repair frequencies).  

- Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):  
  1) Economic evaluations inevitably rely on assumptions and context-specific unit costs; sensitivity analyses show how robust conclusions are to these variations.  
  2) Guidance documents (e.g., CHEERS) recommend examining uncertainty in costs and outcomes.  

- Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason):  
  - If only very narrow or fixed institution-specific costs are available and there is limited variability, some probabilistic sensitivity analyses may not be feasible.  

- When NOT to use:  
  - When data for uncertainty distributions are entirely unavailable and any assumed distributions would be speculative beyond acceptable bounds.  

- Alternatives if assumptions violated:  
  - Use deterministic scenario analyses (e.g., minimum and maximum plausible costs) instead of full probabilistic sensitivity analysis.

B2. Incremental cost analysis with pairwise comparisons

- Test name:  
  - Pairwise comparisons of mean cost differences using nonparametric bootstrap (with bias-corrected, accelerated CIs).  

- Purpose:  
  - Quantify incremental cost differences between each pair of strategies (e.g., GFP+CR+ vs GFP+MC+, GFP vs CPC in no-ferrule group), beyond overall ANOVA-type comparisons.  

- Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):  
  1) Allows clear statements like “GFP+CR+ costs X less per tooth compared to CPC+MC” with uncertainty intervals.  
  2) Nonparametric bootstrap makes few distributional assumptions, well-suited to skewed cost data and modest sample sizes.  

- Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason):  
  - Bootstrap CIs may be unstable if sample size is very small or if a large fraction of cost observations are zero.  

- When NOT to use:  
  - When each group has very few observations (<20) and extreme skewness; resampling may not approximate sampling distribution well.  

- Alternatives if assumptions violated:  
  - Use nonparametric tests (e.g., Mann–Whitney U) to compare distributional differences; present medians with IQRs and discuss with caution.

- Why it may not be appropriate (≥1 reason):  
  - Subgroups may be small, leading to underpowered tests and potentially spurious findings.  

- When NOT to use:  
  - When prespecified subgroup analysis was not in protocol and sample sizes per subgroup are inadequate; risk of data dredging.  

- Alternatives if assumptions violated:  
  - Perform descriptive subgroup summaries without formal interaction tests; clearly label as exploratory.

TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS FOR THIS STUDY

Here are tests/approaches that should NOT be used and why:

1. Simple unadjusted t-tests or ANOVA on raw costs without addressing skewness and clustering  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Cost data are typically skewed and heteroscedastic; t-tests/ANOVA assume approximate normality and equal variances.  
  - Ignoring clustering (multiple teeth per patient) violates independence and underestimates standard errors.  
- Preferred: GLM (gamma/log-link) or nonparametric bootstrap with cluster-robust SEs.

2. Multiple simple chi-square tests for clinical outcomes without accounting for time-to-event  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Failures occur over time with censoring; collapsing to “success/failure at end of follow-up” discards information and biases comparisons if follow-up differs.  
- Preferred: Kaplan–Meier + log-rank, Cox regression.

3. Parametric tests of baseline characteristics interpreted as “proof” of randomization success  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Randomization ensures comparability on expectation; p-values for baseline differences are misleading and often discouraged.  
- Preferred: Descriptive comparison, with attention to magnitude rather than significance.

4. Multiple pairwise comparisons using Bonferroni-corrected t-tests on costs without pre-specified hierarchy or model  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Inflates type II error; doesn’t exploit full regression framework; ignores cluster and skewness issues.  
- Preferred: Regression-based comparisons + bootstrap.

5. Using per-protocol analysis as primary basis for cost-minimization  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Economic conclusions should be based primarily on intention-to-treat populations to preserve randomization and avoid selection bias.  
- Preferred: ITT analysis; per-protocol only as sensitivity analysis.

6. Applying standard linear regression on log-transformed cost and then back-transforming means without correction  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Naive back-transformation (exp of mean log cost) yields geometric means, not arithmetic means, which are required for cost aggregation; may bias conclusions.  
- Preferred: GLM with appropriate link or use smearing estimator if log-transformation is used.

7. Ignoring multiplicity in numerous post hoc subgroup tests with standard p-values  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Increases risk of false positives; subgroup findings may be spurious.  
- Preferred: Limit subgroup analyses to prespecified ones; treat others as exploratory.

TASK 4: SUMMARY OF OVERALL ANALYTICAL APPROACH

- The RCT provides clinical outcome data (success/survival of post-retained restorations) across four strategies with and without ferrule, using stratified randomization by tooth type and multiple teeth per patient.  
- The economic evaluation is a cost-minimization analysis, appropriate because the underlying RCT found no meaningful differences in primary clinical outcomes.  
- Accordingly, the primary analysis should:
  1) Confirm approximate clinical equivalence using appropriate survival methods and/or equivalence margins.  
  2) Estimate mean cost per tooth (and optionally per patient) for each arm using models suited to skewed data and clustering (GLM with gamma/log-link or bootstrap with cluster-robust SEs).  
  3) Compare costs between arms through adjusted regression and bootstrap confidence intervals for incremental cost differences.  
  4) Address clustering (multiple teeth per patient), stratification factors (tooth type), and ferrule status in multilevel or GEE frameworks.  
  5) Conduct sensitivity analyses (unit cost variation, discounting, alternative failure rates) to assess robustness.  

This approach ensures that the economic conclusions (identification of the least costly clinically equivalent strategy) are statistically valid, transparent, and aligned with contemporary standards for clinical trial–based health economic evaluations in dentistry.
