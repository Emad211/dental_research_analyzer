### TASK 1: INFORMATION EXTRACTION

**Study Characteristics:**

1. **Study Design**:  
   - Health economic evaluation nested within a parallel-arm randomized clinical trial (RCT).

2. **Objective**:  
   - To economically assess four post-retained restorative strategies for endodontically treated teeth using cost-minimization analysis.

3. **Groups/Comparators**:  
   - Four restorative strategies based on the type of post and restoration:
     - **No ferrule present**:
       - Glass fiber post (GFP) + metal-ceramic crown (MC) (n=71).
       - Cast post and core (CPC) + metal-ceramic crown (MC) (n=79).
     - **2-mm ferrule present**:
       - GFP + metal-ceramic crown (MC) (n=36).
       - GFP + direct composite resin restoration (CR) (n=39).

4. **Sample Size**:  
   - Total participants: 141.  
   - Total teeth restored: 225.  
   - Sample size calculation: 64 participants required to detect a difference >18% between groups with 90% confidence and 90% power.

5. **Population**:  
   - Inclusion criteria:  
     - Good oral health (no active caries or periodontal disease).  
     - Presence of one or more endodontically treated anterior or posterior teeth with or without ferrules (2 mm or 0–0.5 mm).  
     - Bilateral posterior occlusal contact.  
   - Exclusion criteria:  
     - Periodontal or occlusal problems.  
     - Large prosthesis (Kennedy class I or II) in the opposing arch.

6. **Randomization**:  
   - Stratified by tooth type (anterior, molar, premolar) to minimize bias.  
   - Randomization sequence generated using computerized random number tables.  
   - Allocation concealment with sealed opaque envelopes.

7. **Variables**:  
   - Independent variables: Type of post (GFP or CPC), type of restoration (MC or CR), ferrule presence (2 mm or none).  
   - Dependent variables: Cost of intervention, success rates, survival rates.

8. **Measurements**:  
   - Primary clinical outcome: Success and survival rates of restorations.  
   - Economic outcome: Cost-minimization analysis comparing costs of the four strategies.

9. **Data Collection Methods**:  
   - Clinical procedures performed by 25 supervised undergraduate students.  
   - Costs calculated based on materials (e.g., CoCr alloy, resin cement) and operative techniques.  
   - Success rates derived from the RCT.

### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS
1. **Chi-Square Test (or Fisher’s Exact Test)**  
   - **Purpose**: Compare categorical success rates between groups.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. The primary outcome (success rates) is categorical.  
     2. Groups are independent, and data are stratified by ferrule presence.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Small sample sizes in subgroups may violate assumptions.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If expected cell counts are <5.  
   - **Alternative**: Fisher’s Exact Test for small sample sizes.

2. **One-Way ANOVA (or Kruskal-Wallis Test)**  
   - **Purpose**: Compare mean costs across the four groups.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Cost is a continuous variable.  
     2. There are more than two independent groups.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: If cost data are not normally distributed.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If data violate normality and homogeneity of variance.  
   - **Alternative**: Kruskal-Wallis Test for non-normal data.

3. **Kaplan-Meier Survival Analysis**  
   - **Purpose**: Compare survival rates of restorations over time.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Survival analysis accounts for time-to-event data.  
     2. Censoring (e.g., lost to follow-up) can be handled.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: If follow-up times are inconsistent or incomplete.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If no time-to-event data are available.  
   - **Alternative**: Cox proportional hazards model for multivariable analysis.

---

#### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED TESTS
1. **Logistic Regression**  
   - **Purpose**: Assess the association between success rates and predictors (e.g., ferrule presence, type of post).  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Outcome is binary (success vs. failure).  
     2. Adjusts for potential confounders.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Requires large sample sizes for stable estimates.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If sample size is insufficient.  
   - **Alternative**: Exact logistic regression for small samples.

2. **Cost-Effectiveness Analysis (CEA)**  
   - **Purpose**: Evaluate the cost per unit of success for each strategy.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Integrates economic and clinical outcomes.  
     2. Provides actionable insights for decision-making.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Requires detailed cost and effectiveness data.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If cost data are incomplete.  
   - **Alternative**: Sensitivity analysis to address uncertainty.

---

#### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL TESTS
1. **Subgroup Analysis**  
   - **Purpose**: Evaluate outcomes within specific subgroups (e.g., anterior vs. posterior teeth).  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Identifies differential effects within subpopulations.  
     2. Adds granularity to findings.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Risk of type I error due to multiple comparisons.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If sample sizes in subgroups are too small.  
   - **Alternative**: Pool subgroups to increase power.

2. **Sensitivity Analysis**  
   - **Purpose**: Assess robustness of cost-minimization results to changes in key assumptions.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Addresses uncertainty in cost estimates.  
     2. Enhances credibility of findings.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Requires detailed cost breakdowns.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If cost data are too aggregated.  
   - **Alternative**: Scenario analysis for broader assumptions.
