## TASK 1 — Information extraction (study characteristics)

### Study identification
- **Title/topic:** Radiographic evaluation of the quality of root canal filling performed by undergraduate students
- **Setting:** Riyadh Elm University, Al Namuthajya Campus (Saudi Arabia)
- **Timeframe:** Treatments performed **2013–2019**
- **Ethics:** Approved (RC/IRB/2018/1356). Data retrieved anonymously.

### Objectives
- **Primary aim:** Assess **quality of root canal filling** performed by **undergraduate female students**.
- **Secondary aim:** Assess **frequency of procedural errors** in obturation (as defined radiographically).

### Study design
- **Retrospective observational** (radiographic record review / chart review).
- **Unit of analysis:** **Tooth** (not patient), based on post-endodontic radiographs.

### Sample
- **Sample size:** **306 teeth** (anterior and posterior).
- **Operators:** Female undergraduate dental students (**4th, 5th, 6th year**).
- **Sampling method:** “Randomly selected from patient charts” (no further details).
- **Exclusion criteria:**
  - Incomplete treatment
  - Missing pre- or post-operative radiographs
  - Poor-quality radiographs
  - Radiographs showing **<2 mm** of periapical region
  - Non-evaluable radiographs due to anatomical superimposition

### Data sources and collection
- Retrieved from patient dental records:
  1. File number
  2. **Patient age**
  3. **Date** of endodontic treatment
  4. **Pulpal and periapical diagnosis**
  5. **Tooth number**
  6. Root canal filling procedure details (largely standardized; see below)

### Endodontic technique (standardized in clinic)
- Manual instrumentation with **K-files 0.02 taper**, **step-back technique**
- Irrigation: **2% sodium hypochlorite + saline**
- Obturation: **lateral condensation**, gutta-percha + **AH Plus sealer**

### Radiographic evaluation
- **Radiographs per tooth:** at least **three digital periapical radiographs**
  - Pre-operative
  - Working length determination
  - Post-operative (final)
- **Software:** SIDEXIS XG + Dentalplus

### Outcomes / variables (radiographic quality)
**Quality criteria** (European Society of Endodontology-based):
- **Acceptable RCT** requires ALL of:
  1. Uniform density coronal→apical
  2. No voids
  3. Filling terminates **0–2 mm from radiographic apex**
  4. Tapered filling

- **Unacceptable RCT** if any of:
  1. **Overfilling** (extends beyond apex)
  2. **Underfilling** (ends **≥2 mm short** of apex)
  3. **Inadequate density/voids** (visible voids in mass or between mass and canal wall)

So the outcome can be represented as:
- **Binary:** acceptable vs unacceptable
- **Multinomial:** acceptable / overfilled / underfilled / inadequate density (and potentially multiple errors—unclear if mutually exclusive in their coding)

### Examiner reliability
- Two senior endodontists (≥5 years experience), evaluated **independently** after “internal calibration.”
- Disagreements resolved by **discussion to consensus**.
- **Inter-/intra-examiner agreement:** **Kappa = 0.89** (reported as “considerably good”). (Unclear whether weighted/unweighted; unclear whether intra- and inter- computed separately.)

# CATEGORY A — MANDATORY (analysis would be incomplete)

## A1) Descriptive statistics (with CIs)
- **Test/name:** Estimation + descriptive summaries (proportions/means) with **95% confidence intervals**
- **Purpose:** Quantify acceptable RCT proportion and frequencies of each error; summarize covariates.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Primary aim is *assessment of quality and frequency*, which is fundamentally descriptive.
  2. Confidence intervals communicate precision and allow comparison to other studies.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - If clustering (multiple teeth per patient/student) is strong, naïve CIs may be too narrow.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Do not report only raw percentages without uncertainty; do not treat teeth as independent for precision statements if clustering is present and measurable.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Use **cluster-robust** (sandwich) SEs for proportions, or **GEE-based** marginal estimates, or bootstrap by patient.

## A2) Inter-examiner (and intra-examiner) reliability for categorical ratings
- **Test/name:** **Cohen’s kappa** (and **weighted kappa** if ordinal) + 95% CI
- **Purpose:** Quantify agreement between the two endodontists on quality/error categorization.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Ratings are categorical; kappa adjusts for chance agreement.
  2. Two-rater design fits Cohen’s kappa.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Kappa is sensitive to prevalence/imbalance; can look “low” despite high agreement (or vice versa).
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If more than two raters (then use Fleiss’ kappa) or if outcomes are continuous measurements.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Gwet’s AC1/AC2** (more stable with prevalence problems) and/or report **percent agreement** alongside kappa.

## A3) Comparison of acceptable vs unacceptable across key groups (unadjusted)
- **Test/name:** **Chi-square test of independence** (or **Fisher’s exact** when sparse)
- **Purpose:** Screen for associations between quality (binary) and categorical predictors (tooth type, arch, diagnosis category, student year, calendar period).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Outcome is binary and predictors are categorical.
  2. Provides a transparent, standard unadjusted comparison aligned with the “frequency” objective.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Violated independence if multiple teeth per patient or per student.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When expected cell counts are small (use Fisher) or when clustering is non-negligible and not handled.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Rao–Scott chi-square** for clustered data, or fit a **GEE logistic model** and do a Wald test for group effects.

# CATEGORY B — RECOMMENDED (improves rigor)

## B1) Multivariable modeling of acceptable quality (adjusted associations)
- **Test/name:** **Binary logistic regression** with **cluster-robust SEs** (or **GEE logistic**)
- **Purpose:** Estimate adjusted odds ratios for acceptable quality vs covariates (age, tooth type, diagnosis, student year, time).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Outcome is binary; logistic regression is standard and interpretable (ORs).
  2. Allows adjustment for confounding (e.g., difficult tooth types may be treated more often by senior students).
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - If there is near-separation (e.g., very few overfills) leading to unstable estimates.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - With extremely sparse outcomes per predictor level without penalization; or if the true target is risk ratio and outcome is common (OR may overstate).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Firth penalized logistic regression** for separation/small samples.
  - **Poisson regression with robust SEs** (log link) to estimate **risk ratios** when outcome is common.
  - If clustering by patient/student is known: **mixed-effects logistic regression** (random intercept for patient and/or student).

## B2) Modeling specific error types
- **Test/name:** **Multinomial logistic regression** (or separate binary models per error)
- **Purpose:** Identify predictors of **overfill vs underfill vs inadequate density** relative to acceptable.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Error outcome is nominal with >2 categories.
  2. Enables different covariate effects by error type (e.g., molars may relate to underfill more than overfill).
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - If some categories are rare (overfill often low), causing unstable multinomial estimates.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When categories are not mutually exclusive and are coded as “multiple errors per tooth” without a clear single-category definition.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Use **separate penalized logistic regressions** for each error type (binary: error present vs not), possibly with multiplicity control.

## B3) Trend over time / training level
- **Test/name:** **Cochran–Armitage trend test** (unadjusted) and/or include **year as ordinal** in logistic regression
- **Purpose:** Test whether acceptable quality improves across calendar years or student year (4th→6th).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Ordered groups; tests monotonic trend efficiently.
  2. Aligns with educational auditing objective (improvement with training).
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - If the relationship is non-monotonic or heavily confounded by changing case mix over years.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When group ordering is not meaningful or when clustering invalidates standard SEs.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Model year with **restricted cubic splines** in logistic regression; use **GEE** for clustering.

## B4) Multiple testing control (if many subgroup comparisons)
- **Test/name:** **False discovery rate (Benjamini–Hochberg)** or **Holm correction**
- **Purpose:** Limit false positives when evaluating many teeth/diagnosis/subgroup comparisons.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. The study can easily generate many p-values (multiple error types and predictors).
  2. Improves interpretability and credibility of subgroup findings.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - If the analysis is strictly descriptive and confirmatory inference is not intended.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - For a single pre-specified primary hypothesis (then correction may be unnecessary).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Emphasize **effect sizes + CIs** and pre-specify a limited set of comparisons.

# CATEGORY C — OPTIONAL (supplementary insights)

## C1) Agreement diagnostics beyond kappa
- **Test/name:** **Gwet’s AC1/AC2**, **positive/negative agreement**
- **Purpose:** Provide robustness to prevalence effects and richer agreement interpretation.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Common in quality-rating studies where one category dominates (often “acceptable”).
  2. Helps interpret high kappa vs high agreement paradoxes.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Less familiar to some dental audiences; may complicate reporting.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When journal expects only kappa and space is limited.
- **Alternative:**
  - Report **kappa + percent agreement + category-specific agreement**.

## C2) Sensitivity analysis for clustering and unit-of-analysis
- **Test/name:** Compare **naïve logistic**, **cluster-robust**, and **mixed-effects** models
- **Purpose:** Assess whether conclusions depend on independence assumptions (teeth nested within patients/students).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Teeth are plausibly correlated within patient and within operator.
  2. Demonstrates robustness of inference.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - If patient/student identifiers are unavailable in the extracted dataset.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When clustering cannot be represented and would be speculative.
- **Alternative:**
  - Use conservative inference and clearly state independence limitation.

## C3) Interaction testing (effect modification)
- **Test/name:** Logistic regression interaction terms (e.g., student year × tooth type)
- **Purpose:** Determine whether training level influences difficult teeth differently.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Educationally meaningful: senior students may perform better on molars than juniors.
  2. Can explain heterogeneous error patterns.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Low power; high risk of spurious findings.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When sample sizes within strata are small or interactions were not pre-specified.
- **Alternative:**
  - Stratified descriptive results with CIs; avoid formal interaction p-values.

## TASK 3 — Tests that should NOT be used (and why)

1. **Independent-samples t-test / ANOVA** to compare “quality” between groups  
   - **Why inappropriate:** Primary outcome is **categorical**, not continuous; violates scale assumptions.

2. **Pearson correlation** between quality and predictors (e.g., tooth type, diagnosis)  
   - **Why inappropriate:** Correlation is not suitable for nominal categorical variables and does not model probabilities.

3. **Linear regression** with binary outcome (linear probability model) as primary analysis  
   - **Why inappropriate:** Can predict probabilities outside [0,1] and yields heteroskedastic errors; less appropriate than logistic/GEE.

4. **Kaplan–Meier / Cox regression**  
   - **Why inappropriate:** No time-to-event outcome or follow-up defined; this is not a survival study.

5. **Paired t-test / McNemar’s test**  
   - **Why inappropriate:** No paired pre-post outcome per tooth; radiographs are multiple views for assessment, not repeated binary outcomes intended for change testing.

## TASK 4 — Summary justification of overall approach

Because the study is a **retrospective tooth-level audit** with a **categorical radiographic quality outcome** and defined procedural error categories, the analysis should (1) **estimate overall acceptable quality and error frequencies with CIs**, (2) **document rater reliability**, and (3) evaluate **associations with tooth/patient/training factors** using **chi-square/Fisher** for unadjusted comparisons and **logistic (and multinomial) regression** for adjusted inference—preferably accounting for potential **clustering of teeth within patients/operators** via robust, GEE, or mixed-effects methods.
