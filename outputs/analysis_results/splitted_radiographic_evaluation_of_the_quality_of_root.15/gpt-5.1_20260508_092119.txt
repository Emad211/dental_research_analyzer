CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS

A1. Descriptive statistics (no formal “test,” but essential)

Why appropriate:

---

A2. Cohen’s kappa (or weighted kappa) for inter- and intra-examiner agreement  

- Purpose:  
  - Quantify the reliability of categorical ratings of RCT quality between/within examiners.  

Why appropriate:  
1) Ratings (acceptable/unacceptable or type of error) are categorical; kappa is standard for agreement beyond chance.  
2) Two expert examiners independently evaluated radiographs; kappa is explicitly planned and reported.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- If categories are highly imbalanced (e.g., almost all acceptable), kappa can be paradoxically low or misleading despite high percent agreement.  

When this test should NOT be used:  
- When ratings are continuous or ordinal with many levels and another measure like intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC) is more appropriate.  
- When there is systematic bias (e.g., one rater always more lenient), and kappa interpretation becomes difficult.  

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- Use percent agreement with bias-adjusted kappa or Gwet’s AC1.  
- For ordinal categories (e.g., length over/adequate/under), use weighted kappa.  

---

A3. Chi-square test of independence for categorical comparisons  

Example uses:  
- Acceptable vs unacceptable RCT by:  
  - Tooth type (anterior vs posterior; single vs multirooted).  
  - Student year (4th vs 5th vs 6th).  
  - Jaw (maxilla vs mandible).  
  - Time period (e.g., 2013–2015 vs 2016–2019).  
- Distribution of specific error types (underfill/overfill/voids) by tooth category or student year.

- Purpose:  
  - Test whether quality/error distributions differ between categorical groups.  

Why appropriate:  
1) Outcome and predictors are categorical; chi-square is standard for association between two categorical variables.  
2) Sample size is moderate (n=306), likely providing sufficient expected counts in most cells.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- If expected cell counts in contingency tables are <5 (especially in >20% of cells), chi-square approximation becomes unreliable.  

When this test should NOT be used:  
- For small samples or sparse tables where expected cell counts are low.  
- For paired/dependent categorical data (McNemar’s test is more appropriate).  

Alternative if assumptions are violated:  
- Fisher’s exact test for 2×2 tables, or exact chi-square tests for larger tables.  
- For ordinal variables (e.g., year levels or ordered categories), use a test for trend (e.g., Cochran–Armitage trend test).

---

A4. Logistic regression (binary outcome)  

- Outcome: Acceptable vs unacceptable RCT (binary).  
- Predictors:  
  - Student year (categorical).  
  - Tooth type (anterior vs posterior; or specific tooth types).  
  - Jaw (maxillary vs mandibular).  
  - Patient age (continuous or categorized).  
  - Pulpal and periapical diagnoses (categorical).  
  - Time period or year of treatment (to evaluate changes over time).  

- Purpose:  
  - Identify independent predictors of acceptable RCT quality.  
  - Adjust for confounding between predictor variables (e.g., year and tooth type).  

Why appropriate:  
1) Outcome is binary; logistic regression handles binary outcomes with multiple predictors.  
2) Provides adjusted odds ratios and confidence intervals, enhancing interpretability and comparability with other studies.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- If the number of events (e.g., unacceptable RCTs) is too small relative to predictors, leading to overfitting and unstable estimates. A general rule is ≥10 events per predictor parameter.  

When this test should NOT be used:  
- When the outcome is extremely unbalanced (e.g., >95% acceptable) and model does not converge or yields inflated odds ratios.  
- When sample size is inadequate for multivariable modeling.  

Alternative if assumptions are violated:  
- Use bivariate analyses (chi-square/Fisher) only.  
- Use penalized logistic regression (e.g., Firth’s correction) if separation or small-sample bias occurs.  
- Collapse or combine categories to reduce the number of parameters.

CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED TESTS  
-------------------------------

B1. Fisher’s exact test  

- Purpose:  
  - Evaluate association between categorical variables when sample sizes or expected cell counts are small.  

Example uses:  
- Comparing overfill vs not overfill between tooth subgroups where counts are low.  
- Comparing rare procedural errors (e.g., instrument separation, if recorded) across student years.  

Why appropriate:  
1) Provides exact p-values under the null hypothesis, valid for small samples or sparse contingency tables.  
2) Particularly suitable for 2×2 comparisons (e.g., error present vs absent).  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Computationally intensive and less intuitive for larger tables; less informative about strength of association.  

When this test should NOT be used:  
- For large tables with sufficient expected cell counts where chi-square is more efficient and interpretable.  
- For continuous outcomes.  

Alternative if assumptions are violated:  
- For larger tables with low counts, use exact multinomial tests or Monte Carlo chi-square approximations.

---

B2. Cochran–Armitage test for trend (or Mantel–Haenszel test)  

- Purpose:  
  - Test for linear trend in proportions across ordered categories.  

Example uses:  
- Trend in acceptable RCT proportion across student year (4th, 5th, 6th).  
- Trend across chronological time (e.g., grouped years) in acceptable RCT rates.  

Why appropriate:  
1) Student year and time period are naturally ordered; trend tests are more powerful than generic chi-square for detecting monotonic trends.  
2) Assesses educational impact (e.g., improvement in quality with increasing year).  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Assumes linear trend; if the pattern is non-monotonic (e.g., 4th and 6th similar, 5th different), results may be misleading.  

When this test should NOT be used:  
- When predictor categories are not ordinal.  
- When sample size within each ordered category is very small or proportions are extremely skewed.  

Alternative if assumptions are violated:  
- Use standard chi-square test of independence.  
- Model with logistic regression including year as continuous or dummy-coded categorical variable.

---

B3. Multinomial logistic regression (for specific error categories)  

- Outcome: Nominal multicategory (e.g., acceptable, underfill, overfill, inadequate density).  
- Predictors: similar to binary logistic regression (student year, tooth type, age, etc.).

- Purpose:  
  - Model the relative risk/odds of specific error types vs a reference category (e.g., acceptable) across predictors.  

Why appropriate:  
1) Error types are nominal categories; multinomial logistic regression accommodates multiple outcome categories simultaneously.  
2) Allows simultaneous estimation of factors associated with different error patterns (e.g., underfill more frequent in molars).  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Requires sufficient counts in each outcome category; rare categories can lead to unstable models.  

When this test should NOT be used:  
- When some categories have very low counts (e.g., only a few overfills), causing non-convergence or wide confidence intervals.  

Alternative if assumptions are violated:  
- Collapse infrequent categories (e.g., combine overfill and underfill into “length errors”).  
- Use separate binary logistic models for each error type vs all others (noting multiple testing).  

---

B4. Nonparametric tests for age differences (if age is used analytically)  

- Outcome: Age as continuous.  
- Grouping: Acceptable vs unacceptable RCT; or specific error types.  

- Suggested tests:  
  - Mann–Whitney U test (two groups).  
  - Kruskal–Wallis test (more than two groups).  

Purpose:  
- Evaluate whether patient age differs between quality categories or error types.  

Why appropriate:  
1) Age may not be normally distributed; nonparametric tests do not assume normality.  
2) Sample size relatively modest; robust to outliers and skewed distributions.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Less powerful than parametric tests when age is approximately normal and variances are homogeneous.  

When this test should NOT be used:  
- When age is heavily grouped/categorized (then categorical methods should be used).  
- When comparing very small groups (e.g., n≤5 per group), where exact methods may be needed.  

Alternative if assumptions are violated:  
- If age is approximately normal and variances are equal, use t-tests/ANOVA.  
- If extreme skewness or outliers persist, consider trimmed means or robust regression.

Why it may not be appropriate:

When this should NOT be used:

Alternative:

TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS  
============================

These tests should not be used given the design and data:

1. Independent samples t-test / ANOVA for categorical outcomes  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - The primary outcome (acceptable vs unacceptable RCT) is categorical; t-tests/ANOVA are for continuous outcomes. Using mean “scores” from coding 0/1 would misrepresent the data structure.  
- Alternative:  
  - Chi-square/Fisher’s exact test for proportion comparisons; logistic regression for modeling.

2. Paired t-test / repeated-measures ANOVA  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - The study is cross-sectional/retrospective at the tooth level; there are no repeated measures over time per tooth.  
  - Although multiple radiographs per tooth exist, they are not longitudinal outcome measures but part of the diagnostic process.  
- Alternative:  
  - Use appropriate cross-sectional methods as above.

3. Survival analysis (Kaplan–Meier, Cox regression)  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - No follow-up time to event (such as retreatment or failure) is recorded; the study is limited to immediate post-treatment radiographic quality.  
- Alternative:  
  - Descriptive and cross-sectional analyses only.

4. Pearson correlation for associations between categorical variables  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Pearson correlation assumes continuous, approximately normally distributed variables.  
  - Primary variables here are categorical; Pearson correlation would be meaningless or misleading.  
- Alternative:  
  - Chi-square tests, logistic regression, or appropriate nonparametric correlations (e.g., Spearman) if continuous variables are involved.

5. Parametric tests assuming independence when there is clustering by student or patient  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - The same student may have treated multiple teeth; the same patient might have multiple teeth. Simple tests that ignore clustering can underestimate standard errors.  
- Alternative:  
  - Multilevel logistic regression or generalized estimating equations (GEE), if data on student IDs/patient IDs are available. If not, at least acknowledge this limitation.

6. Linear regression modeling acceptable vs unacceptable RCT as a continuous dependent variable  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Binary outcomes violate assumptions of linear regression (non-normal residuals, heteroskedasticity).  
- Alternative:  
  - Logistic regression.

7. Multiple comparison methods that assume independent tests without adjustment  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Conducting many separate tests without adjusting inflates type I error.  
- Alternative:  
  - Control family-wise error (e.g., Bonferroni) or false discovery rate, or prioritize a small number of primary comparisons.
