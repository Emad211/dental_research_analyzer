CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS

1. Comparison of pain scores between FE and FP at each time point

1.1 Test: Mann–Whitney U Test (Wilcoxon rank-sum)  
- Purpose:
  - Compare distributions/medians of pain scores between FE and FP at each time point (24 h, 48 h, 72 h, 1 week), treating pain as ordinal/non-normal.
- Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):
  1) Pain scores (0–10, with categorical derivation) are ordinal and often skewed with many zeros; Mann–Whitney does not require normality.
  2) Two independent groups (FE vs FP) with reasonably large sample sizes (≈70 per group) fit the test structure.
- Why it may not be appropriate:
  - If pain scores behave as approximately normal continuous variables and the interest is in mean differences with parametric inference, a t-test may be more interpretable.
- When NOT to use:
  - If sample size is very small or highly imbalanced and exact methods are preferred (then use exact Wilcoxon or permutation tests).
  - If you transform pain into a binary outcome (pain vs no pain) at each time point; in that case, a chi-square or logistic regression is preferable.
- Alternative if assumptions violated:
  - If the assumption of similar shapes is strongly violated and data are categorical: use ordinal logistic regression, or Fisher’s exact test on categorized pain (e.g., none vs any).

1.2 Test: Chi-square test (or Fisher’s exact) comparing pain categories  
- Purpose:
  - Compare distribution of pain categories (no/mild/moderate/severe) between FE and FP for each time point.
- Why appropriate:
  1) Outcomes are categorical; groups are independent; frequencies can be cross-tabulated.
  2) Allows direct interpretation at the clinical decision level (e.g., proportion with moderate/severe pain).
- Why it may not be appropriate:
  - If some cells have very small expected counts (<5), chi-square approximations may be unreliable.
- When NOT to use:
  - When sample sizes are too small and expected counts are very low; then Fisher’s exact is preferable.
- Alternative if assumptions violated:
  - Fisher’s exact test; or collapsing categories (e.g., none/mild vs moderate/severe) to ensure adequate cell counts; or use ordinal logistic regression.

2. Comparison of analgesic use between FE and FP

2.1 Test: Chi-square test for proportions (analgesic use yes/no)  
- Purpose:
  - Compare proportion of patients needing ibuprofen at each time point and/or overall between FE and FP.
- Why appropriate:
  1) Binary outcome (use vs no use) in two independent groups.
  2) Sample size is large enough for asymptotic chi-square approximations in most cells.
- Why it may not be appropriate:
  - If the number of patients using analgesics is very small, expected cell counts may be <5.
- When NOT to use:
  - When analgesic use is rare and probabilities are low; then exact tests are better.
- Alternative if assumptions violated:
  - Fisher’s exact test; or logistic regression if adjusting for covariates.

2.2 Test: Mann–Whitney U Test for number of tablets  
- Purpose:
  - Compare distribution of number of tablets (count data, likely skewed and often zero) between FE and FP among those who took any medication, or including zeros (full sample).
- Why appropriate:
  1) Non-parametric comparison of two independent samples for skewed count data.
  2) Robust to non-normality and unequal variances.
- Why it may not be appropriate:
  - If counts are very low and mostly zero, zero-inflated models may be more appropriate for detailed modeling.
- When NOT to use:
  - When the interest is specifically modeling rates or adjusting for covariates; then Poisson/negative binomial regression is more suitable.
- Alternative if assumptions violated:
  - Poisson or negative binomial regression; or exact tests on categorized “0 vs ≥1, ≥2 tablets”.

3. Comparison of sealer extrusion between FE and FP

3.1 Test: Chi-square test (or Fisher’s exact)  
- Purpose:
  - Compare proportion of teeth with sealer extrusion between FE and FP.
- Why appropriate:
  1) Binary outcome, two independent groups; standard 2×2 test.
  2) Clinically direct measure of intervention effect on extrusion.
- Why it may not be appropriate:
  - If extrusion is very rare or very common in one group, leading to sparse data or quasi-complete separation.
- When NOT to use:
  - When significant imbalance or extremely small numbers prevent meaningful chi-square approximations.
- Alternative if assumptions violated:
  - Fisher’s exact test; or logistic regression with Firth correction if separation is an issue.

4. Correlation between sealer extrusion and pain

4.1 Test: Mann–Whitney U Test (pain vs extrusion status)  
- Purpose:
  - Compare pain scores between extruded vs non-extruded teeth.
- Why appropriate:
  1) Pain is ordinal; extrusion is binary; Mann–Whitney compares distributions across two independent groups.
  2) Avoids parametric assumptions; robust with n≈140.
- Why it may not be appropriate:
  - If you want to control for group (FE vs FP) and other covariates; confounding may bias simple comparisons.
- When NOT to use:
  - When multivariable modeling is intended to adjust for treatment, baseline radiolucency, etc.
- Alternative if assumptions violated:
  - Ordinal logistic regression with extrusion as predictor and pain category as outcome; or logistic regression for moderate/severe pain vs none/mild.

4.2 Test: Spearman’s rank correlation  
- Purpose:
  - Evaluate monotonic association between pain scores and a binary coded extrusion variable (0/1) or ordinal extent if available.
- Why appropriate:
  1) Non-parametric, suitable for at least ordinal variables.
  2) Simple measure of association with interpretable effect size.
- Why it may not be appropriate:
  - Correlation with a binary predictor is essentially a transformation of group differences; logistic/ordinal regression might be more informative.
- When NOT to use:
  - When primary interest is causal effect or adjusted association; not just correlation.
- Alternative if assumptions violated:
  - Logistic/ordinal regression models (see Category B).

5. Handling repeated measures (pain over time)

5.1 Test: Friedman Test or Wilcoxon Signed-Rank (within-group time changes)  
- Purpose:
  - Within each group, assess change in pain over time (e.g., 24 h to 1 week).
- Why appropriate:
  1) Non-parametric test for repeated measures on same subjects (ordinal pain scores).
  2) Accounts for paired nature of data.
- Why it may not be appropriate:
  - Does not directly compare between groups over time; just within-group trajectories.
- When NOT to use:
  - When interest focuses on interaction of time and group (different patterns over time between FE and FP).
- Alternative if assumptions violated:
  - Mixed-effects models for repeated measures (Category B), or generalized estimating equations.

CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED TESTS (Enhance rigor and insight)

1. Mixed-effects ordinal regression for repeated pain outcomes

- Test: Mixed-effects cumulative logit (ordinal) model  
  - Outcome: Pain category (no/mild/moderate/severe) at 24, 48, 72 h, 1 week.
  - Fixed effects: Group (FE vs FP), time (4 levels), group × time interaction.
  - Random effect: Patient (random intercept).
- Purpose:
  - Model the trajectory of pain over time and formally test differences in pain profiles between FE and FP.
- Why appropriate:
  1) Pain categories are ordered; ordinal mixed models respect that structure.
  2) Handles intra-subject correlation of repeated measures, missing time points, and unbalanced data properly.
- Why it may not be appropriate:
  - Requires more complex modeling; convergence difficulties may appear if categories are sparse (e.g., few severe pain cases).
- When NOT to use:
  - When modeling resources or software availability is limited; or if there are too few non-zero pain observations to justify.
- Alternative if assumptions violated:
  - Generalized linear mixed model with binary outcome (any pain vs none), or GEE with robust standard errors; or simpler repeated-measures ANOVA on transformed pain scores (with caution).

2. Logistic regression for moderate/severe pain

- Test: Multivariable logistic regression  
  - Outcome: Moderate/severe pain vs no/mild pain at key time point (e.g., 24 h).
  - Predictors:
    - Treatment group (FE vs FP, primary predictor).
    - Baseline covariates: age, sex, apical radiolucency, tooth type (maxillary/mandibular), apical size (Primary vs Large), operator.
- Purpose:
  - Estimate adjusted odds ratio for clinically important pain, controlling for potential confounders.
- Why appropriate:
  1) Binary outcome; logistic regression is standard and gives interpretable ORs.
  2) Adjusts for covariates, increasing precision and addressing imbalances (if any).
- Why it may not be appropriate:
  - If event numbers (moderate/severe pain) are small, model may be unstable; overfitting is a concern.
- When NOT to use:
  - With too many covariates relative to the number of events (rule-of-thumb at least ~10 events per coefficient).
- Alternative if assumptions violated:
  - Penalized logistic regression (Firth) or exact logistic regression; or reduce model to fewer covariates.

3. Logistic regression for sealer extrusion

- Test: Binary logistic regression  
  - Outcome: Sealer extrusion (yes/no).
  - Predictors: Group (FE vs FP), apical size category, operator, tooth type.
- Purpose:
  - Estimate odds ratio of extrusion in FE vs FP and evaluate other influencing factors.
- Why appropriate:
  1) Binary outcome; typical logistic regression application.
  2) Allows exploration of whether FE effect is independent of other anatomical/procedural factors.
- Why it may not be appropriate:
  - If extrusion is extremely common or rare (near-constant); logistic models will be unstable.
- When NOT to use:
  - Very sparse events; or when simple 2×2 comparison sufficiently answers the question.
- Alternative if assumptions violated:
  - Fisher’s exact test; or exact logistic regression.

4. Adjusted analysis of correlation between sealer extrusion and pain

- Test: Ordinal logistic regression  
  - Outcome: Pain category (no/mild vs moderate/severe or four-level).
  - Predictor: Sealer extrusion (yes/no) plus group and covariates.
- Purpose:
  - Test whether extrusion is independently associated with pain level.
- Why appropriate:
  1) Pain is ordinal; logistic model accounts for ordered outcome.
  2) Adjusts for group (FE/FP) to avoid confounding (extrusion more likely in FE).
- Why it may not be appropriate:
  - Same issues as above (sparse categories, convergence).
- When NOT to use:
  - When the primary conclusion is already that no correlation exists and sample is underpowered for this detailed modeling.
- Alternative if assumptions violated:
  - Binary logistic regression on moderate/severe vs no/mild; or non-parametric tests.

5. Baseline comparability checks

- Tests:
  - Independent-samples t-test or Mann–Whitney U for continuous variables (e.g., age).
  - Chi-square/Fisher’s exact for categorical variables (sex, apical radiolucency, tooth type, operator, apical size category).
- Purpose:
  - Assess whether randomization achieved balance across important baseline characteristics.
- Why appropriate:
  1) Standard RCT practice; ensures no substantial imbalance that might confound results.
  2) Helps interpret any observed differences in outcomes.
- Why it may not be appropriate:
  - Some argue against hypothesis testing for baseline balance; descriptive comparisons and standardized differences might be preferred.
- When NOT to use:
  - When sample size is small and tests have low power; or if journal requires descriptive balance assessment only.
- Alternative if assumptions violated:
  - Present descriptive statistics and standardized mean differences without formal testing.

TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS (Specify and explain)

1. One-way ANOVA for pain scores across FE and FP

- Why not appropriate:
  - Only two groups; t-test would be equivalent. But more importantly, pain scores are ordinal with marked skew and many zeros; ANOVA assumes approximate normality and equal variances of residuals, which are implausible here.
- Problems:
  - Violated assumptions may inflate type I error or reduce power; mean-based comparisons poorly reflect categories like “no vs severe pain”.
- Better alternatives:
  - Mann–Whitney U; or ordinal regression.

2. Repeated-measures ANOVA with normally distributed residual assumptions

- Why not appropriate:
  - Pain is ordinal and very likely non-normal and heteroscedastic over time; repeated-measures ANOVA also requires sphericity assumption, which may be violated.
  - Missing data are likely; repeated-measures ANOVA handles missing poorly.
- Better alternatives:
  - Mixed-effects models, GEE, or non-parametric repeated measures tests.

3. Parametric correlation (Pearson’s correlation) between sealer extrusion and pain

- Why not appropriate:
  - One variable is binary and the other ordinal/possibly non-normal; Pearson’s correlation is not designed for such data and can misrepresent association.
- Better alternatives:
  - Spearman’s rank correlation; logistic or ordinal regression.

4. Paired t-test between groups for pain scores

- Why not appropriate:
  - Groups are independent (different patients), not paired or matched; paired t-test assumes matched pairs.
- Better alternatives:
  - Independent-samples tests (Mann–Whitney, t-test if appropriate).

5. Linear regression treating pain as continuous, without checking assumptions

- Why not appropriate:
  - Treating ordinal 0–10 pain as continuous and applying linear regression can be done, but only with careful assumption checks; residuals may be non-normal and heteroscedastic. Interpretation of predicted means may be less natural than odds of higher pain categories.
- Better alternatives:
  - Ordinal logistic regression; or transformation/robust regression if continuous modeling is insisted upon.

6. Multiple unadjusted post hoc tests without correction

- Why not appropriate:
  - Conducting many unadjusted tests across time points and outcomes inflates family-wise error rates.
- Better alternatives:
  - Prespecify primary time point; use Bonferroni or Holm adjustments; or use global mixed-model tests.
