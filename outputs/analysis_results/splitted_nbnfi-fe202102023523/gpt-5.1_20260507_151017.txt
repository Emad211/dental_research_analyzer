TASK 1 – INFORMATION EXTRACTION  
===============================  

1. STUDY DESIGN & OBJECTIVES  
----------------------------  

- **Parent cohort:**  
  - Corogene cohort: prospective cohort of **symptomatic Finnish patients** undergoing coronary angiography (n = 5,788) in Helsinki University Hospital, June 2006–March 2008.  
  - Data: previous medical conditions, cardiovascular risk factors, medications, and angiography results.

- **Sub-study (Parogene):**  
  - Nested, **prospective cohort with cross-sectional baseline oral/radiographic exam and 10‑year mortality follow-up**.  
  - Sample: ~10% random subpopulation of Corogene invited to dental exam.  
  - **Parogene sample:** 508 patients examined at Institute of Dentistry, University of Helsinki, between Oct 2007–May 2008.  
  - CAC assessable in **492/508 (96.8%)**; these constitute the analytic sample for CAC analyses.

- **Timing:**  
  - Oral clinical and radiographic exams performed ≥6 weeks and ≤5 months after coronary angiography.  
  - Follow-up for mortality: up to 10 years or until death (median 9.9 years; IQR 0.45; range 0.21–10.4).

- **Ethics:**  
  - Helsinki University Hospital ethics committee (Dnro 106/2007); written informed consent; STROBE compliant.

- **Main aims / hypotheses:**  
  i) Cross-sectional: CAC (on panoramic radiographs) is associated with:
     - Oral infections/inflammations (periodontitis, apical periodontitis, caries, etc.),  
     - Dysbiotic oral microbiota (subgingival),  
     - Immune response (salivary and serum antibodies) against dysbiotic oral bacterial species.  

  ii) Cross-sectional: CAC is associated with **diagnosis and severity of coronary artery disease (CAD)**.  

  iii) Longitudinal: CAC is associated with **all-cause and CVD mortality** over ~10-year follow-up.

2. STUDY POPULATION & GROUPS  
----------------------------  

- **Base analytic sample for CAC analyses:**  
  - n = 492 with technically evaluable CAC from panoramic radiographs (out of 508 who underwent oral exam).  

- **CAC categories (primary exposure variable):**  
  - **No CAC**  
  - **Moderate CAC** – heterogeneous radiopaque areas in carotid region  
  - **Severe CAC** – masses >10 mm  
  - CAC defined as radiopaque nodular mass adjacent to or below C3–C4 intervertebral space in carotid artery area.  
  - Later analyses often use **presence vs absence** (binary) of CAC, and sometimes ordered categories (no / moderate / severe).

- **CAD diagnostic groups (baseline, from angiography + clinical data):**  
  - **Chronic CAD** (n = 181; 36.8%)  
  - **Acute coronary syndrome (ACS)** (n = 164; 33.3%)  
  - **No significant CAD** (n = 147; 29.9%)

- **CAD severity (baseline angiography):**  
  - Number of coronary arteries with ≥50% stenosis:  
    - None/mild  
    - 1 stenosed artery  
    - 2 stenosed arteries  
    - 3 stenosed arteries

- **Cardiometabolic / CVD risk factor status:**  
  - Hypertension, dyslipidemia, diabetes defined by current **medication use**.  
  - Smoking: **never vs ever** (questionnaire at oral exam).  
  - BMI, age, sex.  
  - hs-CRP (mg/L) from blood.

3. ORAL & RADIOGRAPHIC MEASURES  
--------------------------------  

3.1 Clinical periodontal/oral examination  

- Setting: standard dental office; two calibrated periodontists performed exams.  
- **Measurements:**
  - **Periodontal probing depth (PPD)**:  
    - 6 sites per tooth (manual probe)  
    - Outcomes recorded:  
      - Number/proportion (%) of sites with PPD 4–5 mm  
      - Number/proportion (%) of sites with PPD ≥ 6 mm  
  - **Bleeding on probing (BOP)**:  
    - 4 sites per tooth  
    - Proportion (%) of sites with BOP.  
  - **Suppuration:** presence/absence (per site or tooth; not tabulated but measured).  
  - **Number of teeth** present.  
  - **Number of carious teeth.**  
  - **Periodontal inflammation burden index (PIBI):**  
    - PIBI = [number of 4–5 mm pockets + 2 × (number of ≥ 6 mm pockets)].  
    - Later categorised into **quartiles** (low, mild, moderate, severe).

3.2 Radiographic dental assessment  

- Imaging: Digital panoramic radiographs obtained with **ProOne unit (Planmeca, Helsinki)**.  
- Interpretation: Single dental radiologist, blinded to some clinical data (not specified) but responsible for:  
  - CAC presence/side/severity (as described above).  
  - **Horizontal alveolar bone loss (ABL):**  
    - The tooth with the most severe attachment loss in each dentate sextant selected.  
    - Mean per sextant used to classify:  
      - No ABL  
      - Mild (ABL in cervical third of root)  
      - Moderate (ABL in middle third)  
      - Severe to total (ABL from apical third to complete loss).  
    - In analysis, often collapsed into:  
      - None  
      - Mild  
      - Moderate to total (moderate + severe/total).  
  - **Endodontic lesions / apical periodontitis:**  
    - Root canal fillings (RCF): count per patient.  
    - Widened periapical spaces (irreversible pulpitis / precursors) – count per patient.  
    - Apical rarefactions / apical periodontitis (periradicular destruction at root tip) – count per patient.  
    - **Endodontic lesion score (severity of apical periodontitis) – per patient (dentate):**  
      - No endodontic lesions  
      - Moderate AP: ≥1 widened periapical space and/or 1 tooth with apical periodontitis  
      - Severe AP: ≥2 teeth with apical periodontitis  
  - **Treatment status of apical periodontitis – per patient:**  
    - No endodontic lesions  
    - All endodontic lesions in teeth with root canal filling (i.e. treated AP)  
    - Untreated apical periodontitis (AP in teeth without RCF).

3.3 Microbiological measures (subgingival plaque)  

- Sample: dentate subjects only.  
- **Sampling strategy:**  
  - Pooled subgingival samples from **deepest pathological pocket** in each dentate quadrant.  
  - If no pockets >3 mm: sample from site with deepest pocket or most visible inflammation.  

- **Microbial assay:**  
  - Checkerboard DNA–DNA hybridization for **78 oral bacterial species** (per standard Socransky protocol).  
  - Species classified as:  
    - Gram-positive: n = 45  
    - Gram-negative: n = 33  
  - Bacterial levels:  
    - Quantitative counts per species, **log-transformed**;  
    - Sums of log-transformed counts used for overall Gram-positive and Gram-negative burdens.  
  - In Table 2: Gram-positive and Gram-negative subgingival levels categorized as **above vs below median** per patient.

3.4 Immunological measures (serum and saliva)  

- **Antibody assays:** ELISA (per Pussinen et al. 2002, 2011; Akhi et al. 2017; Liljestrand et al. 2018).  
  - 13 strains from 5 species characteristic of periodontitis-associated dysbiosis used as antigens.  

- **Outcome variables:**
  - Saliva IgA against dysbiotic species – measured in rlu/100 ms; scaled /10⁵.  
  - Saliva IgG against dysbiotic species – rlu/100 ms /10⁵.  
  - Serum IgA against dysbiotic species – expressed in ELISA units (EU).  
  - Serum IgG against dysbiotic species – EU.  
  - For some regression models, antibody responses combined across species (e.g., weighted or summed, as typical in this group’s work).

  - Three-level ordinal variable: no CAC / moderate CAC / severe CAC.

- **Covariate adjustment strategy:**  
  - Core confounders: age, sex, BMI, smoking, diabetes, hypertension, dyslipidemia.  
  - For CAD outcomes: additional adjustment for conventional risk factors as appropriate (e.g., hs-CRP).  
  - For mortality outcomes: adjust at minimum for age, sex, smoking, diabetes, hypertension, dyslipidemia; optionally BMI, CAD severity.

- **Multiple testing:**  
  - Large number of oral/microbiological/antibody variables → strong risk of type I error.  
  - Pre-specify primary exposures (e.g., ABL, PIBI, AP severity, Gram-negative burden, key antibody composite indices) and control family-wise error or FDR for secondary analyses.

- **Assumption checks:**  
  - Normality of continuous variables (histograms, Q-Q plots, Shapiro–Wilk).  
  - Homoscedasticity (plots of residuals vs fitted).  
  - Linearity in logit or log-hazard for continuous predictors (use splines or categorization if violated).  
  - Proportional hazards for Cox models (Schoenfeld residuals).

B. SPECIFIC TESTS AND MODELS  

### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS  

These are essential to address the stated hypotheses.

---

### A1. Descriptive statistics & group comparisons by CAC category

**A1.1 ANOVA or Kruskal–Wallis for continuous variables across CAC categories**  

- **Purpose:**  
  - Compare demographic and systemic characteristics across CAC categories (no / moderate / severe).  
- **Application:**  
  - Variables: age, BMI, number of teeth, counts of endodontic lesions, etc.  

- **Why appropriate:**  
  1) Outcome is continuous with roughly symmetric distribution (or can be transformed).  
  2) CAC categories are independent groups.  

- **Why it may not be appropriate:**  
  - If residuals are heavily non-normal or variances unequal and transformation fails.  

- **When NOT to use:**  
  - When sample sizes are small and distributions are extremely skewed (e.g., count data with many zeros).  

- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**  
  - **Kruskal–Wallis test** for 3-group comparison; for trend across ordered CAC categories use **Jonckheere–Terpstra**.

---

**A1.2 Chi-square tests for categorical variables across CAC categories**  

- **Purpose:**  
  - Compare proportions (e.g., sex, presence of hypertension/diabetes, categories of ABL, AP severity, PIBI quartiles, Gram-negative level above/below median) across CAC categories.  
- **Why appropriate:**  
  1) Categorical data with independent groups and reasonably large cell counts.  
  2) CAC groups are mutually exclusive; counts measured at patient level.  

- **Why it may not be appropriate:**  
  - Expected cell counts <5 for some combinations (e.g., severe CAC x rare category).  

- **When NOT to use:**  
  - When >20% of cells have expected counts <5 or any cell <1.  

- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**  
  - **Fisher’s exact test** (for 2×k tables, or exact tests for R×C where feasible).  
  - For trend across ordered CAC or exposure categories: **Chi-square test for linear-by-linear association** or **Cochran–Armitage trend test**.

---

### A2. Association between CAC presence and oral / microbiological / antibody parameters (cross-sectional)

Here CAC presence (yes/no) is the main dependent variable, following the article’s logistic models.

**A2.1 Binary logistic regression: CAC presence vs absence**  

- **Purpose:**  
  - Estimate adjusted associations between CAC presence and specific exposures:  
    - ABL categories  
    - PIBI categories (periodontal inflammation burden)  
    - Endodontic parameters (RCF count, AP severity, AP treatment status)  
    - Levels of Gram-negative and Gram-positive subgingival bacteria  
    - Salivary and serum IgA/IgG against dysbiotic species  
    - CAD severity categories  
    - CAD diagnosis categories.  

- **Model specification:**  
  - Outcome: CAC presence (0 = none; 1 = moderate/severe).  
  - Predictors: each exposure variable in separate models (to reduce multicollinearity), plus confounders (age, sex, dyslipidemia, diabetes, hypertension, ever smoking; optionally BMI).  
  - Use **dummy variables** for categorical exposures; for ordered categories, test linear trend.  

- **Why appropriate:**  
  1) Binary outcome with logistic model giving ORs that are interpretable for risk contrasts.  
  2) Allows adjustment for multiple confounders.  

- **Why it may not be appropriate:**  
  - If CAC prevalence is high, the OR may overestimate the RR.  
  - Small cell counts in some categories may lead to unstable estimates.  

- **When NOT to use:**  
  - When outcome or predictors are extremely sparse (e.g., severe CAC with rare exposures) leading to complete separation.  

- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**  
  - **Penalized logistic regression (Firth’s correction)** if separation or small-sample bias.  
  - **Log-binomial** or **Poisson with robust variance** if one wishes to estimate risk ratios instead of odds ratios (though more relevant in prospective designs).

---

**A2.2 Ordinal logistic regression (proportional odds) with 3-level CAC**  

- **Purpose:**  
  - Model ordered CAC severity (0=no; 1=moderate; 2=severe) in relation to exposures (e.g., ABL, AP severity, Gram-negative burden).  
- **Why appropriate:**  
  1) CAC severity is an ordinal variable; ordinal logistic regression exploits ordering and potentially more efficient than binary.  
  2) Provides single effect estimate under proportional odds assumption.  

- **Why it may not be appropriate:**  
  - Proportional odds assumption may be violated (effect differs across cut-points).  

- **When NOT to use:**  
  - When the proportional odds assumption is strongly violated (tested by Brant test or score test).  
  - When sample size in severe CAC group is very small (n=21; may limit reliability).  

- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**  
  - **Partial proportional odds model** or **multinomial logistic regression** (treat CAC categories as nominal).  

---

### A3. Association between CAC and CAD diagnosis / CAD severity (cross-sectional)

**A3.1 Multinomial logistic regression: CAD diagnosis as outcome**  

- **Purpose:**  
  - Assess association of CAC presence/severity with **CAD diagnostic category** (no significant CAD, chronic CAD, ACS), controlling for confounders.  

- **Model specification:**  
  - Outcome: 3-level CAD diagnosis (reference: no significant CAD).  
  - Predictor: CAC presence/ordinal CAC (0/1/2).  
  - Adjust: age, sex, BMI, hypertension, diabetes, dyslipidemia, smoking; possibly periodontal variables to test mediation.  

- **Why appropriate:**  
  1) CAD diagnosis is nominal with >2 categories.  
  2) Allows estimation of separate ORs for chronic CAD vs no CAD and ACS vs no CAD.  

- **Why it may not be appropriate:**  
  - If some CAD-CAC category combinations have small numbers.  

- **When NOT to use:**  
  - When common support between predictive covariates and CAD categories is poor (extreme imbalance).  

- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**  
  - Collapse CAD categories into binary outcomes (e.g., any significant CAD vs none; ACS vs non-ACS) and use binary logistic regression.

---

**A3.2 Ordinal logistic regression: CAD severity (0/1/2/3 affected arteries)**  

- **Purpose:**  
  - Evaluate association between CAC and increasing angiographic CAD severity.  

- **Why appropriate:**  
  1) CAD severity is inherently ordinal.  
  2) More powerful than separate binary comparisons.  

- **Why it may not be appropriate:**  
  - Non-proportional odds.  

- **When NOT to use:**  
  - If proportional odds assumption fails or distribution of categories is extremely imbalanced (few in some severity levels).  

- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**  
  - **Generalized ordered logit** or **multinomial logistic regression**.  
  - Or treat CAD severity as numeric (0–3) in a **linear regression** or **Poisson/negative binomial** (count of stenosed arteries), using robust SEs.

---

### A4. Association between CAC and mortality (longitudinal)

**A4.1 Cox proportional hazards regression – all-cause mortality**  

- **Purpose:**  
  - Evaluate whether CAC presence/severity predicts time to death from any cause over follow-up.  

- **Model specification:**  
  - Outcome: time from baseline oral exam to death or censoring (administrative censoring at 10 years).  
  - Event indicator: death from any cause (1) vs alive/censored (0).  
  - Main exposure: CAC categories (no / moderate / severe); test both binary and ordinal.  
  - Covariates: age, sex, BMI, smoking, hypertension, dyslipidemia, diabetes; optionally baseline CAD severity, hs-CRP.  
  - Report HRs with 95% CIs and p-values.  

- **Why appropriate:**  
  1) Data are right-censored time-to-event, with varying follow-up times.  
  2) Cox model does not assume specific baseline hazard and is standard in survival analysis.  

- **Why it may not be appropriate:**  
  - Proportional hazards assumption may be violated (hazard ratio changes over time).  

- **When NOT to use:**  
  - When PH assumption is clearly violated or if sample size is too small for complex multivariable models (few events relative to predictors).  

- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**  
  - **Time-varying coefficient Cox model** (interactions between CAC and log(time)).  
  - **Flexible parametric survival models** (Royston–Parmar).  
  - **Stratified Cox model** if PH holds within strata.  

---

**A4.2 Cox proportional hazards regression – CVD mortality**  

- **Purpose:**  
  - Investigate association between CAC and time to **CVD-specific death**.  

- **Model specification:**  
  - Outcome: time to CVD death (ICD-10 I00–I99 codes) or censoring; treat deaths from other causes as censored.  
  - Exposure & covariates as above.  

- **Why appropriate:**  
  1) Same reasoning as for all-cause mortality, focusing on CVD events.  
  2) Clinically relevant because CAC is a vascular phenomenon.  

- **Why it may not be appropriate:**  
  - If number of CVD deaths is low, leading to sparse events per covariate.  
  - Treating non-CVD death as censored may introduce competing risks bias.  

- **When NOT to use:**  
  - When competing risk of non-CVD death is substantial, and cause-specific hazard is not the target estimand.  

- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**  
  - **Competing risks models** (e.g., Fine–Gray subdistribution hazard model) to estimate effect on CVD death while accounting for non-CVD death as a competing event.

### B1. Trend tests for ordered categories

**B1.1 Jonckheere–Terpstra tests for continuous outcomes vs ordered CAC**  

- **Purpose:**  
  - Evaluate monotonic trends in continuous variables (e.g., number of teeth, endodontic lesion counts, antibody levels) across increasing CAC categories.  

- **Why appropriate:**  
  1) CAC severity is ordinal; Jonckheere–Terpstra is more powerful than Kruskal–Wallis when a priori order exists.  
  2) Handles non-normal or skewed data.  

- **Why it may not be appropriate:**  
  - Requires ordinal grouping and independence; does not adjust for covariates.  

- **When NOT to use:**  
  - When exposure categories are not ordered or interest lies mainly in adjusted relationships.  

- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**  
  - Trend tests in regression (e.g., include CAC category as numeric predictor in linear/logistic models).

---

### B2. Multivariable models of oral variables as outcomes

**B2.1 Linear or generalized linear regression: oral/inflammatory variables vs CAC**  

- **Purpose:**  
  - Quantify how CAC is associated with continuous oral and systemic measures after adjustment (e.g., number of RCF, number of teeth, PPD %).  

- **Why appropriate:**  
  1) Allows adjustment for age and other confounders in interpreting differences in oral health by CAC status.  
  2) Regression-based estimates (e.g., mean difference) are more informative than unadjusted p-values.  

- **Why it may not be appropriate:**  
  - Many oral variables are counts with overdispersion and zero inflation; normal linear model may be inadequate.  

- **When NOT to use:**  
  - When the residual distribution is far from normal even after transformation.  

- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**  
  - **Poisson or negative binomial regression** for counts (e.g., number of apical rarefactions, RCF).  
  - **Beta regression** for proportions (PPD%, BOP%) after transforming to (0,1) scale.

### B3. Mediation/interaction analyses

**B3.1 Exploration of mediation by oral infections in CAC–mortality association**  

- **Purpose:**  
  - Investigate whether the association between CAC and mortality is partly mediated by oral infection burden (e.g., PIBI, ABL, AP severity).  

- **Why appropriate:**  
  1) Directly addresses the biological hypothesis linking oral disease → systemic inflammation → vascular calcification → mortality.  
  2) May reveal whether CAC is simply a marker or a mediator of oral disease effects.  

- **Why it may not be appropriate:**  
  - Cross-sectional baseline oral measures and CAC measured roughly concurrently; temporal order between oral disease and CAC is unclear, complicating causal mediation.  

- **When NOT to use:**  
  - When presenting results as causal mediation without the necessary temporal and identifiability conditions.  

- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**  
  - Restrict analysis to a **descriptive comparison** of HRs with and without oral variables (exploratory).

TASK 3 – INAPPROPRIATE TESTS  
=============================  

Tests that should generally NOT be used for this study, with reasons:

1. **Paired t-tests or McNemar’s tests**  
   - Not appropriate because there is no within-person pre–post or paired measurement structure; groups are independent.

2. **Repeated-measures ANOVA**  
   - No repeated measurements over time at the level of oral or CAC measures; all oral and CAC data are cross-sectional.

3. **Simple linear regression of mortality status (dead/alive) as a continuous outcome**  
   - Mortality is binary/time-to-event, not continuous; using linear regression violates distributional assumptions and yields invalid inferences for binary outcomes with censoring.

4. **Kaplan–Meier curves without covariate adjustment as the main inferential tool**  
   - KM curves are useful descriptively, but the key questions involve adjustment for multiple confounders (age, CVD risk factors), which KM cannot provide. Inferential conclusions should rely on Cox models.

5. **Chi-square tests to compare mean survival times across CAC groups**  
   - Mean survival can be heavily influenced by censoring; standard ANOVA/chi-square on mean survival times is not appropriate for right-censored time-to-event data.

6. **Correlation tests (Pearson/Spearman) treating categorical CAC and CAD variables as continuous without respecting their nature**  
   - Simplistic correlation would ignore confounding and the discrete/ordinal nature of variables; logistic/ordinal regression is more appropriate.

7. **Student’s t-test for heavily skewed count data (e.g., number of apical rarefactions) without transformation or distribution-aware modeling**  
   - Count data are typically non-normal and heteroscedastic; Poisson/negative binomial models or non-parametric tests are preferable.

8. **Stepwise variable selection procedures for multivariable models (forward/backward based purely on p-values)**  
   - Prone to overfitting and unstable model selection, especially with modest sample size and correlated predictors; pre-specification and clinical/statistical reasoning should guide model building.

TASK 4 – SUMMARY OF ANALYTICAL APPROACH  
========================================  

The study is a nested, prospective cohort with a cross-sectional baseline oral/CAC assessment and 10-year mortality follow-up. CAC on panoramic radiographs is the central exposure of interest; outcomes include:

- cross-sectional oral disease burden, microbial/antibody profiles, and CAD diagnosis/severity;  
- longitudinal all-cause and CVD mortality.

The core analytical framework therefore combines:

1. **Descriptive analyses** to characterize the distribution of CAC and potential confounders (ANOVA/Kruskal–Wallis and chi-square).  
2. **Cross-sectional multivariable logistic/ordinal regression** to relate CAC presence/severity to oral infection/inflammation measures, microbiological dysbiosis, antibody profiles, and CAD severity/diagnosis, adjusting for standard cardiovascular risk factors.  
3. **Time-to-event models (Cox regression)** to estimate the independent association of CAC (and, secondarily, oral disease) with all-cause and CVD mortality over 10 years, accounting for confounding and right-censoring.  
4. **Trend tests and model diagnostics** to validate assumptions (e.g., proportional odds, proportional hazards, linearity), plus sensitivity analyses and more advanced models (e.g., competing risks) as needed.

This integrated approach is aligned with the study’s hypotheses on the links between oral infections, vascular calcification, CAD, and mortality, while respecting the observational nature of the data and methodological constraints.
