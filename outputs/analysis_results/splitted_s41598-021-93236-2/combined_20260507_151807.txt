## TASK 2 — Statistical analysis plan

### General principles (apply to all analyses)
- **Analysis population:** all treated teeth with follow-up (here, n=23; no missing follow-up per text).
- **Unit of inference:** ideally **tooth-level**, with **patient-level clustering** handled explicitly.
- **Descriptives:**
  - Continuous: mean(SD) and/or median(IQR); plots over time (spaghetti plots).
  - Binary: counts (%).
- **Primary estimands (examples):**

  - Proportion with radiographic healing (e.g., PAI improvement/resolution) by 6–12 months.
  - Proportion with positive sensibility at each timepoint.
- **Multiplicity:** multiple outcomes and timepoints → control family-wise error (Holm) or use model-based contrasts with adjustment (e.g., Tukey/Šidák).
- **Assumption checks:** residual diagnostics for models; distribution checks for continuous outcomes; evaluate outliers/leverage.
- **Software:** R/SAS/SPSS; ensure methods support clustered longitudinal data.

# CATEGORY A — MANDATORY tests/models

## A1) Linear mixed-effects model (LMM) for continuous repeated measures (with patient random effect; optional tooth random effect)
**Purpose:** Test and estimate **change over time** in continuous outcomes (apical diameter, root length, dentin thickness measures).

**Why appropriate (≥2):**
1. Handles **repeated measures** across 8 timepoints while accounting for **within-tooth correlation**.
2. Can incorporate **clustering of teeth within patient** via random effects (important because 4 patients have 2 teeth).
3. Uses all timepoints simultaneously and supports **post-hoc contrasts** (e.g., pre-op vs 12m, 24m).

**Why it may not be appropriate (≥1):**
- With **small sample size** (23 teeth; only 19 patients), complex random-effects structures may be unstable.

**When NOT to use:**
- If outcome is not approximately continuous (e.g., heavily bounded/ordinal) and residuals remain non-normal/heteroscedastic despite transformation.
- If only two timepoints exist (then simpler paired methods suffice).

**Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
- **Generalized linear mixed model (GLMM)** with appropriate distribution (e.g., Gamma/log for positive skew).
- **Rank-based mixed models** or **GEE with robust SE**.
- **Friedman test** (nonparametric), noting it ignores patient clustering unless adapted.

**Implementation notes (recommended):**
- Fixed effect: **time** (categorical, not necessarily linear).
- Random effects: patient intercept; consider tooth-within-patient intercept if identifiable.
- Covariance structure: start with random intercept + time as fixed; add correlation structure only if supported.

---

## A2) Generalized linear mixed model (logistic) for binary repeated measures (sensibility response; presence/absence of symptoms)
**Purpose:** Model probability of **positive vitality response** over time (and/or symptom resolution).

**Why appropriate:**
1. Binary outcome with repeated follow-ups requires modeling correlated responses.
2. Can include **random patient effects** to address two teeth per patient.

**Why it may not be appropriate:**
- If events are extremely rare/common at most timepoints, model separation/instability can occur.

**When NOT to use:**
- When there is no longitudinal structure (single post-treatment assessment only).
- When the outcome is ordinal/multinomial unless expanded accordingly.

**Alternative if assumptions are violated / model fails:**
- **GEE logistic** with robust sandwich SE (population-average).
- **Exact logistic regression** (limited longitudinal capability).
- Aggregate to a single endpoint (e.g., “ever positive by 24 months”) and use clustered methods.

---

## A3) Model-based post-hoc contrasts with multiplicity control (e.g., estimated marginal means + Holm adjustment)
**Purpose:** Identify **which timepoints differ** (e.g., pre-op vs 12m, 24m) after an overall time effect.

**Why appropriate:**
1. The clinical questions are inherently **time-anchored** (6–12m healing; 12–24m maturation).
2. Controls inflation of Type I error from multiple comparisons across timepoints/outcomes.

**Why it may not be appropriate:**
- With very small samples, adjusted post-hoc tests may be underpowered and yield wide CIs.

**When NOT to use:**
- When only a single pre-specified contrast exists (then adjustment may be unnecessary).

**Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
- **Bootstrap CIs** for contrasts.
- Use **pre-specified key timepoints only** (e.g., baseline vs 12m vs 24m) to reduce multiplicity burden.

# CATEGORY B — RECOMMENDED

## B1) Intraclass correlation / clustering assessment (ICC) for teeth within patient
**Purpose:** Quantify degree of **non-independence** for outcomes due to multiple teeth per patient.

**Why appropriate:**
1. Justifies need for mixed models/cluster-robust SE.
2. Provides transparency about how much patient-level factors influence outcomes.

**Why it may not be appropriate:**
- With only 4 multi-tooth patients, ICC estimates can be imprecise.

**When NOT to use:**
- If every patient contributes exactly one tooth (no clustering).

**Alternative:**
- Use **cluster-robust SE** without estimating ICC; or sensitivity analysis excluding second tooth.

---

## B2) Sensitivity analysis: GEE (continuous and binary) with robust sandwich SE
**Purpose:** Provide population-average estimates robust to some covariance mis-specification.

**Why appropriate:**
1. Less sensitive to random-effects distribution assumptions than mixed models.
2. Robust SE can be helpful with mild model misspecification.

**Why it may not be appropriate:**
- Small number of clusters (19 patients) can make sandwich SE biased downward.

**When NOT to use:**
- When cluster count is too small for reliable robust SE without small-sample corrections.

**Alternative:**
- Mixed models with careful random-effects structure; or **cluster wild bootstrap** SE.

---

## B3) Reliability analysis for radiographic measurements (if ≥2 raters or repeated measures)
**Test(s):** ICC (continuous), weighted kappa (ordinal PAI)
**Purpose:** Ensure measurement reproducibility for radiographic outcomes.

**Why appropriate:**
1. Radiographic measurements (mm) can be rater- and calibration-dependent.
2. Strengthens credibility of longitudinal change.

**Why it may not be appropriate:**
- If only one calibrated examiner measured once and no repeat measurements exist.

**When NOT to use:**
- When no replicate ratings are available.

**Alternative:**
- Conduct duplicate readings on a subset and compute **Bland–Altman** agreement.

---

## B4) Effect size estimation with confidence intervals (standardized and absolute)
**Purpose:** Quantify magnitude of change (clinical relevance), not just p-values.

**Why appropriate:**
1. Clinical interpretation: mm changes in root length/thickness matter.
2. Small-sample studies benefit from estimation emphasis.

**Why it may not be appropriate:**
- Standardized effect sizes can be misleading if SD changes markedly across time.

**When NOT to use:**
- When outcome scale is not meaningful or distribution is highly irregular without transformation.

**Alternative:**
- Report **median change** with **bootstrap CI**; or model-based marginal mean differences with CI.

## TASK 3 — Inappropriate tests (should NOT be used)

1. **Independent-samples t-test / one-way ANOVA (between-groups)**  
   - No independent groups; repeated measures violate independence.

2. **Multiple paired t-tests at each follow-up without multiplicity adjustment**  
   - Inflates Type I error dramatically across many timepoints/outcomes.

3. **Simple linear regression ignoring clustering and repeated measures**  
   - Underestimates SE (pseudo-replication), invalid p-values/CIs.

4. **Chi-square tests treating repeated binary outcomes as independent observations**  
   - Violates independence; repeated measures within tooth and clustering within patient.

5. **Standard repeated-measures ANOVA (RM-ANOVA) without accounting for teeth-within-patient clustering**  
   - Even if it addresses within-tooth correlation, it still assumes one observation unit per subject; here, some subjects contribute 2 teeth.

---

## TASK 4 — Summary justification of overall approach

A single-arm prospective cohort with **longitudinal tooth-level outcomes** requires methods that (1) test **within-tooth change over time**, and (2) respect **correlation** from repeated measurements and **clustering** of teeth within some patients. Mixed-effects models (and/or GEE as sensitivity) provide valid inference, allow clinically relevant timepoint contrasts (6–12 months for healing; 12–24 months for maturation), and support estimation with confidence intervals rather than relying on numerous unadjusted pairwise tests.

### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY
1. **Test Name**: Paired t-test (or Wilcoxon signed-rank test if non-parametric).  
   - **Purpose**: To compare pre- and post-operative measurements of apical diameter, root length, and dentin wall thickness.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. The study involves repeated measurements of the same teeth over time.  
     2. The outcomes are continuous variables (e.g., root length in mm).  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:  
     - Assumes normality of data distribution, which may not hold true for small sample sizes.  
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**:  
     - If data are not normally distributed.  
   - **Alternative if Assumptions Are Violated**: Wilcoxon signed-rank test (non-parametric equivalent).

2. **Test Name**: Repeated Measures ANOVA (or Friedman test if non-parametric).  
   - **Purpose**: To analyze changes in apical diameter, root length, and dentin wall thickness across multiple time points.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Accounts for within-subject correlations across time points.  
     2. Suitable for continuous outcomes measured repeatedly.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:  
     - Assumes sphericity (equal variance of differences between time points).  
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**:  
     - If sphericity assumption is violated and corrections (e.g., Greenhouse-Geisser) are not applied.  
   - **Alternative if Assumptions Are Violated**: Friedman test (non-parametric equivalent).

3. **Test Name**: Chi-square test (or Fisher’s exact test).  
   - **Purpose**: To evaluate categorical outcomes, such as the presence/absence of clinical symptoms (pain/swelling) and pulp vitality.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Suitable for binary categorical data.  
     2. Allows comparison of proportions across time points.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:  
     - Limited power with small sample sizes.  
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**:  
     - If expected cell counts are <5.  
   - **Alternative if Assumptions Are Violated**: Fisher’s exact test.

---

#### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED
1. **Test Name**: Mixed-effects model.  
   - **Purpose**: To model changes in outcomes (e.g., apical diameter) over time while accounting for patient-level clustering (e.g., multiple teeth per patient).  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Accounts for both fixed effects (time) and random effects (patient variability).  
     2. Handles missing data more robustly than repeated measures ANOVA.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:  
     - Requires advanced statistical software and expertise.  
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**:  
     - If sample size is too small to estimate random effects reliably.  
   - **Alternative if Assumptions Are Violated**: Repeated measures ANOVA.

2. **Test Name**: Kaplan-Meier survival analysis.  
   - **Purpose**: To analyze time-to-event data, such as time to resolution of symptoms or achievement of pulp vitality.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Accounts for censoring (e.g., patients lost to follow-up).  
     2. Provides insights into the timing of clinical outcomes.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:  
     - Not suitable for continuous outcomes.  
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**:  
     - If no censoring occurs or if all events occur at the same time.  
   - **Alternative if Assumptions Are Violated**: Logistic regression for binary outcomes.

---

#### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL
1. **Test Name**: Correlation analysis (e.g., Pearson or Spearman).  
   - **Purpose**: To explore relationships between quantitative outcomes (e.g., apical diameter and root length).  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Identifies potential associations between variables.  
     2. Useful for hypothesis generation.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**:  
     - Does not imply causation.  
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**:  
     - If variables are not linearly related.  
   - **Alternative if Assumptions Are Violated**: Spearman correlation (non-parametric).

A2. Within-tooth longitudinal comparison for continuous radiographic outcomes  
(Repeated-measures ANOVA or Linear Mixed-Effects Model)  
--------------------------------------------------------------------------  

Test names:  
- Primary option (simple): Repeated-measures ANOVA (RM-ANOVA) on change-from-baseline.  
- More robust option: Linear mixed-effects model (LMM) with random intercept for tooth (and possibly patient).

Outcomes:  
- Continuous:  
  - Apical diameter (mm) or its change from baseline.  
  - Root length (mm) or its change from baseline.  
  - Dentinal wall thickness measures (mm) at 4 locations.  

Purpose:  
- To evaluate whether there is a statistically significant change over time in each morphological parameter after PRF treatment, and to identify at which time points changes become significant relative to baseline or earlier time points.

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):  
1. Same teeth measured at multiple time points → RM-ANOVA/LMM explicitly models within-tooth correlation.  
2. Continuous outcomes with roughly interval-scale measurement lend themselves to parametric models; RM-ANOVA/LMM can assess overall time effect and pairwise comparisons between time points.  
3. Mixed models can handle unbalanced or missing data (though here follow-up is complete).  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- RM-ANOVA assumptions:  
  - Normally distributed residuals.  
  - Sphericity (equal variances of differences between time points).  
  - No serious outliers.  
- For small n (23), normality may be questionable; sphericity is often violated; teeth nested within patients introduce an extra correlation layer not handled by simple RM-ANOVA.  

When this test should NOT be used:  
- RM-ANOVA should not be used when:  
  - Data are clearly non-normal and robust transformations do not help.  
  - There are substantial missing values with non-random patterns.  
  - Sphericity is severely violated and corrections (Greenhouse–Geisser, Huynh–Feldt) are unreliable due to small sample size.  

Alternatives if assumptions are violated:  
- For non-normal continuous outcomes:  
  - Friedman test (nonparametric repeated-measures test) for overall time effect, followed by post-hoc Wilcoxon signed-rank tests with multiple comparison adjustment.  
- For complex correlation (teeth within patients, non-sphericity):  
  - Linear mixed-effects model (LMM) with:  
    - Random intercept for tooth; optional random intercept for patient if convergence allows.  
    - Time as categorical fixed effect.  
    - Robust (sandwich) standard errors if distributional assumptions are questionable.  
- If change is essentially monotonic and linear: simple repeated-measures regression with time as continuous predictor (LMM).

Implementation detail (plan):  
- For each continuous outcome:  
  - Start with LMM: outcome_ij = β0 + β1*time_ij + u_tooth + ε_ij (time as categorical).  
  - If necessary, add random intercept for patient.  
  - For reporting: estimate mean difference from baseline at each time and 95% CI; test overall time effect (likelihood ratio test or F test).  
  - Use RM-ANOVA only if mixed modelling is unavailable.

A3. Pre-post comparison for key time points using paired tests  
--------------------------------------------------------------  

Test names:  
- Paired t-test (for normally distributed changes).  
- Wilcoxon signed-rank test (nonparametric alternative).

Primary contrasts:  
- Pre-op vs 12 months.  
- Pre-op vs 24 months.  

Outcomes:  
- Apical diameter (mm).  
- Root length (mm).  
- Dentinal wall thickness at 4 locations (mm).  

Purpose:  
- To quantify magnitude and significance of change at clinically relevant follow-up times (12 and 24 months) relative to baseline.

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):  
1. Pairs are clearly defined (same tooth before and after treatment); paired tests control for tooth-level baseline heterogeneity.  
2. Continuous data with small sample size: paired t/Wilcoxon give straightforward effect estimates and p-values; clinically easy to interpret.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Paired t-test assumes approximately normal distribution of within-tooth differences; with n=23 this may not hold.  
- Does not use intermediate time points or longitudinal structure; may ignore patterns like early vs late change.  

When NOT to use:  
- Do not use paired t-test when change scores are severely skewed or contain strong outliers; in that case use Wilcoxon signed-rank.

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- Wilcoxon signed-rank test for non-normal change scores.

A4. Categorical clinical outcomes over time (symptoms, vitality, radiographic healing)

Test names:

Outcomes:

Purpose:

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):  
1. Outcomes are binary, measured repeatedly on the same tooth; McNemar and Cochran’s Q specifically account for within-tooth pairing.

Alternative if assumptions violated:

B1. Linear mixed-effects models accounting for clustering by patient  
-------------------------------------------------------------------  

Test:

Purpose:

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):

B2. Nonparametric repeated measures for continuous outcomes  
-----------------------------------------------------------  

Test:  
- Friedman test for each continuous parameter, across time points.  

Purpose:  
- To provide assumption-free confirmation of time effects when parametric assumptions for RM-ANOVA/LMM are questionable.

Why appropriate (≥2 reasons):  
1. Repeated measurements on the same tooth, but without assuming normality; Friedman uses rank-based method.  
2. Particularly suitable for small samples with potential skewness and outliers, common with radiographic measurements.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Friedman requires complete data across all time points; missing values (if any) cause casewise deletion.  
- It cannot adjust for clustering by patient or covariates; only tests global time effect.  

When NOT to use:  
- When missingness is non-trivial or unbalanced follow-up schedules exist.  
- When interest is in modeling effect modifiers or covariates.  

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- Use LMM with robust SEs; or reduce number of time points (e.g., focus on 0, 12, 24 months) so nonparametric methods are more manageable.

TASK 3 – INAPPROPRIATE TESTS  
=============================  

List of tests/methods that should NOT be used for this study, with rationale:

1. Independent-samples t-test or unpaired tests between time points  
---------------------------------------------------------------  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Measurements at different time points are on the same teeth (paired, correlated), not independent samples. Using unpaired t-tests or Mann–Whitney tests for pre vs post would ignore pairing and inflate standard errors or misrepresent variance.  
- Proper alternative:  
  - Paired t-test, Wilcoxon signed-rank, RM-ANOVA, or LMM.

2. One-way ANOVA treating all measurements across time as independent  
---------------------------------------------------------------------  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Repeated measures violate independence assumption; error term would be incorrect, leading to invalid p-values.  
- Proper alternative:  
  - Repeated-measures ANOVA or mixed-effects model.

3. Chi-square test of independence for repeated binary outcomes over time  
-------------------------------------------------------------------------  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Multiple measurements from the same teeth; chi-square assumes independence of observations.  
- Proper alternative:  
  - McNemar’s test, Cochran’s Q, or GLMM/GEE for binary longitudinal data.

4. Simple linear regression treating all longitudinal observations as independent  
---------------------------------------------------------------------------------  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Ignoring within-tooth correlation and clustering by patient leads to overly narrow confidence intervals.  
- Proper alternative:  
  - Linear mixed-effects regression.

5. Pearson correlation between time point and outcome using pooled data without accounting for repeated measures  
----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - Each tooth contributes multiple data points; standard Pearson correlation would treat them as independent.  
- Proper alternative:  
  - Mixed-effects regression with time as predictor.

6. Survival/event analyses comparing PRF to “control” within this dataset  
-------------------------------------------------------------------------  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - This is a single-arm cohort—no control group (e.g., blood clot, calcium hydroxide). Any “comparison” would be artificial unless external controls are used formally (which would require different methods, e.g., propensity-score matching with external data).  

7. Parametric tests assuming normality without any assessment or with clearly non-normal data  
---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------  
- Example: Paired t-test on extremely skewed differences with n=23 and strong outliers.  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - May produce invalid inference; small sample size makes normal approximation fragile.  
- Proper alternative:  
  - Nonparametric tests, transformations, or robust regression methods.

8. Complex multivariable models with many predictors (e.g., multiple regression with >3 predictors)  
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------  
- Why inappropriate:  
  - With n=23 teeth, including many predictors violates events-per-variable rules and risks severe overfitting.  
- Proper alternative:  
  - Limit any exploratory modeling to 1–2 predictors or rely primarily on univariable analyses.
