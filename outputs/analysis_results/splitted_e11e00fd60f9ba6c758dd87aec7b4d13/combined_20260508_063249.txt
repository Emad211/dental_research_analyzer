## TASK 1 — Information extraction (study characteristics)

### Study design and setting
- **Design:** Parallel-group **randomized clinical trial (RCT)**, **1:1 allocation**.
- **Blinding:** **Participants, legal guardians, and data assessor blinded** to intervention; **operator not blinded**. **Radiographic assessor blinding not feasible** (materials distinguishable on radiographs). Operator did **treatment + clinical/radiographic assessments** (risk of detection bias).
- **Guidelines/registration:** CONSORT; registered at ClinicalTrials.gov **NCT03166748**.
- **Setting:** Outpatient clinic, Department of Pedodontics, Faculty of Dentistry, Cairo University (Egypt).

### Objective
- Compare **clinical and radiographic outcomes** of **5% potassium nitrate (KNO₃) in polycarboxylate cement** versus **mineral trioxide aggregate (MTA)** as **pulpotomy biomaterials** in **asymptomatic vital immature permanent mandibular first molars**.

### Participants / eligibility
- **Population:** Children **6–9 years**.
- **Unit of randomization/analysis (as described):** **Molars**.
- **Inclusion:** Asymptomatic, vital, immature mandibular first molar with **deep extensive caries** causing **pulp exposure during excavation**; no preoperative acute pulp/periapical symptoms; controlled bleeding after access (teeth with excessive uncontrolled bleeding or no bleeding excluded).
- **Exclusion:** Medically compromised; necrotic pulp; acute pulp/periapical inflammation; abnormal anatomy; furcation involvement; canal calcification; nonrestorable tooth.

### Sample size and flow
- **Power analysis:** α=0.05, power=0.80; target **50 participants/teeth** (25/group), inflated for loss to follow-up. Mentions: 42 needed to exclude difference >10% in 95% CI (suggests **non-inferiority/equivalence-style** logic, though not explicitly stated).
- **Enrolled:** 69 screened, 50 randomized.
- **Analyzed sample:** 50 molars from **48 children** (i.e., **some clustering**: 2 children contributed 2 molars).
- **Loss to follow-up:** **1 per group** (per CONSORT diagram).

### Randomization and allocation concealment
- **Sequence generation:** random.org by main supervisor.
- **Concealment:** sequence in **sealed envelopes**; opened after hemostasis, at material placement step.

### Interventions
- **Common steps:** anesthesia, rubber dam, caries excavation → pulp exposure, access preparation, coronal pulp amputation, hemostasis with saline gauze + **ferric sulfate 15.5%** for 15 seconds, temporary GI; periapical radiograph to verify material level; final restoration composite.
- **Group 1 (Intervention):** **5% KNO₃ + polycarboxylate cement**, mixed as 95 mg ZnO + 5 mg KNO₃ with polyacrylic acid, 1:1 powder:liquid; placed **3–4 mm**.
- **Group 2 (Control):** **White MTA**, mixed per manufacturer, placed **3–4 mm**, moistened cotton pellet and temporized due to setting time; next-day revisit to remove cotton pellet and place RMGI base.

### Outcomes and measurement schedule

#### Primary outcomes (binary “yes/no”)
1. **Clinical success/failure** at:
   - **1 week** (phone)
   - **6 months** (clinical visit)
   - **12 months** (clinical visit)  
   Failures: spontaneous pain, sensitivity to percussion/palpation, swelling/sinus tract.
2. **Radiographic success/failure** at:
   - **6 months**
   - **12 months**  
   Success: root maturogenesis progress. Failures include periradicular/interradicular lesion, internal/external root resorption.

- **Protocol for emergencies:** pain/swelling → emergency visit → shifted to full RCT (root canal treatment) and **case excluded** (important for SAP: this is *not* ITT as described).

#### Secondary outcomes (continuous/derived)
- **Radiometric measures** from pre-op, 6m, 12m periapical radiographs (Photoshop):
  - **Root length** (mesial and distal roots) in mm (pixel-to-mm conversion using ruler calibration).
  - **Apical foramen diameter** (mesial and distal roots) in mm.
  - **Magnification adjustment** using crown height to compute factor; apply to postoperative measures.
  - **Derived endpoint:** **percentage difference** between preoperative and postoperative (6m and 12m) for mesial and distal roots.
  - At 12m: count roots with **complete apical closure** vs incomplete.

### Data structure considerations (important)
- Repeated measures over time (baseline, 6m, 12m) for continuous outcomes.
- Multiple correlated units:
  - Some children contribute **two teeth**.
  - Each tooth contributes **two roots** (mesial, distal) and repeated measures.
  - Therefore: **hierarchical clustering** (root within tooth within child) + longitudinal correlation.

## TASK 2 — Statistical analysis plan (tests)

### General analysis principles (apply to all categories)
- **Analysis sets:**
  - **Intention-to-treat (ITT):** analyze all randomized teeth as allocated. Handle missing outcomes via appropriate methods (see below).
  - **Per-protocol (PP):** exclude major protocol deviations; include as sensitivity only.
  - **Avoid excluding “emergency RCT” conversions from the analysis**; instead treat as **failures** for primary success endpoints (clinically conservative and consistent with efficacy evaluation).
- **Baseline comparability:** describe age, sex, tooth characteristics (if available), pre-op radiographic status.
- **Effect sizes:** report **risk difference, risk ratio**, and/or **odds ratio**, with **95% CI**, plus absolute success rates.
- **Multiplicity:** primary outcomes are multiple (clinical + radiographic and multiple timepoints). Pre-specify hierarchy:
  1) 12-month overall success (composite clinical+radiographic), then  
  2) 12-month clinical, 12-month radiographic, then earlier timepoints; control family-wise error or clearly label secondary.
- **Missing data:**
  - For binary outcomes: multiple imputation (MI) or conservative imputation (missing=failure) as sensitivity.
  - For continuous longitudinal outcomes: mixed models accommodate MAR; MI as sensitivity.
- **Clustering:** because 50 molars from 48 children and 2 roots/tooth, use **mixed-effects** or **GEE** where relevant.

# CATEGORY A — MANDATORY tests (analysis incomplete without)

## A1) Mixed-effects logistic regression (or GEE logistic) for binary primary outcomes
- **Test/model:** Generalized linear mixed model (**GLMM**, logit link) *or* **GEE** logistic regression.
- **Purpose:** Compare **probability of success/failure** between groups over time (6m, 12m; optionally include 1-week clinical) while accounting for correlation.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Primary outcomes are **binary** (yes/no).
  2. Handles **correlated observations** (repeated follow-ups; possible 2 teeth/child).
  3. Provides **adjusted effect sizes** (OR/RR via marginal models) with CI.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - With **very few failures**, models can suffer from **separation**/unstable estimates.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If there is only **one timepoint** and no clustering and events are adequate, a simpler risk comparison may suffice; or if event counts are too low for logistic modeling.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated / sparse events:**
  - **Firth penalized logistic regression** (for separation).
  - **Exact logistic regression** (small samples).
  - For single timepoint: **Fisher’s exact test** with risk difference CI.

**Implementation suggestion:**  
Outcome = failure (1/0). Fixed effects: group, time, group×time. Random intercept: child (and possibly tooth nested within child if >1 tooth/child), or use GEE with exchangeable correlation at child level and repeated within tooth.

---

## A2) Linear mixed-effects model (LMM) for continuous radiometric secondary outcomes
- **Test/model:** **Linear mixed-effects model** for:
  - Root length (mm) and/or % change
  - Apical foramen diameter (mm) and/or % change
- **Purpose:** Compare **changes over time** between materials, accounting for repeated measures and clustering of roots within teeth/children.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Outcomes are **continuous** with **baseline + follow-up** measures.
  2. Handles **longitudinal correlation** and **unbalanced data** (loss to follow-up).
  3. Can model **root (mesial/distal)** and time simultaneously.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - If measurement distributions are highly non-normal with heavy tails and small n, LMM inference may be sensitive.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If only a single post value exists and baseline is not available (not the case here), or if there is extreme non-normality with no workable transformation.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Transform outcome (e.g., log for foramen diameter if skewed).
  - **Robust LMM** / **Huber-White sandwich SE**.
  - **Nonparametric longitudinal methods** (e.g., rank-based mixed models) if severe.

**Implementation suggestion:**  
Model outcome at 6m and 12m with baseline as covariate (ANCOVA-style mixed model), fixed: group, time, group×time, root (mesial/distal), interactions as needed; random: child and tooth.

---

## A3) Fisher’s exact test (or Chi-square) for 12-month apical closure counts
- **Test:** **Fisher’s exact test** (preferred) comparing proportion of roots/teeth with **complete apical closure** at 12 months between groups.
- **Purpose:** Simple comparison of a key clinically meaningful binary milestone at final follow-up.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Binary endpoint at a single timepoint.
  2. Sample size is modest and expected cell counts may be small → Fisher is reliable.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - Ignores clustering (two roots per tooth; two teeth in some children).
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When clustering is non-negligible and inference must be cluster-adjusted.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated / clustering important:**
  - **Mixed-effects logistic regression** at root level (random intercept for tooth/child).
  - **GEE logistic** with clustering by child/tooth.

# CATEGORY B — RECOMMENDED tests (improve rigor)

## B1) Baseline comparability: standardized mean differences (SMD) + appropriate tests
- **Tests:**  
  - Continuous baseline: t-test or Mann–Whitney (descriptive), but emphasize **SMD**.  
  - Categorical baseline: chi-square/Fisher (descriptive).
- **Purpose:** Check balance after randomization and describe sample.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Confirms randomization achieved balance; informs need for covariate adjustment.
  2. SMD is not driven by sample size and is preferred for RCT balance reporting.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - Hypothesis tests of baseline differences can be misleading in RCTs (randomization already justifies exchangeability).
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Do not use baseline p-values to decide inclusion/exclusion of covariates mechanically.
- **Alternative:**
  - Report **SMD only** with clinical judgment; pre-specify adjustment covariates.

---

## B2) Adjusted analyses (covariate-adjusted GLMM/LMM)
- **Test/model:** Add covariates to models: age, sex, baseline root length/foramen diameter, initial apex openness, caries extent (if recorded).
- **Purpose:** Improve precision and address residual imbalance.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Covariate adjustment increases efficiency in small-to-moderate RCTs.
  2. Baseline radiometric values are strong predictors of follow-up measures.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - Overfitting risk if too many covariates relative to failures.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If events are very sparse, keep models parsimonious.
- **Alternative:**
  - Use **penalized regression** (Firth) for sparse binary outcomes.

---

## B3) Time-to-failure analysis (if dates available)
- **Test/model:** **Kaplan–Meier** curves + **log-rank test**, and/or **Cox proportional hazards** (with frailty/robust SE for clustering).
- **Purpose:** Use timing of postoperative emergencies/failures rather than fixed timepoint success.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Handles censoring (lost to follow-up) naturally.
  2. More informative when failures occur at varying times.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - Only discrete assessment times (1w/6m/12m) and few failures may limit interpretability.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If failure time cannot be reasonably ascertained.
- **Alternative:**
  - Discrete-time survival model (complementary log-log) using visit intervals.

---

## B4) Reliability analysis for radiographic measurements (if any re-measurement is feasible)
- **Test:** **Intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC)** for continuous measures; **kappa** for binary radiographic outcomes.
- **Purpose:** Quantify measurement repeatability (Photoshop-based measures; unblinded assessor).
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Radiometric outcomes depend on operator measurements and magnification adjustment.
  2. Supports credibility of measurement process.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - If no replicate measurements or second assessor data exist.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If only one measurement per image is available with no repeats.
- **Alternative:**
  - Bland–Altman limits of agreement for repeated continuous measures.

## TASK 3 — Inappropriate tests (should NOT be used)

1. **Multiple independent t-tests at each timepoint** for radiometric outcomes  
   - Why inappropriate: ignores **repeated measures** and **root/tooth/child clustering**; inflates type I error; wastes baseline information.

2. **Paired t-test within each group only (pre vs 12m) without between-group comparison**  
   - Why inappropriate: answers the wrong question (within-group change ≠ treatment effect).

3. **Simple chi-square tests for repeated binary outcomes at multiple follow-ups** without accounting for within-tooth correlation/time  
   - Why inappropriate: violates independence; misleading p-values.

4. **One-way ANOVA** treating timepoints as independent groups  
   - Why inappropriate: time is within-subject; must use repeated measures/mixed modeling.

5. **Stepwise regression to “select” covariates based on p-values**  
   - Why inappropriate: unstable with small sample; increases bias; poor reproducibility.

6. **Correlation (Pearson/Spearman) between treatment group and outcome**  
   - Why inappropriate: treatment is categorical; correlation is not an effect estimate and does not address repeated measures.

## TASK 4 — Summary justification of the analytical approach

A mixed-model framework is the most defensible approach because outcomes are (i) **binary clinical/radiographic success** measured at multiple follow-ups and (ii) **continuous radiometric measurements** collected longitudinally for **two roots per tooth**, with **possible multiple teeth per child**. GLMM/GEE for binary endpoints and LMM for continuous endpoints properly address **non-independence**, accommodate **missing follow-up**, and yield clinically interpretable **effect sizes with confidence intervals**. This plan also avoids the protocol’s problematic “exclude failures” approach by prioritizing **ITT** (with sensitivity analyses), preserving randomization and preventing bias.

### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS
1. **Chi-Square Test (or Fisher’s Exact Test)**:
   - **Purpose**: To compare the binary clinical and radiographic success rates between the two groups.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. The primary outcomes are categorical (success/failure).
     2. The study involves independent groups (intervention vs. control).
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: If expected cell counts in the contingency table are <5, Fisher’s Exact Test should be used instead.
   - **When NOT to Use**: For continuous outcomes or when assumptions of independence are violated.
   - **Alternative**: Fisher’s Exact Test for small sample sizes.

2. **Independent Samples t-Test (or Mann-Whitney U Test)**:
   - **Purpose**: To compare the mean percentage change in root length and apical foramen diameter between the two groups.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. The secondary outcomes are continuous variables.
     2. The study design involves two independent groups.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: If the data are not normally distributed or if variances are unequal.
   - **When NOT to Use**: For non-normal data or when sample size is too small to assume normality.
   - **Alternative**: Mann-Whitney U Test for non-parametric data.

3. **Kaplan-Meier Survival Analysis with Log-Rank Test**:
   - **Purpose**: To assess the time to failure (e.g., clinical or radiographic failure) between the two groups.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Time-to-event data is relevant for follow-up outcomes.
     2. Allows for censoring of cases lost to follow-up.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: If the proportional hazards assumption is violated.
   - **When NOT to Use**: For non-time-to-event data.
   - **Alternative**: Cox proportional hazards model if covariates need to be adjusted.

---

#### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED TESTS
1. **Repeated Measures ANOVA (or Mixed-Effects Model)**:
   - **Purpose**: To analyze changes in root length and apical foramen diameter over time (preoperative, 6 months, 12 months) within and between groups.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Accounts for repeated measurements within the same subjects.
     2. Tests both time and group effects simultaneously.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Assumes sphericity, which may not hold.
   - **When NOT to Use**: For non-normal data or unequal variances.
   - **Alternative**: Mixed-effects model for non-sphericity or missing data.

2. **Cohen’s d (Effect Size)**:
   - **Purpose**: To quantify the magnitude of the difference in outcomes between the two groups.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Provides a standardized measure of effect size.
     2. Complements p-values by indicating clinical significance.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Does not account for confounding variables.
   - **When NOT to Use**: For non-comparable scales.
   - **Alternative**: Partial eta-squared for repeated measures.

---

#### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL TESTS
1. **Subgroup Analysis**:
   - **Purpose**: To explore differences in outcomes based on age, gender, or other baseline characteristics.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Identifies potential effect modifiers.
     2. Provides additional insights into treatment efficacy.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Risk of type I error due to multiple comparisons.
   - **When NOT to Use**: For small sample sizes or unbalanced subgroups.
   - **Alternative**: Adjust p-values using Bonferroni correction.

2. **Regression Analysis**:
   - **Purpose**: To adjust for potential confounders (e.g., baseline root length, age).
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Controls for covariates that may influence outcomes.
     2. Provides adjusted effect estimates.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Overfitting risk with small sample sizes.
   - **When NOT to Use**: For unmeasured confounders or highly correlated predictors.
   - **Alternative**: Propensity score matching for balancing covariates.
