### TASK 1: INFORMATION EXTRACTION

#### Study Characteristics:
1. **Study Design**:
   - Parallel, randomized clinical trial (RCT) with a 1:1 allocation ratio.
   - Conducted in accordance with CONSORT guidelines.

2. **Objective**:
   - To compare the clinical and radiographic outcomes of potassium nitrate (KNO₃) in polycarboxylate cement versus mineral trioxide aggregate (MTA) as pulpotomy biomaterials in asymptomatic vital immature mandibular first molars.

3. **Groups**:
   - **Intervention Group**: 5% potassium nitrate in polycarboxylate cement as the pulpotomy material.
   - **Control Group**: Mineral trioxide aggregate (MTA) as the pulpotomy material.

4. **Sample Size**:
   - 50 molars from 48 children (25 molars in each group).
   - Sample size determined via power analysis (α = 0.05, power = 0.8).

5. **Eligibility Criteria**:
   - **Inclusion**: Children aged 6–9 years with asymptomatic vital immature mandibular first molars, extensive deep caries with pulp exposure during excavation, no preoperative acute pulp or periapical symptoms.
   - **Exclusion**: Medically compromised children, necrotic pulp, acute pulp or periapical inflammation, abnormal tooth anatomy, furcation involvement, canal calcification, or nonrestorable teeth.

6. **Variables**:
   - **Primary Outcomes**:
     - Clinical success: Absence of spontaneous pain, sensitivity to percussion/palpation, soft tissue swelling, or sinus tract.
     - Radiographic success: Root maturogenesis progress, absence of periapical/interradicular lesions, and no internal/external root resorption.
   - **Secondary Outcomes**:
     - Radiometric analysis of root length and apical foramen diameter at preoperative, 6-month, and 12-month intervals.

7. **Measurements**:
   - Clinical evaluations at 1 week (via phone), 6 months, and 12 months.
   - Radiographic evaluations at 6 and 12 months.
   - Radiometric analysis using Photoshop software for root length and apical foramen diameter, with adjustments for magnification discrepancies.

8. **Randomization and Blinding**:
   - Random sequence generation using http://www.random.org/.
   - Allocation concealment using sealed envelopes.
   - Blinding: Participants, legal guardians, and data assessors were blinded. However, the operator was not blinded due to material handling differences.

9. **Data Collection Methods**:
   - Clinical evaluations: Binary outcomes (yes/no) for clinical signs and symptoms.
   - Radiographic evaluations: Binary outcomes (yes/no) for root development and failure signs.
   - Radiometric analysis: Measurements in pixels converted to millimeters.

10. **Follow-Up**:
    - Loss to follow-up: 1 case in each group.

### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS
1. **Chi-Square Test (or Fisher’s Exact Test)**:
   - **Purpose**: To compare the binary clinical and radiographic success rates between the two groups.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. The primary outcomes are categorical (success/failure).
     2. The study involves independent groups (intervention vs. control).
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: If expected cell counts in the contingency table are <5, Fisher’s Exact Test should be used instead.
   - **When NOT to Use**: For continuous outcomes or when assumptions of independence are violated.
   - **Alternative**: Fisher’s Exact Test for small sample sizes.

2. **Independent Samples t-Test (or Mann-Whitney U Test)**:
   - **Purpose**: To compare the mean percentage change in root length and apical foramen diameter between the two groups.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. The secondary outcomes are continuous variables.
     2. The study design involves two independent groups.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: If the data are not normally distributed or if variances are unequal.
   - **When NOT to Use**: For non-normal data or when sample size is too small to assume normality.
   - **Alternative**: Mann-Whitney U Test for non-parametric data.

3. **Kaplan-Meier Survival Analysis with Log-Rank Test**:
   - **Purpose**: To assess the time to failure (e.g., clinical or radiographic failure) between the two groups.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Time-to-event data is relevant for follow-up outcomes.
     2. Allows for censoring of cases lost to follow-up.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: If the proportional hazards assumption is violated.
   - **When NOT to Use**: For non-time-to-event data.
   - **Alternative**: Cox proportional hazards model if covariates need to be adjusted.

---

#### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED TESTS
1. **Repeated Measures ANOVA (or Mixed-Effects Model)**:
   - **Purpose**: To analyze changes in root length and apical foramen diameter over time (preoperative, 6 months, 12 months) within and between groups.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Accounts for repeated measurements within the same subjects.
     2. Tests both time and group effects simultaneously.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Assumes sphericity, which may not hold.
   - **When NOT to Use**: For non-normal data or unequal variances.
   - **Alternative**: Mixed-effects model for non-sphericity or missing data.

2. **Cohen’s d (Effect Size)**:
   - **Purpose**: To quantify the magnitude of the difference in outcomes between the two groups.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Provides a standardized measure of effect size.
     2. Complements p-values by indicating clinical significance.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Does not account for confounding variables.
   - **When NOT to Use**: For non-comparable scales.
   - **Alternative**: Partial eta-squared for repeated measures.

---

#### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL TESTS
1. **Subgroup Analysis**:
   - **Purpose**: To explore differences in outcomes based on age, gender, or other baseline characteristics.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Identifies potential effect modifiers.
     2. Provides additional insights into treatment efficacy.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Risk of type I error due to multiple comparisons.
   - **When NOT to Use**: For small sample sizes or unbalanced subgroups.
   - **Alternative**: Adjust p-values using Bonferroni correction.

2. **Regression Analysis**:
   - **Purpose**: To adjust for potential confounders (e.g., baseline root length, age).
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Controls for covariates that may influence outcomes.
     2. Provides adjusted effect estimates.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Overfitting risk with small sample sizes.
   - **When NOT to Use**: For unmeasured confounders or highly correlated predictors.
   - **Alternative**: Propensity score matching for balancing covariates.
