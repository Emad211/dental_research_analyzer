# CATEGORY A — Mandatory (analysis incomplete without these)

## A1) 2×2 diagnostic tables + sensitivity with 95% CI (exact/binomial or Wilson)
- **Purpose**: Quantify sensitivity of periapical and panoramic radiographs using CBCT as reference (preop and follow-up separately).
- **Why appropriate**
  1. Outcome is **binary** (lesion present/absent) and study goal is explicitly **sensitivity**.
  2. Produces interpretable diagnostic performance with **uncertainty** (CI).
- **Why may not be appropriate**
  - If CBCT is not applied to all eligible cases/timepoints (verification bias) or if lesion definition differs materially between methods.
- **When NOT to use**
  - When the “reference standard” is missing for a non-random subset or when the unit of analysis is unclear and clustering is ignored in inference.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated**
  - Use **cluster-robust** or **GEE-based** CI for sensitivity (see A3), or **bootstrap by patient** to obtain CIs accounting for clustering.

## A2) McNemar test (paired) to compare sensitivities: periapical vs panoramic (within the same teeth) at each timepoint
- **Purpose**: Test whether the proportion of positives differs between two paired index tests (periapical vs panoramic), using only discordant pairs.
- **Why appropriate**
  1. Same tooth is evaluated by both index tests ⇒ **paired binary data**.
  2. Directly addresses whether one 2D modality detects more lesions than the other (paired comparison).
- **Why may not be appropriate**
  - If there are very few discordant pairs; asymptotic McNemar may be unstable.
- **When NOT to use**
  - When comparisons are **unpaired** (different subjects/teeth in each modality) or when there is heavy clustering and you need patient-level inference.
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated**
  - **Exact McNemar** test (small discordant counts) or **GEE logistic** model with modality as predictor and patient as cluster.

## A3) Methods to handle clustering (if multiple teeth/observations per patient): GEE logistic regression or cluster bootstrap
- **Purpose**: Obtain valid standard errors/CIs when multiple teeth are contributed by the same patient.
- **Why appropriate**
  1. Dental studies commonly have **within-patient correlation**.
  2. Sensitivity estimates and modality comparisons otherwise have **too-narrow CIs** (inflated type I error).
- **Why may not be appropriate**
  - If each patient contributes only one tooth/lesion/timepoint (no clustering).
- **When NOT to use**
  - When the cluster size is 1 for all (no correlation to model) or the number of clusters is extremely small (GEE SEs unreliable).
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated**
  - **Cluster bootstrap** by patient, or **mixed-effects logistic regression** (random intercept for patient).

# CATEGORY B — Recommended (improves rigor)

## B1) Agreement between index tests and CBCT: Cohen’s kappa (and/or prevalence-adjusted kappa), with CI
- **Purpose**: Quantify agreement beyond chance between each 2D test and CBCT.
- **Why appropriate**
  1. Provides a standardized measure of **diagnostic agreement**, complementary to sensitivity.
  2. Useful because “lesion present/absent” is **categorical**; Landis & Koch is cited.
- **Why may not be appropriate**
  - Kappa is **prevalence dependent**; high/low lesion prevalence can yield misleadingly low kappa despite high sensitivity.
- **When NOT to use**
  - If marginal distributions are extremely imbalanced or if agreement is not the target construct.
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated**
  - Report **positive/negative agreement**, **Gwet’s AC1**, or **PABAK** alongside kappa.

## B2) Compare sensitivity preoperative vs follow-up within each modality (paired over time): McNemar test or GEE with time factor
- **Purpose**: Determine whether sensitivity changes from preop to follow-up for periapical and for panoramic.
- **Why appropriate**
  1. Same teeth assessed at two timepoints ⇒ **paired** comparison.
  2. Clinically relevant: detection may differ when lesions shrink/heal.
- **Why may not be appropriate**
  - If the set of teeth imaged differs between timepoints (loss to follow-up not random).
- **When NOT to use**
  - When follow-up observations are missing non-ignorable and not addressed.
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated**
  - Use **inverse probability weighting** for follow-up missingness (if data available), or sensitivity analyses under missing-not-at-random scenarios.

## B3) Measurement reliability on calibration images: ICC for continuous area/volume; kappa for lesion presence
- **Purpose**: Quantify **intra-examiner** (test–retest) reliability for size measurements and/or categorical detection.
- **Why appropriate**
  1. The Methods describe repeated readings (10 images, twice) ⇒ reliability is expected.
  2. Manual tracing can introduce measurement error; ICC quantifies it.
- **Why may not be appropriate**
  - The final study measurements were done “jointly/consensus,” limiting independent inter-rater reliability for study data.
- **When NOT to use**
  - When repeated measurements are not available or raters are not independent.
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated**
  - Use **weighted kappa** (if ordinal), or **Bland–Altman** (see B4) for repeated continuous measures.

## B4) Bland–Altman analysis (calibration dataset): agreement of repeated area measurements (and possibly between periapical vs panoramic area)
- **Purpose**: Assess bias and limits of agreement for continuous measurements.
- **Why appropriate**
  1. Manual tracing yields continuous outcomes; Bland–Altman reveals systematic bias.
  2. Complements ICC by showing **magnitude** of disagreement.
- **Why may not be appropriate**
  - If differences are heteroscedastic and not addressed (often lesion size-related).
- **When NOT to use**
  - When measurements are not on the same scale or are not paired.
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated**
  - Log-transform areas or use **ratio limits of agreement**; or regression-based Bland–Altman.

## B5) Relationship between 2D area and CBCT volume: Spearman correlation + regression (possibly log-log)
- **Purpose**: Evaluate whether 2D area tracks 3D volume.
- **Why appropriate**
  1. Area and volume are continuous and likely **non-normal** with skew.
  2. Provides quantitative insight into whether 2D size is a proxy for 3D defect.
- **Why may not be appropriate**
  - Correlation does not imply agreement and can be inflated by range.
- **When NOT to use**
  - When many zero/near-zero lesions at follow-up produce severe floor effects without appropriate handling.
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated**
  - Use **Kendall’s tau**, robust regression, or model with **Tobit/two-part** approach if many zeros.

## TASK 3 — Tests that should NOT be used (and why)

1. **Independent-samples chi-square test** to compare detection rates between modalities  
   - **Why not**: periapical vs panoramic detections are **paired on the same teeth**; violates independence. Use **McNemar** or paired GEE.

2. **Unpaired t-test / one-way ANOVA** on lesion presence (binary outcome)  
   - **Why not**: lesion presence is not continuous/normal. Use logistic models or paired binary tests.

3. **Pearson correlation alone** to claim agreement between 2D area and CBCT volume  
   - **Why not**: correlation ≠ agreement; also likely non-normal and heteroscedastic. Use Spearman + regression; for agreement use Bland–Altman-type approaches (after scale considerations).

4. **Simple (non-clustered) logistic regression** if multiple teeth per patient  
   - **Why not**: underestimates SEs due to clustering. Use **mixed-effects** or **GEE**.

5. **Kappa as the only validity metric**  
   - **Why not**: study aim is sensitivity; kappa is prevalence-dependent and does not directly answer detection probability conditional on disease.

## TASK 3 – INAPPROPRIATE TESTS AND WHY

Below are tests that should **not** be used, or only with caution, in this study context.

1. **Independent-samples t-test for comparing pre- vs follow-up lesion size**
   - **Why inappropriate**
     - Pre- and post-op measurements are paired on the same tooth/lesion; using an independent t-test ignores within-tooth correlation and inflates variance, reducing power.
   - **Correct alternative**: Paired t-test or Wilcoxon signed-rank test.

2. **Chi-square test of independence for comparing periapical vs panoramic sensitivity (unpaired)**
   - **Why inappropriate**
     - Sensitivity comparison is based on paired data (same tooth imaged with both modalities). Ignoring pairing violates independence and misestimates variance.
   - **Correct alternative**: McNemar’s test (or exact McNemar).

3. **Simple Pearson correlation alone to assess agreement between examiners**
   - **Why inappropriate**
     - Pearson correlation measures association, not absolute agreement; two examiners could be perfectly correlated but systematically biased.
   - **Correct alternative**: ICC and Bland–Altman analysis.

4. **Ordinary least squares (OLS) regression of 2D lesion presence (yes/no) on CBCT presence (yes/no) as continuous variables**
   - **Why inappropriate**
     - Binary outcomes violate OLS assumptions (normality, homoscedasticity) and yield predictions outside [0,1].
   - **Correct alternative**: Logistic regression or direct calculation of sensitivity/specificity.

5. **ANOVA for multiple comparisons of sensitivity across modalities without accounting for paired design**
   - **Why inappropriate**
     - Sensitivity is binary; ANOVA on proportions without appropriate pairing/weighting will mis-specify the model.
   - **Correct alternative**: McNemar’s test, GEE, or logistic mixed models.

6. **Parametric tests assuming normality of lesion size without checking distribution**
   - **Why inappropriate**
     - Lesion size distributions (area/volume) often skewed and may include many small or zero values.
   - **Correct alternative**: Nonparametric tests (Wilcoxon), transformations, or robust statistics.
