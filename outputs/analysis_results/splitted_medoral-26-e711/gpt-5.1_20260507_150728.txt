### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY ANALYSES

#### A1. Descriptive statistics

**Test/Method:** Descriptive statistics (counts, proportions, means/medians)

- **Purpose**
  - Summarize sample characteristics, number of lesions, distribution of imaging findings, lesion sizes, follow‑up times.
- **Why appropriate**
  1. Essential baseline description for any clinical study.

- **Alternative if assumptions violated**
  - Use nonparametric summaries (median, IQR) if distributions are skewed instead of mean/SD alone.

---

#### A2. Sensitivity estimation for periapical and panoramic radiographs vs CBCT

**Test/Method:** Calculation of sensitivity and 95% confidence intervals (CIs) using binomial methods

- **Purpose**
  - Quantify the ability of periapical and panoramic radiographs to detect lesions that are present on CBCT (reference standard), separately for:
    - Preoperative stage.
    - Follow-up stage.
- **Why appropriate**
  1. The study’s explicit aim is to determine the **sensitivity** of 2D radiographs.
  2. CBCT is treated as the diagnostic gold standard; sensitivity is defined relative to it.
- **Why it may not be appropriate**
  - If the reference standard (CBCT) is itself imperfect, sensitivity estimates may be biased.
- **When this test should NOT be used**
  - If CBCT is not consistently available for all units, or not clearly used as reference, or if verification bias exists (only some cases get CBCT based on 2D findings).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated**
  - If CBCT is not a perfect gold standard, consider **latent class models** for diagnostic accuracy without a gold standard, though this is more complex and requires additional assumptions.
  - For small sample sizes, use **Clopper–Pearson** exact CIs instead of normal approximations.

---

#### A3. Comparison of sensitivity between periapical and panoramic radiographs (paired)

**Test/Method:** McNemar’s test (for paired binary outcomes)

- **Purpose**
  - Compare sensitivity of periapical vs panoramic radiographs using paired data (same tooth imaged by both).
  - Could be done separately at preoperative and follow-up stages.
- **Why appropriate**
  1. Each tooth has paired binary outcomes (detected vs not detected) by the two 2D modalities, conditional on CBCT being positive.
  2. McNemar’s test is tailored for **paired nominal data**, focusing on discordant pairs.
- **Why it may not be appropriate**
  - Requires that each unit has both periapical and panoramic images; if many missing pairs, power and validity are reduced.
- **When this test should NOT be used**
  - If observations are independent between groups instead of paired (i.e., different teeth imaged with different modalities).
  - If sample size is extremely small with very few discordant pairs; standard asymptotic McNemar might be unstable.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated**
  - Use **exact McNemar’s test** for small numbers of discordant pairs.
  - If groups are independent rather than paired, use **chi‑square test** or **Fisher’s exact test** for sensitivity comparisons.

---

#### A4. Inter- and intra-examiner reliability for lesion presence (categorical)

**Test/Method:** Cohen’s kappa (κ) or weighted kappa; 95% CIs

- **Purpose**
  - Assess agreement between examiners in:
    - Presence/absence of lesions on each modality at each time point.
  - Possibly also between repeated readings.
- **Why appropriate**
  1. Binary categorical outcome (lesion yes/no) analyzed between two readers fits Cohen’s kappa.
  2. The text cites Landis & Koch; kappa is the standard for categorical inter-rater agreement, with interpretation thresholds from that reference.
- **Why it may not be appropriate**
  - Kappa depends on prevalence and may be low even when percentage agreement is high if prevalence is very high/low.
- **When this test should NOT be used**
  - When there are more than two observers and you want overall agreement; then use **Fleiss’ kappa** or other multirater statistics.
  - When data are ordinal with clear ordering (though weighted kappa can handle this).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated**
  - Use **Gwet’s AC1** or **percentage agreement** when prevalence paradox compromises kappa interpretation.
  - For multiple raters, use **Fleiss’ kappa** or **ICC** if treating ratings as continuous/ordinal.

---

#### A5. Reliability of lesion size measurements (continuous)

**Test/Method:** Intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC) for continuous measurements (area and volume); Bland–Altman plots

- **Purpose**
  - Evaluate inter- and intra-examiner reliability of:
    - Area measurements on periapical and panoramic radiographs.
    - Volume measurements on CBCT.
- **Why appropriate**
  1. Measurements are continuous (mm², mm³), and ICC quantifies reproducibility between raters/measures.
  2. The design uses repeated measurements on the same images; ICC accounts for within-image and between-image variance.
- **Why it may not be appropriate**
  - ICC assumes approximate normality and equal variance; outliers or strong skew can distort ICC.
- **When this test should NOT be used**
  - When data are strongly non-normal with many zero values (e.g., many healed lesions with area=0).
  - If only one measurement per imaging modality per examiner is available.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated**
  - Use **Spearman’s rank correlation** for monotonic relationships, though it assesses association, not agreement.
  - Use **Bland–Altman limits of agreement** alone without relying solely on ICC.

#### B2. Comparison of diagnostic performance between preoperative and follow-up stages

**Test/Method:** McNemar’s test and/or generalized estimating equations (GEE) for repeated binary measures

- **Purpose**
  - Compare sensitivity (and possibly specificity) of each modality between preoperative and follow-up stages, accounting for the same tooth at two time points.
- **Why appropriate**
  1. Binary outcomes measured repeatedly in the same unit (tooth), so paired/repeated measures methods are needed.
  2. GEE can handle correlation and adjust for covariates (e.g., tooth type, baseline lesion size).
- **Why it may not be appropriate**
  - Requires sufficient sample size with both time points available; missing data can bias results.
- **When this test should NOT be used**
  - If follow-up data are missing nonrandomly and not accounted for (e.g., only failures got CBCT).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated**
  - Use **McNemar’s test** separately for preoperative vs follow-up if there is full pairing.
  - Use **mixed-effects logistic regression** for more flexible modeling of correlated binary data.

#### B3. Agreement between lesion size on 2D and 3D imaging

**Test/Method:** Correlation analysis (Pearson or Spearman), regression modeling, Bland–Altman analysis

- **Purpose**
  - Explore how well 2D **area** measurements (periapical and panoramic) reflect 3D **volume** (CBCT) measurements both pre- and postoperatively.
- **Why appropriate**
  1. Provides quantitative linkage between the 2D and 3D metrics; helps interpret whether area can approximate volume.
  2. Bland–Altman assesses agreement and potential systematic bias (e.g., 2D underestimation of large volumes).
- **Why it may not be appropriate**
  - Area and volume may not be linearly related; using Pearson correlation might be misleading if relationship is nonlinear.
- **When this test should NOT be used**
  - If large numbers of cases have zero values (complete healing) leading to highly skewed distributions.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated**
  - Use **Spearman’s rank correlation** for monotonic but non-linear relationships.
  - Apply **log-transformation** on area and volume (after adding a small constant for zeros) before regression.

#### B4. Change in lesion size (healing) within modalities

**Test/Method:** Paired t-test (if normal) or Wilcoxon signed-rank test (for non-normal) for pre- vs follow-up area/volume

- **Purpose**

## TASK 3 – INAPPROPRIATE TESTS AND WHY

Below are tests that should **not** be used, or only with caution, in this study context.

1. **Independent-samples t-test for comparing pre- vs follow-up lesion size**
   - **Why inappropriate**
     - Pre- and post-op measurements are paired on the same tooth/lesion; using an independent t-test ignores within-tooth correlation and inflates variance, reducing power.
   - **Correct alternative**: Paired t-test or Wilcoxon signed-rank test.

2. **Chi-square test of independence for comparing periapical vs panoramic sensitivity (unpaired)**
   - **Why inappropriate**
     - Sensitivity comparison is based on paired data (same tooth imaged with both modalities). Ignoring pairing violates independence and misestimates variance.
   - **Correct alternative**: McNemar’s test (or exact McNemar).

3. **Simple Pearson correlation alone to assess agreement between examiners**
   - **Why inappropriate**
     - Pearson correlation measures association, not absolute agreement; two examiners could be perfectly correlated but systematically biased.
   - **Correct alternative**: ICC and Bland–Altman analysis.

4. **Ordinary least squares (OLS) regression of 2D lesion presence (yes/no) on CBCT presence (yes/no) as continuous variables**
   - **Why inappropriate**
     - Binary outcomes violate OLS assumptions (normality, homoscedasticity) and yield predictions outside [0,1].
   - **Correct alternative**: Logistic regression or direct calculation of sensitivity/specificity.

5. **ANOVA for multiple comparisons of sensitivity across modalities without accounting for paired design**
   - **Why inappropriate**
     - Sensitivity is binary; ANOVA on proportions without appropriate pairing/weighting will mis-specify the model.
   - **Correct alternative**: McNemar’s test, GEE, or logistic mixed models.

6. **Parametric tests assuming normality of lesion size without checking distribution**
   - **Why inappropriate**
     - Lesion size distributions (area/volume) often skewed and may include many small or zero values.
   - **Correct alternative**: Nonparametric tests (Wilcoxon), transformations, or robust statistics.
