### TASK 1: INFORMATION EXTRACTION

#### Study Characteristics:
1. **Study Design**:  
   - Prospective, in vivo, clinical study.

2. **Objective**:  
   - To compare postoperative pain levels at three different time intervals (6 hours, 24 hours, and 48 hours) after manual dynamic agitation of irrigation during endodontic treatment.

3. **Groups**:  
   - Single group of patients undergoing manual dynamic agitation during endodontic treatment. Pain levels were assessed at three time intervals (6, 24, and 48 hours).

4. **Sample Size**:  
   - 22 female patients.

5. **Inclusion Criteria**:  
   - Female patients aged 25–45 years.  
   - Medically free of systemic diseases.  
   - Symptomatic irreversible pulpitis in lower molars with mature roots.  
   - Teeth without calcified root canals, root caries, internal/external root resorption, or periodontal involvement.

6. **Exclusion Criteria**:  
   - Male patients.  
   - Pregnant females.  
   - Patients with bruxism/clenching.  
   - Previously endodontically treated teeth.  
   - Teeth with abnormal mobility, periapical abscess, periodontal space widening, or root fractures.

7. **Variables**:  
   - **Independent Variable**: Time intervals (6 hours, 24 hours, 48 hours).  
   - **Dependent Variable**: Pain levels measured using the Numerical Rating Scale (NRS).

8. **Measurements**:  
   - Pain levels assessed using NRS (0: no pain; 1–3: mild pain; 4–6: moderate pain; 7–10: severe pain).

9. **Data Collection Methods**:  
   - Patients were given the NRS scale to self-report pain at the specified time intervals.  
   - Swelling was monitored, and patients were instructed to contact the operator if antibiotics or drainage were needed.

10. **Procedures**:  
    - Local anesthesia, rubber dam isolation, apex locator for working length determination, rotary file instrumentation, manual dynamic agitation of 2.5% sodium hypochlorite for 60 seconds, canal drying, lateral compaction obturation, and temporary filling placement.

### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS
1. **Test Name**: Repeated Measures ANOVA  
   - **Purpose**: To compare mean pain levels across the three time intervals (6, 24, and 48 hours).  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Pain levels are measured repeatedly for the same participants at different time points.  
     2. The study design involves within-subject comparisons.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Assumes normality and sphericity of data, which may not hold true for pain scores.  
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**: If the assumption of sphericity is violated or the data is not normally distributed.  
   - **Alternative if Assumptions Are Violated**: Friedman test (non-parametric alternative).

2. **Test Name**: Descriptive Statistics  
   - **Purpose**: To summarize pain scores at each time interval (mean, standard deviation, median, range).  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Provides an overview of the data distribution.  
     2. Essential for understanding the central tendency and variability of pain scores.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Not a hypothesis test; does not infer statistical significance.  
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**: Always appropriate.  
   - **Alternative if Assumptions Are Violated**: None; descriptive statistics are non-inferential.

---

#### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED TESTS
1. **Test Name**: Friedman Test  
   - **Purpose**: To compare pain levels across the three time intervals when data is non-normally distributed.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Non-parametric test suitable for ordinal data (NRS pain scores).  
     2. Does not assume normality or sphericity.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Less powerful than parametric tests when assumptions of normality are met.  
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**: If data is normally distributed and sphericity is satisfied.  
   - **Alternative if Assumptions Are Violated**: Repeated Measures ANOVA.

2. **Test Name**: Post-Hoc Pairwise Comparisons (Bonferroni or Dunn’s Test)  
   - **Purpose**: To identify specific time intervals with significant differences in pain levels.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Provides detailed insights into which intervals differ significantly.  
     2. Controls for Type I error in multiple comparisons.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: May not be necessary if no significant differences are found in the overall test.  
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**: If the overall test (Repeated Measures ANOVA or Friedman) is not significant.  
   - **Alternative if Assumptions Are Violated**: Use non-parametric post-hoc tests (e.g., Dunn’s test).

---

#### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL TESTS
1. **Test Name**: Effect Size Calculation (e.g., Partial Eta Squared or Cohen’s d)  
   - **Purpose**: To quantify the magnitude of differences in pain levels across time intervals.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Provides practical significance beyond p-values.  
     2. Useful for interpreting clinical relevance.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Not mandatory for hypothesis testing; requires additional computation.  
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**: If the study is purely exploratory without a focus on clinical significance.  
   - **Alternative if Assumptions Are Violated**: None; effect size is robust to violations of normality.

2. **Test Name**: Trend Analysis  
   - **Purpose**: To assess whether pain levels decrease linearly or follow another trend over time.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Provides insights into the pattern of pain reduction.  
     2. Useful for understanding the trajectory of postoperative pain.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Requires sufficient sample size and evenly spaced time intervals.  
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**: If the sample size is too small or intervals are unevenly spaced.  
   - **Alternative if Assumptions Are Violated**: Non-parametric trend tests.
