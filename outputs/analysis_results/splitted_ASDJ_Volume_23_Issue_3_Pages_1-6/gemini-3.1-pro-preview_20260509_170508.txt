### TASK 1: INFORMATION EXTRACTION

*   **Study Design:** Single-arm, longitudinal, repeated-measures *in vivo* clinical study. (Note: There is no control group mentioned; the study compares time intervals within the same group).
*   **Objective:** To compare the levels of postoperative pain at three different time intervals (6, 24, and 48 hours) following manual dynamic activation of irrigation during endodontic treatment.
*   **Null Hypothesis:** There is no difference in the level of postoperative pain across different time intervals after manual dynamic agitation.
*   **Study Population/Groups:** 1 group (Patients receiving manual dynamic agitation).
*   **Sample Size:** 22 patients.
*   **Inclusion Criteria:** Females, aged 25-45, medically free of systemic diseases, symptomatic irreversible pulpitis in lower molars with mature roots, restorable teeth, no calcified canals, no internal/external root resorption.
*   **Exclusion Criteria:** Pregnant females, males, bruxism/clenching, previously treated teeth, periodontally affected teeth, abnormal mobility, periapical abscess, periodontal space widening, root fracture.
*   **Independent Variable:** Time (Within-subjects factor with 3 levels: 6 hours, 24 hours, 48 hours).
*   **Dependent Variable:** Postoperative pain, measured using the Numerical Rating Scale (NRS) from 0 to 10. (Categorized as 0: no pain, 1-3: mild, 4-6: moderate, 7-10: severe).
*   **Data Collection Method:** Patients self-reported their pain using the NRS at 6, 24, and 48 hours post-treatment.

### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

Given the small sample size ($N=22$) and the ordinal nature of the dependent variable (NRS pain scale), non-parametric statistical methods are the most appropriate choice.

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY (Core Analysis)

**1. Friedman Test**
*   **Purpose:** To determine if there is a statistically significant overall difference in pain scores across the three time points (6h, 24h, 48h).
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) It is the non-parametric equivalent of repeated-measures ANOVA, perfectly suited for ordinal data like the NRS. 
    2) It accounts for the within-subjects (repeated measures) design, as the same 22 patients are measured three times.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It only indicates that *at least one* time point is different from the others, but does not specify exactly where the differences lie (requires post-hoc testing).
*   **When this test should NOT be used:** If the data were collected from different, independent groups of patients at each time point.
*   **Alternative if assumptions are violated:** Repeated Measures ANOVA (only if the NRS data miraculously meets the assumption of normality, which is highly unlikely in this scenario).

**2. Wilcoxon Signed-Rank Test (with Bonferroni Correction)**
*   **Purpose:** To serve as a post-hoc test to make pairwise comparisons between specific time points (6h vs 24h; 6h vs 48h; 24h vs 48h) if the Friedman test is significant.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) It correctly handles paired/repeated ordinal data. 
    2) Applying a Bonferroni correction (adjusting the alpha level to $p < 0.0167$) prevents the inflation of Type I error (false positives) that occurs when conducting multiple comparisons.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It discards some magnitude information by converting differences into ranks, which can lower statistical power compared to parametric tests.
*   **When this test should NOT be used:** When comparing independent groups (e.g., males vs. females).
*   **Alternative if assumptions are violated:** Paired t-test (if data distribution is proven to be perfectly normal).

#### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED (Enhancing Quality)

**1. Descriptive Statistics (Medians, Interquartile Ranges [IQR], and Frequencies)**
*   **Purpose:** To summarize the central tendency and dispersion of the pain scores, and to report the proportion of patients in each pain category.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) Medians and IQRs are the mathematically correct way to report ordinal, non-normally distributed data. 
    2) Calculating frequencies/percentages aligns perfectly with the authors' predefined pain categories (None, Mild, Moderate, Severe).
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** Descriptive statistics do not allow for hypothesis testing or inferential conclusions.
*   **When this test should NOT be used:** Means and Standard Deviations should NOT be used as the primary descriptors if the data is highly skewed (e.g., if most patients have 0 pain at 48 hours, creating a floor effect).
*   **Alternative:** Means and 95% Confidence Intervals (only if data is continuous and normally distributed).

**2. Kendall’s W (Coefficient of Concordance)**
*   **Purpose:** To calculate the effect size for the Friedman test.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) It provides the magnitude/clinical significance of the time effect, rather than just a p-value. 
    2) It standardizes the effect on a scale from 0 (no relationship) to 1 (perfect relationship).
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It can be difficult for non-statisticians to interpret clinically compared to raw pain score differences.
*   **When this test should NOT be used:** If the primary omnibus test used was parametric (e.g., RM-ANOVA).
*   **Alternative:** Partial Eta Squared ($\eta_p^2$) if using RM-ANOVA.

#### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL (Supplementary Insights)

**1. Cochran’s Q Test**
*   **Purpose:** To compare the proportion of patients experiencing *any* pain vs. *no pain* across the three time points.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) It allows the researcher to dichotomize the data (Pain > 0 vs. Pain = 0) to answer a simple clinical question: "Does the number of pain-free patients increase over time?" 
    2) It handles repeated categorical measures perfectly.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** Dichotomizing data results in a loss of detailed information (e.g., the difference between severe and mild pain is lost).
*   **When this test should NOT be used:** If you want to analyze the severity of pain rather than just the presence/absence of it.
*   **Alternative:** Generalized Estimating Equations (GEE) for binary outcomes.

### TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS

The following tests should **NOT** be used in this study:

1.  **Independent Samples t-test / Mann-Whitney U test:** These tests assume that the groups being compared are independent (different people). Because this study measures the *same* 22 patients three times, these tests would violate the assumption of independence.
2.  **One-Way ANOVA:** This test assumes independent groups, continuous data, and normal distribution. It fails on all three counts for this study design.
3.  **Repeated Measures ANOVA (RM-ANOVA):** While it accounts for the repeated measures, RM-ANOVA assumes the dependent variable is continuous and normally distributed. Pain scores (NRS) in a small sample ($N=22$) are ordinal and highly prone to skewness (especially as pain resolves over 48 hours), making parametric testing invalid.
4.  **Pearson Chi-Square Test of Independence:** If the researchers try to compare the frequencies of pain categories (Mild, Moderate, Severe) across time points using a standard Chi-Square, it would be a critical error. Standard Chi-Square does not account for paired/repeated data.

### TASK 4: SUMMARY

**Overall Analytical Approach:**
The study utilizes a single-arm, repeated-measures design with a small sample size ($N=22$) and an ordinal dependent variable (NRS pain scores). Because the data violates the assumptions required for parametric testing (normality and continuous data), a strictly **non-parametric analytical approach** is justified and required. 

The core analysis should rely on the **Friedman test** to evaluate overall changes in pain across the 6, 24, and 48-hour intervals. If significant, this must be followed by **Wilcoxon Signed-Rank tests with a Bonferroni correction** to pinpoint exactly when pain reduction occurs while protecting against Type I errors. Data should be summarized using Medians, IQRs, and categorical frequencies rather than Means and Standard Deviations, ensuring the statistical reporting accurately reflects the ordinal nature of the clinical pain scale.
