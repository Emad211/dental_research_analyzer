# CATEGORY A — MANDATORY (analysis incomplete without these)

## A1) Descriptive statistics (baseline and follow-up characterization)
- **Test/Method:** Descriptive summaries (means/SD or medians/IQR; counts/%), plus standardized differences if comparing material groups.
- **Purpose:** Describe sample, tooth characteristics, restorations, and follow-up time; identify imbalance and missingness.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Retrospective cohort requires transparency on baseline distributions and potential confounding.
  2. Essential before modeling to understand variable types, sparsity, and feasibility of multivariable adjustment.
- **Why may not be appropriate (≥1):** No formal inferential comparison; can be misused as “baseline significance testing.”
- **When NOT to use:** Never “omit” descriptives; but **do not** use p-values to “prove” groups are comparable.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:** Use robust summaries (median/IQR) for skewed distributions; exact counts for sparse categories.

## A2) Kaplan–Meier (KM) survival estimation (overall and by restorative material; possibly by tooth type)
- **Test/Method:** **Kaplan–Meier survival curves** with survival probabilities at clinically relevant times (e.g., 1, 3, 5, 10 years) and median survival if reached.
- **Purpose:** Nonparametric estimation of tooth/restoration survival over time.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Handles **right-censoring** (patients not failing by last visit).
  2. Provides clinically interpretable survival estimates over long follow-up.
- **Why may not be appropriate:** Standard KM assumes **independence** of observations; violated if multiple teeth per patient.
- **When NOT to use:** When clustering is substantial and you intend to present inference without adjustment; KM still okay descriptively but inference should adjust clustering.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:** Use KM for descriptive plots but obtain CIs via **clustered bootstrap by patient**; or use marginal survival estimates from frailty models.

## A3) Log-rank test (group comparison of survival curves), with clustering-aware inference if feasible
- **Test/Method:** **Log-rank test** comparing KM curves across restorative materials (and other key categorical predictors).
- **Purpose:** Unadjusted comparison of time-to-failure distributions between groups.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Standard first-pass comparison for survival outcomes.
  2. Sensitive to persistent differences over time.
- **Why may not be appropriate:** 
  - Does not adjust for confounding (age, tooth type, remaining structure, opposing dentition).
  - Independence assumption violated by multiple teeth per subject.
- **When NOT to use:** If proportional hazards is grossly violated and differences are strongly time-dependent; if clustering cannot be addressed and inference would be misleading.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:** **Weighted log-rank tests** (e.g., Fleming–Harrington) for non-PH; or proceed directly to multivariable Cox with time-varying effects and robust SE.

## A4) Cox proportional hazards regression with patient-level clustering adjustment (primary multivariable model)
- **Test/Method:** **Cox PH model** with:
  - **Robust sandwich SE clustered by subject** *or*
  - **Shared frailty (random effect) for subject**.
- **Purpose:** Estimate adjusted associations (hazard ratios) between predictors (especially **restorative material**) and time-to-failure.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Matches the cohort’s **time-to-event** structure with censoring.
  2. Allows adjustment for key confounders (tooth type, jaw, opposing dentition, remaining structure, age/sex, etc.).
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - **Proportional hazards** assumption may not hold.
  - Sparse events in some strata (e.g., small GIC group) can destabilize estimates.
- **When NOT to use:** If event counts are too low per parameter (separation/overfitting), or if follow-up times are not reliably measured.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Non-PH: **Cox with time-varying coefficients** (interaction with log(time)) or stratified Cox.
  - If functional form issues: use **restricted cubic splines** for continuous age.
  - If very sparse: penalized Cox (e.g., **Firth penalization**) or reduce model complexity.

# CATEGORY B — RECOMMENDED (improves rigor)

## B1) Proportional hazards (PH) assumption diagnostics
- **Test/Method:** **Schoenfeld residual test** + graphical checks (log(-log) curves; time-varying residual plots).
- **Purpose:** Validate Cox model assumptions.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Cox model validity depends on PH; violations bias HR interpretation.
  2. Long follow-up increases chance of time-varying effects.
- **Why may not be appropriate:** Global tests can be underpowered with few events; may flag trivial deviations with many events.
- **When NOT to use:** Never skip entirely if reporting Cox HRs; but avoid over-interpreting p-values—use plots too.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:** Stratified Cox, time-varying effects, or parametric survival models (below).

## B2) Parametric survival modeling (AFT or PH forms) for robustness and prediction
- **Test/Method:** **Weibull**, **log-normal**, or **log-logistic** survival models (AFT or PH parameterization), optionally with frailty.
- **Purpose:** Provide alternative effect measures (time ratios in AFT) and potentially better extrapolation/fit.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Can model baseline hazard shape explicitly; may fit dental failure processes better.
  2. Enables estimation of absolute time metrics (e.g., predicted survival time) beyond Cox.
- **Why may not be appropriate:** Misspecification of distribution leads to biased estimates.
- **When NOT to use:** If no plausible parametric form fits (checked via AIC, residuals, probability plots).
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:** Flexible parametric survival (Royston–Parmar splines) or Cox with splines for baseline hazard.

## B3) Competing risks analysis (if endodontic/periodontal loss occurs and is observed)
- **Test/Method:** 
  - **Cumulative incidence function (CIF)** and **Gray’s test**
  - **Fine–Gray subdistribution hazard model** (with clustering-robust SE if possible)
- **Purpose:** Account for alternative causes of tooth loss that preclude restorative failure.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. In real follow-up, teeth may be lost for reasons other than restorative/tooth fracture; treating them as simple censoring can bias failure incidence.
  2. Provides clinically relevant absolute risk of specific failure types.
- **Why may not be appropriate:** The paper says teeth lost for endodontic/periodontal reasons were excluded; if truly excluded, competing risks may not be applicable.
- **When NOT to use:** If competing event types are not recorded or are structurally excluded from dataset.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:** Cause-specific Cox models (separate hazards per event type).

## B4) Multi-level/marginal modeling choice justification (clustered teeth within patient)
- **Test/Method:** Compare:
  - Cox with **cluster-robust SE** (marginal inference) vs
  - **Shared frailty Cox** (subject-specific inference)
- **Purpose:** Ensure valid inference with correlated tooth outcomes.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. 300 teeth within 148 subjects implies non-independence.
  2. Effect estimates/SEs can change materially depending on correlation handling.
- **Why may not be appropriate:** Frailty distribution assumptions (often gamma) may be wrong; robust SE may perform poorly with few clusters (though 148 is usually adequate).
- **When NOT to use:** Frailty not advised with extremely small numbers of patients or minimal within-patient multiplicity.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:** Patient-level analysis (one tooth per patient) as sensitivity analysis.

## B5) Missing data handling
- **Test/Method:** **Multiple imputation** (e.g., MICE) for covariates + sensitivity analyses (complete-case vs imputed).
- **Purpose:** Reduce bias and loss of precision due to incomplete covariates (common in retrospective records).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Retrospective charts often have missing predictors (remaining tooth structure, adjacent teeth).
  2. Complete-case can bias associations if missingness relates to outcome/covariates.
- **Why may not be appropriate:** Not valid if missingness is MNAR and cannot be reasonably modeled; complex with time-to-event (must include event indicator and log(time) in imputation model).
- **When NOT to use:** If missingness is trivial (<~5%) or variables are structurally missing.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:** Pattern-mixture sensitivity analyses; missing-indicator approach (with caution).

# CATEGORY C — OPTIONAL (supplementary insights)

## C1) Incidence rate estimation (failures per tooth-year) by material and tooth type
- **Test/Method:** **Poisson** or **negative binomial regression** with offset = log(tooth-time), clustered SE.
- **Purpose:** Provide simple rate-based summaries complementing survival curves.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Communicates event burden per exposure-time (useful with unequal follow-up).
  2. Can handle multiple predictors similarly to Cox.
- **Why may not be appropriate:** Assumes constant hazard within intervals; ignores exact event timing unless structured appropriately.
- **When NOT to use:** If precise time-to-event is available and Cox is primary; use as supplement only.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:** Negative binomial (overdispersion), or piecewise exponential models.

## C2) Subgroup / interaction analyses (pre-specified)
- **Test/Method:** Interaction terms in Cox (e.g., restorative material × tooth type; material × opposing dentition).
- **Purpose:** Assess effect modification (e.g., restorations behave differently in molars vs premolars).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Biomechanically plausible heterogeneity by tooth type/occlusal load.
  2. Helps clinical decision-making for specific tooth classes.
- **Why may not be appropriate:** Multiple testing inflation; small subgroup sizes (e.g., GIC n=17) lead to unstable interaction estimates.
- **When NOT to use:** If not pre-specified or events per parameter are insufficient.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:** Combine categories (e.g., anterior vs posterior), or present stratified descriptive KM without formal interaction testing.

## C3) Recurrent event analysis (only if multiple failures/interventions per tooth are recorded)
- **Test/Method:** Andersen–Gill, Prentice–Williams–Peterson, or frailty recurrent-event models.
- **Purpose:** Model repeated restorative interventions (repair/replacement) if teeth can fail multiple times.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. The dataset mentions “all interventions after endodontic treatment.”
  2. Captures restorative “burden,” not just first failure.
- **Why may not be appropriate:** Outcome definition in the article appears “first failure”; recurrent event methods require clear event ordering and at-risk definitions.
- **When NOT to use:** If only first failure is reliably captured or if later events are inconsistently recorded.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:** Analyze only time-to-first-failure (primary) plus count outcomes descriptively.

## TASK 3 — Inappropriate tests (should NOT be used)

1. **Independent-samples t-test / one-way ANOVA on “survival time” without censoring handling**
   - **Why not:** Ignores right-censoring and unequal follow-up; biased means and invalid p-values.

2. **Chi-square test comparing “failure yes/no” at 10 years without accounting for censoring**
   - **Why not:** Many teeth will not have full 10-year observation; creates informative missingness and biased risk estimates.

3. **Simple logistic regression for failure (binary) ignoring time-to-event**
   - **Why not:** Discards timing information and mishandles censoring; also ignores within-patient clustering unless explicitly modeled (GEE/mixed).

4. **Paired t-test / McNemar’s test**
   - **Why not:** No paired/matched design described.

5. **Pearson correlation between predictors and “survival”**
   - **Why not:** Not appropriate for censored time-to-event outcomes and mixed variable types.

6. **Standard KM/log-rank inference treating each tooth as independent without any clustering consideration**
   - **Why not:** Underestimates SEs (inflated type I error) when multiple teeth come from the same subject.

### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY
1. **Test Name**: Kaplan–Meier Survival Analysis
   - **Purpose**: To estimate the survival probability of ETTs over time.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. The study involves time-to-event data (e.g., time until failure or survival of ETTs).
     2. Censoring is present (e.g., teeth that did not fail by the end of the study period).
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Assumes that censoring is non-informative, which may not always hold true.
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**: If the assumption of non-informative censoring is violated.
   - **Alternative if Assumptions Are Violated**: Cox proportional hazards model with adjustments for informative censoring.

2. **Test Name**: Chi-Square Test or Fisher’s Exact Test
   - **Purpose**: To compare categorical variables (e.g., survival rates across different restorative materials or tooth types).
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. The study involves categorical independent variables (e.g., tooth type, restorative material).
     2. It tests for associations between categorical variables and survival outcomes.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Small sample sizes in subgroups can violate the assumptions of the chi-square test.
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**: When expected frequencies in any cell are less than 5.
   - **Alternative if Assumptions Are Violated**: Fisher’s exact test for small sample sizes.

#### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED
3. **Test Name**: Cox Proportional Hazards Model
   - **Purpose**: To identify independent predictors of survival while adjusting for covariates.
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Allows for multivariable analysis of time-to-event data.
     2. Accounts for the effect of multiple independent variables (e.g., age, gender, restorative material).
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Assumes proportional hazards, which may not be true for all variables.
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**: If the proportional hazards assumption is violated.
   - **Alternative if Assumptions Are Violated**: Stratified Cox model or time-varying covariates.

4. **Test Name**: Log-Rank Test
   - **Purpose**: To compare survival distributions between groups (e.g., different restorative materials).
   - **Why Appropriate**:
     1. Non-parametric test suitable for comparing survival curves.
     2. Does not require assumptions about the shape of the survival distribution.
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Sensitive to differences in survival rates at early or late time points.
   - **When This Test Should NOT Be Used**: When survival curves cross significantly.
   - **Alternative if Assumptions Are Violated**: Generalized Wilcoxon test.

**2. Propensity Score Matching or Inverse Probability Treatment Weighting (IPTW)**
*   **Purpose:** To adjust for selection bias in the choice of restorative material.
*   **Why appropriate:** 1) The authors explicitly note non-random allocation as a limitation. 2) Balances baseline covariates (like remaining tooth structure) across the different material groups.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** May drastically reduce the effective sample size if many cases cannot be matched.
*   **When NOT to use:** If there are too few covariates available to build a robust propensity model.
*   **Alternative:** Standard multivariable covariate adjustment in the Cox model.

---

### TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS

*   **Standard Chi-Square Test or Fisher's Exact Test:** Should NOT be used to compare overall success/failure rates between materials. These tests ignore the time-to-event nature of the data (censoring) and treat a tooth followed for 6 months the same as a tooth followed for 10 years. They also ignore patient-level clustering.
*   **Independent Samples t-test / ANOVA:** Should NOT be used to compare the "mean survival time" between groups. Survival times are highly skewed and contain censored data; calculating a standard mean is mathematically invalid for right-censored data.
*   **Standard Logistic Regression:** Should NOT be used for the primary analysis. While it could assess binary success at a specific cutoff (e.g., exactly 5 years), it discards valuable time-to-event information and mishandles patients lost to follow-up prior to the cutoff.
*   **Standard (Unadjusted) Cox Regression:** Should NOT be used because it assumes all 300 teeth are entirely independent. Because they belong to only 148 patients, a patient with poor oral hygiene or heavy bite force will likely experience multiple failures. Failing to use a Frailty model or Robust Standard Errors will result in a high risk of false-positive results (Type I error).

---

### TASK 4: SUMMARY OF ANALYTICAL APPROACH

The fundamental statistical challenge of this study is twofold: **time-to-event data with variable follow-up** (ranging from 6 months to 10.3 years) and **clustered observations** (300 teeth nested within 148 patients). 

The analytical approach must center on **Survival Analysis**. Descriptive statistics and Kaplan-Meier curves will provide a foundational understanding of the cohort's longevity. However, because treatments were not randomly assigned and multiple teeth belong to the same individuals, naive statistical tests will yield biased results. The core of the analysis must rely on a **Shared Frailty Cox Proportional Hazards Model** (or a Marginal Cox model with robust standard errors). This approach will allow the researchers to determine which factors (e.g., tooth location, material) significantly impact the survival of uncrowned ETTs, while mathematically adjusting for both confounding variables and the shared biological environment of teeth residing in the same patient's mouth. *(Note: The authors' claim in Table 2 of "0 censored" events is highly improbable for a 10-year study and indicates a potential misunderstanding of censoring by the authors; the proposed survival plan corrects for this).*
