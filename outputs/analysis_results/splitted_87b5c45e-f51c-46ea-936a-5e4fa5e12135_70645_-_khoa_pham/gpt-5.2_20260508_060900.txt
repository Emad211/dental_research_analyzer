## TASK 1 — Information extraction (study characteristics)

### Study overview
- **Title/Topic:** Postoperative pain after single-visit root canal treatment comparing instrumentation **with vs without a connected electronic apex locator (EAL)**.
- **Objective:** Evaluate whether using an endodontic motor **with connected EAL** reduces postoperative pain compared with a **traditional motor + unconnected EAL**.
- **Null hypothesis:** No difference in pain intensities between groups.
- **Design:** **Parallel-group randomized controlled clinical trial**, 2 arms, **1:1 allocation**.
- **Setting/period:** Department of Operative Dentistry & Endodontics, University of Medicine and Pharmacy at Ho Chi Minh City; **May 2019–March 2020**.
- **Registration/ethics:** TCTR20200118001 (retrospective registration); ethics approval no. 306/ĐHYD-HĐĐĐ.

### Participants
- **Population:** Adults (≥18) needing endodontic therapy on **first or second molars** (maxillary or mandibular).
- **N enrolled/randomized/analyzed:** **42 patients / 42 molars**, **21 per group**; **no loss to follow-up** per CONSORT diagram.
- **Sex:** 26 women, 16 men.
- **Unit of analysis:** One tooth per patient (explicit: “For each patient, one molar was treated”).
- **Blinding:** **Patients blinded** to modality; operator not blinded (single operator performed all procedures).

### Eligibility criteria
- **Inclusion:** Healthy without systemic diseases/allergies; **symptomatic irreversible pulpitis** and/or **symptomatic apical periodontitis**; first/second molar.
- **Exclusion:** systemic disease; sinus tract; swelling; clenching/bruxism; severely damaged tooth; severe periodontal disease; resorption; previously endodontically treated tooth; root crack; analgesics in prior 24h; absence of opposing tooth; age <18.

### Interventions (groups)
- **Group 1 (Unconnected EAL):**
  - Working length measured with **ProPex Pixi EAL**; traditional motor **WaveOne**.
  - Instruments: **ProGlider + ProTaper Next (X1–X3)**, 300 rpm, 2 N·cm.
  - WL confirmed radiographically (0.5–1 mm short).
  - Rubber stop set to WL; pecking motion to WL.

- **Group 2 (Connected EAL):**
  - **E Connect S motor + E-Pex Pro EAL** (apex 0.0), 300 rpm, 2 N·cm.
  - Same instruments (ProGlider + PTN X1–X3).
  - Instrument advances until **auto stop/reverse** at apex; WL radiographically confirmed.

- **Common to both:**
  - 3% NaOCl irrigation during treatment; patency with #10 K-file after each instrument (slightly past foramen, <0.5 mm).
  - Final irrigation: 5 mL 3% NaOCl then 5 mL 17% EDTA.
  - Obturation: matched single gutta-percha + **AH Plus**; radiographic check; restoration with SDR + Ceram X.
  - **Rescue analgesic:** Ibuprofen 400 mg for “unbearable pain”.

### Outcomes and measurements

**Primary outcome (implied by sample size rationale and objective):**
- **Postoperative pain intensity** recorded by patient using **Heft-Parker VAS (HP-VAS), 0–170 mm**.

**Pain measurement schedule:**
- **Preoperative pain**: recorded (HP-VAS).
- **Postoperative pain self-recorded** at **6, 24, 48, 72 hours, and 1 week** after treatment.
- At **1 week visit**: VAS collected and **pain on percussion** measured using HP-VAS.

**Other recorded variables (potential covariates/confounders):**
- Age, gender
- Diagnosis
- Tooth characteristics (maxillary/mandibular molars mentioned; “number of teeth” noted but unclear meaning)
- Analgesic intake after procedure (yes/no; possibly amount/timing, but not specified)

**Data type notes:**
- HP-VAS: continuous 0–170 mm but often skewed/zero-inflated.
- Repeated measures within subject over time.
- Analgesic intake is a post-randomization variable that can mediate pain and complicate interpretation.

## TASK 2 — Statistical Analysis Plan (SAP)

### 1) General principles
- **Analysis population:** Intention-to-treat (ITT): all randomized participants analyzed in assigned group (here, no attrition expected, but still define).
- **Significance level:** Two-sided α = 0.05.
- **Estimation emphasis:** Report **effect sizes with 95% CIs** (not only p-values).
- **Software:** R / Stata / SPSS (specify in manuscript).
- **Handling missing pain diaries:** Pre-specify strategy (even if none occurred in the published flow): mixed models naturally accommodate missing-at-random; otherwise multiple imputation as sensitivity.

### 2) Data preparation
- Convert HP-VAS marks to **mm (0–170)** as described.
- Create a **time variable** (6h, 24h, 48h, 72h, 1w).
- Define **baseline pain** (preoperative HP-VAS).
- Define **binary analgesic use** during follow-up (and ideally time-to-first-dose if available).

### 3) Descriptive statistics (baseline and outcomes)
- Baseline comparability: age, sex, diagnosis, jaw (max/mand), baseline pain.
- Summarize pain at each time by group: mean (SD) and median (IQR); include plots (profile/spaghetti + group means).

### 4) Primary estimand (recommended to define explicitly)
A clinically interpretable primary estimand could be:
- **Difference between groups in mean postoperative pain trajectory over 1 week**, adjusted for baseline pain (and optionally diagnosis), using a repeated-measures model; or
- **Difference at a clinically important time point** (e.g., 24h or 48h), adjusted for baseline pain.

Because the study collected multiple time points, a **longitudinal model** is the most coherent primary analysis.

# CATEGORY A — MANDATORY analyses/tests

## A1) Linear mixed-effects model (LMM) for repeated pain scores
- **Test/model name:** Linear mixed-effects model (random intercept for patient; fixed effects for group, time, and group×time; include baseline pain as covariate).
- **Purpose:** Compare postoperative pain **trajectories over time** between groups and estimate group differences at each time point.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Pain is measured **repeatedly** within the same patient; LMM accounts for within-subject correlation.
  2. Uses **all time points simultaneously**, reducing multiple-testing and improving precision.
  3. Can incorporate **baseline pain** and other covariates to improve efficiency.
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - HP-VAS data may be **non-normal, bounded, and zero-inflated**, making Gaussian residual assumptions questionable.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If pain scores are extremely non-normal with heavy floor effects and model diagnostics show severe violations that cannot be remedied.
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
  - **Robust LMM** (Huber/sandwich SEs), or
  - **Generalized linear mixed model (GLMM)** with an appropriate distribution (e.g., Gamma with log link for positive values), or
  - **Ordinal mixed model** if you analyze the HP-VAS categories (none/mild/moderate/severe), or
  - **Nonparametric longitudinal methods** (e.g., rank-based models).

**Implementation notes (recommended):**
- Use **restricted maximum likelihood (REML)** for estimation (or ML if comparing nested models).
- Report:
  - overall **group×time interaction** p-value (whether effect differs by time),
  - estimated **marginal mean differences** at each time with 95% CI (with multiplicity adjustment if needed).

## A2) Baseline comparability checks (descriptive + targeted tests)
Not required for causal validity but mandatory for reporting clarity in RCTs.

### A2a) Two-sample t-test (or Welch t-test) for age and baseline pain (if treated as continuous)
- **Purpose:** Describe/quantify baseline differences between randomized groups.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Variable is continuous.
  2. Simple summary consistent with CONSORT tables.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Baseline pain may be skewed; small sample size (n=21/group).
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Clear non-normal distributions with strong outliers and no reasonable transformation.
- **Alternatives:**
  - **Mann–Whitney U** for distributional shift; or report standardized differences without hypothesis tests.

### A2b) Chi-square test / Fisher’s exact test for categorical baseline variables (sex, diagnosis, jaw)
- **Purpose:** Summarize balance of categorical characteristics across groups.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Compares proportions between independent groups.
  2. Fisher’s exact handles small expected cell counts.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - With multiple categories and sparse cells, chi-square may be invalid.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Expected counts <5 in multiple cells (then chi-square unreliable).
- **Alternative:**
  - **Fisher’s exact** (or exact Monte Carlo), or report standardized differences only.

---

## A3) Primary effect size reporting (estimation “test”)
- **Name:** Mean difference (or standardized mean difference) with **95% CI** derived from the LMM contrasts.
- **Purpose:** Quantify magnitude and uncertainty of group differences.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Aligns with clinical interpretation of pain reduction (mm on HP-VAS).
  2. Avoids overreliance on p-values.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - If distribution is highly skewed, mean difference may be misleading.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If pain scale is analyzed ordinally; then mean difference is not coherent.
- **Alternative:**
  - Median difference via **quantile regression**, or odds ratios from ordinal models.

# CATEGORY B — RECOMMENDED analyses/tests

## B1) Generalized estimating equations (GEE) as a population-averaged sensitivity analysis
- **Purpose:** Confirm group differences over time with fewer distributional assumptions about random effects.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Handles **correlated repeated measures**.
  2. Provides **robust (“sandwich”) SEs**; focuses on marginal effect.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - With small samples (42 total), sandwich SEs can be biased downward.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Very small samples without small-sample corrections; high missingness.
- **Alternative if issues:**
  - LMM with Kenward–Roger/Satterthwaite df correction; or cluster bootstrap.

## B2) Mann–Whitney U test at each time point (only if pre-specified as secondary)
- **Purpose:** Compare pain distributions between groups at each follow-up time.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Nonparametric; tolerates skewness/outliers.
  2. Simple interpretability for each time point.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Ignores repeated-measures structure and inflates multiplicity if used at many times.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - As the sole/primary analysis of repeated measures; when multiple time points are tested without adjustment.
- **Alternative:**
  - LMM/GLMM with time effect; or adjust p-values (Holm) if doing multiple time-point tests.

## B3) Multiplicity control for time-point contrasts
- **Name:** Holm–Bonferroni (or Benjamini–Hochberg FDR if many secondary endpoints).
- **Purpose:** Control family-wise error rate (or FDR) across multiple time comparisons.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Multiple pain assessments increase false-positive risk.
  2. Maintains interpretability of “significant at time t”.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - If time-point inferences are not the goal (trajectory model is primary).
- **When NOT to use:**
  - For the single pre-specified primary contrast (no need).
- **Alternative:**
  - Use global group×time test as primary; limit number of confirmatory contrasts.

## B4) Analysis of analgesic intake between groups
Two complementary approaches:

### B4a) Fisher’s exact / chi-square (any analgesic use yes/no)
- **Purpose:** Compare proportion using rescue analgesic by group.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Binary outcome.
  2. Clinically relevant and easy to report.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Analgesic use is **post-randomization** and influenced by pain; interpreting as treatment effect can be confounded by outcome-driven behavior.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - As evidence that one treatment “reduces pain” without integrating pain scores; it’s a secondary endpoint.
- **Alternative:**
  - Joint modeling / treat as mediator; or time-to-event approach below.

### B4b) Time-to-first analgesic (Kaplan–Meier + log-rank; Cox model)
- **Purpose:** Compare timing of rescue medication use.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Incorporates **censoring** (no analgesic by 1 week).
  2. More informative than yes/no.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - If exact timing is not collected; small number of events yields unstable Cox estimates.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When event counts are very low (<~10 total).
- **Alternative:**
  - Fisher’s exact for any use; or exact methods / Firth penalized Cox.

## B5) Baseline-adjusted ANCOVA at a single clinically chosen time (e.g., 24h) as a confirmatory secondary
- **Purpose:** Estimate group difference at a clinically key time point controlling for baseline pain.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Increases precision vs comparing change scores.
  2. Clinicians often care about early postoperative pain peak (24–48h).
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Still ignores other time points; choosing time post hoc biases inference.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If the time point was not pre-specified.
- **Alternative:**
  - LMM contrast at that time point (preferred).

# CATEGORY C — OPTIONAL (supplementary) analyses/tests

## C1) Area Under the Curve (AUC) of pain over time + group comparison
- **Purpose:** Summarize overall pain burden from 6h to 1 week.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Produces a single patient-level summary incorporating repeated measures.
  2. Clinically interpretable as “total pain experienced”.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Requires complete or well-imputed longitudinal data; sensitive to missingness.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If many missing diary entries or irregular timing.
- **Alternative:**
  - LMM-based estimated marginal means integrated over time.

## C2) Ordinal analysis using HP-VAS categories (none/mild/moderate/severe)
- **Test/model name:** Mixed-effects ordinal logistic regression (cumulative logit).
- **Purpose:** Compare probability of being in higher pain categories over time.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Aligns with the scale’s **categorical interpretation** provided in methods.
  2. Robust to non-normality and boundedness.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Loss of information vs continuous mm; proportional odds assumption may fail.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If category counts are sparse (e.g., almost all mild/none).
- **Alternative:**
  - Partial proportional odds model; or continuous LMM with robust SE.

## C3) Clinically relevant binary endpoint: “moderate/severe pain” at any time
- **Test:** Logistic regression (or mixed logistic if repeated), estimating risk ratio/odds ratio.
- **Purpose:** Compare incidence of clinically important pain.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Communicates clinical relevance beyond mean differences.
  2. Handles floor effects.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Dichotomization loses information; choice of cutoff can be arbitrary.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - As primary endpoint when continuous pain is available and meaningful.
- **Alternative:**
  - Ordinal model or continuous model.

## C4) Exploratory subgroup/interaction analyses (pre-specified only)
- **Possible modifiers:** baseline diagnosis (pulpitis vs apical periodontitis), jaw (max vs mand), baseline pain severity.
- **Test:** Add interaction terms (group×modifier) in the mixed model.
- **Purpose:** Identify heterogeneity of treatment effect.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Clinical plausibility (different baseline pathology may influence pain).
  2. Helps interpret inconsistent effects.
- **Why may not be appropriate:**
  - Underpowered (n=42); high false-positive risk.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Post hoc fishing without pre-specification.
- **Alternative:**
  - Report descriptively; meta-analytic combination with future studies.

## TASK 3 — Inappropriate tests (should NOT be used) and why

1. **Paired t-test (between groups)**  
   - Wrong because groups are **independent**; paired tests assume matched/paired observations.

2. **Multiple independent t-tests at each time point as the main analysis (without multiplicity control and without modeling correlation)**  
   - Inflates type I error; ignores within-subject correlation; inefficient and potentially misleading.

3. **One-way ANOVA ignoring repeated measures**  
   - Violates independence because each subject contributes multiple pain scores.

4. **Change-score t-tests (post–pre) at each time as primary without baseline adjustment strategy**  
   - Baseline pain is recorded once; repeated post times make “change” ambiguous; ANCOVA/LMM is superior.

5. **Pearson correlation between group and pain**  
   - Group is categorical; correlation is not an appropriate primary comparison framework.

6. **Linear regression at each time point without checking distribution/heteroscedasticity**  
   - HP-VAS often skewed; small sample; may produce invalid inference if assumptions fail.

7. **Interpreting analgesic intake using simple group comparison as proof of pain reduction (causal overreach)**  
   - Analgesic use is outcome-driven and post-randomization; must be treated as secondary/mediational.

---

## TASK 4 — Summary justification of the overall approach

The study is a 2-arm parallel RCT with **repeated postoperative pain measurements** on a continuous (bounded) scale. The core analysis should therefore be a **longitudinal between-group comparison** using a **mixed-effects model (or GEE)** to correctly account for within-patient correlation, leverage all time points, and estimate clinically interpretable **group differences over time** with confidence intervals. Secondary analyses can address analgesic use, clinically meaningful pain categories, and overall pain burden (AUC), while controlling multiplicity and avoiding inappropriate independent-at-each-time testing as the main inference framework.
