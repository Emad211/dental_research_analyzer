## TASK 1: INFORMATION EXTRACTION (study characteristics)

### Study identification/design
- **Design:** Blinded, parallel-group **randomized controlled clinical trial** (CONSORT).
- **Setting/time:** Department of Conservative Dentistry and Endodontics, Pacific Dental College and Hospital, Udaipur, India; **Jan–Aug 2021**.
- **Registration/ethics:** IRB approval (PDCH/19/EC-140); registered (REF/2021/03/041572).
- **Blinding:**
  - **Participants blinded** (control received saline placebo similar to “no activation” irrigation).
  - **Outcome assessor blinded** (entered VAS scores).
  - **Statistician blinded**.
  - **Operator not blinded** (procedural constraint).

### Objectives/endpoints
- **Primary objective:** Compare **postendodontic pain** after single-visit endodontics (SVE) among:
  1) aqueous ozone **no activation (NA)**  
  2) aqueous ozone **manual dynamic activation (MDA)**  
  3) aqueous ozone **passive ultrasonic activation (PUA/PSA wording varies)**  
  4) aqueous ozone **sonic activation (SA)**  
  5) **no ozone control** (saline)
- **Primary outcome:** Pain intensity by **Visual Analog Scale (VAS)** (0–10 cm).
- **Pain measurement times (repeated measures):** **baseline (pre-treatment)**; **6, 12, 24, 48 hours**; **7 days**.
- **Secondary objective:** Effect of **sex**, **age group**, and **arch** (maxilla/mandible) on pain.

### Participants
- **Eligibility:**
  - Age **18–65**, generally healthy, available for follow-up.
  - **Single-rooted teeth** with **necrotic pulp** + **apical periodontitis**, **PAI < 3**, sound periodontium, sufficient coronal structure.
- **Key exclusions:** systemic disease/immunocompromised, pregnancy, drug/irrigant allergy, sinus tract, acute abscess, calcified canal/immature apex/mobility, and specific **ozone contraindications** (recent MI, intoxication, active bleeding, uncontrolled hyperthyroidism, thrombocytopenia, G6PD deficiency).

### Randomization/allocation
- **Randomization method:** Patient picked **computer-generated random sequence** in **sealed opaque envelope** after shaping/cleaning, before ozone step.
- **Allocation ratio stated:** “**4:1**” (but actual groups were **equal n=24 each** at randomization; ratio statement appears inconsistent with the flow chart).

### Sample size
- **Pilot:** 40 patients, 5 groups, n=8 each.
- **Effect size basis:** partial eta² for **group×time interaction** = 0.128 → **F = 0.383**.
- **Planned sample size:** 110 for 90% power, 95% CI stated (more properly α=0.05).
- **Inflated for dropout:** +10% → **121** planned; “final 24 per group” (≈120).
- **Actually randomized:** **120 (24/group)**.
- **Follow-up/incomplete forms:** Loss to follow-up and incomplete diaries occurred; medication use led to exclusion. (Full analyzed n not completely visible in provided excerpt; analysis plan should predefine handling.)

### Intervention/procedure (common endodontic protocol; single visit)
- Standard anesthesia, rubber dam, working length (apex locator + radiograph), crown-down rotary NiTi, EDTA; NaOCl 5.25% (2 mL each step) with 27G side-vented needle.
- **Ozone preparation:** 4 mg/mL gaseous ozone bubbled into distilled water for 15 min.
- **Irrigation protocol for ozone groups:** **15 mL ozonated water**, **3–5 min**, tip 2 mm short of working length.
- **Activation:**
  - NA: no agitation
  - MDA: gutta-percha push-pull (~100 strokes/min)
  - PUA: ultrasonic file ISO #10, **3 cycles × 20 sec**, power 3
  - SA: Pro Agitator tip system
- **Control:** saline irrigation (placebo, similar procedure to NA).
- Obturation: AH Plus sealer + lateral condensation; occlusal reduction + temporary restoration.
- **Analgesics:** none routinely; **rescue ibuprofen** provided. **If rescue used within 7 days → excluded** (i.e., per-protocol-type exclusion).

### Data collection
- **Outcome capture:** Patient marks VAS in a pain diary at specified times; returned at 48h and 7d recalls; assessor transcribed to master chart blinded to group.

## CATEGORY A – MANDATORY tests/models

### A1. Linear Mixed-Effects Model (LMM) for repeated VAS
- **Purpose:** Test whether **pain over time differs by group** (group×time interaction) while accounting for within-patient correlation.
- **Why appropriate (≥2):**
  1. Outcome is **repeated continuous** VAS over 6 timepoints → LMM directly models correlation.
  2. Handles **unbalanced data/missing timepoints** without deleting entire subjects (under MAR).
  3. Provides **time-specific and overall** contrasts with CIs.
- **Why it may not be appropriate (≥1):**
  - If VAS shows strong non-normality/ceiling effects or is better treated as ordinal; LMM may fit poorly.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If outcome is intentionally analyzed as **ordinal categories** (none/mild/moderate/severe) or as counts; or if residuals are extremely non-Gaussian and cannot be stabilized.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - **Generalized linear mixed model (GLMM)** with appropriate distribution (e.g., beta regression for bounded continuous after rescaling; ordinal mixed model), or **rank-based mixed models**.
- **Implementation details to pre-specify:**
  - Fixed effects: group (5), time (categorical), **group×time**, baseline VAS (optional; see A2), sex, age, arch (if adjusting).
  - Random effects: random intercept for subject; consider random slope for time if supported.
  - Covariance: start with unstructured or AR(1); choose using AIC/BIC.

### A2. Baseline comparability / covariate adjustment strategy
Two linked mandatory components:

**(i) Descriptive baseline balance (no hypothesis testing emphasis)**
- **Purpose:** Describe baseline characteristics by group to assess clinical comparability.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. RCT requires transparent reporting of baseline distributions.
  2. Identifies potential chance imbalances (noted by authors for age/sex).
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Significance testing at baseline can mislead (randomization implies differences are by chance).
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Do **not** use p-values as “proof” of balance/imbalance; avoid using baseline p-values to decide adjustment.
- **Alternative:**
  - Use **standardized mean differences (SMDs)** and graphical summaries.

**(ii) Covariate-adjusted primary model (ANCOVA principle within LMM)**
- **Test/model:** LMM including **baseline VAS** (and prespecified covariates: age, sex, arch).
- **Purpose:** Improve precision and address noted imbalance.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Baseline pain strongly predicts follow-up pain → increases power/precision.
  2. Prespecified adjustment mitigates chance imbalances (age/sex).
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - If baseline VAS is conceptually “pre-treatment” but measured after anesthesia or is inconsistently collected.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If baseline measurement is missing for many subjects or not comparable.
- **Alternative if issues:**
  - Analyze **change-from-baseline** via LMM; or MI for missing baseline values.

### A3. Primary hypothesis test: global group×time interaction (from LMM)
- **Purpose:** Formal test of whether pain trajectories differ across the five interventions.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Matches the primary objective (compare pain across times and groups).
  2. Controls type I error better than many separate timepoint tests.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - If clinical focus is a single prespecified timepoint (e.g., 24h), interaction may dilute power.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If only one timepoint is valid/available or if timepoints are not consistently collected.
- **Alternative:**
  - Prespecify one/few clinically primary times and model those (e.g., LMM but focus contrast at 24h), or use **area under the curve (AUC)** (see B2).

## CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED analyses/tests

### B1. Post-hoc pairwise comparisons (estimated marginal means from LMM) with multiplicity control
- **Purpose:** Identify which ozone activation methods differ from control and from each other at specific times.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Clinically interpretable (e.g., SA vs control at 24h).
  2. Uses the same model, preserving correlation structure and handling missingness.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Many comparisons inflate type I error if not adjusted and may overemphasize secondary contrasts.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If not prespecified and the study is underpowered for multiple pairwise claims.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Model-based comparisons from GLMM/robust mixed model; adjust p-values similarly (Holm).

### B2. Summary measure of pain burden: Area Under the Curve (AUC) over 0–48h (and/or 0–7d)
- **Purpose:** Compare overall postoperative pain experience rather than individual timepoints.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Reduces multiplicity and captures **total pain burden**.
  2. Robust to minor timing irregularities; clinically meaningful.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Requires sufficiently complete repeated measures; missingness can bias AUC if not handled carefully.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If many participants have missing intermediate VAS scores without reliable imputation.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:**
  - Use LMM-derived **model-based AUC** (integrate predicted means) or MI before AUC calculation; compare using **linear regression**/robust regression.

### B3. Sensitivity analysis for rescue medication use (informative censoring)
- **Test/model options:**
  - (i) Include a binary time-varying indicator “rescue medication taken” in mixed model (if timing known), or
  - (ii) Treat rescue use as **worst pain** imputation after intake, or
  - (iii) Joint/mixture approach: analyze pain plus rescue as composite endpoint.
- **Purpose:** Address bias from excluding those with severe pain.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Excluding medicated patients is outcome-dependent → biases treatment effect.
  2. Sensitivity analyses quantify robustness.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Timing/dose may be unknown; composite definitions can be arbitrary.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If rescue medication data are unreliable or not collected.
- **Alternative:**
  - Report ITT and PP side-by-side; perform **tipping-point** sensitivity for missing not at random (MNAR).

### B4. Model checking and robust inference
- **Tests/tools:** residual plots; Q-Q plots; check heteroscedasticity; influence diagnostics; robust (sandwich) SEs.
- **Purpose:** Validate model assumptions; reduce risk of misleading inference.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. VAS often non-normal and heteroscedastic across time/groups.
  2. Robust SEs provide protection against mild misspecification.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - With very small sample sizes per group/time, diagnostics are less informative.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Never “not use”; but avoid over-interpreting formal normality tests.
- **Alternative:**
  - Robust mixed models; transformation; GLMM.

### B5. Secondary objective: effects of sex, age, arch (and interactions)
- **Test/model:** Extend LMM with covariates and possibly **covariate×time** interactions (pre-specify limited set).
- **Purpose:** Evaluate whether pain differs by these factors and whether they modify treatment effect.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Directly addresses stated secondary aim.
  2. Adjusts for imbalances and explores clinically plausible effect modification.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Risk of overfitting/multiplicity with many interactions.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If sample sizes within subgroups are too small (sparse cells) leading to unstable estimates.
- **Alternative:**
  - Main-effects-only adjustment; or stratified descriptive results without formal interaction tests.

## CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL analyses/tests (supplementary)

### C1. Ordinal pain categories analysis (e.g., none/mild/moderate/severe)
- **Test/model:** **Ordinal logistic mixed model** (cumulative logit) over time.
- **Purpose:** Present results in clinically intuitive categories.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Avoids assuming equal interval scaling of VAS.
  2. Handles skewed distributions and floor effects.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Requires choosing cutpoints; proportional odds assumption may fail.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If cutpoints are arbitrary and not prespecified.
- **Alternative:**
  - Partial proportional odds model; multinomial mixed model.

### C2. “Time to minimal pain” endpoint
- **Test/model:** Kaplan–Meier and Cox model (if time can be discretized/approximated), or discrete-time survival.
- **Purpose:** Compare speed of pain resolution.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Patient-centered: “How quickly do patients feel better?”
  2. Uses multiple timepoints in a different way.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Pain recorded at fixed times (interval-censoring); exact event time unknown.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - If event definition is unstable or most patients already have low pain at baseline.
- **Alternative:**
  - Interval-censored survival models; or compare probability of pain-free at each time via GLMM.

### C3. Nonparametric timepoint comparisons (supplement only)
- **Test:** Kruskal–Wallis at each timepoint (with multiplicity adjustment).
- **Purpose:** Provide assumption-light cross-sectional comparisons at each time.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Works when VAS is heavily non-normal.
  2. Simple to communicate.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Ignores within-subject correlation; increases multiplicity; less efficient.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - As the primary analysis of repeated measures.
- **Alternative:**
  - Rank-based mixed models.

## TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS (should NOT be used)

1. **Multiple independent-samples t-tests** comparing groups at each timepoint without correction  
   - Inflates type I error; ignores repeated-measures correlation; not aligned with primary objective.

2. **One-way ANOVA on a single post-treatment timepoint only** (e.g., 24h) *without prespecification*  
   - Discards longitudinal information and reduces power/validity.

3. **Paired t-tests within each group across time** as main evidence  
   - Answers a different question (change within group) and does not compare interventions appropriately.

4. **Repeated-measures ANOVA (rANOVA) with complete-case listwise deletion** as the only analysis  
   - Requires sphericity; performs poorly with missing follow-up/common in diaries; can bias results.

5. **Chi-square tests on mean VAS** (misuse of categorical tests for continuous outcomes)  
   - Wrong scale/type.

6. **Baseline “significance testing” to decide whether to adjust**  
   - Data-dependent modeling increases bias; baseline tests are not a sound criterion.

7. **Excluding rescue-medication users from the primary analysis** (pure per-protocol only)  
   - Outcome-dependent exclusion likely biases treatment effects toward benefit.

---

## TASK 4: SUMMARY (overall analytical approach)

A longitudinal RCT with five parallel groups and repeated VAS measurements requires a **mixed-effects modeling framework** to (i) properly account for within-patient correlation, (ii) use incomplete repeated measures without biased complete-case deletion, and (iii) directly test the primary clinical question via a **group×time interaction** while providing clinically interpretable pairwise contrasts with multiplicity control. Covariate adjustment (baseline VAS; age/sex/arch) and sensitivity analyses for missing data and rescue medication strengthen internal validity given acknowledged imbalances and informative dropout risk.

### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY
1. **Test Name**: Repeated Measures ANOVA (or Mixed-Effects Model).  
   - **Purpose**: To compare VAS pain scores across time points (baseline, 6, 12, 24, and 48 hours, and 7 days) and between treatment groups.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. The study involves repeated measurements of pain scores over time.  
     2. It allows for the evaluation of interaction effects (group × time).  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Assumes normality and sphericity of data, which may not hold for VAS scores.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If sphericity is violated and cannot be corrected with Greenhouse-Geisser or Huynh-Feldt adjustments.  
   - **Alternative**: Generalized Estimating Equations (GEE) or nonparametric methods like Friedman’s test.

2. **Test Name**: Post-hoc Pairwise Comparisons (Bonferroni or Tukey).  
   - **Purpose**: To identify specific group differences in pain scores at each time point.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Necessary to determine which groups differ significantly after ANOVA.  
     2. Controls for Type I error in multiple comparisons.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: May lack power if sample sizes are small.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If ANOVA assumptions are violated.  
   - **Alternative**: Pairwise Wilcoxon Rank-Sum Test with Bonferroni correction.

#### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED
1. **Test Name**: Chi-Square Test (or Fisher’s Exact Test).  
   - **Purpose**: To analyze categorical variables like sex and arch distribution across groups.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Evaluates whether demographic factors are evenly distributed among groups.  
     2. Ensures no confounding due to uneven group characteristics.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Requires sufficient sample size in each category.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If expected cell counts are <5.  
   - **Alternative**: Fisher’s Exact Test.

2. **Test Name**: Multivariable Linear Regression.  
   - **Purpose**: To assess the effect of demographic factors (sex, age, arch) on pain scores.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Adjusts for potential confounders.  
     2. Identifies independent predictors of pain.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Assumes linearity and normality of residuals.  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If residuals are non-normal or heteroscedastic.  
   - **Alternative**: Quantile Regression.

#### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL
1. **Test Name**: Kaplan-Meier Survival Analysis.  
   - **Purpose**: To evaluate the time to pain resolution across groups.  
   - **Why Appropriate**:  
     1. Accounts for censored data (e.g., patients lost to follow-up).  
     2. Provides insights into pain resolution trends over time.  
   - **Why It May Not Be Appropriate**: Requires clear definition of "event" (e.g., pain score = 0).  
   - **When NOT to Use**: If pain scores do not reach 0 for most participants.  
   - **Alternative**: Cox Proportional Hazards Model.

TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN  
==================================  

The primary structure is a 5-group (between-subjects) × 6-time (within-subjects) design with continuous outcome (VAS). Secondary analyses examine sex, age group, and arch effects on pain.  

CATEGORY A – MANDATORY ANALYSES  
--------------------------------  

A1. Descriptive Statistics  
--------------------------  
Test/Method:  
- Descriptive statistics (means, SDs, medians, IQRs, counts, percentages) by group and time.

Purpose:  
- To summarize pain trajectories and baseline characteristics by group.  

Why appropriate:  
1. Essential to understand distributions, central tendency, and variability of VAS pain scores over time and across groups.  
2. Allows inspection for baseline comparability (age, sex, arch, baseline pain) and to detect potential imbalances or outliers.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Not a test per se; does not assess statistical significance; over-interpretation of descriptive differences without inferential analyses is possible.  

When not to use:  
- Never omitted; but should not be used alone to infer treatment efficacy.  

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- NA (descriptive statistics can always be computed; if distribution is highly skewed, emphasize medians/IQR and use robust summaries).

A2. Primary Inferential Analysis: Repeated-Measures ANOVA or Mixed-Effects Model  
---------------------------------------------------------------------------------  

Preferred: Linear Mixed-Effects Model (LMM) with random subject intercepts, fixed effects for group, time, and group×time; or, if software/experience is limited, a two-way repeated-measures ANOVA with Greenhouse–Geisser correction.  

Test name:  
- Linear mixed-effects model (or repeated-measures ANOVA).  

Purpose:  
- To compare the pattern of postendodontic pain over time among the five groups and to formally test whether group differences depend on time (group×time interaction).  

Why appropriate:  
1. The design is inherently repeated measures: same patients measured at 6 time points; mixed models / RM-ANOVA appropriately handle within-subject correlation.  
2. Between-subject factor (5 groups) and within-subject factor (time) match the trial design and prior sample size calculation (based on group×time interaction).  
3. LMM allows inclusion of participants with partially missing time points (if diaries incomplete at some time but not all), reducing bias and preserving power more than classical RM-ANOVA.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- VAS scores may be non-normally distributed (e.g., skewed, floor effects); sphericity assumption (for RM-ANOVA) may be violated.  
- With modest sample size per group and potential non-homogeneity of variance, classical RM-ANOVA may perform poorly.  

When this test should NOT be used:  
- Repeated-measures ANOVA should not be used if:  
  - Missing data are not completely at random and are unbalanced across time/ groups;  
  - Sphericity is severely violated and corrections are insufficient or not applied;  
  - Outcome distribution is markedly non-normal and transformations or robust methods are not used.  

Alternative if assumptions are violated:  
- For RM-ANOVA:  
  - Use Greenhouse–Geisser or Huynh–Feldt corrections for sphericity.  
  - Transform VAS (e.g., log, square root) if extreme skew, though VAS is often treated as approximately continuous and normal.  
- For strong non-normality or ordinal treatment:  
  - Use a nonparametric repeated-measures approach:  
    - Friedman test within groups plus post hoc tests;  
    - Or a rank-based mixed model (e.g., cumulative link mixed models).  
- If data are heavily zero-inflated:  
  - Consider two-part models (probability of any pain vs intensity when pain present).  

Minimum core specification for LMM:  
- Outcome: VAS_ij (patient i, time j).  
- Fixed effects: group (5 levels), time (6 levels), group×time interaction.  
- Random effects: random intercept for patient; optionally random slope for time if data support.  
- Covariance structure: unstructured or autoregressive (AR1) for repeated measures.  
- Primary hypothesis: group×time interaction (does pattern of pain over time differ by group?).  
- Secondary: between-group differences at each time point (planned contrasts).

A3. Baseline Comparability Analysis  
-----------------------------------  

Test name:  
- One-way ANOVA or Kruskal–Wallis test for continuous baseline variables (e.g., baseline VAS, age).  
- Chi-square test or Fisher’s exact test for categorical variables (sex, arch, PAI category).  

Purpose:  
- To describe and verify that randomization produced reasonably balanced groups at baseline.  

Why appropriate:  
1. Although RCTs rely on randomization, demonstrating approximate baseline balance reassures readers and informs covariate adjustment choices.  
2. Important for interpreting any observed treatment effect (e.g., if baseline pain differs, adjustment is needed).  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Over-reliance on significance tests for baseline comparability can be misleading (random differences occur by chance); descriptive comparison is usually more important.  

When not to use:  
- Should not be used to decide whether randomization “worked” in a strict inferential sense; imbalance does not invalidate RCT but suggests covariate adjustment.  

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- For continuous non-normal variables: Kruskal–Wallis instead of ANOVA.  
- For small cell counts: Fisher’s exact test instead of chi-square.

A4. Between-Group Comparisons at Individual Time Points  
-------------------------------------------------------  

Test name:  
- One-way ANOVA (or Kruskal–Wallis) at each post-treatment time point for VAS scores, with appropriate multiple-comparison corrections.  

Purpose:  
- To evaluate specific time points (e.g., 6h, 24h, 48h, 7d) where clinical interest is high, and quantify differences among groups.  

Why appropriate:  
1. Clinicians often want to know at which particular time intervals ozone activation reduces pain compared with control.  
2. These tests support the global group×time conclusions by detailing pairwise differences at each clinically relevant time.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Multiple testing inflates type I error if not properly adjusted; ANOVA assumptions may not hold at every time point.  

When not to use:  
- Should not be used without multiple-comparison adjustment (e.g., Bonferroni, Holm) or without reference to the primary repeated-measures analysis.  

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- Use Kruskal–Wallis test (nonparametric) per time point; if significant, follow with Dunn’s or Conover post hoc comparisons with p-value adjustment.

A5. Secondary Analysis: Effect of Sex, Age Group, and Arch  
----------------------------------------------------------  

Test name:  
- Extended linear mixed-effects model including sex, age group, arch, and their interactions with time and treatment.  

Purpose:  
- To assess whether pain trajectories differ by sex, age group, or arch and whether treatment effects are modified by these factors (effect modification).  

Why appropriate:  
1. Explicitly addresses the stated secondary objective.  
2. Mixed models can incorporate these covariates without compromising the RCT design; they increase precision by accounting for covariate-related variability.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Sample size per subgroup may be small; models may be overparameterized (limited power for higher-order interactions like group×time×sex).  

When not to use:  
- If sample sizes in certain strata are extremely small (e.g., few males in some groups), high-order interactions should not be fitted; model simplification is required.  

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- Use stratified analyses (e.g., separate mixed models for males and females) or simpler models with fewer interaction terms.  
- For age group or arch, use two-way ANOVA or nonparametric equivalents at key time points instead of full interaction models.

CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED ANALYSES  
----------------------------------  

B1. Responder Analysis (Categorical Outcome)  
--------------------------------------------  

Test name:  
- Chi-square or Fisher’s exact test comparing proportions of patients with clinically significant pain vs no/mild pain at each time point and across groups.  

Definition example:  
- “Responder” = VAS < 1 or <2 cm; or  
- Categorize pain: none (0), mild, moderate, severe, and compare distributions.  

Purpose:  
- Provide clinically interpretable categories (e.g., proportion pain-free) beyond mean VAS differences.  

Why appropriate:  
1. Clinicians interpret categories (e.g., pain-free) more easily than small differences in mean VAS.  
2. Provides robustness to non-normality since categories are ordinal/nominal.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Dichotomizing continuous VAS leads to loss of information and reduced statistical power.  

When not to use:  
- When sample size is too small to support multiple categorical analyses or many cells have low counts; or when the primary outcome is clearly continuous and well modeled by mixed models.  

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- Use exact tests (Fisher’s exact) for small cell counts;  
- Ordinal logistic regression for multi-level pain categories.

B2. Sensitivity Analyses for Missing Data and Exclusions  
--------------------------------------------------------  

Test name:  
- Sensitivity analysis using different data sets:  
  - Per-protocol (as done),  
  - Intention-to-treat (ITT) with imputation (e.g., multiple imputation or last observation carried forward, though LOCF is not ideal).  

Purpose:  
- To assess robustness of conclusions to exclusion of patients (lost to follow-up, incomplete diaries, analgesic use).  

Why appropriate:  
1. Dropouts and exclusions can bias treatment effect estimates; sensitivity analyses increase credibility.  
2. ITT analyses maintain randomization benefits and reduce risk of attrition bias.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- For small sample sizes, multiple imputation may be unstable; assumptions about missingness mechanism may be unverifiable.  

When not to use:  
- When missing data are minimal (<5%) and patterns are clearly random; complex imputation may not be worth added complexity.  

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- Use best-case/worst-case scenarios (e.g., assigning extreme pain values to missing cases by group) to bracket the possible impact of missingness.

B3. Effect Size Estimation and Confidence Intervals  
---------------------------------------------------  

Test name:  
- Estimation of effect sizes, such as:  
  - Partial eta squared for group, time, interaction in RM-ANOVA / mixed models.  
  - Differences in mean VAS between selected groups at each time point with 95% CIs.  

Purpose:  
- To quantify magnitude of treatment differences and provide interval estimates.  

Why appropriate:  
1. Supports interpretation of clinical relevance, not just statistical significance.  
2. Aligns with CONSORT recommendations to report effect sizes and CIs.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- If models are unstable or assumptions clearly violated, effect estimates may be unreliable or misleading.  

When not to use:  
- Should not be omitted; but choice of effect size metric may be reconsidered if distribution is very non-normal.  

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- Use nonparametric effect sizes (e.g., Cliff’s delta) or bootstrapped CIs for mean differences.

B4. Model Diagnostics and Assumption Checks  
-------------------------------------------  

Test name:  
- Residual diagnostics for LMM/RM-ANOVA: normality (Q–Q plots), homoscedasticity (residual vs fitted), influence analysis.  
- Mauchly’s test for sphericity (if using RM-ANOVA).  

Purpose:  
- To verify modeling assumptions and guide appropriate transformations or alternative methods.  

Why appropriate:  
1. Ensures validity of p-values and CIs.  
2. Identifies influential outliers that may distort results.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- With small sample size, formal tests may have low power; overreliance can mislead.  

When not to use:  
- Never completely omitted; but interpret tests cautiously and rely more on graphical diagnostics.  

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- Transform outcomes; use robust or nonparametric methods; use alternative covariance structures in mixed models.

B5. Comparison of Ozone vs Non-Ozone (Collapsed Groups)  
--------------------------------------------------------  

Test name:  
- Two-group comparison (pooled ozone groups vs control) using LMM or RM-ANOVA.  

Purpose:  
- To examine overall effect of ozone (any activation) compared with no ozone.  

Why appropriate:  
1. Reflects trial schema (overall allocation ratio 4:1 ozone:control); helps answer “does ozone per se help?”  
2. Increases power by combining four ozone subgroups, addressing whether any activation technique matters relative to no ozone.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Collapsing groups assumes all ozone activation methods are similar; if they differ substantially, this mixture may obscure important differences.  

When not to use:  
- If primary interest is in comparing specific activation modalities, not “any ozone” vs none.  

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- Use nonparametric counterparts (Mann–Whitney U at each time) or mixed models with binary factor (ozone vs no ozone) if distributional assumptions hold.

CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL ANALYSES  
-------------------------------  

C1. Post Hoc Pairwise Comparisons Between Activation Methods  
-------------------------------------------------------------  

Test name:  
- Pairwise comparisons between the four ozone groups (NA vs MDA vs PUA vs SA) and each vs control using Tukey’s or Bonferroni corrections within LMM/ANOVA framework.  

Purpose:  
- To rank activation methods (e.g., ultrasonic vs sonic vs manual vs no activation) in terms of pain reduction.  

Why appropriate:  
1. Clinically relevant to know if advanced activation methods offer incremental benefit over passive ozone or saline.  
2. Could guide clinical choice among activation technologies.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Multiple comparisons with relatively modest group sizes may reduce power and increase type I error if not carefully controlled.  

When not to use:  
- If primary analysis finds no significant group or interaction effect; extensive post hoc testing in absence of overall effect is inappropriate.  

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- Use nonparametric pairwise tests (e.g., Dunn’s test) on ranked data with appropriate p-value adjustment.

C2. Time-to-Event (Pain Resolution) Analysis  
--------------------------------------------  

Test name:  
- Kaplan–Meier survival analysis and log-rank test for time to “pain-free state” (e.g., first timepoint with VAS=0 or below threshold and remaining pain-free).  

Purpose:  
- To compare how quickly different groups become pain-free.  

Why appropriate:  
1. Time to resolution is clinically intuitive and complements repeated VAS measurements.  
2. Can handle censored cases (patients not pain-free by day 7).  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- Sample size and number of events (pain resolution) may be inadequate; VAS schedule (fixed time points) limits time resolution and may produce many ties.  

When not to use:  
- When exact time of pain resolution is not reliably captured (only discrete visits) or event definitions are ambiguous.  

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- Use cumulative incidence curves of “pain-free” proportions over time with categorical analyses instead of formal survival modeling.

C3. Exploratory Analysis of Baseline PAI or Tooth-related Factors  
-----------------------------------------------------------------  

Test name:  
- ANOVA or mixed model including PAI category or tooth location (e.g., anterior vs premolar) if available.  

Purpose:  
- To explore whether baseline periapical status modifies pain outcomes or treatment effects.  

Why appropriate:  
1. May identify patient subgroups where ozone is more/less effective.  
2. Adds exploratory mechanistic insight into inflammatory load vs pain.  

Why it may not be appropriate:  
- PAI <3 inclusion criterion yields a restricted range; limited variability may make findings uninformative.  

When not to use:  
- If data on PAI or tooth type are incomplete or unreliable.  

Alternative if assumptions violated:  
- Descriptive subgroup summaries instead of formal modeling.

TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS AND WHY  
====================================  

1. Independent-Samples t-test for Primary Outcome  
-------------------------------------------------  

- Why not appropriate:  
  - The design involves 5 groups and repeated measures over time; a simple two-sample t-test cannot handle multiple groups or within-subject correlation.  
  - Using t-tests at multiple times and for multiple group pairs massively inflates type I error and ignores correlation.  

2. Simple One-Way ANOVA Ignoring Time (Using Only One Time Point or Averaging Across Times)  
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------  

- Why not appropriate:  
  - Averaging VAS over time or picking a single time point discards temporal information and the dynamic nature of pain.  
  - Fails to address the primary endpoint which explicitly involves pain trajectories (group×time interaction).  

3. Multiple Unadjusted Pairwise t-tests Across All Groups and Time Points  
-------------------------------------------------------------------------  

- Why not appropriate:  
  - Leads to severe multiplicity and inflated family-wise error rate.  
  - Does not account for repeated measures (correlation within subject); invalid standard errors.  

4. Parametric Tests Treating VAS Strictly as Ordinal without Justification  
-------------------------------------------------------------------------  

- Example: using ordinal logistic regression across many time points without accounting for repeated measures.  
- Why not appropriate:  
  - VAS is typically treated as continuous; re-coding into arbitrary categories and applying single-level ordinal models would misrepresent structure and correlation over time.  

5. Simple Pre–Post Paired t-tests Within Each Group Only  
--------------------------------------------------------  

- Why not appropriate:  
  - Testing changes within groups without comparing between groups fails to answer the comparative effectiveness question.  
  - Does not control for multiple comparisons or between-group differences; easily misleads interpretation.  

6. MANOVA (classical multivariate ANOVA) Treating Each Time Point as a Separate Outcome Without Repeated Structure  
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------  

- Why not appropriate:  
  - MANOVA for multiple dependent variables assumes independence; repeated measures are correlated in a structured way, better handled by RM-ANOVA or mixed models.  
  - Interpretation becomes complex, and sphericity/compound symmetry assumptions may not be satisfied.  

7. Logistic Regression Treating Group as Outcome and Pain as Predictor  
---------------------------------------------------------------------  

- Why not appropriate:  
  - In RCTs, treatment group is an exposure/predictor, not an outcome; modeling the reverse is conceptually wrong.  

8. Nonparametric Tests that Ignore Time (e.g., Single Kruskal–Wallis on Collapsed VAS)  
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------  

- Why not appropriate:  
  - Collapsing over time loses key information on changes and timing; cannot test the primary hypothesis regarding trajectory differences.

TASK 4: SUMMARY OF ANALYTICAL APPROACH  
=======================================  

- The study is a 5-arm parallel-group RCT with repeated measures of continuous pain scores (VAS) at 6 time points. The central inferential question is whether different ozone application/activation strategies alter the trajectory of postendodontic pain compared with no ozone.

- A linear mixed-effects model (or, second-best, repeated-measures ANOVA) with fixed effects for group, time, and group×time interaction, and random intercepts for patients, is the appropriate core analytic framework. This approach:  
  - Accounts for within-subject correlation over time.  
  - Aligns with the sample size planning (based on group×time interaction).  
  - Allows handling of unbalanced data from dropouts or incomplete diaries.  

- Baseline comparability analyses, between-group comparisons at key time points, and incorporation of sex, age group, and arch as covariates address both primary and secondary objectives and enhance interpretation. Nonparametric alternatives and robust sensitivity analyses are planned to ensure validity if parametric assumptions are violated.

- The overall plan respects CONSORT standards, is consistent with the trial’s design and aims, controls multiplicity, and yields both statistically and clinically interpretable results for the effectiveness of activated ozone in postendodontic pain management.
