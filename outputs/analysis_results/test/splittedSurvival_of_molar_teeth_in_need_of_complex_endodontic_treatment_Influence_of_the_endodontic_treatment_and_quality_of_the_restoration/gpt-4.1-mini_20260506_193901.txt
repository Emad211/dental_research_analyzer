## TASK 1: INFORMATION EXTRACTION

### Study Characteristics

**Study Design:**  
- Retrospective practice-based cohort study.

**Study Setting:**  
- Referral dental clinic specialized in endodontics at Martini Hospital Groningen, the Netherlands.

**Study Period:**  
- January 2011 to October 2017.

**Study Population:**  
- Adult patients (≥18 years) with molar teeth (maxillary and mandibular first and second molars) undergoing primary or secondary (retreatment) root canal treatment by two specialist endodontists.

**Inclusion Criteria:**  
- Molar teeth with primary or secondary root canal treatment.  
- Patients aged ≥18 years.  
- ASA score ≤ 2.  
- ETC score II or III (moderate to high difficulty endodontic cases).

**Exclusion Criteria:**  
- Teeth with open apices.  
- Combined non-surgical and surgical treatment.  
- ETC score I cases.  
- Patients <18 years or ASA score >2.  
- Operator not an endodontic specialist.

**Sample Size:**  
- Initially assessed: 416 treatments.  
- After exclusions: 358 eligible.  
- Final included for tooth survival analysis: 279.  
- Final included for endodontic success analysis: 268.  
- Final included for restorative success analysis: 215.

**Objectives:**  
- Primary: Evaluate survival of molar teeth with ETC scores II and III after at least 12 months post-treatment.  
- Secondary: Evaluate quality of coronal seal, restoration, and endodontic treatment.  
- Test null hypotheses regarding influence of type of endodontic treatment, ETC score, and coronal seal quality on tooth survival; and difference in restoration quality between direct and indirect restorations.

---

### Groups / Comparison Factors

- **ETC Score:** II vs III.  
- **Type of Endodontic Treatment:** Primary vs Retreatment.  
- **Technical Quality of Endodontic Treatment:** Adequate vs Inadequate.  
- **Quality of Coronal Seal:** Adequate vs Inadequate.  
- **Restoration Type:** Direct vs Indirect.  
- **Cusp Coverage:** Yes vs No.  
- **Other Patient Factors:** Caries risk (low/medium/high), parafunction (yes/no), gender, jaw (upper/lower).

---

### Variables and Measurements

**Patient Variables:**  
- Age (continuous).  
- Caries risk (categorical: low, medium, high).  
- Parafunction (binary: yes/no).  
- Gender (male/female).

**Endodontic Variables:**  
- ETC score (II/III).  
- Type of treatment (primary/retreatment).  
- Number of canals treated (3 or 4).  
- Preoperative radiolucency (yes/no).  
- Preoperative complications (broken instrument, perforation, obliteration; yes/no).  
- Technical quality of root canal filling (adequate/inadequate) assessed radiographically based on compactness, length (0–2 mm from apex), and morphology preservation.  
- Endodontic healing status at follow-up (healed, healing, nonhealed) based on clinical symptoms and radiographic evaluation (Periapical Index - PAI).  
- Presence of postoperative radiolucency (PAI ≥ 3).

**Restorative Variables:**  
- Restoration type (direct/indirect/unknown).  
- Cusp coverage (yes/no/unknown).  
- Quality of coronal seal (adequate/inadequate/unknown).  
- Restoration quality scored by FDI criteria (graded as clinically excellent/sufficient/unsatisfactory/poor).  
- Restoration surfaces and materials (composite, amalgam, glass ceramic, PFM, zirconia).

---

### Data Collection Methods

- Endodontic procedures performed under microscope and rubber dam by two specialists.  
- Radiographs taken postoperatively and at recall (9–12 months post-treatment). Additional radiographs taken if clinical symptoms or compromised coronal seal.  
- Clinical examination at recall: percussion, palpation, probing, patient symptom anamnesis.  
- Restoration quality assessed by calibrated observers using FDI criteria and photographic documentation.  
- Endodontic healing assessed by four calibrated observers using clinical and radiographic criteria (PAI).  
- Patient follow-up data supplemented by contacting general practitioners when patients unable to attend.

## TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS

1. **Kaplan-Meier Survival Analysis**  
   - **Purpose:** Estimate cumulative survival probability of molar teeth over time post-endodontic treatment.  
   - **Why appropriate:**  
     - Accounts for censored data (loss to follow-up).  
     - Provides survival curves for time-to-event (tooth loss).  
   - **Why it may not be appropriate:**  
     - Assumes independent censoring; if censoring related to prognosis, bias may occur.  
   - **When NOT to use:**  
     - If exact time-to-event data unavailable or if follow-up times are uniform and short.  
   - **Alternative:**  
     - Life-table (actuarial) method if exact event times unavailable.

2. **Log-Rank Test**  
   - **Purpose:** Compare survival curves between groups (e.g., ETC II vs III, primary vs retreatment).  
   - **Why appropriate:**  
     - Non-parametric test for equality of survival distributions.  
     - Suitable for censored survival data.  
   - **Why it may not be appropriate:**  
     - Sensitive to proportional hazards assumption; differences in hazard rates over time may affect results.  
   - **When NOT to use:**  
     - If hazards cross or proportional hazards assumption violated.  
   - **Alternative:**  
     - Wilcoxon (Breslow) test or stratified tests.

3. **Cox Proportional Hazards Regression**  
   - **Purpose:** Assess effect of multiple covariates (ETC score, treatment type, coronal seal quality, technical quality, patient factors) on hazard of tooth loss.  
   - **Why appropriate:**  
     - Handles censored survival data.  
     - Allows adjustment for confounders.  
   - **Why it may not be appropriate:**  
     - Requires proportional hazards assumption.  
   - **When NOT to use:**  
     - If proportional hazards assumption violated.  
   - **Alternative:**  
     - Time-dependent Cox models or parametric survival models (e.g., Weibull).

4. **Chi-Square Test of Independence**  
   - **Purpose:** Test associations between categorical variables (e.g., healing status vs type of treatment, restoration type vs restoration quality).  
   - **Why appropriate:**  
     - Suitable for categorical data with adequate cell counts.  
     - Simple and widely used.  
   - **Why it may not be appropriate:**  
     - Expected cell counts <5 violate assumptions.  
   - **When NOT to use:**  
     - Small sample sizes or sparse data.  
   - **Alternative:**  
     - Fisher’s exact test.

5. **Descriptive Statistics**  
   - **Purpose:** Summarize baseline characteristics, frequencies, means, medians, standard deviations.  
   - **Why appropriate:**  
     - Essential for understanding sample and variables distribution.  
   - **Why it may not be appropriate:**  
     - None; always appropriate.  
   - **When NOT to use:**  
     - N/A.

### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED TESTS

1. **Multivariable Logistic Regression**  
   - **Purpose:** Model binary outcomes such as endodontic healing (healed vs non-healed) or restoration success (clinically sufficient vs unsatisfactory/poor) adjusting for multiple predictors.  
   - **Why appropriate:**  
     - Adjusts for confounders.  
     - Estimates odds ratios for predictors.  
   - **Why it may not be appropriate:**  
     - Requires sufficient events per predictor variable to avoid overfitting.  
   - **When NOT to use:**  
     - Small sample size or rare outcomes.  
   - **Alternative:**  
     - Penalized logistic regression or exact logistic regression.

2. **Ordinal Logistic Regression**  
   - **Purpose:** Analyze ordered categorical outcomes such as restoration quality graded by FDI criteria (excellent, sufficient, unsatisfactory, poor).  
   - **Why appropriate:**  
     - Accounts for ordinal nature of data.  
     - More informative than collapsing categories.  
   - **Why it may not be appropriate:**  
     - Assumes proportional odds (parallel lines) assumption.  
   - **When NOT to use:**  
     - If proportional odds assumption violated.  
   - **Alternative:**  
     - Partial proportional odds model or multinomial logistic regression.

3. **Interobserver Reliability Analysis (e.g., Cohen’s Kappa or Weighted Kappa)**  
   - **Purpose:** Assess agreement between observers on categorical ratings (e.g., endodontic healing, restoration quality).  
   - **Why appropriate:**  
     - Ensures calibration and reliability of subjective assessments.  
   - **Why it may not be appropriate:**  
     - Requires paired ratings on same subjects.  
   - **When NOT to use:**  
     - If only one observer or no paired ratings.  
   - **Alternative:**  
     - Intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC) for continuous ratings.

4. **Mann-Whitney U Test or Wilcoxon Rank-Sum Test**  
   - **Purpose:** Compare continuous or ordinal variables between two independent groups when normality is questionable (e.g., comparing restoration quality scores between direct and indirect restorations).  
   - **Why appropriate:**  
     - Non-parametric, no normality assumption.  
     - Suitable for ordinal data.  
   - **Why it may not be appropriate:**  
     - Less powerful than parametric tests if normality holds.  
   - **When NOT to use:**  
     - If data are normally distributed and interval scale.  
   - **Alternative:**  
     - Independent samples t-test.

5. **Kaplan-Meier Curves Stratified by Key Covariates**  
   - **Purpose:** Visualize survival differences across subgroups (e.g., by restoration type, coronal seal quality).  
   - **Why appropriate:**  
     - Provides intuitive graphical representation.  
   - **Why it may not be appropriate:**  
     - Does not adjust for confounders.  
   - **When NOT to use:**  
     - When multivariable adjustment needed.  
   - **Alternative:**  
     - Adjusted survival curves from Cox model.

### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL TESTS

1. **Competing Risks Analysis**  
   - **Purpose:** Account for competing events (e.g., extraction vs retreatment) that preclude the event of interest (tooth survival).  
   - **Why appropriate:**  
     - Provides more accurate risk estimates when competing events present.  
   - **Why it may not be appropriate:**  
     - Requires detailed cause-specific event data.  
   - **When NOT to use:**  
     - If competing events not recorded or rare.  
   - **Alternative:**  
     - Standard survival analysis with caution.

2. **Sensitivity Analysis**  
   - **Purpose:** Assess robustness of results to assumptions, e.g., excluding patients lost to follow-up or imputing missing data.  
   - **Why appropriate:**  
     - Enhances credibility of findings.  
   - **Why it may not be appropriate:**  
     - Requires additional assumptions.  
   - **When NOT to use:**  
     - If missing data minimal or completely at random.  
   - **Alternative:**  
     - Multiple imputation.

3. **Subgroup Analysis**  
   - **Purpose:** Explore effect modification by variables such as jaw (maxillary vs mandibular), caries risk, or parafunction.  
   - **Why appropriate:**  
     - Identifies groups with differential outcomes.  
   - **Why it may not be appropriate:**  
     - Risk of multiple testing and reduced power.  
   - **When NOT to use:**  
     - If sample size too small for subgroups.  
   - **Alternative:**  
     - Interaction terms in regression models.

4. **Correlation Analysis (e.g., Spearman’s rho)**  
   - **Purpose:** Explore associations between continuous or ordinal variables (e.g., age and restoration quality).  
   - **Why appropriate:**  
     - Non-parametric, no normality assumption.  
   - **Why it may not be appropriate:**  
     - Does not imply causation.  
   - **When NOT to use:**  
     - If variables are nominal.  
   - **Alternative:**  
     - Point-biserial correlation for binary variables.

## TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS

1. **Paired t-test or Repeated Measures ANOVA**  
   - **Why inappropriate:**  
     - No repeated measures on the same teeth over time reported beyond baseline and one recall; data are mostly cross-sectional or survival times, not repeated continuous measures.

2. **Independent Samples t-test for Non-Normally Distributed Ordinal Data**  
   - **Why inappropriate:**  
     - Restoration quality and healing status are ordinal or categorical, violating t-test assumptions.

3. **Simple Linear Regression for Binary or Ordinal Outcomes**  
   - **Why inappropriate:**  
     - Outcomes like healing status and restoration quality are categorical; linear regression assumptions violated.

4. **Chi-Square Test with Small Cell Counts Without Correction**  
   - **Why inappropriate:**  
     - Some categories have small sample sizes; Fisher’s exact test preferred.

5. **Ignoring Censoring in Survival Data (e.g., using only proportions or logistic regression for survival)**  
   - **Why inappropriate:**  
     - Survival data require time-to-event analysis methods that account for censoring.
