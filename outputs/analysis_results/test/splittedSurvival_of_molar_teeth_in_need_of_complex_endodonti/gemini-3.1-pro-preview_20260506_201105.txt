### TASK 1: INFORMATION EXTRACTION

**Study Characteristics:**
*   **Study Design:** Retrospective practice-based cohort study.
*   **Primary Objective:** To evaluate the survival of molar teeth with Endodontic Treatment Classification (ETC) scores of II and III in a referral setting (minimum service time of 12 months).
*   **Secondary Objectives:** To evaluate the quality of the coronal seal, the restoration, and the endodontic treatment.
*   **Hypotheses:** 
    *   *Primary Null:* Type of endodontic treatment, ETC score, and quality of coronal seal have no influence on tooth survival.
    *   *Secondary Null:* There is no difference in restoration quality between direct and indirect restorations.
*   **Sample Size:** 416 assessed $\rightarrow$ 358 eligible $\rightarrow$ 279 included for tooth survival $\rightarrow$ 268 for endodontic success $\rightarrow$ 215 for restorative success. (Note: A priori power calculation required 362 teeth, which was not met due to loss to follow-up).
*   **Follow-up:** Recall appointment at 9–12 months, with some data extracted from general practitioner charts for longer-term survival.

**Variables & Measurements:**
*   **Dependent/Outcome Variables:**
    1.  *Tooth Survival:* Binary/Time-to-event (Retained vs. Extracted/Lost).
    2.  *Endodontic Healing:* Categorical/Ordinal (Healed, Healing, Nonhealed) based on clinical symptoms and Periapical Index (PAI).
    3.  *Restoration Quality:* Ordinal (FDI criteria: clinically excellent to clinically poor).
*   **Independent/Predictor Variables:**
    *   *Patient:* Age (Continuous), Gender (Male/Female), Caries risk (Low/Medium/High), Parafunction (Yes/No).
    *   *Tooth/Restorative:* Jaw (Upper/Lower), Number of canals (3/4), Restoration type (Direct/Indirect), Cusp coverage (Yes/No), Coronal seal (Adequate/Inadequate).
    *   *Endodontic:* Operator (1/2), ETC score (II/III), Preoperative radiolucency (Yes/No), Preoperative complications (Yes/No), Treatment type (First/Retreatment), Technical quality (Adequate/Inadequate).

### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY (Core to answering hypotheses)

**1. Kaplan-Meier Survival Analysis**
*   **Purpose:** To estimate the cumulative survival probability of the endodontically treated molars over time.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) The primary outcome is "tooth survival," which involves time-to-event data. 
    2) It effectively handles right-censored data (patients lost to follow-up or teeth still surviving at the end of the study period).
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It cannot adjust for multiple confounding variables simultaneously.
*   **When NOT to use:** If all patients have the exact same follow-up time (making it a simple binary outcome rather than time-to-event).
*   **Alternative:** Life Table analysis.

**2. Cox Proportional Hazards Regression**
*   **Purpose:** To test the primary null hypothesis by evaluating the influence of multiple predictors (treatment type, ETC score, coronal seal) on tooth survival.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) Allows for multivariable adjustment of confounders (e.g., age, caries risk) while analyzing time-to-event data. 
    2) Provides Hazard Ratios (HR) to quantify the risk of tooth loss associated with each predictor.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** The assumption of proportional hazards over time might be violated.
*   **When NOT to use:** If the proportional hazards assumption fails (e.g., the risk of failure for retreatment vs. initial treatment changes drastically over time).
*   **Alternative:** Extended Cox model (with time-dependent covariates) or Parametric survival models (e.g., Weibull).

**3. Mann-Whitney U Test (or Wilcoxon Rank-Sum Test)**
*   **Purpose:** To test the secondary hypothesis comparing restoration quality between direct and indirect restorations.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) The outcome variable (FDI criteria) is ordinal (excellent, sufficient, unsatisfactory, poor). 
    2) It does not assume a normal distribution, which is appropriate for ranked categorical data.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It does not account for potential confounders (e.g., caries risk or parafunction) that might dictate why a direct vs. indirect restoration was chosen.
*   **When NOT to use:** If the outcome variable is strictly nominal or continuous and normally distributed.
*   **Alternative:** Ordinal Logistic Regression.

**4. Chi-Square Test of Independence (or Fisher’s Exact Test)**
*   **Purpose:** To compare categorical outcomes (e.g., Endodontic healing: Healed/Healing/Nonhealed) across binary predictors (e.g., Adequate vs. Inadequate coronal seal).
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) Both independent and dependent variables are categorical. 
    2) It provides a straightforward assessment of crude associations before multivariable modeling.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** It cannot adjust for covariates.
*   **When NOT to use:** If expected cell counts are less than 5 (use Fisher's Exact Test instead).
*   **Alternative:** Fisher's Exact Test.

#### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED (Enhances quality and rigor)

**1. Fleiss’ Kappa or Cohen’s Kappa**
*   **Purpose:** To measure inter-rater reliability among the four calibrated observers.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) The study relies on subjective radiographic and clinical assessments (PAI, FDI criteria) by multiple observers. 
    2) Validates the calibration process mentioned in the methods.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** Kappa can be paradoxically low if the prevalence of a specific finding is extremely high or low, even with high absolute agreement.
*   **When NOT to use:** If only one observer evaluated all cases.
*   **Alternative:** Gwet’s AC1 statistic (more robust to the prevalence paradox).

**2. Generalized Estimating Equations (GEE) or Mixed-Effects Models**
*   **Purpose:** To account for clustering effects if multiple teeth were treated within the same patient.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) The sample size refers to "416 root canal treatments," implying some patients may have contributed more than one tooth. 
    2) Teeth within the same mouth share systemic risk factors (caries risk, parafunction), violating the assumption of independent observations.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** If every single tooth in the dataset belongs to a unique patient, the model becomes unnecessarily complex.
*   **When NOT to use:** When there is strictly one observation (tooth) per patient.
*   **Alternative:** Standard multivariable logistic/Cox regression with robust standard errors.

**3. Ordinal Logistic Regression**
*   **Purpose:** To model the predictors of Endodontic Healing (Healed $\rightarrow$ Healing $\rightarrow$ Nonhealed) or FDI restoration scores.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) Respects the natural ordering of the outcome variables without assuming equal spacing between categories. 
    2) Allows for multivariable adjustment.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** The assumption of proportional odds (that the effect of the predictor is the same across all threshold levels of the outcome) may be violated.
*   **When NOT to use:** If the proportional odds assumption is violated.
*   **Alternative:** Multinomial Logistic Regression.

#### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL (Supplementary insights)

**1. Multiple Imputation by Chained Equations (MICE)**
*   **Purpose:** To handle missing data (noted as "Unknown" in Table 1 for caries risk, parafunction, and restoration type).
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) Prevents the loss of statistical power associated with listwise deletion. 
    2) Reduces bias if the data is Missing at Random (MAR).
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** The percentage of missing data in Table 1 is very low (1-5%); imputation may overcomplicate the analysis for minimal gain.
*   **When NOT to use:** If data is Missing Not At Random (MNAR) or if missingness exceeds acceptable thresholds without reliable auxiliary variables.
*   **Alternative:** Complete case analysis (listwise deletion).

**2. Propensity Score Matching (PSM)**
*   **Purpose:** To balance baseline characteristics between teeth receiving direct vs. indirect restorations.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) Treatment assignment was not randomized; dentists likely chose indirect restorations for more severely damaged teeth. 
    2) Mimics a randomized control trial by comparing "apples to apples."
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** Reduces the effective sample size, which is already diminished due to loss to follow-up.
*   **When NOT to use:** When the sample size is too small to find suitable matches.
*   **Alternative:** Inverse Probability of Treatment Weighting (IPTW).

### TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS

*   **Independent Samples t-test / ANOVA:** Inappropriate because the primary outcomes (survival, healing status, FDI criteria) are categorical, ordinal, or time-to-event, not continuous.
*   **Pearson Correlation:** Inappropriate because the variables are predominantly categorical or ordinal. Pearson requires two continuous, normally distributed variables.
*   **Paired t-test / Wilcoxon Signed-Rank Test:** Inappropriate because the study does not compare pre- and post-treatment continuous/ordinal scores on the same teeth; it compares outcomes across different groups (e.g., direct vs. indirect).
*   **Linear Regression:** Inappropriate because the dependent variables are not continuous.
