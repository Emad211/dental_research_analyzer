## TASK 1: INFORMATION EXTRACTION (study characteristics)

### Study identification
- **Title/topic:** Vital pulp therapy (VPT) in permanent posterior teeth with *clinically diagnosed irreversible pulpitis caused by caries* using **iRoot BP Plus**.
- **Setting:** Single center, Department of Endodontics, Affiliated Hospital of Stomatology, Zhejiang University School of Medicine (China).
- **Ethics/registration:** Ethics approved; registered (ChiCTR2100044580). Informed consent obtained.
- **Reporting guideline mentioned:** STROBE.

### Objectives
1. **Primary objective:** Evaluate **clinical and radiographic outcomes (success/failure)** of VPT using iRoot BP Plus.
2. **Secondary objective:** Analyze **preoperative/baseline factors affecting prognosis** of VPT.

### Study design
- **Prospective cohort study** (single-arm cohort receiving VPT; comparisons are *within cohort* across VPT types and baseline characteristics).
- **Allocation to VPT type:** Not randomized; determined intraoperatively by **hemostasis time** and extent of pulp removal.

### Participants and sampling
- **Eligibility screening:** 382 assessed; 310 excluded (305 not meeting criteria; 5 refused).
- **Enrolled:** 72 included initially.
- **Intraoperative exclusions after pulp assessment:** 13 excluded (hemostasis >5 min after full coronal pulp removal or necrotic appearance).
- **Treated with VPT:** 59 teeth/patients initially treated.
- **Analyzed:** 57 teeth/patients (2 lost to follow-up; follow-up rate 96.6% among treated).
- **Unit of analysis:** **Tooth**, restricted to **one tooth per participant** to maintain independence (priority tooth selection rule specified).
- **Age range:** 6–20 years (mean in analyzed cohort 11.75 ± 3.81).
- **Sex (analyzed):** 27 male, 30 female.

### Inclusion criteria (key)
- Posterior teeth with **deep caries and pulp exposure**.
- Symptoms consistent with irreversible pulpitis (referred/spontaneous/thermal pain lasting seconds to hours vs control).
- No prominent **periapical/furcation radiolucency**.
- One tooth per participant.

### Exclusion criteria (key)
- Medical contraindications to dental treatment.
- Previous RCT or VPT.
- External/internal resorption; prominent periapical radiolucency/furcation lesion.
- Pulpitis due to non-caries etiology (wedge defect, crack, periodontitis, fracture).
- No response to vitality tests.

### Interventions / exposure definition
All cases received VPT using **iRoot BP Plus** (3 mm layer), protected with resin-modified glass ionomer, restored with composite or stainless-steel crown.

**VPT types (exposure groups):**
- **DPC (Direct pulp capping):** If hemostasis achieved within 5 min after exposure.
- **PP (Partial pulpotomy):** If initial hemostasis >5 min, remove ~2–3 mm coronal pulp; if then hemostasis <5 min.
- **FP (Full pulpotomy):** If after partial removal hemostasis still >5 min, remove all coronal pulp to canal orifices; include only if hemostasis <5 min.
- If hemostasis still >5 min after full coronal pulp removal or pulp appears necrotic → excluded and treated otherwise (RCT/revascularization).

**Operators:** Three endodontic postgraduate students under supervision; same protocol.

### Follow-up schedule and retention
- Planned visits: 3, 6, 12 months; then annually.
- Available follow-up durations (analyzed): >6 mo n=57; >12 mo n=42; >24 mo n=11; >36 mo n=1.
- Mean follow-up: 16.0 ± 6.58 months (range 6–36).

### Outcomes

#### Primary outcome (binary)
- **VPT success vs failure**, defined by combined **clinical + radiographic** criteria:
  - No spontaneous discomfort/pain history
  - No tenderness to percussion/palpation/cold/heat
  - Mobility ≤ Grade I
  - No swelling/sinus tract; normal soft tissue
  - Restoration intact; normal function
  - No periradicular pathosis; no external resorption radiographically
  - No intraradicular pathosis; no internal resorption radiographically
  - For immature roots: continued radiographic root development

#### Pain measurement (mentioned)
- **vNRS** used to record postoperative pain intensity (timing not fully specified in provided text; likely during early postoperative period).

#### Reliability
- Radiographic/clinical evaluators: intra-/interobserver agreement reported using **Cohen’s kappa** (0.75–0.85).

# CATEGORY A – MANDATORY (core analyses)

## A1) Descriptive statistics (no hypothesis test)
**Test/Procedure:** Descriptive summaries (counts/%, mean±SD or median[IQR])  
**Purpose:** Describe cohort, baseline predictors, VPT type distribution, follow-up time, and outcome rates.  
**Why appropriate (≥2):**
1. Observational cohort requires transparent characterization and potential imbalance across VPT types.
2. Provides denominators for success rates overall and by VPT type.  
**Why may not be appropriate (≥1):** None (always appropriate).  
**When NOT to use:** Never.  
**Alternative if assumptions violated:** Use medians/IQR for skewed continuous variables (e.g., follow-up time).

## A2) Estimation of success proportions with confidence intervals
**Test:** Exact (Clopper–Pearson) or Wilson **binomial proportion CI** for success overall and by VPT type.  
**Purpose:** Quantify precision around success rates (overall and stratified).  
**Why appropriate:**
1. Outcome is binary (success/failure).
2. Small sample sizes in groups (e.g., FP n≈13) make normal approximations unreliable.  
**Why may not be appropriate:** If success definition varies over time and follow-up is heterogeneous, a single proportion ignores censoring.  
**When NOT to use:** As the only outcome summary if follow-up times differ materially and failures can occur later.  
**Alternative:** Kaplan–Meier survival estimates of success over time (see B2).

## A3) Primary modeling of prognostic factors: penalized multivariable logistic regression
**Test/Model:** **Firth penalized logistic regression** (or exact logistic regression if needed) with outcome success (1/0).  
**Purpose:** Estimate associations (adjusted ORs) between baseline factors (including VPT type) and success.  
**Why appropriate:**
1. Binary endpoint with multiple predictors; goal explicitly includes “preoperative factors affecting prognosis.”
2. Small number of failures (~5) risks separation and biased MLE; Firth reduces small-sample bias and handles separation better than standard logistic regression.  
**Why may not be appropriate:**
- With too many predictors relative to events, even penalized models can be unstable and overfit.  
**When NOT to use:**
- If events are extremely sparse within categories leading to non-identifiability even after collapsing levels; or if the intent is causal effect estimation without adequate confounding control structure.  
**Alternative if assumptions violated / data limitations:**
- Reduce model complexity using **clinically justified predictor selection** (pre-specify a small set), collapse categories, or use **penalized regression with shrinkage** (ridge) and report predictive performance rather than causal interpretation.
- For very sparse strata: **exact logistic regression** (computationally heavy).

**Mandatory implementation details:**
- Pre-specify a *parsimonious* model given event count (e.g., include VPT type + age + root maturation + key symptom variables), not all 14 unless events permit.
- Report adjusted ORs with 95% CIs; avoid relying solely on p-values.

# CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED (improves rigor)

## B1) Unadjusted group comparisons of success by VPT type
**Test:** **Fisher’s exact test** (3×2) or pairwise Fisher with multiplicity control.  
**Purpose:** Compare crude success proportions across DPC/PP/FP.  
**Why appropriate:**
1. Categorical outcome with small cell counts; Fisher is valid where chi-square is not.
2. Provides an easily interpretable unadjusted comparison complementing regression.  
**Why may not be appropriate:**
- Not adjusted for confounding; VPT type is determined by bleeding/hemostasis pathway and may correlate with disease severity.  
**When NOT to use:**
- If interpreted causally as “best treatment,” which is not justified in nonrandomized allocation.  
**Alternative if assumptions violated:** Not applicable (Fisher is assumption-light). For effect size, use **risk difference/ratio** with exact CIs.

## B2) Time-to-failure analysis (handles unequal follow-up)
**Test/Model:** **Kaplan–Meier** survival curve of “survival” = maintained success; optionally **log-rank test** by VPT type; and/or **Cox proportional hazards** (penalized) with key predictors.  
**Purpose:** Account for varying follow-up durations; estimate success probability over time.  
**Why appropriate:**
1. Follow-up ranges 6–36 months; a simple success proportion mixes short and long follow-up.
2. Allows visualization of when failures occur and comparison of durability by VPT type.  
**Why may not be appropriate:**
- Very few failures limit power; Cox models may be unstable and PH assumption hard to assess.  
**When NOT to use:**
- If failure times are not recorded (only status at last visit) or interval-censored without visit dates.  
**Alternatives:**
- If only visit-based status: discrete-time survival (complementary log–log) or report success at fixed landmarks (e.g., 12-month success among those reached) with CIs.

## B3) Model diagnostics and reporting for logistic model
**Tests/Procedures:**  
- **Calibration:** calibration plot; **Brier score**; (Hosmer–Lemeshow is unstable at small n).  
- **Discrimination:** **AUC (c-statistic)** with bootstrap CI.  
- **Influence/outliers:** delta-beta or leverage diagnostics.  
**Purpose:** Evaluate model fit and reliability of predictions/associations.  
**Why appropriate:**
1. With small n, apparent associations can be driven by a few observations.
2. Improves transparency and reproducibility.  
**Why may not be appropriate:**
- Overemphasis on predictive metrics when goal is etiologic/prognostic inference; AUC unstable with few events.  
**When NOT to use:** Avoid HL test as “proof of good fit” in small samples.  
**Alternatives:** Bootstrap internal validation; penalized calibration slope.

## B4) Reliability analysis for outcome assessment (if multiple raters)
**Test:** **Cohen’s kappa** (already used), with 95% CI; consider **weighted kappa** if ordinal ratings exist.  
**Purpose:** Quantify intra-/interobserver agreement for radiographic/clinical scoring.  
**Why appropriate:**
1. Outcome includes radiographic judgments; agreement impacts validity.
2. Kappa accounts for chance agreement.  
**Why may not be appropriate:**
- Kappa affected by prevalence (high success rate can deflate kappa).  
**When NOT to use:** If only one rater or outcomes are continuous.  
**Alternative:** Gwet’s AC1 (less prevalence-sensitive) or percent agreement alongside kappa.

## B5) Sensitivity analyses for variable coding and sparse categories
**Procedure:**  
- Collapse symptom test categories (e.g., cold test 3 levels → binary delayed pain yes/no), age as continuous (if available), alternative cutpoints for EPT difference, etc.  
**Purpose:** Assess robustness to arbitrary categorization and sparse cells.  
**Why appropriate:**
1. Many predictors are categorized; small cell counts can distort estimates.
2. Demonstrates robustness of conclusions.  
**Why may not be appropriate:**
- Multiple re-codings can inflate researcher degrees of freedom if not pre-specified.  
**When NOT to use:** If done post hoc without transparency.  
**Alternative:** Pre-specify primary coding; use splines for continuous variables when sample permits (optional).

# CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL (supplementary insights)

## C1) Multinomial/ordinal modeling of VPT type determinants (process analysis)
**Model:** Multinomial logistic regression with VPT type as outcome, baseline predictors as covariates.  
**Purpose:** Describe which baseline features are associated with receiving DPC vs PP vs FP (selection mechanism).  
**Why appropriate:**
1. Helps quantify confounding/selection since VPT type is determined by intraoperative hemostasis and likely correlates with severity.
2. Supports interpretation of outcome comparisons.  
**Why may not be appropriate:**
- VPT type is largely determined intraoperatively (hemostasis time), which is not fully captured by baseline variables; limited sample.  
**When NOT to use:** If categories are too sparse or quasi-separation occurs.  
**Alternative:** Descriptive cross-tabs only.

## C2) Effect size measures beyond ORs
**Method:** Compute **risk ratio** or **risk difference** (with exact or bootstrap CIs) for VPT types; optionally marginal effects from logistic model.  
**Purpose:** Provide clinically interpretable measures (absolute risk).  
**Why appropriate:**
1. ORs can exaggerate effects when outcome is common (success ~91%).
2. Absolute differences are clinically meaningful.  
**Why may not be appropriate:** Small group sizes lead to wide CIs.  
**When NOT to use:** If readers might misinterpret unstable estimates without CIs.  
**Alternative:** Report predicted probabilities from penalized model.

## C3) Multiple testing adjustment (if many univariable comparisons)
**Method:** Holm or Benjamini–Hochberg (FDR).  
**Purpose:** Control false positives if many exploratory tests are performed.  
**Why appropriate:**
1. There are many baseline predictors; unadjusted univariable screening inflates type I error.
2. Supports transparent exploratory inference.  
**Why may not be appropriate:** Overcorrection in small samples increases type II error; primary inference should be effect-size based.  
**When NOT to use:** If analyses are strictly pre-specified and limited in number.  
**Alternative:** Emphasize CIs and avoid mass univariable testing.

## TASK 3: INAPPROPRIATE TESTS (should NOT be used)

1. **Independent-samples t-test / ANOVA on the binary outcome (success)**  
   - **Why inappropriate:** Outcome is not continuous/normal; violates model assumptions; yields uninterpretable mean differences of a 0/1 variable compared to logistic/binomial methods.

2. **Pearson chi-square test for success by VPT type when expected counts are small**  
   - **Why inappropriate:** FP failures likely very few → expected cell counts <5; chi-square approximation invalid. Prefer Fisher’s exact.

3. **Standard (MLE) multivariable logistic regression with 14 predictors given ~5 failures**  
   - **Why inappropriate:** Severe events-per-variable problem → overfitting, separation, inflated/unstable coefficients, misleading p-values and CIs. Use penalized methods + parsimonious model.

4. **Hosmer–Lemeshow test as primary evidence of “good fit”**  
   - **Why inappropriate:** Low power and unstable grouping with small n; a non-significant result does not confirm adequate calibration.

5. **Linear regression for vNRS without specifying timing/repeated measures**  
   - **Why inappropriate:** Pain likely measured over time; repeated measures require mixed models or summarized endpoints. Also vNRS is bounded/ordinal-like; distribution may be skewed.

6. **Kaplan–Meier/log-rank if failure time is not available**  
   - **Why inappropriate:** Requires time-to-event or at least interval information; otherwise results are not valid.

## TASK 4: SUMMARY (overall analytical approach justification)

Because this is a **prospective cohort** with a **binary composite clinical+radiographic success outcome** and **nonrandom assignment** to VPT types, the analysis should (i) report **precise success estimates with exact CIs**, (ii) compare crude outcomes across VPT types using **exact methods**, and (iii) evaluate prognostic factors using a **parsimonious, penalized logistic regression** to address **very few failures** and avoid overfitting. Given heterogeneous follow-up, **time-to-event methods** are recommended when failure timing is available; otherwise, use **landmark analyses** (e.g., ≥12 months) as sensitivity checks.
