### TASK 2: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

#### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY (Essential for basic analysis)

**1. Shapiro-Wilk Test**
*   **Purpose:** To assess the normality of the distribution for continuous data (Instrumentation Time).
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) It is highly sensitive and accurate for small to medium sample sizes typical in dental research. 
    2) It dictates whether parametric or non-parametric tests should be used for subsequent analysis.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** 1) In very large sample sizes, it can be overly sensitive, flagging minor deviations from normality as statistically significant.
*   **When this test should NOT be used:** When analyzing categorical or ordinal data (e.g., Quality of Obturation categories).
*   **Alternative if assumptions are violated:** Kolmogorov-Smirnov test (though generally less powerful for small samples) or visual inspection via Q-Q plots.

**2. One-Way ANOVA (Analysis of Variance) OR Independent Samples t-test**
*   **Purpose:** To compare the mean instrumentation time between the study groups. (Use t-test for 2 groups; ANOVA for 3+ groups).
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) It is the standard method for comparing continuous dependent variables across independent categorical groups. 
    2) ANOVA controls the overall Type I error rate, which would be inflated if multiple t-tests were used.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** 1) It assumes homogeneity of variances and normal distribution; endodontic time data is often skewed depending on operator experience.
*   **When this test should NOT be used:** When the continuous data is heavily skewed, or when comparing ordinal data (like cleaning capacity scores).
*   **Alternative if assumptions are violated:** Kruskal-Wallis H test (for ANOVA) or Mann-Whitney U test (for t-test).

**3. Chi-Square Test of Independence**
*   **Purpose:** To compare the frequencies of obturation quality categories (e.g., Optimal, Underfilled, Overfilled) across the different instrumentation groups.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) It is specifically designed to test for associations between two categorical variables. 
    2) It provides a straightforward interpretation of whether the proportion of optimal obturations differs by file system.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** 1) It loses statistical power and validity if the expected frequency in any cell of the contingency table is less than 5.
*   **When this test should NOT be used:** When dealing with continuous variables or when the observations are not independent (e.g., multiple canals from the same tooth treated as independent without adjustment).
*   **Alternative if assumptions are violated:** Fisher’s Exact Test.

#### CATEGORY B – RECOMMENDED (Enhances quality and rigor)

**1. Cohen’s Kappa (or Fleiss' Kappa)**
*   **Purpose:** To measure inter-examiner and intra-examiner reliability for the radiographic evaluation of obturation quality.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) Radiographic assessment is subjective; proving that examiners agree ensures the data is reliable. 
    2) It accounts for the possibility of agreement occurring by chance, unlike simple percentage agreement.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** 1) Kappa values can be paradoxically low if the prevalence of a specific category (e.g., optimal obturation) is extremely high, even if actual agreement is high.
*   **When this test should NOT be used:** When the variable being measured is continuous (e.g., measuring exact length of fill in millimeters).
*   **Alternative if assumptions are violated:** Gwet’s AC1 statistic or Percentage Agreement (with limitations).

**2. Post-Hoc Tests (e.g., Tukey's HSD or Bonferroni)**
*   **Purpose:** To determine exactly which groups differ from each other regarding instrumentation time, following a significant One-Way ANOVA.
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) ANOVA only tells you that *at least one* group is different; post-hoc tests identify the specific pairs. 
    2) They adjust the p-value to prevent Type I error inflation during multiple pairwise comparisons.
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** 1) Tukey's assumes equal variances across groups; if variances are unequal, it will yield inaccurate results.
*   **When this test should NOT be used:** When the initial ANOVA is not statistically significant, or if there are only two groups in the study.
*   **Alternative if assumptions are violated:** Games-Howell post-hoc test (if variances are unequal) or Dunn's test (if Kruskal-Wallis was used).

#### CATEGORY C – OPTIONAL (Provides supplementary insights)

**1. Multinomial Logistic Regression**
*   **Purpose:** To predict the likelihood of a specific obturation outcome (Optimal vs. Underfilled vs. Overfilled) based on the instrumentation technique, while controlling for covariates (e.g., tooth type, canal curvature).
*   **Why appropriate:** 
    1) Allows for the adjustment of confounding variables that a simple Chi-square test cannot handle. 
    2) Provides Odds Ratios, giving a quantifiable measure of effect size (e.g., "Rotary system A is 3 times more likely to yield optimal obturation than manual").
*   **Why it may not be appropriate:** 1) Requires a relatively large sample size to ensure stable estimates; small sample sizes can lead to overfitting.
*   **When this test should NOT be used:** When the outcome variable is continuous (e.g., instrumentation time).
*   **Alternative if assumptions are violated:** Ordinal Logistic Regression (if the obturation categories are strictly ranked from worst to best).

*   **Pearson Correlation Coefficient:** Inappropriate because the primary goal is to compare differences between distinct groups (manual vs. rotary), not to measure the linear relationship between two continuous variables.
*   **Linear Regression:** Inappropriate for the primary outcome (Quality of Obturation) because the outcome is categorical, not continuous.
