### CATEGORY A – MANDATORY TESTS

#### 1. Descriptive Statistics

- **Test/Method Name:** Descriptive statistics (frequencies, percentages, mean ± SD, median [IQR]).
- **Purpose:** Summarize baseline characteristics and outcomes for each group.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Essential to understand distribution and central tendency before inferential tests.
  2. Allows assessment of comparability between groups and detection of outliers/skewness.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:** Not a hypothesis test; can be misleading if misinterpreted as proof of “no difference”.
- **When NOT to use:** Never omitted; only adapt form (mean/SD vs median/IQR) depending on distribution.
- **Alternative if assumptions violated:** Use robust summaries (median, IQR, trimmed means) and graphical displays (histograms, boxplots).

---

#### 2. Comparison of Ordinal/Categorical Primary Outcome Across Groups

Assume primary outcome is obturation quality with 3–4 ordered categories (e.g., under‑filled, optimal, over‑filled, voids).

##### 2a. Chi‑square test of independence

- **Test name:** Pearson Chi‑square test.
- **Purpose:** Compare distribution of obturation quality categories across groups.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Designed for association between two categorical variables (group × obturation quality).
  2. Easy to interpret via cross‑tabulations and percentages.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Requires adequate expected cell counts (>5 in most cells); with small sample or many categories this can be violated.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Very small samples or sparse tables (expected cells <5 in >20% of cells).
  - Non‑independent observations (e.g., multiple teeth from same child analyzed as independent).
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
  - Fisher’s exact test for 2×2 or small tables.
  - Monte Carlo exact test for larger but sparse tables.
  - Use generalized estimating equations (GEE) or mixed‑effects models if clustering (multiple teeth per child) is present.

##### 2b. Ordinal logistic regression (proportional odds model)

- **Test name:** Ordinal logistic regression.
- **Purpose:** Model ordinal obturation quality as a function of instrumentation group, adjusting for covariates.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Utilizes the ordinal nature of the outcome, providing more power than treating it as nominal.
  2. Allows adjustment for confounders (e.g., tooth type, root curvature, operator) and can account for clustering if fit as mixed‑effects.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Assumes proportional odds (parallel lines)—the effect of group is constant across cutpoints.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Proportional odds assumption clearly violated (tested via Brant or similar tests).
  - Very small sample size or too few observations in each category.
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
  - Multinomial logistic regression (treat categories as nominal).
  - Partial proportional odds models.
  - Collapsing categories to binary and using logistic regression (with caution).

---

#### 3. Comparison of Continuous Secondary Outcome (Instrumentation Time)

##### 3a. One‑way ANOVA

- **Test name:** One‑way analysis of variance.
- **Purpose:** Compare mean instrumentation time among 2–3 or more groups.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Designed for continuous normally distributed outcomes with ≥2 groups.
  2. More powerful than multiple t‑tests and controls Type I error for a single comparison across groups.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Requires approximately normal distribution and homogeneity of variances in each group; independence of observations.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Strong skewness, heavy tails, or outliers that cannot be addressed with transformations.
  - Unequal variances combined with unequal group sizes.
  - Clustered data (multiple teeth per child) without adjusting for clustering.
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
  - Kruskal–Wallis test for non‑normal distributions.
  - Welch ANOVA for unequal variances.
  - Linear mixed‑effects models or GEE for clustered data.

##### 3b. Post‑hoc multiple comparison tests

- **Test name:** Tukey’s HSD (for ANOVA), or Dunn’s test (for Kruskal–Wallis).
- **Purpose:** Identify which pairs of groups differ significantly in mean instrumentation time.
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Controls family‑wise error rate when multiple pairwise comparisons are made.
  2. Provides adjusted p‑values and confidence intervals for each comparison.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Reduced power in small samples; multiple comparison procedures can be conservative.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - When ANOVA or Kruskal–Wallis does not show an overall group effect (to avoid fishing).
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
  - Pairwise t‑tests with Holm or Bonferroni correction.
  - Pairwise Wilcoxon rank‑sum tests with adjusted p‑values.

#### 4. Comparison of Binary Outcomes (e.g., presence of voids, procedural errors, acceptable vs unacceptable obturation)

##### 4a. Chi‑square / Fisher’s exact test

- **Test name:** Chi‑square test or Fisher’s exact test (for 2×2 tables).
- **Purpose:** Compare proportion of events between groups (e.g., void present vs absent).
- **Why appropriate:**
  1. Standard test for association between binary outcome and group.
  2. Fisher’s exact test is valid even in very small samples.
- **Why it may not be appropriate:**
  - Assumes independence of observations; violated if multiple canals per tooth or multiple teeth per child are treated as independent.
- **When NOT to use:**
  - Clustered data without adjustment for clustering.
- **Alternative if assumptions are violated:**
  - Logistic regression with robust (clustered) standard errors.
  - Mixed‑effects logistic regression (random intercept for patient).

## TASK 3 – INAPPROPRIATE TESTS FOR THIS TYPE OF STUDY

Below are tests that typically should NOT be used in this context and why.

1. **Independent samples t‑tests for multiple >2 group comparisons without adjustment**
   - **Why inappropriate:** Inflates family‑wise Type I error; ANOVA or regression is superior for multiple groups.

2. **Parametric tests (t‑test, ANOVA) directly on ordinal scores (e.g., 3‑category obturation quality)**
   - **Why inappropriate:** Ordinal categories do not have equal intervals; mean differences have unclear meaning. Use chi‑square, ordinal logistic regression, or non‑parametric tests instead.

3. **Simple chi‑square tests ignoring clustering when multiple teeth/canals per child**
   - **Why inappropriate:** Violates independence assumption; leads to underestimated standard errors and potentially spurious significance. Use cluster‑robust methods instead.

4. **Pearson correlation between categorical group indicator and obturation quality**
   - **Why inappropriate:** Pearson correlation assumes continuous, approximately normal variables; not suitable for nominal/ordinal category vs group.

5. **Linear regression treating a nominal outcome (multi‑category obturation score) as continuous**
   - **Why inappropriate:** Mis‑specifies link and distribution; coefficients are difficult to interpret and standard errors unreliable. Use ordinal or multinomial logistic regression.

6. **Stepwise variable selection (automatic forward/backward) in multivariable models**
   - **Why inappropriate:** Data‑driven, inflates Type I error, and produces unstable models, especially with small n typical in dental studies.

7. **Multiple unadjusted comparisons for many outcomes without any correction or prioritization**
   - **Why inappropriate:** High risk of false positives. Prefer prespecifying a primary outcome and controlling for multiplicity where needed.
