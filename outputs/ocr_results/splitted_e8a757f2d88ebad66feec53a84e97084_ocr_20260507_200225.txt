CONSORT RANDOMIZED CLINICAL TRIAL

Effect of the Piezoelectric Device on Intraoperative Hemorrhage Control and Quality of Life after Endodontic Microsurgery: A Randomized Clinical Study

Jaya Bharathi, MDS,
Shweta Mittal, MDS,
Sanjay Tewari, MDS,

SIGNIFICANCE

Piezosurgery resulted in better hemorrhage control during surgery and quality of life of patients in the first week postsurgery. Piezoelectric bone surgery might be a predictable alternative to conventional surgical carbide burs and curettes to enhance treatment acceptance by the patients because of its improved postoperative sequelae.

The postoperative quality of life of the patient depends on the level of pain, tissue swelling, drug intake, chewing ability, phonetics, and conduction of normal daily routine activities¹. Pain and swelling are the common sequelae observed immediately in the postsurgical period. The assessment of the quality of life of patients after the postsurgical period is a valuable tool in predicting postoperative symptoms². Only a few prospective studies (1 longitudinal and 3 randomized controlled trials) have evaluated the quality of life of patients after endodontic surgery²⁻⁵.

Piezoelectric bone surgery is an innovative technology permitting selective cutting of hard tissue while sparing soft tissues. Piezoelectric surgery works similar to a dental scaler with 3 to 6 times higher power. It has many advantages like precise cutting, preservation of neurovascular structures, minimal

thermal damage to bone, greater visibility,
hemorrhage control, cavitation effect, and an
overall improvement in healing$^6$.
Patients who had osteotomies with the
piezoelectric device showed significantly less
postoperative edema, trismus, and pain as
well as analgesic intake compared with
conventional rotary instruments in mandibular
third molar surgery$^7$. *In vivo* animal studies
have found better initial neo-osteogenesis with
piezosurgery compared with conventional
burs$^{8–10}$. Piezosurgery was found to be better
than conventional curettage in the enucleation
of cysts with no cyst recurrence$^{11,12}$.
Also, 1 of the major concerns during
periapical surgery is the attainment of
adequate hemostasis. This will improve
visibility, reduce operating time, facilitate
surgical procedures (root-end resection, root
end preparation, and filling), and minimize
surgical hemorrhage. The bleeding inside the
bony crypt also must be controlled for proper
sealing of retrofilling material, which may affect
the outcome of endodontic surgery. Few
studies in oral surgery also reported that
piezosurgery results in less bleeding compared
with standard surgical burs$^{13,14}$.
The application of the piezoelectric
device has been used recently in 2 case
reports to create a bony window on buccal
bone in cases of the intact buccal cortical plate
with large periapical lesions$^{15,16}$. The
management of both hard and soft tissues with
the piezoelectric device might limit
postoperative symptoms and discomfort,
which may enhance the patient’s quality of life
and acceptance of endodontic surgery,
although hemorrhage control during surgery
may enable the surgeon to perform the
surgical procedures with greater ease. Hence,
the aim of this study was to evaluate whether
the use of piezoelectric surgery might have an
impact on quality of life during the first week
postsurgery. The secondary aim was to
compare intraoperative hemorrhage control
between piezosurgery versus a carbide bur.

**MATERIALS AND METHODS**

The clinical trial was performed after approval
by the institutional ethics committee (PGIDS/
IEC/2018/26) in the postgraduate section of
the Department of Conservative Dentistry and
Endodontics, Postgraduate Institute of Dental
Sciences, Rohtak, India. Patients requiring
endodontic surgery were enrolled from
December 2018–June 2019. The trial was
registered on clinicaltrials.gov with ID:
NCT04358887.

**Patient Inclusion**

The eligibility criteria included patients who
were medically fit for oral surgical procedures
(American Society of Anesthesiologists 1 or 2),
patients 16–55 years of age, and patients with
the diagnosis of symptomatic/asymptomatic
apical periodontitis in previously treated teeth
with a large (≥5 mm) periapical lesion and with
radiographic evidence of an intact buccal
cortical plate on cone-beam computed
tomographic (CBCT) imaging. The root canal
treatment of the involved teeth was adequate
as observed on both periapical radiography
and CBCT imaging. Maxillary and mandibular
anterior teeth with periradicular lesions of
endodontic origin and a size <20 mm were
included. The tooth had an appropriate final
restoration with no coronal leakage.
Patients with systemic diseases
(diabetes mellitus, uncontrolled hypertension
grade III, hepatic/renal disease, or systemic
bleeding disorders), pregnant and lactating
women, and patients on anticoagulant/
antiplatelet drugs were excluded. Teeth that
were unrestorable, fractured/perforated teeth,
teeth with apicomarginal defects, and teeth
with deep pockets (probing depth >4 mm)
were also excluded. Information regarding the
surgical procedure and its associated risks
and benefits and the necessary follow-up care
were provided to all the patients in written
form, and those who gave consent were
included in the study.

**Sample Size**

Sample size calculation was based on
postoperative pain (which was considered the
most important manifestation affecting the
quality of life)$^2$. Based on data obtained from a
previous study with a power of 80%, a
significance level equal to 5%, a clinically
significant difference of 0.8, and pooled
standard deviation of 0.824, 16 patients for
each group were needed$^2$. Assuming an
attrition rate of 20%, a total of 40 patients (21
women and 19 men with 74 teeth) were
included, 20 patients in each group.

**Randomization**

Forty patients (74 teeth) were randomized
equally to both the piezo ($n = 20$) and the
control groups ($n = 20$) using a block of an
equal number of opaque envelopes containing
concealed codes. Patients were asked to pick
the envelope before the start of the surgical
procedure. Patients were blinded for surgical
allotment. The sham surgical setup was
created by the placement of a piezoelectric
surgical device for both groups during surgery
for blinding of independent observers for
intraoperative bleeding assessment. Operator
blinding was not possible because of the use
of a piezoelectric surgical device in 1 group.

**Surgical Procedure**

All clinical procedures were performed by a
single operator. Except for incision, flap
elevation, and suturing, all surgical procedures
were performed under an operating
microscope (OPMI PICO; Carl Zeiss,
Göttingen, Germany; with $f_{tube} = 170$, $f_{lens} =$
250, and $m_{eyepiece} = 12.5$) in an operating
room. Low magnification of 0.4–0.6$\times$ was
used for orientation and the alignment of
surgical tips, midrange magnification of 1.0–
1.6$\times$ for surgical procedures and
retropreparations, and high magnification of
1.6–2.5$\times$ for observation of fine details like
inspection of the resected root surface.
A full-thickness mucoperiosteal flap was
reflected under sterile conditions after
administering local anesthesia with lidocaine
2% with epinephrine 1:1,00,000. After flap
elevation, in the piezo group, bone cutting was
performed using the piezoelectric surgical
device (Woodpecker Ultrasurgery USII,
Guangxi, China) with a power input of 100–240
V (50 Hz/60 Hz), a working frequency of 24–36
kHz, and an irrigation flow rate of 25–110 mL/
min. The entry point on intact bone was
estimated preoperatively using CBCT imaging.
The osteotomy was performed using a US6
piezoelectric tip (Woodpecker Ultrasurgery
USII, Guangxi, China) set in a bone mode in a
linear to-and-fro motion with continuous
ejection of sterile saline preventing heat buildup
(Fig. 1$A$). After that, refinement of the ragged
edges was performed using a UL 4 diamond
insert. Piezo tips with different angles like UL1
(120°), UL2, and UL5 (95°) (Woodpecker
Ultrasurgery USII, Guangxi, China) were used
to separate the lining of the cyst or granulation
tissue from the bone cavity (Fig. 1$B$).
In the control group, after flap elevation,
bone cutting was performed using a surgical
carbide bur (#557 SL; Brasseler USA,
Savannah, GA) (Fig. 1$D$). Complete
debridement of the bony lesion was performed
by surgical curettes (Fig. 1$E$).
After curettage, the principal surgeon
and 2 blinded observers recorded the level of
hemorrhage independently. Once the
recording was completed for additional
hemorrhage control during surgery,
epinephrine-soaked cotton pellets were
applied topically as and when required.
Root-end resection of 2–3 mm with a
0°–10° angle was performed using a US6

Root-end filling was performed with mineral trioxide aggregate (Dentsply, York, PA). The wound site was closed and sutured with a 5-0 silk suture after irrigating the defect properly.

**Postoperative Instructions**
The patients were advised not to gargle and to avoid hard, hot, and spicy foods; heavy physical activity; smoking; and tooth brushing on the day of surgery. Patients were prescribed chlorhexidine digluconate 0.2% mouthwash to rinse their mouth twice daily up to 1 week postsurgically. Nonsteroidal analgesics (ibuprofen 400 mg) were advised for the relief of pain/swelling control after the surgical procedure if needed. No antibiotics were prescribed. Patients were recalled after 5 days for suture removal.

The quality of life of patients was evaluated
for limitations of oral and general functions, pain,
and other symptoms. For limitations of oral and
general functions, a modified version of the
patient’s perception questionnaire provided by
Del Fabbro et al was used$^4$. Current instruments
of quality of life mainly focus to assess the impact
on the quality of life of various chronic oral
conditions and treatments. The patient’s
perception questionnaire focuses on
postoperative signs and symptoms after surgical
procedures that may influence the patient’s
quality of life$^4$. The questionnaire was given to all
patients to evaluate postoperative restraint in
function (mouth opening, chewing, speaking,
sleeping, daily routine, and work) and the
existence of any symptoms (swelling, nausea,
and bad taste/breath), which were evaluated by a
5-point Likert type scale ranging from 1–5 (1 =
not at all, 2 = very little, 3 = some, 4 = quite a bit,
and 5 = very much). For pain assessment, a
visual analog scale (VAS) was used where 0 = no
pain and 100 = the worst possible pain. All the
patients were given training regarding filling out
the questionnaire and VAS and recording
symptoms. The patients were given a validated
questionnaire in his or her local language, and the
recording of grading of symptoms was explained
to each participant by the single analyzer.
Furthermore, they were advised to contact the
analyzer in case of any doubts or clarification.
They were also given the telephone number of
the analyzer in case of doubt while filling out the
form at home, and patients were called at least
twice during a 1-week period as a reminder and
also to enquire whether they were facing any
doubts. Finally, patients were asked to note the
number of analgesics taken each day until 7
days. Patients were asked to fill out the
questionnaire every day, starting from the day of
surgery up to 7 days postoperatively.
Questionnaires were returned by the patient after
7 days.
