**The effect of two calcium silicate-based and one epoxy resin-based root canal sealer on postoperative pain: a randomized controlled trial**

**T. Aslan¹ ID & H. Dönmez Özkan² ID**
¹Department of Endodontics, Faculty of Dentistry, Erciyes University, Kayseri; and ²Department of Endodontics, Faculty of Dentistry, Aydın Adnan Menderes University, Efeler, Turkey

**Introduction**

Postoperative pain arising after root canal treatment is undesirable but may manifest in hours and may persist for days following treatment (Wang *et al.* 2010). The incidence of post-treatment endodontic pain has been reported to be between 3 and 58% (Sathorn *et al.* 2008, Gondim *et al.* 2010) with severe pain being observed at a rate of less than 12% of the entire population after treatment (Ng *et al.* 2004). Acute inflammation was thought to be one of the main reasons for post-treatment endodontic severe pain, that occurs following chemical, mechanical or microbial injuries in periapical tissue (Seltzer & Naidorf 2004). Inappropriate instrumentation or filling of the root canals has been ascribed as one of the factors predisposing to postoperative pain (Goreva & Petrikas 2004). Calcium silicate-based endodontic sealers promote periapical tissue healing by reducing inflammation and inducing the differentiation of odontoblasts and mineralization in preosteoblasts (Lee et al. 2019). In an in vitro study, calcium silicate-based endodontic sealers were reported to induce less severe cytotoxic effects compared to their resin-based counterpart AH Plus (Dentsply DeTrey GmbH, Konstanz, Germany; Zhang et al. 2010). In contrast, in a recent randomized clinical trial, the use of a bioceramic sealer or an epoxy resin sealer (AH Plus) during root canal treatment was reported to lead to similar postoperative pain in teeth with acute apical periodontitis (Graunaite et al. 2018).
EndoSeal MTA (Maruchi, Wonju, Korea) has been marketed as a pozzolan-based MTA sealer. Previous reports have demonstrated that this injectable biotight root canal sealer provides satisfactory biological and physical properties (Seo et al. 2019) and adequate bond strength to dentine (Silva et al. 2016). However, to date, the effect of this sealer on postoperative pain has never been investigated. Furthermore, there are few studies in the literature regarding the potential effects of calcium silicate-based root canal sealer on postoperative pain. Therefore, the present study aimed to evaluate the effect of two calcium silicate-based root canal sealers, Endoseal MTA and EndoSequence BC Sealer, on postoperative pain following single-visit root canal treatment on molar teeth compared to their epoxy/amine resin-based counterpart AH Plus. The null hypotheses tested in this study were as follows:
1. The type of sealer used would not change the incidence and the intensity of post-treatment endodontic pain
2. The analgesic intake of patients following single-visit root canal treatment would not differ amongst the experimental groups.

Materials and methods
The Ethics Committee of Erciyes University of Medical Sciences (Decision no: 2018/08) approved this randomized prospective clinical trial. The study protocol was recorded in www.ClinicalTrials.gov databases, with the NCT number (NCT/03919318). The required minimum sample size was calculated using the G*Power v.3.1.9.2 program (Heinrich Heine, Düsseldorf University, Düsseldorf, Germany) according to the data of a previous study (Alonso-Ezpeleta et al. 2012). An alpha-type error of 0.05 and a beta power of 0.85 was specified, and the minimal estimated sample size for each group was computed as 24 to determine the postoperative pain differences between three study groups. To increase the statistical power of this study and considering potential patient dropouts, it was decided to enrol 32 patients per group. Therefore, a total of 96 patients were included in the study.
The patients were selected from those who were referred to the Hospitals of Dental Faculty of Erciyes University, Kayseri, Turkey and Dental Faculty of Adnan Menderes University, Aydın, Turkey for root canal treatment from February 2018 to July 2019. Only patients who had mandibular first and second molar teeth diagnosed with asymptomatic irreversible pulpitis due to deep caries were included. The clinical diagnosis of asymptomatic irreversible pulpitis was based on the presence of a deep carious lesion that would cause a large pulp exposure during its removal and in the absence of clinical and radiographic pathosis and symptoms. Additional identifiers for this diagnosis were that after exposing the pulp, profuse bleeding of the pulp having a thick consistency and an inability to achieve haemostasis within 2–3 min. Inclusion and exclusion criteria are listed in Table 1. All participants were informed accordingly and signed a written informed consent form.

Treatment procedures
All procedures were performed by two specialists having at least 10 years of clinical experience in the university clinics. Throughout the entire procedure, the operators used dental operating loupes at ×4 magnification. All patients received a single-visit root canal treatment to limit the potential pain-inducing factors that might be caused by multiple visits. During the diagnostic examination, thermal tests (EndoIce; Coltene/Whaledent Inc, Altstätten, Switzerland) and electric pulp tests (Parkell, Edgewood, NY, USA) were performed to determine pulp sensibility.
Inferior alveolar nerve block anaesthesia (4% articaine hydrochloride with 1 : 100 000 adrenalin) was performed. None of the patients required supplementary anaesthesia. **Evaluation of postoperative pain**

After treatment, two forms were given to the participants. The first form designed to record pain levels was based on the Visual Analog Scale (VAS), where VAS pain levels were indicated on the chart by the participants. The value "0" was defined as "no pain," and "100" was defined as "unbearable pain."

In the second form, participants were asked to report the frequency of analgesic drug intake. The patients were asked to choose one of the three options: "0: No pain, or pain which does not require the use of analgesics," "1: Moderate pain which can be well controlled by the use of analgesics and does not affect daily activities or sleep," "2: Impaired daily activities due to unbearable pain that cannot be controlled by the use of analgesics." Participants were asked to complete these two forms at 6, 12, 24 and 48 h after treatment and on the 3rd, 4th, 5th, 6th and 7th days. A phone call was made every day for 7 days to obtain information on the postoperative pain and the frequency of analgesic intake. The patients were asked to call the contact number on the form if they encountered severe pain or if they needed to ask any questions regarding the treatment.
