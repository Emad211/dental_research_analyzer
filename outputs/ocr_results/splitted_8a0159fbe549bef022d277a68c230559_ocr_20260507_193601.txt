Evaluating Combined Effect of Oral Premedication with Ibuprofen and Dexamethasone on Success of Inferior Alveolar Nerve Block in Mandibular Molars with Symptomatic Irreversible Pulpitis: A Prospective, Double-blind, Randomized Clinical Trial

SIGNIFICANCE

A combination of preoperative ibuprofen 800 mg and dexamethasone 0.5 mg can improve the success rate of inferior alveolar nerve block in patients with symptomatic irreversible pulpitis in mandibular molars.

KEY WORDS

Dexamethasone; ibuprofen; inferior alveolar nerve block; local anesthesia; premedication; symptomatic irreversible pulpitis

Endodontic pain management mainly relies on successful local anesthesia. Inferior alveolar nerve block (IANB) is the standard injection technique to achieve local anesthesia for endodontic treatment in mandibular teeth. However, the success rate of IANB decreases drastically in patients with irreversibly inflamed pulps. Various clinical studies have reported an IANB failure rate between 43% and 83%¹⁻⁴. pulpitis (SIP) of mandibular molars$^{1-5}$. In irreversible pulpitis, breakdown of damaged cell membranes and release of arachidonic acid (AA) occur. This AA is acted on by cyclooxygenase (COX) and lipoxygenase enzymes and gets converted into biologically active prostaglandins (PGs) and leukotrienes, respectively. The greater level of PGs can affect the tetrodotoxin resistance receptors and slack off the nerve response to the anesthetic solution. PGs also sensitize nerve endings to bradykinins and histamines, causing allodynia and hyperalgesia$^{6-8}$. Leukotrienes act synergistically with PGs, thereby increasing local infiltration of neutrophils and considerably reducing the pain threshold.

One effective way of counteracting pulpal inflammation is preoperative administration of nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs. Ibuprofen has been rated as the safest and most effective nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drug in endodontic pain. Various studies have investigated the effect of preoperative ibuprofen on the success rate of IANB$^{9-19}$. Although some studies have shown significant improvement$^{9-13}$, others have reported no effect of ibuprofen on the efficacy of IANB in patients with SIP of mandibular molars$^{14-19}$.

Glucocorticoids have been used for pain management in endodontics before, during, and after treatment because of their potent anti-inflammatory properties$^{20-23}$. Very few endodontic studies have used preoperative dexamethasone orally or as buccal infiltration, but improved outcomes were noticed only with oral administration$^{9,14,24}$. Therefore, the use of preoperative dexamethasone will provide more data regarding its effect on enhancing the success of IANB.

It seems logical to assume that preoperative administration of ibuprofen and dexamethasone together will augment the anti-inflammatory effect and result in enhanced anesthetic success in teeth with irreversibly inflamed pulps. To the best of our knowledge, no study so far has evaluated their combined effect on the efficacy of IANB in mandibular molars with SIP. Therefore, the aim of this study was to determine the combined effect of preoperative ibuprofen and dexamethasone on the success rate of IANB in cases of mandibular molars with SIP. The null hypothesis was that there will be no effect of the combination of ibuprofen and dexamethasone on the success of IANB in mandibular molars with SIP.

**MATERIALS AND METHODS**

This prospective, double-blind, randomized clinical study was approved by the Institutional Review and Ethical Committee of JCD Dental College, Sirsa, India (approval number JCDV/DC/18/2152). Ninety-four adult patients were randomly selected from among the emergency patients referred to the Department of Conservative Dentistry and Endodontics. Written informed consent was obtained from each patient. All included patients were experiencing moderate to severe pain (55–170 mm readings on the Heft-Parker visual analog scale [VAS]) in mandibular molars, had a normal periapical radiographic appearance, were in good health (American Society of Anesthesiologists class I or II), and had not taken any analgesics for at least 12 hours before enrollment in the study. Patients under 18 years old; patients with allergies and hypersensitivity to medications, local anesthetic solution, or its components used in the study; pregnant or nursing women; immunocompromised patients; patients with active sites of pathosis in the area of injection; and patients unable to give consent were excluded from the study. The clinical diagnosis of SIP was confirmed with electric pulp testing (Digitest; Parkell Inc, Brentwood, NY) and cold testing (Endo Ice 1,1,1,2 tetrafluoroethane; Coltene, which resulted in an early and prolonged painful response.
